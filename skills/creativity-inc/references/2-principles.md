# Key Principles — Protecting the New

> Source: Creativity, Inc., Chapters 2, 4, 6, 7, Starting Points

## Eight Operating Principles

### 1. The Team Over the Idea
"Give a good idea to a mediocre team, and they'll screw it up. Give a mediocre idea to a great team, and they'll either fix it or come up with something better." This is Catmull's foundational belief. Pixar hires for talent and passion first, trusting that good people will generate good ideas.

### 2. The Ugly Baby Principle
Every original creation starts ugly — awkward, unformed, vulnerable. Like a caterpillar in a cocoon, early ideas need protection from forces that would destroy them. The natural impulse is to compare early work to finished masterpieces, which is unfair and destructive. Leaders must actively shield "ugly babies" from premature judgment.

### 3. Candor is a System, Not a Value
You can't just say "be candid" and expect it to happen. Candor requires structural mechanisms — the Braintrust, Notes Day, dailies — that make it safe and expected. Without systems, fear wins.

### 4. Failure as R&D
"Mistakes aren't a necessary evil. They aren't evil at all. They are an inevitable consequence of doing something new." Catmull treats the cost of failure as an investment in the future. The goal is not to avoid failure but to fail early and learn fast.

### 5. Protection is Active, Not Passive
Protecting the new doesn't mean leaving things alone. It means actively intervening to shield vulnerable ideas from the hungry beast of production pressure, premature evaluation, and risk aversion.

### 6. Balance is Dynamic
The push-pull between different constituencies — story, art, technology, finance, marketing — should be constant and productive. No single group should win. A healthy culture is one where all constituencies feel heard but none dominates.

### 7. Confront the Hidden
The most dangerous problems are the ones you can't see — the table that silences junior staff, the success that blinds leadership, the fear that no one admits. Leaders must actively look for what's hidden.

### 8. Steve Jobs' Warning
When Toy Story 3 was going smoothly, Jobs warned Catmull: "Watch out. That's a dangerous place to be." Smooth means you're not seeing the problems, which means they're compounding silently.

> **Case: Finding Nemo's Failed Pre-Plan** (Chapter 7)
> After Toy Story 2's painful reboot and Monsters, Inc.'s five-year production, Pixar tried to finalize Finding Nemo's script before production began. Andrew Stanton's brilliant pitch convinced everyone this would work. It didn't. The flashback structure confused test audiences. The chronology had to be reworked mid-production. The "plan first, build later" approach failed completely.
> **Key takeaway**: You cannot plan your way out of creative problems. The messy iterative process is not a bug — it's the feature.

> **Case: Monsters, Inc.'s Long Journey** (Chapter 6)
> Pete Docter's original concept for Monsters, Inc. was about a 30-year-old man visited by monsters representing his childhood fears. Through years of iteration — the human became a 6-year-old girl named Mary, then a boy, then a girl named Boo — the story eventually found its true form. Each wrong turn led closer to the right answer.
> **Key takeaway**: Exploring the neighborhood — trying wrong approaches — is necessary to discover the right one. There is no shortcut.
