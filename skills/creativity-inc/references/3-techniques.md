# Techniques & Practices — Taming the Beast

> Source: Creativity, Inc., Chapters 7, 10, 11, 13, Starting Points

## Practical Mechanisms for Creative Culture

### 1. Notes Day
An annual company-wide event where all Pixar employees gather to discuss problems facing the studio. Groups are deliberately mixed across departments — animators sit with accountants, marketers with engineers. Every group reports its findings to the whole company. The purpose: surface hidden problems and model candor from top to bottom.

### 2. The Braintrust Meeting Protocol
| Element | Specification |
|---------|--------------|
| Cadence | Every 3-6 months per project |
| Attendance | Directors, writers, heads of story — no executives with authority |
| Input | Watch the current reel, then discuss |
| Output | Notes only — no mandated fixes |
| Tone | Candor expected, personal attacks forbidden |
| Follow-up | Director decides what to act on |

### 3. Postmortems Without Blame
After every film, Pixar conducts a postmortem. The rule: it's about the process, not the people. No finger-pointing. The goal is to understand what worked, what didn't, and why. Findings are shared openly so the next team can avoid the same mistakes.

### 4. Dailies (The Creative Check-in)
Every morning, animators screen their work-in-progress to colleagues. The ritual serves two purposes: it forces people to show unfinished work (normalizing imperfection) and it creates a daily habit of giving and receiving feedback in low-stakes doses.

### 5. The "Beast-Taming" Framework
| Problem | Symptom | Solution |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Beast overpowers Baby | All resources go to production, none to R&D | Create dedicated innovation budgets and "skunkworks" time |
| Schedule drives quality | "We need to ship" becomes the only metric | Push back on deadlines by showing the cost of premature release |
| Marketing dictates creative | "Proven formulas" dominate | Give creative teams a protected "no-marketing zone" during early development |
| Finance optimizes cost instead of value | Low-risk derivative projects get funded | Use separate evaluation criteria for exploratory vs. production projects |

### 6. The Coexistence Strategy
Recognize that the Beast is not evil — it provides deadlines, urgency, and resources. The goal is not to kill the Beast but to keep it balanced against the Baby. Practical tactics:
- Reserve a percentage of resources for high-risk, high-reward projects
- Give creative leads a "veto on the schedule" during early development
- Rotate production and creative staff through each other's reviews
- Institute "no-comment" periods where new ideas can develop without scrutiny

> **Case: Disney's Post-Lion King Decline** (Chapter 7)
> After The Lion King grossed $952M worldwide, Disney expanded its animation infrastructure — studios in Burbank, Florida, France, Australia. The "feed the Beast" mentality took over: every studio needed product, so the schedule drove output. For 16 years (1994-2010), not a single Disney animated film opened at #1. The employees had the same talent and desire — but the system was optimized for throughput, not quality.
> **Key takeaway**: Success creates the Beast. Without deliberate counter-balance, the Beast will cannibalize the creative culture that created the success in the first place.

> **Case: Cars 2 and Monsters University Director Changes** (Chapter 6)
> After a string of 11 mostly smooth productions, Pixar hit two consecutive misfires. Cars 2 and Monsters University both required replacing the original directors mid-production. These were painful, costly moves. But because they caught the problems before release, the losses were contained. Catmull framed these as R&D investments.
> **Key takeaway**: When you catch failure early, it's learning, not loss. The cost of fixing a problem mid-production is always lower than the cost of releasing a bad product.
