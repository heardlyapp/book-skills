# The Braintrust Model — Candor-Driven Peer Feedback

> Source: Creativity, Inc., Chapters 5-6, Introduction

## Core Concept

The Braintrust is Pixar's primary mechanism for straight talk. It's a group of smart, passionate people who meet every few months to assess each movie in production. The premise is simple: put the right people in a room, charge them with identifying and solving problems, and encourage them to be candid with one another.

### Three Structural Principles

| Principle | What it means | Why it matters |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|
| Peer-only composition | Members are directors, writers, and storytellers — peers of the filmmaker | Feedback comes from people who've been through the process, not from hierarchical superiors |
| Zero authority to mandate | The Braintrust can suggest anything but can order nothing | The director owns the decision, which removes fear and defensiveness |
| Focus on problems, not solutions | The group diagnoses what's wrong but doesn't prescribe the fix | Surface the root cause; let the director find the path forward |

### Candor vs. Honesty

Catmull makes a crucial distinction: "Candor" carries less moral baggage than "honesty." People feel freer to be candid because nobody thinks being less than candid makes them a bad person. This small language shift lowers the psychological barrier to real feedback.

### The Iterative Reel Cycle

1. Storyboard the script → edit together with temp voices/music → create a "reel" (crude mock-up)
2. Braintrust watches the reel and discusses what's not ringing true
3. Director decides what to address and how
4. New version of the movie is generated every 3-6 months
5. Repeat until the story finds its throughline

> **Case: Toy Story 2 Crisis** (Chapter 5, Braintrust origins)
> In 1999, Toy Story 2 was in crisis — the story was broken, the team was burnt out. The original five directors (Lasseter, Stanton, Docter, Unkrich, Ranft) gathered to reboot the film. Their meetings were brutally candid, completely focused on the project, and free of personal agendas. The term "Braintrust" was born during this period. The film went on to be a critical and commercial triumph.
> **Key takeaway**: The Braintrust emerged organically from a crisis, not from a top-down mandate — an important lesson for teams trying to build their own.

> **Case: The West One Table** (Chapter 1)
> For 13 years, Pixar held meetings around a long, skinny table in the West One conference room. The table created a de facto hierarchy — people at the center spoke, people at the edges stayed silent. Leaders didn't notice because they sat at the center. Only when they moved to a square table in a smaller room did they realize how the physical space had been sabotaging their candor.
> **Key takeaway**: Physical environment shapes communication dynamics in ways leaders can't see from their privileged position. The space must be designed for equality, not convenience.
