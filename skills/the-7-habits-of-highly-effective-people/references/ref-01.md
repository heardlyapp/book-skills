# Reference: Be Proactive

## Overview
Habit 1 is the foundation of the 7 Habits. Between stimulus and response, there is a space. In that space is our freedom to choose our response.

## Case: The Stimulus-Response Model
Covey begins with the work of Viktor Frankl. Frankl, a psychiatrist who survived Nazi concentration camps, discovered that even in the most degrading conditions, he could choose his response. This insight — that between stimulus and response there is a space, and in that space is our freedom to choose — is the foundation of proactivity.

## Case: Circle of Influence vs. Circle of Concern
Proactive people focus on their Circle of Influence (things they can control). Reactive people focus on their Circle of Concern (things they cannot control). The reactive person's energy is consumed by worrying about things outside their control, which shrinks their Circle of Influence. The proactive person's energy is directed at things they can control, which expands their Circle of Influence.

## Case: Proactive Language
Reactive language: "There's nothing I can do," "That's just the way I am," "He makes me so mad." Proactive language: "Let's look at our alternatives," "I can choose a different approach," "I control my own feelings." Language is not just a reflection of our attitude — it shapes it. Changing your language is one of the most powerful ways to become more proactive.

## Case: The Commitment Power
Proactive people make commitments and keep them. The ability to make and keep promises — to yourself and others — is the foundation of personal integrity. Each kept commitment builds self-trust, which is the foundation of trust with others.
