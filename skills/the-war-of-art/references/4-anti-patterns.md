> Source: The War of Art, Book One "Resistance" & Book Three "The Higher Realm"

# Anti-Patterns: What Resistance Looks Like

## ❌ Waiting for Inspiration
"I'll write when I feel inspired." "I'll paint when the muse visits." This is Resistance at its most seductive. The professional knows that inspiration follows work, not the reverse. Waiting for inspiration is a form of creative suicide.

## ❌ Overpreparing
"I need to do more research." "I need more training." "I need to read more books first." Preparation can be a legitimate part of the creative process — or it can be Resistance in disguise. Ask yourself honestly: is this preparation necessary, or is it avoidance?

> **Case: The Perpetual Student** (Book One): Pressfield tells of a writer who had taken every creative writing course available, read every craft book, attended every conference. He had been "preparing to write" for seven years. He had never submitted a single piece for publication. His entire writing career was preparation. The preparation was the Resistance.
> **Key takeaway:** Training can become a form of avoidance. At some point, you have to stop preparing and start doing. The only bad creative work is the work you don't do.

## ❌ Perfectionism
"I can't share this until it's perfect." Perfectionism is not a commitment to quality — it's a commitment to never finishing. The professional produces imperfect work and improves it through iteration. The amateur tries to make it perfect on the first attempt and never finishes at all.

## ❌ Comparing Yourself to Others
"His work is so much better than mine." "She's more talented." "I'll never be that good." Comparison is Resistance's favorite weapon. The professional competes only with themselves. The only question is: is my work better today than it was yesterday?

## ❌ Seeking Validation
"I need someone else to tell me this is good before I can believe it." External validation is a trap. The professional knows whether the work is good because they did the work. They trust their own judgment.

## ❌ Multitasking
"I'll write while checking email." "I'll paint while watching TV." Divided attention produces divided results. The professional gives the work full attention, or none at all.

The key insight: Resistance is not your enemy trying to destroy you. It is your guardian trying to protect you. It fears the changes that creative work will bring. It wants to keep you safe in the familiar. But safety is the enemy of creation. To create is to risk. The professional accepts this and creates anyway.


> **Case: The Entrepreneur's Fear** (Book One): Pressfield describes an entrepreneur who had a brilliant business idea but couldn't execute because he was terrified of failure. Resistance manifested as endless market research, competitor analysis, and business plan revisions. Years passed. Someone else executed the same idea and succeeded. The entrepreneur's fear had been Resistance protecting him - protecting him from the risk of success.
> **Key takeaway:** Sometimes Resistance protects us not from failure but from success. The fear of succeeding can be more powerful than the fear of failing. Recognize this and act anyway.

The only way through fear is action. Resistance wants you to think your way out. You can't. You have to act your way out. Every creative breakthrough in history was preceded by someone acting despite fear.
