> Source: The War of Art, Book Two "Turning Pro" & Book Three "The Higher Realm"

# Techniques: Practical Creative Methods

## Technique 1: The 15-Minute Start
Commit to 15 minutes of creative work. That's all. Set a timer. Work for exactly 15 minutes. When the timer rings, you can stop. Most of the time, you won't — you'll keep going. But even if you stop, you've done 15 minutes more than the amateur.

## Technique 2: The Morning Pages
Before any other work, write three pages of stream-of-consciousness. No editing. No judgment. Just write. This clears the mind and defeats Resistance before it can organize its attack.

> **Case: The Novelist's Routine** (Book Two): Pressfield describes a novelist who writes every morning from 5:30 to 7:30, before the rest of the world wakes up. During these two hours, he is unreachable. No phone, no email, no interruptions. He has trained his brain to expect creative work at this time. The ritual itself is more important than the content.
> **Key takeaway:** A consistent time and place for creative work trains your brain to enter creative mode on command. The ritual is as important as the work itself.



> **Case: The Painter's Ritual** (Book Three): A painter Pressfield knew had a specific ritual before each session: she would set up her easel, mix her paints, light a candle, and bow to the canvas. The ritual took 15 minutes. It was her way of telling Resistance: "I'm here. I'm ready. Do your worst." The ritual itself was an act of war against Resistance.
> **Key takeaway:** A pre-work ritual signals to your brain and to Resistance that creative time has begun. It transforms the transition from "regular life" to "creative mode."

## Technique 3: The Commitment Calendar
Mark your creative work time on your calendar. Treat it as a non-negotiable appointment. If someone asks you to do something else during that time, tell them you have a prior commitment. You do — to your creative work.

## Technique 4: Finish Something
The amateur starts many projects and finishes none. The professional finishes. Even if the result is imperfect, finishing is the victory. A finished imperfect work is worth more than an unfinished perfect one.

## Technique 5: The Daily Victory Log
At the end of each creative session, write down what you accomplished, no matter how small. This builds evidence against Resistance's argument that you're not making progress. Small victories, recorded daily, compound into an unshakeable creative practice.

## Technique 6: The Hierarchy of Resistance
Rank your creative Resistance from most powerful to least. The most resistant task is usually the one that matters most. Do that one first, when your Resistance-fighting energy is highest.

Each time you defeat Resistance, you grow stronger. Each time you give in, Resistance grows stronger. This is why the daily practice is so important. Consistency builds creative muscle. Intermittent effort keeps it weak. The choice is made every day, in the first moments of your creative practice.
