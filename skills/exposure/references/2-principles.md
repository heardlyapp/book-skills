# 2 — Principles of Corporate Accountability

> Source: "Exposure" by Robert Bilott

## Principle 1: Follow the Documents

Bilott's entire case was built on paper. DuPont had generated decades of internal memos, reports, and studies documenting what they knew about C8's toxicity. The key was forcing them to produce these documents through discovery.

> **Case: The Document War** (Chapters 8-12): DuPont resisted document production for years, claiming attorney-client privilege, trade secrets, and undue burden. Bilott spent years fighting motion after motion to compel. Eventually, over 110,000 pages were produced — and they showed exactly what DuPont had known and when they knew it. The paper trail was the case.
> **Key takeaway**: Corporations generate massive documentation. The truth is in the files. The legal system's discovery process is the tool to extract it.

## Principle 2: Science Can Be Bought — But So Can Independent Science

DuPont funded dozens of studies designed to find C8 safe. They manipulated the EPA's science advisory board. But the C8 Science Panel — an independent body of epidemiologists appointed by the court — found the truth.

> **Case: The C8 Science Panel** (Chapter 15): Under the class action settlement, a panel of three independent scientists was appointed to determine whether C8 was linked to human disease. Their job was not to assign blame — only to answer the question: does C8 cause disease? Over years of analysis, they found probable links to kidney cancer, testicular cancer, thyroid disease, ulcerative colitis, and pregnancy-induced hypertension. This finding triggered the personal injury lawsuits.
> **Key takeaway**: Independent science is the ultimate weapon against corporate-funded pseudoscience. The structure matters: the panel was court-appointed and DuPont could not influence its membership.

## Principle 3: Corporate Secrets Have Expiration Dates

DuPont kept its C8 secret for 50+ years. But secrets are like pressure — they eventually find a release valve. A farmer with a camcorder, a lawyer who grew up in the town, a court order, 110,000 documents — the truth came out.

> **Case: The EPA Letter** (Chapter 5): In 2001, Bilott filed an administrative complaint with the EPA, laying out the evidence DuPont had hidden for decades. This triggered a federal investigation. The EPA ultimately fined DuPont $16.5M for failing to report the dangers of C8 — the largest civil administrative penalty the EPA had ever obtained.
> **Key takeaway**: Sometimes you can't win in court alone. Use every forum: state court, federal agency, public opinion. Each pressure point weakens the wall of secrecy.

## Principle 4: The Most Vulnerable Are Always the First Victims

The Parkersburg community was poor, rural, and politically powerless. DuPont was the largest employer. People were afraid to speak up. The contamination hit the poorest residents hardest — those on well water, unable to afford bottled water, reliant on the land.

> **Case: The Cattle Team** (Chapter 4): DuPont sent a "Cattle Team" to investigate Earl Tennant's dying cows. The team included DuPont employees and a paid veterinarian. Their conclusion: the cows were dying because Earl was a bad farmer. Nothing to do with DuPont. This "independent" report was used to discredit Earl for years — until Bilott's discovery revealed that the team had found high levels of C8 in Earl's cows and had suppressed the data.
> **Key takeaway**: When a company investigates itself, the result is preordained. Never trust a corporate "investigation" of its own wrongdoing. Always demand independent oversight.
