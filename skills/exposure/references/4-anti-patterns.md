# 4 — Anti-Patterns: What Lets Corporate Pollution Continue

> Source: "Exposure" by Robert Bilott

## Anti-Pattern 1: Denial and Delay

**The mistake:** When confronted with evidence of harm, the first response is always to deny, delay, and deflect. DuPont did this for 50+ years.

> **Case: The 1960s Studies** (Chapter 5): DuPont's own scientists found in the 1960s that C8 accumulated in human blood and caused liver damage in test animals. They didn't disclose this. Instead, they funded new studies designed to find the chemical safe. Every decade, a new study came out saying "no evidence of harm" — even as their internal data showed the opposite.
> **Key takeaway**: When a company says "there's no evidence" — ask whether they're looking for it. Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence, especially when the company controls the research.

**The fix:** Demand independent research. Do not accept a company's self-funded study as proof of safety. The C8 Science Panel model — independent, court-appointed, with full access to data — should be the standard.

## Anti-Pattern 2: Regulatory Capture

**The mistake:** The agency responsible for regulating the company becomes its ally. DuPont had deep relationships with the EPA, the West Virginia Department of Environmental Protection, and local officials.

> **Case: The EPA Revolving Door** (Chapter 8): Key EPA officials who regulated DuPont later went to work for DuPont or its consulting firms. Environmental agency staff in West Virginia were reluctant to take enforcement action against the state's largest employer. This is regulatory capture: the regulator becomes the regulated's advocate.
> **Key takeaway**: A captured regulator is worse than no regulator, because it gives the illusion of oversight. Recognize when an agency is compromised and find alternative pressure points — courts, media, public opinion.

**The fix:** Use multiple forums. If the state agency won't act, go to the EPA. If the EPA won't act, go to court. If the court is slow, go to the press. Never rely on a single avenue for accountability.

## Anti-Pattern 3: Intimidation of Whistleblowers

**The mistake:** Making people afraid to speak up. DuPont threatened employees, influenced local politicians, and made the community dependent on their payroll.

## Anti-Pattern 4: Learned Helplessness

**The mistake:** The community gives up. "Nothing can be done. It's always been this way. DuPont is too powerful." This is what the company counts on.

> **Case: Earl's Neighbors** (Chapter 1): Other farmers in the area had the same problems, but they didn't speak up. They were afraid of DuPont. They were afraid of losing their jobs, their land, their community standing. Earl Tennant was the only one who called a lawyer. "No one would help him."
> **Key takeaway**: Silence is the corporation's greatest ally. The moment one person speaks, others find courage. Earl's call started it all.

**The fix:** Find your Earl Tennant. One person who refuses to accept the unacceptable. Support them. Their courage makes it easier for others to come forward.

## Anti-Pattern 5: Accepting "Acceptable Levels"

**The mistake:** Believing that a government-set "safe level" of a chemical means it's actually safe. DuPont's internal documents showed they knew C8 was dangerous at any level — but they fought to set the "acceptable level" as high as possible.

> **Case: The 1 ppb vs 150 ppb** (Chapter 14): EPA's initial "safe" level for C8 was 150 parts per billion. The C8 Science Panel later found harm at levels below 1 ppb. The difference was not science — it was politics. DuPont lobbied for higher limits. The "safe" level was a negotiation, not a medical fact.
> **Key takeaway**: Regulatory limits are often the result of political negotiation, not medical science. When a regulator says "this level is safe," ask: who set this level, on what evidence, and who influenced the decision?
