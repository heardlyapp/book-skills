# 3 — The Legal Battle: Strategies That Took on DuPont

> Source: "Exposure" by Robert Bilott

## Strategy 1: The Community Class Action

In 2001, Bilott filed a class action on behalf of 70,000+ residents of the Parkersburg area whose drinking water was contaminated. This was the first step — not for money, but to force testing and discovery.

> **Case: The Creative Cause of Action** (Chapter 6): Bilott had to invent a new legal theory. Traditional tort law required showing specific injury — but C8's effects might not appear for decades. He argued that DuPont should pay for medical monitoring — that the mere presence of a toxic chemical in the community's blood entitled residents to have their health tracked. The court agreed. This was precedent-setting.
> **Key takeaway**: When existing law doesn't fit, create a new framework. Medical monitoring became a precedent for other environmental contamination cases.

## Strategy 2: The Court-Appointed Science Panel

The class action settlement in 2004 created an innovative mechanism: an independent C8 Science Panel of three epidemiologists. Their mandate: determine whether C8 caused human disease. Only if they said yes could residents sue for personal injury.

## Strategy 3: Document Discovery

Bilott spent years fighting for documents. DuPont claimed privilege, trade secrets, and undue burden. Bilott won motion after motion, eventually obtaining 110,000+ pages.

> **Case: The Deep Well Injection** (Chapter 9): One of the most shocking discoveries: DuPont had been injecting C8-laced waste into deep wells for decades. This was not disclosed to regulators. The wells were supposedly "deep enough" that the chemicals would never reach groundwater. But internal documents showed DuPont knew the injection was contaminating the aquifer.
> **Key takeaway**: The most damning evidence is what a company does when it thinks no one is watching. Deep well injection was DuPont's method of hiding waste — and their own documents showed they knew it wasn't safe.

## Strategy 4: The EPA Complaint

Parallel to the class action, Bilott filed an administrative complaint with the EPA. This forced a federal investigation and ultimately resulted in a $16.5M penalty — the largest in EPA history at the time.

## Strategy 5: The Bellwether Trials

After the Science Panel found probable links to disease, individual plaintiffs could sue for personal injury. The first few "bellwether" trials tested the waters. The first trial resulted in a $1.6M verdict for the plaintiff.

> **Case: The First Trial** (Chapter 23): The jury found DuPont liable and awarded damages. But the trial also revealed something deeper: DuPont's attorneys had themselves been exposed to C8. One of DuPont's own expert witnesses testified that if he lived in Parkersburg, he wouldn't drink the water. Even their own team couldn't defend the indefensible.
> **Key takeaway**: When a company's own experts won't drink the water, you know the science is solid. The first verdict broke the dam — hundreds of other cases followed.
