# Reference: Imperial Ukraine

## Overview
From the 18th to early 20th centuries, Ukraine was divided between two empires: the Russian Empire controlled most of the territory; the Austro-Hungarian Empire controlled Galicia (western Ukraine).

## Case: The Russian Empire
After Catherine the Great destroyed the Zaporozhian Sich in 1775, the Russian Empire imposed serfdom, banned the Ukrainian language, and suppressed Ukrainian national identity. The Russian Empire viewed Ukraine as "Little Russia" — a regional variation of the Russian people, not a separate nation. This imperial view — that Ukraine is not a real country — would be revived by Putin in the 21st century.

## Case: Austrian Galicia
Under the Habsburg Empire, western Ukraine (Galicia) had a very different experience. The Habsburgs were more tolerant of Ukrainian nationalism. The Ukrainian language was allowed in schools. The Ukrainian Greek Catholic Church was permitted. Lviv became the center of Ukrainian national revival. This created a fundamental difference between eastern and western Ukraine that persists today: western Ukraine is more nationalist, more pro-European, more Ukrainian-speaking.

## Case: The National Revival
The 19th century saw a Ukrainian national revival, led by writers, poets, and intellectuals. Taras Shevchenko (1814-1861), Ukraine's national poet, is the most important figure. His poetry combined social justice, national liberation, and a vision of Ukraine free from Russian rule. The Russian Empire banned his works. The Ukrainian language was suppressed multiple times. But the national idea survived.
