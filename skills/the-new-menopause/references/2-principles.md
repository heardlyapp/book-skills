# Key Principles — The New Menopause

## I. Menopause Is Inevitable; Suffering Is Not

Haver's mantra. Menopause is a natural process — but "that doesn't mean it is not harmful." Declining estrogen increases risks for heart disease, Alzheimer's, osteoporosis, and diabetes. "You should be fully informed about the range of those risks, as well as the options for mediating them."

## II. The WHI Study Was Misinterpreted — and We Lost a Generation

The 2002 Women's Health Initiative studied older women (average age 63) on a specific drug combination and found increased risks. The media reported: "Hormones are dangerous." Millions stopped overnight. Doctors stopped prescribing. The re-analysis (showing safety for women under 60) took years to trickle out. "We lost a generation of hormone therapy."

## III. Estrogen Is Not Just a Reproductive Hormone — It's a Systemic Protectant

Estrogen receptors are in the brain, heart, blood vessels, bones, skin, bladder, and more. As levels drop, every system is affected. "Heart disease, stroke, Alzheimer's, type 2 diabetes — all linked to estrogen loss." Treating menopause is not about vanity — it's about longevity.

## IV. The "Window of Opportunity" Matters

Starting MHT within 10 years of menopause (or before age 60) maximizes benefits and minimizes risks. Starting after 60 or 10+ years postmenopause increases risks of stroke, blood clots, and heart attack. "The time to start is when symptoms begin, not when they become unbearable."

## V. Most Doctors Are Not Trained in Menopause Care

80% of medical residents feel "barely comfortable" with menopause. Only 20% of ob-gyn residencies offer menopause training. 94% of residents said it was important but weren't taught. "I went through four years of medical school, four years of residency, and never received adequate training." Advocacy and self-education are essential.

## VI. Symptoms Are Highly Individual — There Is No "Normal"

50+ possible symptoms. No standardized diagnostic criteria. No blood test that definitively diagnoses perimenopause. "It depends on the day, on the time of day even, and on a whole lot more." The experience is unique to each woman.

## VII. Lifestyle Matters, But It's Not Enough Without Hormones

The Galveston Diet (protein-focused, anti-inflammatory, Mediterranean-style) and exercise (resistance training, aerobic, balance) are powerful tools. "But I firmly believe that you should be fully informed about the range of those risks, as well as the options for mediating them." For most women, lifestyle + MHT is the optimal combination.
