# 1. Core Framework — The Three Acts of the Snowden Story

> Source: "No Place to Hide" by Glenn Greenwald (2014). The book follows a three-act structure: the Contact, the Revelation, the Fallout.

## Act I: The Contact

Snowden reached out to Greenwald in late 2012 through encrypted email, introducing himself as a government employee with evidence of illegal surveillance programs. They communicated for months before Snowden trusted Greenwald enough to meet.

> **Case: The First Encrypted Email** (Chapter 1): Snowden's first message to Greenwald was cautious. He did not reveal his identity. He offered a sample of documents to prove his credibility. Greenwald verified the documents and agreed to meet. This cautious approach protected both of them.
> **Key takeaway**: Trust between a source and a journalist is built slowly, through verification and encrypted communication.

## Act II: The Hong Kong Meeting

In June 2013, Greenwald and filmmaker Laura Poitras flew to Hong Kong to meet Snowden in his hotel room. For ten days, they interviewed him and reviewed the documents.

> **Case: The Hotel Room** (Chapter 2): Greenwald describes the cramped Hong Kong hotel room where Snowden had been living. The curtains were drawn. Snowden was calm, articulate, and certain about his decision to leak. He said: "I can't do this alone. I need journalists who will report the truth."
> **Key takeaway**: The most consequential meetings between journalists and sources happen in small rooms, far from public view. The circumstances are often banal. The consequences are historic.

## Act III: The Revelations and Fallout

Greenwald published the first story on June 5, 2013, revealing the NSA's collection of Verizon phone records. More stories followed: PRISM, surveillance of foreign leaders, XKeyscore. Snowden was charged under the Espionage Act and fled to Russia. A global debate about surveillance and privacy was ignited.
