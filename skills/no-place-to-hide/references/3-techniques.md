# 3. Techniques — Investigative Journalism

> Source: "No Place to Hide" by Glenn Greenwald (2014). Techniques drawn from Greenwald's reporting process.

## The Encrypted Source Communication

Greenwald and Snowden communicated using encryption tools (PGP, Tor) that Snowden insisted on. This protected both the source and the journalist.

> **Case: The Verification Process** (Chapter 1): Greenwald verified Snowden's identity by asking him to perform a task only an NSA employee could do — access a specific classified system. This sort of verification protects against impostors.
> **Key takeaway**: Source verification must be rigorous. A verified source is a protected source.

## The Document Management System

Greenwald faced the challenge of reviewing thousands of classified documents. His approach: read for understanding, identify the most newsworthy stories, and publish strategically over time, not all at once.

> **Case: The First Story** (Introduction): Greenwald chose the Verizon phone records story as the first publication because it was the most concrete and understandable for the general public. More complex stories (PRISM, XKeyscore) followed later.
> **Key takeaway**: When publishing classified information, start with the most accessible story. Build public understanding gradually.

## The Collaborative Approach

Greenwald worked with filmmaker Laura Poitras and The Guardian newspaper. The collaboration allowed for simultaneous publication across platforms — articles, video, and documentary.

> **Case: The Guardian Partnership** (Introduction): The Guardian provided legal resources, editorial support, and global reach that Greenwald alone could not have mustered. The collaboration model — independent journalist + major newspaper + documentary filmmaker — became a template for future investigative projects.
> **Key takeaway**: Complex investigations require teams. The lone journalist model is insufficient for stories of this scale.
