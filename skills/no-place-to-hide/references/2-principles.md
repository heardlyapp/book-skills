# 2. Principles — The Surveillance State

> Source: "No Place to Hide" by Glenn Greenwald (2014). Principles drawn from the Snowden revelations.

## The 7 Principles

### Principle 1: Mass Surveillance Is Incompatible with Democracy
If the government can monitor every communication, citizens cannot freely associate, protest, or dissent. The chilling effect of surveillance is the end of political freedom.

### Principle 2: Secrecy Is the Enemy of Accountability
The NSA programs were approved by a secret court (FISA), based on secret legal interpretations, and overseen by a Congress that was not fully informed. Secrecy on this scale is itself a threat to democratic governance.

> **Case: The FISA Court** (Chapter 3): The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court approves surveillance warrants in secret. The government argues its case without opposition. Between 1979 and 2013, the court approved over 33,000 warrants and rejected only 11. This is not oversight — it is a rubber stamp.
> **Key takeaway**: A court that hears only one side of an argument is not a court. It is a permission-granting agency.

### Principle 3: The Fourth Amendment Protects Digital Data
The government's argument that metadata (phone records, email logs) is not protected by the Fourth Amendment would have been unthinkable to the Founders.

### Principle 4: Whistleblowers Are Essential to Democracy
When official channels of oversight fail, whistleblowers are the last resort. Snowden tried to report his concerns through internal channels. Those channels failed him.

> **Case: Snowden's Internal Reporting** (Chapter 1): Before leaking, Snowden raised his concerns with NSA supervisors and the agency's internal oversight office. His concerns were dismissed. Only when internal channels failed did he turn to journalists.
> **Key takeaway**: Whistleblowing is a last resort, not a first choice. The fact that Snowden felt he had no other option is a failure of the system, not a failure of Snowden.

### Principle 5: Journalism Is a Check on Secret Power
Without journalists who can access and publish classified information, the government has no external accountability. The press is the only check on secret power.

### Principle 6: Technology Has Outpaced the Law
The laws governing surveillance were written in the 1970s, before the internet, before mass data storage, before metadata. They are inadequate to regulate 21st-century surveillance.

### Principle 7: Privacy Is a Collective Good
The "nothing to hide" argument assumes privacy is an individual concern. It is not. A society without privacy is a society without freedom for everyone, not just those with "something to hide."
