# 4. Anti-Patterns — Critiques of the Snowden Revelations

> Source: "No Place to Hide" by Glenn Greenwald (2014). Critiques of Greenwald and Snowden.

## Critique 1: Snowden Was a Traitor

**What it looks like**: Snowden leaked classified information to foreign media, fled to Russia, and damaged national security. Therefore he is a traitor.

**Why it fails**: Snowden did not leak to an enemy. He leaked to journalists who then decided what to publish. The government has not charged him with aiding an enemy — they charged him under the Espionage Act, which is designed for leaking, not for treason.

> **Case: The Espionage Act** (Chapter 4): Snowden was charged with theft of government property and two counts under the Espionage Act. Notably, he was not charged with treason. The government's choice of charges reveals that they do not believe he aided an enemy.
> **Key takeaway**: Calling Snowden a traitor is a political judgment, not a legal one. The actual charges tell a different story.

## Critique 2: The Revelations Damaged National Security

**What it looks like**: Publishing the NSA programs gave terrorists information about how to avoid surveillance.

**Why it works**: The programs were already known to foreign intelligence services and sophisticated adversaries. The value of the revelations was to inform the American public, who had been kept in the dark.

## Critique 3: Greenwald Was Too Sympathetic to Snowden

**What it looks like**: Greenwald became an advocate rather than an objective journalist.

**Why it doesn't hold**: Greenwald was transparent about his views. He is a civil liberties attorney and commentator, not an objective reporter. His advocacy is part of his credibility.

## Critique 4: Snowden Should Have Used Internal Channels

**What it looks like**: Snowden should have reported his concerns through official channels instead of leaking.

**Why it fails**: Snowden did use internal channels. His concerns were dismissed. The internal oversight systems failed.

> **Case: The Damage Assessment** (Chapter 4): The intelligence community claimed the leaks damaged national security. When asked for specific examples, they cited the fact that terrorists changed their communication methods. Greenwald argues this proves the surveillance was working — but also that the public deserves to know they are being monitored.
> **Key takeaway**: The "national security" argument against transparency is circular — the government claims secrecy is necessary, but when secrecy is broken, they say the damage proves the need for secrecy.
