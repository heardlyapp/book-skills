# Anti-Patterns: What to Avoid

> Source: Antifragile, Chapters 18-22

## The Inverse Philosopher's Stone

Just as alchemists sought the philosopher's stone that could turn lead into gold, Taleb identifies an "inverse philosopher's stone" — the mechanism by which modern systems turn gold into mud. The inverse philosopher's stone is the tendency to take robust, self-correcting natural systems and replace them with fragile, brittle, error-prone artificial ones.

The mechanism works through several anti-patterns:

**Neomania**: The love of the new for its own sake. We replace things that have worked for centuries with new inventions that haven't been tested by time. A chair design that has worked for 500 years is replaced by an ergonomic disaster. A building technique that has survived earthquakes for millennia is replaced by glass-walled towers that shatter. Neomania is the rejection of the Lindy effect — the old outlives the new in exact proportion to how long it has already survived.

**Over-optimization**: The drive for efficiency strips a system of redundancy, which is its buffer against shocks. Just-in-time inventory, single-supplier dependencies, and lean staffing all look efficient on paper. Then a shipping container gets stuck in the Suez Canal and the whole system seizes up. The apparent inefficiency of inventory and redundancy is actually the cost of robustness.

**Complication without necessity**: Adding layers of rules, processes, and systems that create hidden interdependencies. Each new complication is a potential cascade point. The more complicated a system, the more likely it is to produce the kind of unexpected chain reactions that Taleb calls "explosive branching responses" — an intervention causes a problem, which requires another intervention, which causes a worse problem, ad infinitum.

## The Iatrogenics Blind Spot

Iatrogenics — harm caused by the healer — is the most underappreciated source of fragility in modern life. The doctor who prescribes a medication that has a side effect, then prescribes another medication for the side effect, then another for that medication's side effects — creating a cascade of interventions that leave the patient worse off than if they had done nothing.

The first principle of iatrogenics: **do no harm**. But this is routinely violated because the benefits of intervention are visible and immediate, while the harms are invisible and delayed. The patient feels better after the painkiller and doesn't connect the subsequent liver damage to the treatment. The economy feels better after the stimulus and doesn't see the inflation and malinvestment building up.

The second principle of iatrogenics: **nonlinearity in response**. A small dose of a medication might be therapeutic, but a slightly larger dose — still in the "safe" range — might be toxic. Doubling the dose doesn't double the effect; it creates a qualitatively different, and often dangerous, outcome. This is Jensen's inequality in medicine: the average of the response is not the response of the average.

## The Trouble with Large Systems

Size is the enemy of antifragility. Large systems are fragile because:
- They concentrate risk in fewer nodes
- Failure of one component threatens the whole
- They are too complex to understand or manage
- They mask small problems until they become catastrophic

A thousand small banks are more antifragile than one giant bank. If one small bank fails, it's absorbed. If the giant bank fails, the economy crashes. A thousand small farms are more resilient than one industrial farm. A thousand small businesses are more adaptable than one monopoly.

Taleb's heuristic: **anything that cannot be broken into smaller, independent units is fragile**. The test for fragility is simple: if a single failure brings down the whole system, the system is fragile. The antidote is modularity, redundancy, and decentralization.

> **Case: Medical Iatrogenics**
> 
> A healthy 55-year-old man goes for a routine checkup. The doctor finds slightly elevated cholesterol. He prescribes a statin. The statin causes muscle pain and fatigue, so the patient stops exercising. The lack of exercise worsens his blood pressure. The doctor adds a beta-blocker. The beta-blocker causes dizziness, leading to a fall and a broken hip. The hip surgery leads to a hospital-acquired infection. The infection requires strong antibiotics, which damage his gut microbiome. He develops chronic digestive issues and depression. Eighteen months later, he is on seven medications and is worse off than when he started. The original problem was minor. The cascade of interventions destroyed his health.

> **Case: The Antifragility of Small Business**
> 
> A city with one large factory employing 80% of the workforce is fragile. If the factory closes, the city collapses. A city with five hundred small businesses, each employing no more than 20 people, is antifragile. When one business fails (and some will — that's natural), the workers are absorbed by the others. The failure of any single business doesn't threaten the city. Moreover, the small businesses compete and learn from each other, becoming stronger. The large factory, by contrast, learns nothing from its monopoly position — it just gets complacent until the shock hits.

## Via Negativa in Action

The most powerful anti-pattern remedy is via negativa — removing the harmful rather than adding the beneficial. In diet, stop eating junk food before adding superfoods. In business, stop serving unprofitable customers before acquiring new ones. In life, remove the toxic relationship before seeking a better one. Subtraction is more reliable than addition because it's harder to mess up.

The via negativa approach acknowledges a deep truth about complex systems: we know far more about what harms them than what helps them. A physician knows more about what makes people sick than what makes them healthy. An economist knows more about what crashes economies than what grows them. Use this asymmetry. Before adding anything, ask: what can I remove?
