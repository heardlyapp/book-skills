# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *Black Elk Speaks* by John G. Neihardt (as told by Black Elk) — Chapters 1-3, 7-11, 24-25.

## The Three Frameworks

### Framework A: The Great Vision (Chapters 2-3)

At age 9, Black Elk fell into a trance lasting 12 days. During this time, he was taken by two spirit men to the Six Grandfathers — the Powers of the West, North, East, South, Sky, and Earth. They gave him sacred gifts: the cup of water (healing), the bow (power), the sacred pipe (peace), the white wing (cleansing), the herb (healing), the flowering stick (the nation's heart).

> **Case: The Boy Who Died and Came Back** (Chapter 3): "I was lying in our tepee and my mother and father were sitting beside me. I could see out through the opening, and there two men were coming from the clouds, headfirst like arrows slanting down… They came clear down to the ground this time and stood a little way off and looked at me and said: 'Hurry! Come! Your Grandfathers are calling you!'" Black Elk was gone for 12 days. His people thought he was dead. Whirlwind Chaser, a medicine man, was credited with bringing him back. But Black Elk knew differently: he had been with the Six Grandfathers.
> **Key takeaway**: A true vision takes you out of time. When you return, the world has not changed — but you have.

### Framework B: The Sacred Hoop (Chapters 3, 25)

The central image of Black Elk's spirituality: all life is a circle. The people camp in a circle. The year is a circle. The universe is a circle of circles. When the hoop is broken, the people suffer. The holy tree at the center of the hoop is the life of the nation.

> **Case: The Mountain at the Center of the World** (Chapter 3, Author's Postscript): In his vision, Black Elk stood on "the highest mountain of them all… and round about beneath me was the whole hoop of the world. And while I stood there I saw more than I can tell and I understood more than I saw… And I saw that the sacred hoop of my people was one of many hoops that made one circle, wide as daylight and as starlight, and in the center grew one mighty flowering tree to shelter all the children of one mother and one father." Decades later, Neihardt took Black Elk to Harney Peak in the Black Hills. Black Elk identified it as the mountain from his vision. He wept and prayed.
> **Key takeaway**: The hoop is broken, but it can be mended. The tree is withered, but the roots remain.

### Framework C: Wounded Knee and the End of the Dream (Chapters 24-25)

The book's devastating final chapters. Black Elk participated in the Ghost Dance — a religious movement among Plains tribes that promised the return of the buffalo and the ancestors. The U.S. government saw it as a threat. On December 29, 1890, the 7th Cavalry massacred 300 unarmed Lakota at Wounded Knee Creek.

> **Case: The Butchering at Wounded Knee** (Chapter 24): Black Elk arrived at Wounded Knee after the shooting began. "I could see that they were all dead — women and children and men… I could see the little children lying dead with no clothes on, and their faces were green." He led a charge to rescue survivors. Then he saw a baby suckling at its dead mother's breast. "And I said: 'There is no hope for my people.'"
> **Key takeaway**: Some wounds do not heal in one generation. But the vision remains true. The people endure.
