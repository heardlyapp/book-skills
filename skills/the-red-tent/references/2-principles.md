> Source: The Red Tent, Anita Diamant (1997).

# Key Principles — Female Community, the Chain of Mothers, and the Wisdom of the Body

## Core Principles

> **Case: Leah and Rachel** (Early): Dinah's two mothers — Leah (her birth mother) and Rachel (Jacob's beloved) — are rivals and sisters. They compete for Jacob's love. But they also love each other. They raise Dinah together. Their complex relationship teaches Dinah that women's bonds can survive competition, jealousy, and grief.
> **Key takeaway**: Women's relationships are not simple. They are complicated, fierce, and enduring. The complexity does not diminish the love.

> **Case: The Midwives** (Throughout): The midwives are the carriers of ancient knowledge. They know which herbs induce labor, which ease pain, which stop bleeding. They know how to turn a breech baby. They know when to wait and when to act. This knowledge is passed from woman to woman. It is not written down. It lives in their hands.
> **Key takeaway**: Knowledge that lives in the body is as real as knowledge that lives in books. The midwives' wisdom was passed through touch, not text. Both are valid.

> **Case: Dinah as Midwife** (Later): Dinah becomes a midwife. She learns the craft from the women who raised her. She delivers babies. She holds dying women. She is present at the threshold of life and death. Her hands become the hands of her mothers before her.
> **Key takeaway**: When you learn the work of the women who came before you, you become part of an unbroken chain. You are not alone. You are their hands now.
