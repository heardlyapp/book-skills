# Anti-Patterns — Silencing, Erasure, and the Violence of Interpretation

## The Central Fallacy

The most dangerous assumption of the biblical interpreter: that a woman's story can be adequately told in a few verses. Dinah's entire existence in Genesis is a brief detour. She is defined by what happens to her, not by who she is. The novel is a protest against that reduction.

> **Case: The Brothers' Revenge** (Mid-novel): Dinah's brothers believe they are avenging her honor. They kill every man in Shechem. They take the women and children as slaves. They believe they are acting for Dinah. They never ask her what she wants. Her voice is irrelevant to their vengeance.
> **Key takeaway**: The people who claim to act on your behalf may be the ones who silence you most effectively. Ask before you act.

> **Case: Dinah's Exile** (After Shechem): Dinah is sent away by Jacob. She is not killed. She is not punished. She is simply removed. Her story is erased from the family narrative. She becomes the aunt no one mentions. The exile is more complete than death would have been.
> **Key takeaway**: Erasure is a form of violence. Being forgotten can hurt more than being attacked. To be remembered is to exist.

> **Case: The Gaps in the Text** (Throughout): The Bible does not record Dinah's thoughts, her feelings, her voice. She is a character in someone else's story. The novel fills the gaps. The anti-pattern is accepting the gaps as complete. They are not. There is always more to the story.
> **Key takeaway**: The gaps in any story are where the truth is hiding. Do not accept the official version as complete. Ask what is missing.
