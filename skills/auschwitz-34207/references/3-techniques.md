# 3 — The Death March and Liberation

> Source: "Auschwitz #34207" by Nancy Sprowell Geise

## The Final Ordeal

As the Allies advanced, the Nazis evacuated the camps, forcing prisoners on death marches. For Joe, this was the most harrowing phase of his Holocaust experience.

> **Case: The March** (Chapter on Death March): "The prisoners were marched westward in the dead of winter. They had no food, no water, no warm clothing. Anyone who fell behind was shot. Joe survived by sheer will — and by helping others. He shared his meager rations, carried those who stumbled, and refused to give up." Geise describes the death march.
> **Key takeaway**: The death march was the final test. After surviving Auschwitz and other camps, Joe faced death not in a gas chamber but on a frozen road. The march killed thousands. Those who survived did so through a combination of luck, will, and mutual support.

> **Case: Liberation** (Chapter on Freedom): "When American soldiers arrived, Joe was barely alive. He weighed less than 80 pounds. He was covered in lice and filth. But he was alive. An American soldier gave him chocolate. Joe cried. He had not tasted chocolate in three years. He was free." Geise describes the moment of liberation.
> **Key takeaway**: Liberation was not instant healing. Joe was physically destroyed and emotionally shattered. But the taste of chocolate from an American soldier was the first taste of kindness in three years. It symbolized the end of one journey and the beginning of another.
