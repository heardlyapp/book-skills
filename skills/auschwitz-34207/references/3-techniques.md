# Survival Techniques from Auschwitz

Joe Rubinstein did not have a manual for surviving Auschwitz. But the techniques he used are observable, repeatable patterns of behavior that kept him alive when so many others died.

## Technique 1: The Narrowing of Attention

When faced with overwhelming horror, Joe learned to narrow his attention to the smallest possible unit. Not "survive the war" — that was too large. Not "survive the next week" — still too large. Just "survive the next minute."

**How to do it:** When you cannot control your circumstances, control your attention. Focus on something small and immediate: your breathing, a body sensation, a simple task. Do not let your mind wander to the enormity of what you face.

**Case: The Toe-Wiggling on the Truck.** During the freezing night on the open truck, Joe writes: "I kept wiggling my toes, no longer able to feel them." The act of wiggling was not going to save his life. But the act of focusing on wiggling kept his mind from collapsing into despair.

**Case: The Counting of Steps.** On the death march, Joe counted steps. One, two, three, four. When he reached one hundred, he started over. The counting was meaningless as navigation but essential as psychological survival. It gave his mind a structure when everything else was chaos.

## Technique 2: The Strategic Presentation of Self

Joe learned that survival in the camps depended partly on how you appeared to the SS. If you looked weak, you were selected for death. If you looked strong, you were selected for work — which at least gave you a chance.

**How to do it:** When you cannot change the circumstances, change how you appear within them. Stand straight. Make eye contact. Do not show weakness in front of those who would exploit it.

**Case: The Selections.** During the Auschwitz selections, prisoners were stripped and lined up. The SS officers walked down the line, pointing left or right. Left meant the gas chamber. Right meant work. Joe forced himself to stand erect, to look healthy, even when he was starving and exhausted. "I stood straight, holding myself up even when I didn't know if I could."

**Case: Walking Tall in America.** After the war, Joe applied for a job at a shoe company. He had no experience, no network, no formal education. But he walked in confident, told them what he knew about shoes, and got the job. The technique that kept him alive in Auschwitz — projecting strength when you feel none — also built his career.

## Technique 3: The Acceptance of Help

Joe survived because he accepted help when it was offered. In the camps, pride was a luxury. The men who refused help because they were too proud to ask died.

**How to do it:** When someone offers help, take it. Do not worry about obligation, reciprocity, or pride. Survival comes before pride.

**Case: The Bread from a Stranger.** A man Joe did not know shared a piece of bread with him. Joe did not refuse. He did not say "no, I'm fine." He took it and survived that day. "That piece of bread kept me alive. I never forgot it."

**Case: The Invitation from Max.** After liberation, Joe was offered a ride to Germany with Max, an older man he barely knew. He accepted. That ride led to a job, a friend, and eventually to his new life. If he had been too proud to accept help from a stranger, he might never have left Europe.

## Technique 4: The Discipline of Gratitude

Joe practiced gratitude not because things were good, but because gratitude was a way of maintaining his humanity. When you can find something to be grateful for, you are still a person.

**How to do it:** Every day, find one thing to be grateful for — no matter how small. A drink of water. A moment of warmth. A kind word. The discipline of gratitude keeps the soul alive when the body is dying.

**Case: The Scoop of Water.** On the train to Auschwitz, the prisoners were given one scoop of water each. Joe writes: "When the man next to me offered me the scoop, my hands were trembling so badly that I feared dropping it. I wrapped both hands around the scoop and closed my eyes as liquid trickled down my throat." He was grateful for a single scoop of water.

**Case: The Sunday Dinner.** After the war, Joe's friend Sig invited him to Sunday dinner with his mother. She dressed in Norwegian clothing and cooked authentic food. Joe writes: "Eating with a family was something I never thought I would experience again. It meant more to me than I had words to express."

## Technique 5: The Forward Momentum

Joe's final technique: keep moving forward. Do not look back with regret. Do not look around with envy. Just keep moving.

**How to do it:** When you have lost everything, the only direction is forward. Do not dwell on what was taken from you. Focus on what you can build now.

**Case: The Street Corner Decision.** After liberation, Joe stood on a street corner in Czechoslovakia, paralyzed by choices. He decided not to return to Radom — the memories were too painful. He chose to go forward, not backward, even though forward meant Germany. "Going there was not an option. Doing so would kill me. Of that I was certain."

**Case: The Shoe Factory.** Joe stopped being a Holocaust survivor and became a shoe designer. He bought American magazines, studied the designs, and began sketching his own. Then he went to New York and built a career. He did not tell people he was a survivor. He showed them he was a master craftsman.
