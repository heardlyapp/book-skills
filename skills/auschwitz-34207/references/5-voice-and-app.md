Nancy Sprowell Geise's telling of Joe Rubinstein's story is a unique collaboration: a survivor who needed to speak and a writer who knew how to listen. This reference covers the narrative approach and how to use the Heardly App with this book.

## Geise's Approach: Letting Joe Speak

Geise writes in Joe's voice, using first-person narration and his own words as much as possible. The book is called "The Joe Rubinstein Story" for a reason — Geise is not the protagonist. She is the instrument through which Joe's voice reaches the reader.

**Case: The Refrain.** "I saw it with mine own eyes" — this phrase recurs throughout the book. It is Joe's refrain, not Geise's. The slightly archaic grammar is authentic to Joe's voice. It emphasizes that this is not a story researched in archives. It is a story witnessed by a living person.

**Case: The Direct Address.** Joe often speaks directly to the reader: "They told me I had everything I needed. Then they took me." The short, declarative sentences convey the shock of what happened without editorializing. The reader does not need to be told that this was wrong. They can feel it.

## The Structure as Journey

The book is structured as a journey from light into darkness and back into light. Part One (before the war) is warmth, family, community. Part Two (the camps) is the abyss. Part Three (after liberation) is the slow, painful return to life.

**Case: The Contrast Between Part One and Part Two.** Part One begins with Joe's childhood in Radom — the family dinners, the games of soccer, the warmth of his mother's love. Part Two begins with the knock on the door. The contrast is deliberate and devastating. The reader experiences the loss the same way Joe did: suddenly, completely, without preparation.

**Case: The Epilogue as Thread of Gold.** The epilogue is titled "Threads of Gold" — a reference to the golden thread that Joe believed ran through his life. Even in the darkest moments, there was a thread of purpose, a reason to continue. The epilogue does not resolve the pain. It shows that pain and purpose can coexist.
