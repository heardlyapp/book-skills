# 5 — Geise's Voice + 5 Application Scenarios

> Source: "Auschwitz #34207" by Nancy Sprowell Geise

## Geise's Voice

Nancy Sprowell Geise is a writer who spent years interviewing Joe Rubinstein and documenting his story. Her voice: respectful, detailed, unobtrusive. She lets Joe's story speak for itself.

> **Case: The Author's Role** (Introduction): "I met Joe Rubinstein when he was in his 80s. He was a warm, generous man who loved life. You would never guess what he had survived. When he began to tell me his story, I knew I had to write it down. This is not my book — it is his." Geise establishes her position as a scribe, not the subject.
> **Key takeaway**: Geise's humility is the right approach for this kind of book. She does not insert herself into the story. She does not interpret or analyze. She simply records Joe's words and presents them with the dignity they deserve.

> **Case: The Purpose** (Epilogue): "Joe did not survive to be a hero. He survived because he wanted to live. He survived so that his family's memory would not be erased. He survived so that future generations would know what happened. His story is a testament to the human spirit — and a warning about what happens when hatred goes unchecked."
> **Key takeaway**: Joe's purpose in telling his story was twofold: to honor the dead and to educate the living. He believed that knowledge was the best defense against the repetition of history.

## Key Quote

"I survived because I wanted to live. I wanted to tell the world what happened. I wanted my family to be remembered."

## 5 Application Scenarios

1. **Learning about the Holocaust for the first time** → Start with one story. Joe's story is human-scale and ultimately hopeful.
2. **Teaching about the Holocaust** → Use individual testimony. The power of one person's story is greater than a thousand statistics.
3. **Struggling with personal adversity** → Read about Joe's resilience. If he could rebuild his life after losing everything, your challenges are survivable.
4. **Discussing genocide prevention** → Read the conclusion. "Never again" is a promise that requires active work.
5. **Grateful for freedom** → Joe's love for America is a reminder of how precious freedom is — and how easily it can be lost.
