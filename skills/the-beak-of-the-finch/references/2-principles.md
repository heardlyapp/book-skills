## 7 Principles of Evolution in Action

### 1. Variation Is the Raw Material

Every population has variation. No two finches have the same beak size, body weight, or song. This variation is the fuel of evolution.

### 2. The Environment Selects

Nature does not choose randomly. The drought selected for larger beaks. The environment picks which traits survive.

### 3. Heritability Transmits Traits

Evolution requires that traits be heritable. Beak size is heritable — offspring resemble parents. This is why the population shifted.

> **Case: The Heritability Measurement** (Chapter 6): The Grants measured beak size in parents and offspring. They found that beak size is highly heritable — about 80% of the variation is genetic. This is why the population responded so quickly to selection.
> **Key takeaway**: Evolution requires heritability. If a trait is not passed from parent to offspring, selection cannot act on it.

### 4. Selection Pressures Change Direction

After the drought, heavy rains returned. Large seeds became less common. Selection reversed — smaller-beaked birds had an advantage. The population shifted again. Evolution does not have a direction — it responds.

### 5. Speciation Is a Process, Not an Event

The hybrid finch on Daphne Major shows that speciation takes time. The population is diverging, but it is not yet a separate species. The process is observable.

### 6. Science Requires Patience

The Grants spent 30+ years on a tiny island. They banded thousands of birds. They measured beaks. They tracked generations. The results were revolutionary because the commitment was extraordinary.

> **Case: The Daily Routine** (Chapter 2): The Grants woke before dawn every day, climbed into the island's interior, captured finches in mist nets, measured them, and released them. They did this for decades. The work was monotonous. The results were spectacular.
> **Key takeaway**: Great science looks boring from the outside. The Grants' patience was the key to their discovery.

### 7. Evolution Is Everywhere

The finches are the most famous example, but evolution is happening all around us — in bacteria, in insects, in every living thing. The principle is universal.
