# Anti-Patterns — Shadow Divers

## The Central Error: "Official Records Are Always Right"

"The official record said U-869 was sunk off Africa by U.S. Navy depth charges. It was wrong." The wreck was off New Jersey, depth-charged by U.S. Navy — but from a different attack on a different date. Case: "No government, expert, historian, or navy had a clue which submarine it was or why it was in New Jersey."

### Mistake 1: "Experts Know Everything"
Historians and navies couldn't identify the U-boat because their records were incomplete. Experts rely on official sources — Chatterton and Kohler relied on the wreck itself. Case: "They challenged professional historians and rewrote a page of history long presumed to be gospel."

### Mistake 2: "The Ocean Is Charted"
"In a world where even the moon had been traveled, the floor of the Atlantic remained uncharted wilderness." Most people assume the ocean floor is fully mapped. It's not. Case: "The deep wrecks hadn't been seen since their victims last looked at them."

### Mistake 3: "Deep Diving Is Safe Enough"
"Scuba equipment was still rudimentary, not much advanced past 1943." The sport was new. There was no ancient wisdom. "Men died — often — diving the shipwrecks that called to Nagle." Case: "A single bad experience could reroute all but the hardiest souls to more sensible pursuits."

### Mistake 4: "Treasure Is the Only Motivation"
"Treasure never figured into the equation." These divers weren't after gold. They wanted history, story, the experience of being the first to see a wreck since it went down. Case: "They saw stories in the Modiglianied faces of broken ships."

### Mistake 5: "One Dive Is Enough"
"Most people would have given up after the first dead end." The search took six years. Multiple dives, multiple research trips, multiple dead ends. Case: "For six years, Chatterton and Kohler were shadow divers."

### Mistake 6: "It's Just a Wreck"
"The U-boat is our moment." For the divers, the wreck was everything — the focus of obsession, the key to identity, the measure of a man. Case: "Chatterton wanted to know the name of the U-boat because he needed to know."
