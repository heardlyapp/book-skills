# 3 — Speeches and Writings

> Source: "The Autobiography of Martin Luther King, Jr."

## Letter from Birmingham Jail (1963)

Written on scraps of paper in jail. MLK's response to white clergy who called the protests "unwise and untimely." The most important defense of civil disobedience ever written.

> **Case: Writing the Letter** (Chapter 8): MLK was in solitary confinement. He was not allowed to write. He smuggled notes out on toilet paper and newspaper margins. "I was in a dark cell, thinking about all the criticisms. I had to respond." The letter was published nationally and became the movement's defining document.
> **Key takeaway**: The Letter from Birmingham Jail was written under the worst conditions and became the most important document of the movement. Constraints can produce clarity.

## I Have a Dream (1963)

The most famous speech in American history. Delivered at the Lincoln Memorial during the March on Washington.

> **Case: The Dream** (Chapter 9): MLK was the last speaker. He had prepared a written speech. But as he spoke, he departed from his notes. Gospel singer Mahalia Jackson shouted: "Tell them about the dream, Martin!" He abandoned his prepared text and began to improvise. The "I Have a Dream" section was not in his original draft.
> **Key takeaway**: Some of the most powerful moments in history come from being present and responsive, not from reading a script. MLK listened to the moment and spoke from his heart.
