# 2 — Nonviolence

> Source: "The Autobiography of Martin Luther King, Jr."

## The Six Principles

1. Nonviolence is a way of life for courageous people
2. Nonviolence seeks to win friendship and understanding
3. Nonviolence attacks forces of evil, not persons
4. Nonviolence accepts suffering without retaliation
5. Nonviolence avoids internal violence of the spirit
6. Nonviolence believes that justice will prevail

## Agape Love

The Greek word for unconditional love. MLK's framework: agape is understanding, creative, redemptive goodwill toward all people. It's not a sentimental feeling — it's a deliberate choice to love even your enemies.

> **Case: The Kitchen Table Experience** (Chapter 5): During the darkest days of the boycott, MLK received a death threat late one night. He sat at his kitchen table, unable to sleep. He was afraid. He felt alone. He prayed: "Lord, I'm taking a stand for what I believe is right. But I'm afraid." He heard a voice say: "Stand up for righteousness. Stand up for justice. I will be with you." He called this his "kitchen table experience" — the moment his faith became real.
> **Key takeaway**: MLK was human. He was afraid. His courage came from faith, not from the absence of fear. The kitchen table experience is where his commitment to nonviolence was forged.

> **Case: The Nonviolent Training** (Birmingham, 1963): Before the Birmingham campaign, MLK and SCLC trained hundreds of volunteers in nonviolent resistance. They role-played: how to protect your head during a beating, how to not retaliate when attacked, how to maintain dignity. "We trained them to be prepared for violence without returning it."
> **Key takeaway**: Nonviolence is not passive. It requires discipline, training, and preparation. It is a form of combat — spiritual combat.
