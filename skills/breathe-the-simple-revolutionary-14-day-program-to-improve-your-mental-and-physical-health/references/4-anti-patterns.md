# Anti-Patterns: Breathing Mistakes

> Source: Breathe, throughout

## Chest Breathing

The most common breathing mistake. Lifting the shoulders and upper chest with each inhale, using the intercostals and scalenes instead of the diaphragm. Inefficient, tiring, and stress-inducing.

> **Case: The Always-Anxious Person** (Introduction): Someone who constantly sighs, yawns, or feels like they can't get a satisfying breath. This is the classic chest breather. Their body is fighting for air because the diaphragm isn't doing its job properly.

> **Key takeaway**: If you can see your chest move when you breathe at rest, you're breathing wrong. The belly should be the primary mover.

## Mouth Breathing at Rest

Breathing through the mouth when not exercising. This bypasses the nose's filtration, warming, and nitric oxide system.

> **Case: The Sleep Disorder** (Chapter 3): Mouth breathing during sleep causes snoring, dry mouth, and worsens sleep apnea. Taping the mouth at night (with surgical tape) forces nasal breathing. Vranich reports dramatic improvements in sleep quality within days.

## Over-Breathing (Chronic Hyperventilation)

Breathing more than the body needs. This reduces CO2, constricts blood vessels, and reduces oxygen delivery to cells.

> **Case: The Runner Who Can't Recover** (Chapter 4): A client who felt winded during light exercise was over-breathing at rest. Her BOLT score was 8 seconds. After two weeks of the program, it rose to 25 seconds, and her exercise tolerance improved dramatically.
