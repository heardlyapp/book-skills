# Core Framework: Breathing Mechanics

> Source: Breathe, Introduction - Week 1

## The Diaphragm

The diaphragm is a dome-shaped muscle at the base of the lungs. When you inhale correctly, it contracts and moves downward, creating space for the lungs to expand into the belly. Most adults breathe by lifting the ribs and shoulders instead — using accessory neck and chest muscles that tire easily and create tension.

> **Case: The Book Test** (Introduction): Lie on your back. Place a book on your belly. Breathe naturally. If the book rises with each inhale, you're using your diaphragm correctly. If it doesn't move, you're chest breathing. Most adults fail this test. Babies pass it naturally — we unlearned correct breathing.

> **Case: The Dysfunctional Breathing Epidemic** (Introduction): Vranich estimates 90% of adults breathe incorrectly. Three main causes: stress (shallow breathing becomes habitual), poor posture (slouching constricts the diaphragm), and cultural norms (sucking in the stomach trains the belly to stay tight). The good news: it's learned, not structural. It can be unlearned.

## Nose vs Mouth

The nose filters, warms, and humidifies air. It releases nitric oxide, a powerful vasodilator that increases oxygen absorption by 10-15%. Mouth breathing bypasses this system.

> **Key takeaway**: Nose breathing should be the default for rest and light activity. Mouth breathing is for emergencies and intense exercise only. If you breathe through your mouth while sleeping, you'll snore more and wake up less rested.
