# Principles: Preparing Your Yes

> Source: The Power of a Positive No, Chapters 4-6

## The First Step: Know Your Yes

Before saying no, clarify what you stand for. Your Yes is the foundation that makes your No principled.

> **Case: The Value Conflict at Work** (Chapter 4): A colleague asks you to cut corners on a project. Your Yes is integrity and quality work. Your No: "I can't do that — it compromises our standards. Let me help find another way."

> **Case: The Time-Boundary** (Chapter 4): A boss asks you to work through the weekend. Your Yes is work-life balance. Your No: "I need to recharge. I'll be fully focused Monday morning."

> **Key takeaway**: Know your Yes before you need your No. Your values are your anchor in difficult conversations.

## BATNA: The Power of Alternatives

Your Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement. Knowing you have options makes your No stronger.

> **Case: The Job Offer** (Chapter 5): If you have another offer, you can say no to a lowball salary. Without alternatives, your No is weak. Build your BATNA before the conversation.

> **Key takeaway**: A strong BATNA transforms your confidence. Know what you'll do if the answer is no.

## The Golden Bridge

After saying no, offer a path forward. A positive No doesn't leave the other person stranded.

> **Case: The Declined Invitation** (Chapter 6): "I can't attend. Let me recommend someone who'd be perfect." This turns a no into an act of help.

> **Key takeaway**: After saying No, build a bridge. Offer alternatives that respect the other person's needs.
