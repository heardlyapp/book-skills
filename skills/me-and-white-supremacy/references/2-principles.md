# Key Principles — Me and White Supremacy

## I. White Supremacy Is a System, Not Just Individual Racism

"White supremacy is a system of oppression that maintains the dominance of white people and the subordination of people of color." It's not just Klan rallies or explicit slurs. It includes colorblindness ("I don't see color"), white exceptionalism ("I marched for civil rights"), and tone policing ("I'd listen if you were calmer"). The system was not created by anyone alive today, but it is maintained and upheld by everyone who holds white privilege. Case: The journal prompt "How have I benefited from white privilege?" forces examination of this.

## II. The Work Starts With You — Not "Those White People Out There"

"This work is not about those white people 'out there.' It is about you. Just you." Saad refuses the common deflection of pointing at worse-offenders. The workbook is structured as personal journaling, not external critique. Case: Day after day, the prompts ask "How have **I** done this?" Not "How have other white people done this?"

## III. Discomfort Is Non-Negotiable

"Racial discomfort is inherent to an authentic examination of white supremacy." DiAngelo's foreword: "Building the racial stamina required to challenge the racist status quo is a critical part of our work as white people." Saad warns: you will feel overwhelmed, intimidated, unrewarded. "I won't lie to you: it is." Case: The journal prompt for white fragility asks readers to describe a specific moment they felt defensive — and sit with the discomfort.

## IV. Ally Is a Practice, Not an Identity

"Ally is not a title you give yourself — it's a practice you engage in." Saad distinguishes performative allyship (posting a black square on Instagram) from substantive allyship (taking action that risks your comfort, privilege, or relationships). Case: Week 3 is entirely devoted to turning good intentions into sustained action.

## V. Good Ancestor Is the Goal

Saad's driving purpose: "To become a good ancestor." The Octavia Butler quote from Earthseed frames antiracism as future-oriented purpose, not guilt management. "To shape God with wisdom and forethought to benefit your world, your people, your life. Consider consequences. Minimize harm." Case: When the work feels pointless, Saad brings it back to this question: "What kind of ancestor will you be?"

## VI. Avoidance Is a Choice

"Not choosing is also a choice." Many white people opt out of engaging with racism because it's uncomfortable. Saad argues this avoidance is itself a form of upholding white supremacy. Case: "How have you managed not to know?" — DiAngelo's challenge in the foreword. In the Google age, claiming ignorance is no longer innocent.

## VII. The Work Is Never Done

"Antiracism is not a destination — it's a continuous practice." The 28-day structure is a beginning, not an end. Saad provides a "Now What?" section guiding readers beyond the workbook. Case: The final day asks readers to write commitments with specific timelines and accountability structures.
