# Core Framework — Me and White Supremacy

## Foreword by Robin DiAngelo

"All right! You say. I get it! Now what do I do?" This is the #1 question DiAngelo gets from white audience members. Her diagnosis: "Most white people don't really want to know what to do about racism if it will require anything of them that is inconvenient or uncomfortable." She challenges white readers: "How have you managed not to know?" In the era of Google and social media, the information on what white people can do is everywhere. "Why haven't you looked it up, as you would have done for any other topic that interested you?" Her conclusion: the list of reasons (I wasn't educated, I don't talk about it, I don't want to feel guilty) is itself a guide for moving forward. Saad's book addresses each point. "This book is a gift of compassion from a brilliant Black woman willing to guide you through a deep examination of white racial conditioning in service of your liberation."

## Part I: Welcome to the Work

Saad introduces herself: a Black, Arab, Muslim woman of Palestinian, Lebanese, and East African descent. She lives in Qatar. Her driving purpose: "The primary force that drives my work is a passionate desire to become a good ancestor. My purpose is to help create change, facilitate healing, and seed new possibilities for those who will come after I am gone."

**The book's origin:** She started a 28-day Instagram challenge on white supremacy. It went viral. She turned it into a free PDF workbook, downloaded by more than 100,000 people worldwide. The published book is the expanded version.

**What is white supremacy?** Not just obvious forms (white hoods, slurs, explicit discrimination). "White supremacy is a system of oppression that maintains the dominance of white people and the subordination of people of color. It operates in both overt and subtle ways." This includes colorblindness ("I don't see color"), white exceptionalism ("But I'm one of the good ones"), and tone policing.

**Who this is for:** People with white privilege who are ready to do the work. "This work is not about those white people 'out there.' It is about you. Just you." Saad is explicit: this will be overwhelming, intimidating, and unrewarding. "I won't lie to you: it is. You will become overwhelmed when you begin to discover the depths of your internalized white supremacy."

## Part II: The Work — 28 Days

**Week 1: The Basics (Days 1-7).** White privilege — "How have I benefited from white privilege?" White fragility — "When have I demonstrated white fragility?" Tone policing — "When have I dismissed a BIPOC's point because of their tone?" Colorblindness — "When have I said 'I don't see color'?" Exceptionalism — "When have I thought 'I'm not like those other white people'?"

**Week 2: Anti-Blackness, Stereotypes, Cultural Appropriation (Days 8-14).** Anti-Blackness is a specific form of racism uniquely harmful to Black people. "Anti-Blackness is a form of racism that is particularly virulent and pervasive." Racial stereotypes — examining stereotypes you hold. Cultural appropriation — "taking elements of a culture that is not your own without permission or credit." Case: the line between appreciation and appropriation.

**Week 3: Allyship (Days 15-21).** "Ally is not a title you give yourself — it's a practice you engage in." Distinguishing performative allyship (posting on social media, saying the right things) from substantive allyship (taking action that risks your comfort or privilege). Calling in vs calling out. How to take feedback without getting defensive.

**Week 4: Power, Relationships, Commitments (Days 22-28).** How power operates in cross-racial relationships. Making specific, measurable commitments. "What will you do differently after these 28 days?" Not "I'll be less racist" — specific actions with timelines.

## Key Concepts with Book Cases

**White Fragility:** Coined by Robin DiAngelo. Case: A white reader who starts feeling defensive on Day 3 of the workbook. Saad's instruction: don't stop — sit with the discomfort. "Racial discomfort is inherent to an authentic examination of white supremacy."

**Tone Policing:** "I'd listen to you if you were calmer." Case: Saad's journal prompt asks "When have I dismissed what a BIPOC said because of the tone they used to say it?" This forces white readers to examine how they've used tone as a reason to avoid engaging with content.

**The Good Ancestor Frame:** Saad quotes Octavia Butler's Earthseed from Parable of the Talents: "To shape God with wisdom and forethought to benefit your world, your people, your life. Consider consequences. Minimize harm. Ask questions. Seek answers. Learn. Teach." This reframes antiracism from guilt-driven to purpose-driven.
