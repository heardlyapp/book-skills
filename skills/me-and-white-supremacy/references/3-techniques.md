# Techniques — Me and White Supremacy

## How to Do Antiracism Work (Based on Layla F. Saad)

### Technique 1: The Daily Journal Protocol
Each day, read the topic introduction, then answer 3-5 journal prompts in writing. No skimming. Write physically (in the book or a separate journal). The act of writing externalizes thoughts that can remain comfortably vague when kept in your head. Case: Saad's original Instagram challenge had thousands of people journaling daily. The published book retains this structure — it is not a book to read, it is a book to write in.

### Technique 2: The Complicity Audit
For each topic, the prompt is never "Do I do this?" — it's always "How have I done this?" Assume complicity. Investigate. Case: For white fragility, don't ask "Am I fragile?" — the real question is "When have I demonstrated white fragility? Describe the situation, your reaction, and what you could have done differently."

### Technique 3: The "I Notice" Deflection Pause
When you feel defensive, say to yourself: "I notice I'm feeling defensive. I'm going to sit with this discomfort instead of acting on it." Defensiveness is a reflex — noticing it without acting is the first step to changing it. Case: Tone policing ("I'd listen if you were calmer") is often a defensive reaction to discomfort. Recognizing it in the moment creates a choice.

### Technique 4: The Good Ancestor Reframe
When the work feels overwhelming, ask: "Will this make me a good ancestor? Will this benefit those who come after me?" This shifts motivation from guilt (I'm bad) to purpose (I'm building). Case: Saad's Octavia Butler quote: "To shape God with wisdom and forethought to benefit your world, your people, your life. Consider consequences. Minimize harm."

### Technique 5: The Book Circle Structure
Saad provides group circle guidelines in the appendix: set agreements (no fixing, no saving, no advising), use a talking piece, share from personal experience (not theory). Case: The Circle Way framework — each person shares their journal insights without being interrupted, "fixed," or debated. This prevents the pattern of white people debating abstract theory instead of sitting with personal complicity.

### Technique 6: The "Resource Up" Commitment
Don't ask BIPOC to educate you. Saad provides extensive resources. The technique: before asking a question, Google it. Read the books in the bibliography. "How have you managed not to know?" Not knowing is no longer an excuse. Case: When a white reader wants to ask about cultural appropriation, Saad expects them to read the relevant section, do the journal prompts, and only then bring questions to a circle.

### Technique 7: The Specific Commitment Covenant
At the end of 28 days, write specific, measurable, time-bound commitments. Not "I'll be less racist." Specific: "I will call out the next racist joke I hear in my family. I will read one book per month by a Black author. I will donate 5% of my income to this organization. I will audit my hiring practices." Case: Saad's framework makes accountability possible by forcing specificity.
