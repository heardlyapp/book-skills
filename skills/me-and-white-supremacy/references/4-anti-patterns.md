# Anti-Patterns — Me and White Supremacy

## The Central Error: "I'm a Good Person, So I'm Not Racist"

"White supremacy is not about good people — it's about a system." The "good person" defense is the most common barrier. Saad: good intentions don't negate systemic participation. Case: DiAngelo's foreword points out that asking "What do I do?" is often disingenuous — it's a way to deflect discomfort while preserving the self-image of a good person.

### Mistake 1: "I don't see color"
Colorblindness is not a solution — it's a form of denial. "When you say you don't see color, you are saying you don't see me." Case: Saad's Week 1 journal prompt asks readers to examine specific moments they used colorblind language. The goal is not to see less race — it's to see more clearly how race operates.

### Mistake 2: "White people experience racism too"
Prejudice can happen to anyone. Racism requires systemic power. Saad distinguishes: an individual BIPOC can hold prejudice against white people, but they cannot enact racism against white people in a system designed to benefit white people at their expense. Case: The workbook explores this distinction in the context of understanding the difference between individual bias and systemic oppression.

### Mistake 3: "You're being too sensitive — I was just joking"
This is tone policing combined with gaslighting. Saad's journal prompt: "When have I dismissed what a BIPOC said because of the tone they used?" Case: "I'd listen to you if you were calmer" is a classic maneuver that shifts focus from the content of the message to the delivery — and always to the advantage of the person in power.

### Mistake 4: "But I posted a black square / marched / donated"
Performative allyship is about looking like you care, not actually doing the work. Case: Saad distinguishes between optical allyship (publicly signaling virtue without taking personal risk) and substantive allyship (taking actions that may cost you comfort, privilege, or relationships). Week 3 is designed to move readers from the former to the latter.

### Mistake 5: "I feel so guilty — I don't know what to do"
White guilt can be a form of resistance. Case: Saad's framework: guilt is not an action. If guilt leads to paralysis, it's just another way of staying comfortable. "Move through it." The entire workbook is designed to channel discomfort into action.

### Mistake 6: "People of color should teach me"
This places the burden of education on the people most harmed by racism. Case: Saad provides extensive resources (bibliography, glossary, further learning) precisely so white people can educate themselves without relying on BIPOC emotional labor. "How have you managed not to know?"
