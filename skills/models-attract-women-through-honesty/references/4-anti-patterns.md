# Anti-Patterns — Models: Attract Women Through Honesty

## The Central Error: "There's a Magic Formula to Attract Any Woman"

"Most dating advice is based on the premise that if you say the right words and do the right things, you can make any woman want you." Manson argues this is false. "Attraction is not a choice — it's a response." Case: PUA techniques create the illusion of control but produce hollow results.

### Mistake 1: "Fake it till you make it"
"Faking confidence is not the same as being confident." Women can sense when you're playing a role. "The truth is always shining through." Case: A man using a scripted opener may get a number — but he hasn't actually developed genuine social skills.

### Mistake 2: "You need to be an asshole"
"Women don't want assholes — they want men who aren't needy." The "nice guy vs asshole" dichotomy is false. Both are manifestations of insecurity. The real alternative: a man who is honest, vulnerable, and self-assured.

### Mistake 3: "More money / better looks / higher status will fix everything"
"Age, money and looks are not the deciding factors." Manson provides evidence: "Many men with money and looks still fail with women. Many men without them succeed." The difference is internal — not external. Case: The "rich guy who can't get a girlfriend" is a cliché for a reason.

### Mistake 4: "You need to hide your sexual interest"
"Creepiness is when a man's behavior doesn't match his intentions." If you're attracted to a woman but act like you just want to be friends, she'll sense the mismatch and feel uncomfortable. Case: "Hiding your interest is more creepy than expressing it."

### Mistake 5: "Rejection means there's something wrong with you"
"It's usually not about you." A woman may have a boyfriend, may not be in the mood, may have had a bad day. Manson: "Men who only experience rejection are those who take every rejection personally."

### Mistake 6: "You need to be perfect to approach"
"You don't need to be perfect — you need to be honest." Case: Manson's students who approached with honest vulnerability ("I'm a little nervous, but I wanted to say hi") often got better results than those who attempted to project flawless confidence.
