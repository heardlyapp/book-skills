## 7 Principles of Nonverbal Communication

### 1. The Limbic System Never Lies

The ancient brain that controls survival responses — freeze, flight, fight — operates below conscious awareness. Body language is limbic, not cortical. This is why it is reliable.

### 2. Establish a Baseline First

Before you can detect deviations, you must know what "normal" looks like for that person. Observe them in a neutral setting before drawing conclusions.

> **Case: The Nervous Executive** (Chapter 2): Navarro describes an executive who always touched his neck during meetings. His colleagues thought he was lying. Actually, he had a chronic neck condition. The behavior was baseline for him — not a deception cue. Without establishing baseline, the interpretation would have been wrong.
> **Key takeaway**: The same behavior (neck touching) can mean stress for one person and a medical condition for another. Baseline is essential.

### 3. Look for Clusters, Not Single Cues

One behavior alone is not reliable. Multiple signals pointing in the same direction create confidence.

### 4. Pacifying Behaviors Are the Most Powerful Cues

Neck touching, face stroking, thigh rubbing, lip touching — these self-soothing gestures reveal stress. They are the most reliable indicators of discomfort.

### 5. Feet First, Face Last

Read from the ground up. Feet reveal true emotions. The face is the most controlled and deceptive part.

> **Case: The Woman Who Said Yes But Pointed Away** (Chapter 3): A woman told her husband she was happy to attend a social event. But her feet were pointed toward the door. Navarro notes: the feet revealed her true desire — to leave. The mouth said yes; the feet said no.
> **Key takeaway**: When words and body language conflict, believe the body.

### 6. Incongruence Is the Strongest Deception Signal

When what a person says does not match what their body shows, that person is concealing something. The mismatch itself is the signal.

### 7. Context Changes Everything

Body language must be interpreted in context. Crossed arms in a cold room mean something different than crossed arms in an interrogation.
