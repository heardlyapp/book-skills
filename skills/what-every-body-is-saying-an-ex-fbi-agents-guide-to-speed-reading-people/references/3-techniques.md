## Practical Techniques

### 1. The Baseline Observation

Before an important conversation, observe the person in a neutral setting. Note their normal posture, hand position, foot position, and speech patterns. Deviations from this baseline are significant.

### 2. The Pacifying Identification

Watch for self-soothing gestures: neck touching, face stroking, thigh rubbing, lip pressing, ear pulling. These signals of stress are the most reliable indicators of discomfort.

> **Case: The Neck Toucher** (Chapter 5): Navarro interviewed a man who repeatedly touched his neck with his palm. The behavior intensified when certain topics were raised. Navarro learned that the man was hiding knowledge about those topics. Neck touching is one of the most powerful pacifying behaviors — it is a literal attempt to soothe anxiety by touching a high-density nerve area.
> **Key takeaway**: The neck is full of nerve endings. Touching it is the body's way of self-soothing. Watch for changes in pacifying behavior intensity.

### 3. The Ventral Fronting Test

Notice when someone turns their torso toward or away from you. Turning toward (ventral fronting) signals comfort. Turning away (ventral denial) signals discomfort or disagreement.

### 4. The Steeple Detection

Watch for hand steepling — fingers touching, pointing up like a church steeple. This is a signal of confidence and authority. A person who steeples is comfortable and sure.

### 5. The Eye Block Awareness

Notice when someone closes their eyes tightly or looks down briefly when exposed to something negative. This is eye blocking — a hardwired limbic response to protect the brain from unpleasant visual input.

> **Case: The Interview with the Spy** (Chapter 7): Navarro describes an interview where the subject kept touching his nose and covering his mouth while answering specific questions. These are pacifying behaviors that reveal discomfort with the topic. Combined with eye blocking, the cluster of signals was strong enough to indicate deception.
> **Key takeaway**: Pacifying clusters (multiple behaviors together) are far more reliable than any single gesture.
