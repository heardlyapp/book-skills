# 2. Principles — Digital Privacy and Self-Determination

> Source: "Goodbye Google" by John Thomas (2017). Principles drawn from Part 1 (reasons to leave) and the Goodbye Books philosophy.

## The 7 Principles of Digital Privacy

### Principle 1: You Are the Product
If you're not paying for a service, the revenue comes from selling access to you. Google's "free" services are funded by advertising, which requires collecting as much data about you as possible. Understanding this fundamental economic reality is the prerequisite for all privacy decisions.

### Principle 2: Informed Consent Is Meaningless Without Transparency
Google's Terms of Service and Privacy Policy are legally binding documents that most users never read. The ones who do find language like "Our automated systems analyze your content (including emails)" — which means Google reads your email. Informed consent requires knowing what you're consenting to.

> **Case: The Terms of Service Translation** (Part 1): The book provides a "translation" of Google's ToS and Privacy Policy into plain English. For example: "We provide services 'AS-IS'" translates to "If something bad happens, Google isn't responsible." The translations reveal that Google's legal protections are designed to protect Google, not its users.
> **Key takeaway**: Legal documents are written to protect the company, not you. If you haven't read what you agreed to, you haven't given informed consent.

### Principle 3: Power Imbalance Is the Real Problem
The issue isn't that Google might read one email. It's that Google has the power to read ALL your emails, your location history, your search queries, your private photos, and your voice recordings — and can use that data in ways you can't predict or control. The power imbalance, not the specific data point, is the issue.

### Principle 4: Convenience Has a Hidden Cost
The ease of "one Google account for everything" comes at the price of total dependence. Every Google service you use makes it harder to leave. The cost of convenience is lock-in.

### Principle 5: Partial Progress Is Real Progress
You don't have to delete every Google account tomorrow. Moving one service, reducing your use of one product, or simply becoming more aware of data collection is a step forward.

> **Case: The "Not All-or-Nothing" Framework** (Introduction): The book explicitly states that leaving Google doesn't have to be all-or-nothing. You can migrate your email while keeping YouTube, or switch to a privacy-focused browser while keeping Gmail for now. Each step reduces your exposure, even if you never fully leave.
> **Key takeaway:** Privacy is not a binary state. Every reduction in data sharing is a meaningful gain.

### Principle 6: Technology Is Political
The design of Google's services reflects political choices about who benefits and who loses. The choice to scan emails for advertising data is a political decision about how to allocate the value created by users' data.

### Principle 7: Your Data Belongs to You
The fundamental assumption of the privacy movement: your personal data is your property. It should not be collected, analyzed, or sold without your explicit, informed, and revocable consent.
