# 4. Anti-Patterns — Migration Traps and Google Lock-In

> Source: "Goodbye Google" by John Thomas (2017). Anti-patterns drawn from Part 1 (why Google is hard to leave) and Part 2 Step 5 (loose ends and gotchas).

## Mistake 1: The All-or-Nothing Trap

**What it looks like**: You decide to leave Google completely or not at all. Since a complete migration seems overwhelming, you do nothing.

**Why it fails**: The all-or-nothing mindset is the biggest barrier to improving your privacy. The book explicitly states that partial progress — migrating email while keeping YouTube, reducing Google search use, switching browsers — is meaningful progress. The perfect is the enemy of the good.

> **Case: The "Goodbye Books" Philosophy** (Part 2): The Goodbye Books philosophy is about removing what doesn't add value to your life. It's not about removing everything — it's about making conscious choices about what stays and what goes. A partial migration is a victory.
> **Key takeaway**: Don't let the scope of a complete migration prevent you from taking the first step. Moving your email is a win. Celebrate it.

## Mistake 2: Deleting Before You're Ready

**What it looks like**: You delete your Gmail account in a fit of privacy concern, only to discover you forgot to migrate a critical service — bank statements, a professional certification, a government login.

**Why it fails**: Once Gmail is deleted, you can't receive verification emails. Some services don't allow email changes without email verification. You can find yourself locked out of accounts.

**The fix**: 2-4 weeks of parallel running. Don't delete until you're certain.

## Mistake 3: Underestimating Google's Ecosystem Lock-In

**What it looks like**: You think leaving Gmail is "just changing email." Then you discover that YouTube requires a Google account, Google Drive files are tied to your account, Android phone features depend on Google services, and family sharing features break.

**Why it fails**: Google's services are designed to be interdependent. The more Google services you use, the higher the switching cost. This is intentional on Google's part.

> **Case: The Services That Don't Need Gmail** (Part 2 Step 5): The book provides a critical breakdown: You can use YouTube with a non-Gmail email address (but it still requires a Google account). Google Drive and Google Photos are tied to your Google account but can be used without Gmail. Android can be used without Google Play Services (but it's difficult). Understanding these distinctions is essential to planning your migration.
> **Key takeaway**: Know which Google services are truly independent and which are locked together. Migration requires untangling, not just deleting.

## Mistake 4: Not Backing Up Before Migrating

**What it looks like**: You start migrating without downloading your emails, contacts, calendars, and files from Google.

**Why it fails**: Google Takeout exists precisely because Google knows leaving is hard. Use it. Download everything before you start. You may not need the backup — but if you do and don't have it, your data is gone.

> **Case: Google Takeout** (Part 2 Step 5): Google Takeout allows you to export your data — emails, contacts, calendar events, Drive files, Photos — in standard formats (MBOX, CSV, ICS). The book recommends using Takeout as the first step of any migration, even before choosing a new provider.
> **Key takeaway**: Your data belongs to you. Download it before you make any changes. Takeout is your insurance policy against migration mistakes.
