# 1. Core Framework — The 6-Step Migration Plan

> Source: "Goodbye Google — Gmail/Inbox Edition" by John Thomas (2017). Part 2: A Step-by-Step Guide to Replacing Gmail with Something Better.

## The Migration Framework

The core framework of the book is a 6-step migration plan designed to take a user from full Gmail dependence to a self-chosen alternative.

### Step 1: What Do You Want to Achieve?
Define your goals before you start. Are you concerned about privacy? Do you want to reduce your Google footprint? Do you need specific features? Clarifying your "why" determines which alternative to choose and how aggressively to migrate.

### Step 2: Choosing an Alternative
The book covers three primary alternatives:
- **ProtonMail**: End-to-end encryption, Swiss privacy laws, free tier with limited storage
- **FastMail**: Feature-rich, calendar + contacts integrated, paid only
- **Outlook.com**: Familiar interface, integrates with Microsoft ecosystem, free

### Step 3: Create a List of Everywhere You Use Gmail
This is the most time-consuming but most critical step. Every website, service, bank, social media account, and newsletter that has your Gmail address needs to be documented. The book provides a master checklist.

### Step 4: Directing Email Away from Your Gmail Account
Set up forwarding from Gmail to your new account. Update your primary accounts (banking, government, work, social media) to use the new address. Run both accounts in parallel for at least 2 weeks.

### Step 5: Loose Ends and Gotchas
Some Google services don't require Gmail — others are tied to your Google account. YouTube, Google Drive, Google Photos, and Android all have different relationships with your Gmail address. Know which is which.

### Step 6: Going Nuclear — Delete Your Gmail Account
Optional but final. Once you're confident all critical services have been migrated, you can permanently delete your Gmail account. The book provides step-by-step instructions for account deletion.

> **Case: Google Reading Airbnb Confirmation Emails** (Part 1, Introduction): The author describes how he booked a room on Airbnb and later saw the address highlighted on Google Maps with "staying here" and the date. Google was reading his Gmail, parsing confirmation emails, and turning the data into Google Maps features. The same happened with Expedia travel arrangements. This personal experience motivated the author to write the book.
> **Key takeaway**: Google's data collection is invisible until you notice it. The Airbnb story is not an anomaly — it's how the system is designed to work.

> **Case: The "Don't Be Evil" Timeline** (Part 1, Introduction): The book traces Google's motto evolution: 2000 "Don't be evil" → 2010 "Evil is difficult to define" → 2015 "Do the right thing" → 2017 "We make autonomous battlefield robots." This timeline illustrates the author's argument that Google's culture changed as it grew from a startup into a conglomerate.
> **Key takeaway**: Corporate mottos change as business models evolve. Judge companies by their behavior, not their marketing.
