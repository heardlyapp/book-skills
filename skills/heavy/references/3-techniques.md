# Weight and Body

> Source: Heavy, Boy Man, Black Abundance, Addict Americans

## The Body as Archive

Laymon's body — at 319 pounds — is not just a physical fact. It is an archive of trauma.

### What the Weight Holds

| Function | What the Weight Did |
|----------|-------------------|
| Protection | Made him invisible, undesirable, safe from attention |
| Storage | Held the pain he could not process |
| Rebellion | A refusal to become the thin, disciplined, perfect black son |
| Evidence | Proof that something was wrong |

> **Case: The 319-to-165 Lie** (Been)
> The book Laymon initially wrote was a weight-loss story: start at 319, finish at 165, with a neat moral about discipline and self-love. He threw it out because it was not true. The true story was not about the calories — it was about the secrets. The weight was not the problem. The weight was a symptom. He writes: "I wanted to write a lie. I wrote that lie. It was titillating. You would have loved it."
> **Key takeaway:** The conventional weight-loss memoir is a genre that sells — because it reduces complex human suffering to a simple problem (calories in < calories out) with a simple solution (discipline). Laymon refuses this lie. His body was not a math problem. It was a story.

> **Case: Food as Love, Food as Weapon** (Black Abundance)
> Grandmama's cooking was love: fried chicken, collard greens, cornbread, sweet potato pie. The abundance of food in Grandmama's house was "black abundance" — a refusal of scarcity, a declaration of wealth in the midst of poverty. But his mother weaponized food — controlling what Kiese ate, commenting on his weight, using his body as evidence of his failures. Food was both the language of love and the instrument of shame.
> **Key takeaway:** The same food that meant safety and abundance at Grandmama's meant failure and shame at his mother's. Laymon shows that the meaning of food is never just nutritional. It is relational. It is emotional. It is historical.
