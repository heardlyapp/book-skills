# Anti-Patterns — The Lies We Tell

> Source: Heavy, Been, Addict Americans

## The Five Lies

### 1. The Weight-Loss Lie
**Error**: "I can tell my story as a simple before-and-after."
**Reality**: Bodies are not math problems. Weight loss without addressing trauma is just a thinner lie.

### 2. The Redemption Lie
**Error**: "The story should end with forgiveness and healing."
**Reality**: Some stories do not resolve neatly. Kiese and his mother do not have a Hollywood ending.

### 3. The Race Card Lie
**Error**: "If I just explain racism, white readers will understand."
**Reality**: White readers often want a simple explanation of black suffering. Laymon refuses to provide it.

### 4. The Exceptional Black Person Lie
**Error**: "If I am excellent enough, I will escape."
**Reality**: Excellence does not protect you. Kiese was brilliant, educated, and published — and still struggled with addiction, debt, and despair.

### 5. The Forgive and Forget Lie
**Error**: "You must forgive your mother to move on."
**Reality**: Kiese does not forgive his mother in any conventional sense. He loves her. He continues to work on their relationship. He does not erase what she did.

> **Case: The Gambling Addiction** (Addict Americans)
> In his late twenties, Kiese developed a severe gambling addiction. He lost thousands of dollars. He lied to everyone. He was ashamed. The gambling was a substitute for the emotions he could not process — the weight of his childhood, the pressure of being a successful black writer, the unresolved relationship with his mother. The addiction was not a moral failure. It was a survival strategy that had stopped working.
> **Key takeaway:** Laymon's gambling addiction is not a separate story from the rest of the book. It is the same story. When the weight becomes unbearable, you find ways to escape. Gambling was his escape. The lesson: addiction is never just about the substance or the behavior — it is about what you are trying to escape.

> **Case: Reading While Black** (Home Worked)
> Laymon became a professor. He taught writing. But he carried the weight of being the "exceptional black person" in white institutions. He was tokenized, overworked, expected to represent his race. He was praised for his excellence — and required to be excellent at all times. The pressure was crushing. He writes: "I was tired of being exceptional. I wanted to be ordinary."
> **Key takeaway:** The demand for exceptionality is a form of violence. Black people in white institutions are often required to be twice as good — and then resented for achieving it. Laymon's exhaustion is political. The lesson: the system is not designed for you to succeed comfortably — even when you do succeed.
