> **Case: The Opening — "I wanted to write a lie"** (Been)
> Laymon opens the book by confessing: his first draft was a lie. It was a conventional weight-loss memoir — start at 319 pounds, end at 165. A story of discipline and triumph. He threw it away. He realized the real story was not about weight loss — it was about what the weight was hiding. The real story was about his mother, his grandmother, Mississippi, abuse, addiction, and the impossibility of telling the truth to the people you love most. The opening is a manifesto: this book will not be easy. It will not be comfortable. It will be true.
> **Key takeaway:** Laymon's decision to admit his first draft was a lie is the most honest opening of any memoir. He tells the reader: I wanted to give you what you want. But I owe you the truth instead. This is the courage that defines the book.

> **Case: The Vegas Slots** (Boy Man)
> At 11 years old, Kiese's mother takes him to Las Vegas. She gives him $5 and tells him to stand next to her and play the slot machine. He wins 260 quarters. They look at each other, get on their knees, and rake the quarters into a cup. This is the first moment of shared abundance — but it is also the moment when Kiese learns: his mother uses him as a partner, not as a child. She is his mother and his friend, his protector and his abuser.
> **Key takeaway:** The Vegas scene captures the duality of their relationship. The love is real. The exploitation is real. The joy is real. The inappropriateness is real. Kiese does not resolve this tension — he holds it.
