# Mother and Son

> Source: Heavy, all parts

## The Central Relationship

The book is written as a letter to "you" — Kiese's mother. This choice transforms the memoir into an act of direct address, an appeal, an accusation, and a love letter all at once.

### The Contradictions

| What She Gave | What She Took |
|---------------|---------------|
| A love of words and reading | Physical safety |
| Intellectual ambition | Emotional stability |
| A standard of excellence | Any sense of enough-ness |

> **Case: The Beatings** (Boy Man)
> Kiese's mother beat him severely — with books ("she knew the weight and length of every book in the house"), with extension cords, with her hands. The beatings were not random. They were punishments for lying, for underperforming, for not being the brilliant black son she needed him to be. But the beatings also taught him something: that his body was not his own, that pain was a condition of love, that survival required submission and resistance simultaneously.
> **Key takeaway:** The beatings are the hardest part of the book — and they are not the whole story. Kiese's mother also took him to the library, taught him to love language, and demanded excellence. The beatings and the love coexisted. Heavy refuses to resolve this contradiction.

> **Case: The Library Card** (Boy Man)
> Amid the violence, there was literacy. Kiese's mother gave him a library card and required him to write. She checked his work. She pushed him. She told him: "You can write your way out of Mississippi." She was right. Writing became his escape — but also his burden. He had to be the brilliant writer she demanded. The pressure to perform excellence was a different kind of weight.
> **Key takeaway:** Kiese's mother's gift of literacy was both liberation and oppression. It freed him to leave Mississippi — but it also bound him to her expectations. The complexity of this gift is the book's deepest theme: the things that save us are also the things that weigh us down.
