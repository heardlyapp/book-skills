# Reference: Investment vs. Speculation

## Overview
Bogle's central distinction is between investment (owning businesses for the long term) and speculation (betting on short-term price movements). He argues that the culture of speculation has come to dominate the culture of investment.

## Case: What Investment Is
Investment is the purchase of a security with the expectation of earning a return from the earnings and dividends generated by the underlying business over time. The investor cares about the company's fundamentals and its long-term prospects. The holding period is measured in years and decades. The investor is a business owner.

## Case: What Speculation Is
Speculation is the purchase of a security with the expectation of profiting from a change in its price, driven by the behavior of other market participants. The speculator cares about momentum, sentiment, and short-term price movements. The holding period is measured in minutes, days, or months. The speculator is a gambler.

## Case: The Clash
The culture of investment was dominant from the 1920s to the 1960s. Since then, speculation has grown to dominate. Trading volume has exploded. Holding periods have collapsed from years to months to microseconds. The financial industry has evolved to serve speculation, not investment. Bogle argues that this shift has harmed investors and society.

## Case: The Consequences
When speculation dominates, markets become volatile, corporate managers focus on short-term earnings rather than long-term investment, and ordinary investors are drawn into a casino mentality that destroys their wealth. Bogle's mission was to restore the culture of investment and protect the individual investor from the speculator's temptation.
