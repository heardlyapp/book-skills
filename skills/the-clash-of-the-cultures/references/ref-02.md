# Reference: The Rise of Index Funds

## Overview
The index fund was Bogle's greatest innovation. It transformed investing by giving ordinary people access to the market's returns at minimal cost.

## Case: The First Index Fund
Bogle launched the First Index Investment Trust in 1975. It tracked the S&P 500. The fund was ridiculed by the investment industry as "Bogle's folly." Critics said it was un-American to settle for average. In its first year, the fund attracted only $11 million. Bogle had to fight to keep it alive. Today, Vanguard's Total Stock Market Index Fund alone has over $1 trillion in assets.

## Case: The Evidence for Indexing
The case for indexing is based on simple arithmetic: the market return is the average of all investors. Active managers cannot all be above average. Their average return equals the market return minus fees. A low-cost index fund captures the market return minus minimal fees. Over time, this mathematical advantage compounds into a massive performance gap.

## Case: The SPIVA Data
Standard & Poor's publishes semiannual data comparing active funds to their benchmarks. The results are consistent: over 1 year, about 60% of active funds underperform. Over 5 years, about 80% underperform. Over 15 years, about 90% underperform. The evidence is so one-sided that the debate between active and passive investing is essentially over.

## Case: The Vanguard Advantage
Vanguard's unique mutual ownership structure allows it to operate at cost. While other fund companies charge 1-2% to maximize profits for their shareholders, Vanguard charges 0.03-0.10% because its only shareholders are the fund investors themselves. This structural advantage is impossible for competitors to replicate.
