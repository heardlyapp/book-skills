# Reference: Practical Guidance

## Overview
Bogle's investment philosophy is simple, evidence-based, and accessible to any investor willing to follow it.

## Case: The Bogle Portfolio
Bogle recommended a simple portfolio: a total stock market index fund, a total international stock index fund, and a total bond market index fund. The allocation should be determined by age (more bonds as you get older). No individual stocks, no sector funds, no active managers. Rebalance once a year. The portfolio is boring — but it works.

## Case: Stay the Course
Bogle's most important advice: stay the course. Investors who panic during bear markets and sell at the bottom lock in their losses. Investors who stay invested through the downturn capture the recovery. The market will fall — it always does — but over the long term, it has always risen. Discipline, not market timing, is the key to investment success.

## Case: Ignore the Noise
The financial media creates a constant stream of information designed to make you feel like you need to do something. Interest rates are changing, a new CEO is hired, a company missed earnings, a recession is coming. Bogle's advice: ignore it. None of this noise should change your long-term investment plan. The most successful investors are often the most boring ones.

## Case: The Wisdom of Simplicity
Complex investment strategies almost always fail. Bogle showed that the simplest portfolio — a single balanced index fund (60% stocks, 40% bonds) — outperformed most actively managed portfolios over any extended period. The reason is simple: complexity adds cost, and cost destroys return. The simplest portfolio is usually the best.
