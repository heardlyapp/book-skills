# Reference: Financialization

## Overview
Bogle was concerned not just about individual investors but about the effect of speculation on society. The financialization of America — the growth of the financial sector as a share of the economy — was a symptom of the clash of cultures.

## Case: The Growth of Trading
In 1960, the annual turnover rate on the New York Stock Exchange was about 12% (average holding period: 8 years). By 2000, turnover exceeded 100%. By 2010, with high-frequency trading, the holding period had collapsed to months, weeks, and in many cases seconds. The relationship between investor and corporation — owner and owned — had been severed by hyperactive trading.

## Case: Short-Termism
Corporate managers are evaluated on quarterly earnings. CEO compensation is tied to stock price. The pressure to deliver short-term results leads to decisions that harm long-term value: cutting R&D, reducing capital investment, taking on debt to buy back stock. Bogle argued that the financial industry's focus on short-term trading had infected corporate governance.

## Case: The Consequences for Society
When financial speculation dominates, capital is misallocated, inequality increases, and the economy becomes less stable. Bogle believed that the culture of speculation had corrupted the culture of investment and that restoring the balance was essential not just for investors but for society as a whole.

## Case: The Shareholder Revolution
The rise of activist investors, hedge funds, and private equity has further intensified short-term pressure. Companies are bought, broken up, and resold. Bogle argued that this "shareholder revolution" had gone too far — shareholders had become owners in name only, treating stocks as trading chips rather than ownership stakes.
