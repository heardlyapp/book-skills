# Reference: The Mutual Fund Industry

## Overview
Bogle was a passionate critic of the mutual fund industry he helped create. He believed the industry had abandoned its fiduciary duty to investors in favor of marketing and profit.

## Case: The Fee Problem
The average actively managed mutual fund charges 1.0-2.0% in expense ratios. Many also charge front-end or back-end loads (sales charges). The turnover generates trading costs that are not included in the expense ratio. Bogle calculated that the total annual cost to the average active fund investor is 2.5% or more. Over a career, that level of cost consumes more than 60% of the potential return.

## Case: The Marketing Machine
Mutual fund companies spend billions on marketing. They advertise past performance, even though past performance does not predict future results. They create complex fund families designed to confuse investors and encourage trading. They compensate financial advisors to recommend high-fee funds. The industry's marketing is designed to obscure the single most important fact: cost matters.

## Case: The Conflict of Interest
Mutual fund companies are owned by management companies, not by fund shareholders. The managers have a fiduciary duty to shareholders, but they also have a duty to their own corporate owners. These duties conflict when the fund company can increase its profits by charging higher fees. Bogle argued that this conflict is the fundamental problem with the mutual fund industry.

This conflict is why Bogle founded Vanguard as a mutual structure — to eliminate it entirely.
