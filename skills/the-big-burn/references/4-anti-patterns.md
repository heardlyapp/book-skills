# 4. Anti-Patterns — The Mistakes That Made the Big Burn Worse

> Source: "The Big Burn" by Timothy Egan (2009). Anti-patterns drawn from the political and tactical failures surrounding the 1910 fire, particularly Chapters 1-5 (the creation) and 10-12 (the aftermath).

## Mistake 1: Underestimating the Fire Season

**What it looks like**: Assuming that a normal fire season will continue to be normal, ignoring the accumulated conditions that make a catastrophic season possible.

**Why it fails**: The summer of 1910 was the driest in memory. By August, fires were burning across the entire Pacific Northwest. Yet the Forest Service had only a few hundred rangers and a tiny budget. The fire season had been building for months, but no one prepared for the worst case.

> **Case: The Spring That Never Came** (Chapters 4-5): The winter of 1909-1910 had little snow. The spring was dry. By June, forests in Idaho and Montana were tinder-dry. And then the summer brought no rain — week after week of high temperatures and desiccating winds. The conditions for the Big Burn were visible months before August 20.
> **Key takeaway**: The signs of a catastrophic fire season are visible in advance. Don't wait for the fire to start to take the threat seriously.

## Mistake 2: Fighting a Fire with Politics

**What it looks like**: Making decisions about fire policy based on political considerations rather than scientific or operational ones.

**Why it fails**: Pinchot's firing in early 1910 was a political act — Taft wanted to distance himself from Roosevelt's progressive policies. The Forest Service entered the fire season without its founding leader. Morale was low, funding was uncertain, and the agency's survival was in question. This political crisis made the Service less effective when the fire came.

> **Case: The Pinchot-Taft Break** (Chapters 3, 6): Taft and Pinchot had a fundamental disagreement over conservation. Pinchot wanted aggressive federal management; Taft favored states' rights and private development. When Pinchot went public with his criticism, Taft fired him. The political fallout consumed Washington while the forests of Idaho were burning.
> **Key takeaway**: Leadership transitions during a crisis window are devastating. If you know a crisis is coming (and the Forest Service knew fire season was coming), resolve leadership disputes before it arrives.

## Mistake 3: The 10 A.M. Policy (Good Intentions, Bad Consequences)

**What it looks like**: Responding to a catastrophic failure with an overcorrection that creates new problems.

**Why it fails**: After the Big Burn, the Forest Service adopted the "10 A.M. policy" — the rule that every wildfire must be contained by 10 A.M. the day after it's discovered. This aggressive suppression policy was understandable: no one ever wanted to see another Big Burn. But a century of suppression created an enormous fuel load in America's forests. When fires inevitably escape containment — as they always do — they burn with unprecedented intensity.

> **Case: The Suppression Paradox** (Chapter 12, Epilogue): The 10 A.M. policy was wildly successful at one level: for decades, the Forest Service was able to contain most fires quickly. But this success created a dangerous condition. Forests that would have burned in small, natural fires became choked with dead wood, thick undergrowth, and dense young trees. When modern fires (1988 Yellowstone, 2020 California megafires) finally burn, they burn hotter and faster than anything the 1910 firefighters could have imagined.
> **Key takeaway**: The solution to one problem can create the next problem. Fire suppression was the right response to the Big Burn — but it was applied too rigidly for too long. Adaptive management, not permanent rules, is the lesson.

## Mistake 4: Sending Inexperienced People into Dangerous Situations

**What it looks like**: Deploying people who are untrained, unequipped, or unprepared into a situation that requires expertise.

**Why it fails**: Many rangers in 1910 had been on the job for weeks. They had no firefighting training. They were given shovels and axes and told to fight walls of flame. Some died. Others saved themselves through improvisation, not skill.

> **Case: The Greenhorns on the Fire Line** (Chapters 6-8): The Forest Service in 1910 was staffed by idealistic young men — college graduates, immigrants, city boys — who had no experience with wildfire. They learned on the job, and some paid for that education with their lives. The few experienced rangers — men like Pulaski and Bill Weigle — carried their crews through sheer force of personality.
> **Key takeaway**: Experience is not optional in high-risk environments. If you can't train people before the crisis, pair them with someone who has been through it before.
