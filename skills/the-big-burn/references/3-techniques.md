# 3. Techniques — Fighting Fire in 1910

> Source: "The Big Burn" by Timothy Egan (2009). Techniques drawn from the firefighting practices of the U.S. Forest Service in 1910, Chapters 6-10.

## Firefighting Techniques of 1910

### The Fire Line Method
In 1910, the primary technique for stopping a wildfire was the fire line: a strip of land cleared of all combustible material, dug by hand with shovels, axes, and pulaskis. The fire line is both a physical barrier and a psychological one — as long as the line holds, the fire hasn't won.

### The Dynamite Option
When fire lines failed, rangers used dynamite to blow up trees, create firebreaks, and even redirect the fire's path. The 1910 fire was fought with more explosives than any military operation on American soil since the Civil War.

### The Backfire
Rangers would set intentional fires ahead of the main fire, designed to remove fuel so the main fire had nothing to burn. This was dangerous — the backfire could become uncontrollable — and controversial.

> **Case: Pulaski's Tunnel Survival** (Chapter 9): When Pulaski and his 45 men were surrounded, he made a series of tactical decisions under extreme duress: (1) Find shelter — the abandoned mine tunnel was dark, but it was solid rock. (2) Create a barrier — wet blankets at the entrance to filter smoke. (3) Control panic — he fired his pistol, shouted, and physically prevented men from running. (4) Accept loss — he knew some men would die. His choices saved 39 out of 45.
> **Key takeaway**: Under extreme conditions, the leader's primary job is to prevent panic. A panicked crew makes worse decisions than any individual would alone.

### The Evacuation Protocol
The mayor of Wallace, Idaho, implemented an improvised evacuation:
1. Sound the alarm
2. Women and children only on the trains
3. All able-bodied men stay to fight
4. Open the jail — inmates become firefighters
5. Designate escape routes (the railways)
6. No pets — every seat is precious

> **Case: The Wallace Evacuation** (Prologue): The mayor of Wallace gave the order at 6 P.M. on August 20. The trains — it was decided — would only take women and children. Men were required to stay and fight. The jail was opened, and prisoners were put to work. The evacuation was chaotic and desperate, but it saved most of the town's 3,500 residents.
> **Key takeaway**: An imperfect plan executed with conviction is better than a perfect plan that comes too late.

## Leadership Techniques

### The Ranger's Two-Week Notice
Many of the rangers fighting the 1910 fire had only been on the job a few weeks. They were college students, recent immigrants, adventurers — not trained firefighters. But they had the essential quality: they stayed when they could have run.

### The Pinchot Recruiting Method
Pinchot recruited rangers from the best universities — Yale, Harvard, Cornell — by appealing to idealism. He offered low pay, hard work, and a chance to be part of something historic. The rangers who came were committed not because of compensation but because of purpose.

> **Case: The College Boy Rangers** (Chapters 4-5): The Forest Service in 1910 was staffed by young idealists — many from elite eastern universities — who had come west to be part of Pinchot's experiment. Their inexperience was a vulnerability, but their commitment was a strength. They stayed to fight the Big Burn when every instinct told them to run.
> **Key takeaway**: Purpose-driven organizations attract people who will do more than their job description. The rangers of 1910 were not well paid, well trained, or well equipped — but they were well motivated.
