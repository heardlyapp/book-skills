# Children's Voices

> Source: Savage Inequalities, all chapters

## Listening to Children

Kozol's most powerful method is letting children speak for themselves. He argues that the voices of children have been "missing from the whole discussion."

### What the Children Say

|-------|----------|-------|

| High school girl | Chicago | "Why do the rich kids get everything and we get nothing?" |

> **Case: The Girl Who Memorized Langston Hughes** (Looking Backward)
> Kozol opens the book with a story from his first year teaching in Boston, 1964. A fourth-grade girl, one of the most embittered children in the class, began to cry when she first heard Langston Hughes's poem "A Dream Deferred." She went home and memorized the lines. The next day, Kozol was fired — for teaching poetry that was "too advanced" and "inflammatory." The girl's tears and Kozol's firing encapsulate the book's theme: the suppression of hope in the lives of poor children.
> **Key takeaway:** The children know. They understand the dream being deferred. They feel the injustice. And the system punishes anyone who tries to give them hope.

> **Case: The Students of East St. Louis** (Chapter 1)
> Kozol spent time with students in East St. Louis's schools. One boy said: "I want to be a doctor. But I don't think I can be because our school doesn't have the right classes." A girl said: "Sometimes I think they want us to be like this — stuck here forever." Another student asked Kozol: "Do you think they would let their children go to school in a building like this?" Kozol could not answer.
> **Key takeaway:** The children's awareness of their own situation is the book's most devastating feature. They know. They have not been fooled. They are just powerless to change it.
