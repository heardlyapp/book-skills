# Jonathan Kozol's Voice & Application Scenarios

> Source: Savage Inequalities, all chapters

## Authorial Voice

Jonathan Kozol writes as a former teacher turned activist-journalist. His voice is:

- **Righteously angry**: This is not a dispassionate analysis. It is a moral indictment.
- **Poetic and biblical**: Kozol quotes Scripture, poetry, and the Constitution with equal force.
- **Grief-stricken**: He loves children, and he grieves for what is being done to them.
- **Unforgiving**: He will not let the reader off the hook. We are all responsible.

### Classic Kozol Quotes

- "In public schooling, social policy has been turned back almost one hundred years."
- "The children know. They have not been fooled."
- "Every budget is a moral document."
- "Why do the rich kids get everything and we get nothing?"
- "We have turned our backs on the moral implications of Brown v. Board."

## Five Application Scenarios

### Scenario 1: A Teacher in an Underfunded School
You work in a school like the ones Kozol describes.
**Lesson**: You are not alone. The system is broken, not you. Kozol's book is a reminder that the problem is structural — and that you are fighting a structural injustice, not failing your students.

### Scenario 2: A Parent Choosing a School District
You are moving and considering school quality.
**Lesson**: The difference between school districts is not about "good" vs "bad" schools — it is about funding. Property values equal school quality. This is the system Kozol exposes.

### Scenario 3: A Policy Maker
You make decisions about education funding.
**Lesson**: Kozol would say: equalize funding. It is not complicated. We know what works — smaller class sizes, better teachers, adequate facilities. We choose not to fund it.

### Scenario 4: A Suburban Resident
You live in a well-funded district and benefit from the system.
**Lesson**: Kozol does not let you off the hook. If your school is well-funded because your neighbors are wealthy, you benefit from inequality. The question is: what will you do about it?

### Scenario 5: A Student Who Survived Underfunded Schools
You made it out. You succeeded despite the system.
**Lesson**: Kozol honors your success — but asks you to look back. The system is still broken. Your survival does not excuse the system.

> **Case: The Teacher Who Stayed** (Looking Backward)
> Kozol's first teaching job was in a segregated Boston school. He was fired for teaching Langston Hughes. But he did not leave education — he became one of its most powerful critics. His career shows that fighting the system does not mean abandoning the students.
> **Key takeaway:** Kozol's own journey — from fired teacher to the nation's most eloquent voice for educational justice — is a case study in turning anger into action.

> **Case: The Children of East St. Louis** (Chapter 1)
> Kozol ends his portrait of East St. Louis with the children's faces. He does not offer easy solutions. He does not pretend that writing a book will change their lives. But he insists: we must see them. We must know what we are doing to them.
> **Key takeaway:** The first step toward justice is seeing clearly. Kozol's book is an act of seeing. The rest is up to us.
