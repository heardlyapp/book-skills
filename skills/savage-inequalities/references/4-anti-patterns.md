# Anti-Patterns — The Myths That Perpetuate Inequality

> Source: Savage Inequalities, all chapters, Looking Backward

## The Five Fatal Beliefs

### 1. "Brown v. Board Solved Segregation"
**Error**: The 1954 Supreme Court decision ended segregation.
**Reality**: Kozol found schools that were 95-99% nonwhite in 1990. De facto segregation is as entrenched as de jure segregation ever was.

### 2. "Money Doesn't Matter in Education"
**Error**: Student outcomes depend on family, culture, and effort — not school funding.
**Reality**: Money buys: smaller class sizes, better teachers, science labs, textbooks, counselors, air conditioning, heat. These matter.

### 3. "Choice and Competition Will Fix It"
**Error**: Charter schools and vouchers create pressure that improves all schools.
**Reality**: Choice without equity is a way for the motivated few to escape. The system remains broken for those left behind.

### 4. "Racial Segregation Is an Accident of Demographics"
**Error**: Segregation is a natural result of housing patterns.
**Reality**: Segregation is produced and maintained by policy: redlining, zoning, property-tax-based school funding.

### 5. "The Poor Don't Care About Education"
**Error**: Poor parents value education less.
**Reality**: Kozol found parents who were desperate for better schools but powerless to achieve them.

> **Case: The End of the Dream** (Looking Backward)
> Kozol notes that Martin Luther King's dream — of black and white children going to school together — has been "effaced almost entirely." Dutiful references to "The Dream" appear on school brochures and Black History Month posters, but the content of the dream — integration, equality, shared destiny — has been abandoned. The nation has "turned its back" on the moral implications of Brown v. Board.
> **Key takeaway:** The language of civil rights has been preserved, but the substance has been abandoned. We talk about "The Dream" while maintaining a system of segregated, unequal schools.

> **Case: The Suburban Defense** (Chapters 2, 6)
> Kozol documents how wealthy suburban districts resist equalization. When courts order states to equalize school funding, wealthy districts lobby their legislatures to delay, dilute, or reverse the reforms. In Texas, the Edgewood ruling was followed by years of legislative resistance. In Illinois, the Chicago funding gap persisted. The wealthy use the political system to protect their advantage.
> **Key takeaway:** Educational inequality is not an accident — it is actively maintained by those who benefit from it. The suburban defense of privilege is the real obstacle to reform.
