# The Property Tax Funding Trap

> Source: Savage Inequalities, Chapters 2, 6, Appendix

## How School Funding Works

### The Mechanism

| Factor | Rich District | Poor District |
|--------|---------------|---------------|
| Property values | $500,000+ per home | $30,000-80,000 per home |
| Tax rate | Low (1-2%) | Cripplingly high (4-6%) |
| Revenue per pupil | $12,000-18,000 | $2,000-5,000 |
| Per-staff spending | High salaries, good benefits | Low salaries, no extras |

> **Case: Chicago vs. New Trier** (Chapter 2)
> Chicago city schools spent approximately $3,000 per pupil in 1990. New Trier High School, in a wealthy Chicago suburb, spent over $15,000. New Trier had an Olympic-sized pool, a planetarium, a video studio, and 19 Advanced Placement courses. Chicago high schools had metal detectors, armed guards, and classes in converted storage rooms. The children in Chicago asked Kozol: "Why do the rich kids get everything?"
> **Key takeaway:** The gap between Chicago and New Trier is not about effort or merit. It is about property values. The state of Illinois could have equalized funding — but it chose not to.

> **Case: Edgewood vs. Alamo Heights, San Antonio** (Chapter 6)
> In San Antonio, the Edgewood Independent School District (95% Hispanic and black) spent $2,000 per pupil. Alamo Heights, a few miles away (90% white), spent $12,000. Edgewood had no air conditioning, no science labs, no counselors. Alamo Heights had language labs, computer rooms, and a full-time college counselor. The Edgewood case went to the Texas Supreme Court, which ruled that the state's funding system was unconstitutional. But the ruling was never fully implemented.
> **Key takeaway:** Even when courts rule that unequal funding is unconstitutional, the political system resists equalization. Wealthy districts fight to keep their advantage.
