> **Case: East St. Louis** (Chapter 1)
> Kozol's opening chapter is a devastating portrait of East St. Louis, described as "America's Soweto." The city is 98% black. It has no obstetric services, no regular garbage collection, and few jobs. Chemical plants (Monsanto, Pfizer) spew toxins. The child asthma rate is among the highest in the nation. Schools have broken windows, rats, and no heat. One science teacher spent $500 of her own money on lab equipment — and her school served a city where the average family income was $7,500.
> **Key takeaway:** East St. Louis is not an anomaly — it is a systemic outcome. The combination of racial segregation, industrial pollution, and property-tax-based funding creates a trap that children almost never escape.

> **Case: The South Bronx vs. Long Island** (Chapter 3)
> In New York City, Kozol contrasts schools in the South Bronx with schools on Long Island. In the Bronx: classes held in basements, no gym, no library, textbooks from the 1970s. In Manhasset (Long Island): Olympic-sized swimming pool, planetarium, art studios, computer labs, and a faculty with advanced degrees. The funding gap: $5,000 per pupil in the Bronx vs $18,000 in Manhasset.
> **Key takeaway:** The difference between schools 20 miles apart is not natural — it is policy. The property tax system guarantees that affluent areas will always have better schools. The children in the Bronx know it.
