# Anti-Patterns

## 1. Chasing Happiness Through Achievement

**The mistake**: Believing that getting what you want will make you happy.

**Fix**: External achievements provide temporary satisfaction, not lasting happiness. Train your mind, not your circumstances.

## 2. Trying to Eliminate Negative Emotions

**The mistake**: Treating difficult emotions as problems to be solved.

**Fix**: Emotions are not the enemy. They are visitors. Let them come and go.

> **Case: The Suppressor** (Chapter 6): A man tried to "get rid of" his anger through years of meditation. But it kept coming back. When he learned to be with his anger instead of fighting it, the anger lost its power over him.
> **Key takeaway**: Suppression is not transformation. You can't eliminate emotions — but you can change your relationship with them.

## 3. Comparing Your Meditation to Others

**The mistake**: Measuring your meditation by how "good" it is.

**Fix**: There is no "good" meditation. Sitting with a chaotic mind is meditation. Sitting with a calm mind is meditation. Both are practice.

> **Case: The Comparison Trap** (Chapter 5): A meditator heard others describe blissful experiences in meditation. His own sessions were mundane — lots of thinking, some awareness, nothing special. He felt he was doing it wrong. The teaching: ordinary meditation IS the practice. Extraordinary experiences are not the goal.
> **Key takeaway**: Don't compare your meditation to anyone else's. The practice is in the doing, not in the experiencing.

## 4. Making Meditation a Chore

**The mistake**: Treating meditation as another task on your to-do list.

**Fix**: Approach meditation as a gift to yourself, not an obligation. A few mindful breaths are better than a forced 20-minute session.

## 5. Expecting Immediate Results

**The mistake**: Expecting one meditation session to transform your mind.

**Fix**: Mind training is like physical training. One workout doesn't change your body. Consistent practice over time does.
