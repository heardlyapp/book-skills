# Principles: The Foundations of Successful Breastfeeding

## Principle 1: Preparation Matters
Breastfeeding is a learned skill, not an instinct. Just as you prepare for birth with classes and reading, preparing for breastfeeding dramatically improves outcomes. The book recommends: read about breastfeeding during pregnancy, attend a La Leche League meeting *before* the baby arrives, talk to other nursing mothers, and prepare your environment.

**Key insight:** The mother who prepares is less likely to be derailed by normal challenges. Most breastfeeding problems are solved faster when you already know where to find help.

## Principle 2: Support Networks Are Essential
"You were designed to have a network." The book is emphatic: no mother was meant to breastfeed alone. In traditional societies, new mothers were surrounded by experienced women. Modern isolation is a barrier to breastfeeding success. Build your network before you need it: La Leche League, family, friends, online groups, lactation consultants.

> **Case: The Isolated Mother** (Chapter 2, "Building Your Network"): A mother living far from family with a husband who traveled for work was struggling with breastfeeding. She had no one to ask basic questions. She found a La Leche League online group and posted: "Should nursing hurt?" The responses she got — positioning advice, encouragement, and a local Leader's phone number — transformed her experience. Within a week, pain was gone.
> **Key takeaway:** You don't need a village to breastfeed. You need one knowledgeable person who can answer your specific question.

## Principle 3: Watch the Baby, Not the Clock
Breastfeeding isn't a scheduled activity. Babies nurse in bursts, cluster feed during growth spurts, and take breaks. The clock creates anxiety — "He's only been nursing for 5 minutes!" — when the baby may have gotten everything he needs. The real measures are diaper output, weight gain, and baby's contentment between feeds.

## Principle 4: Every Mother-Baby Pair Is Unique
What worked for your sister might not work for you. What worked for your first baby might not work for your second. The book's philosophy is that there is no single "right way" — only what works for you and your baby. This applies to positions, schedules, pumping routines, weaning timing, and everything else.

## Principle 5: The First Weeks Are for Learning
The book advises giving breastfeeding at least 4-6 weeks before making a final judgment. The first weeks are hard for almost everyone: sleep deprivation, sore nipples, engorgement, crying babies, and crying mothers are all normal. Most problems resolve with time, practice, and support.

> **Case: The Six-Week Turning Point** (Chapter 7, "The Older Baby"): A mother who nearly quit breastfeeding at three weeks due to pain and frustration wrote in her journal at six weeks: "I can't believe I almost gave up. It's easy now. He latches in seconds. No pain. I'm so glad I stuck with it." Her story mirrors thousands of others.
> **Key takeaway:** Give it six weeks. Almost everyone finds it gets dramatically easier around this point.

## Principle 6: Sleep and Milk Are Connected
When mother doesn't sleep, milk supply suffers. Prolactin (the milk-making hormone) is highest during sleep. The book's advice: rest when the baby rests. Don't clean the house when the baby sleeps — sleep yourself. Trade off with your partner for one uninterrupted 4-hour block.
