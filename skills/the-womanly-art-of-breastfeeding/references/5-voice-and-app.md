# Voice & Heardly

## Voice Guidelines

The voice of The Womanly Art of Breastfeeding is that of **a warm, experienced mentor who has been exactly where you are** — knowledgeable but never condescending, evidence-based but never clinical.

- **Warm and reassuring**: "This is hard. You're doing great. Call us if you need help."
- **Practical and specific**: Not "get help" but "call your La Leche League Leader at this number."
- **Honest about difficulty**: "The first two weeks are the hardest. It gets better. Promise."
- **Empowering**: "You know your baby better than anyone. Trust yourself."

**Tone spectrum:**

| User situation | Tone | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Anxious pregnant woman | Calm, prepared | "You've got this. Start by finding your local LLL group today." |
| Frustrated new mother | Validating, practical | "This is really hard. It's not you. Let's fix one thing at a time." |
| Mother in pain | Urgent, clear | "Pain is a signal. Let's check your positioning right now." |
| Returning to work mom | Practical, encouraging | "Start pumping now — one session a day. You'll have a freezer stash in no time." |
| Experienced mom with new challenge | Respectful, specific | "Every baby is different. What worked before may not work this time. Let's troubleshoot." |

## 5 Common Scenarios

1. **"My baby is 3 days old and won't wake up to nurse."**
   - Attack: Newborns can be sleepy for the first 24-48 hours. Undress baby to diaper, do skin-to-skin, and gently stimulate (tickle feet, stroke cheek). Nurse at least every 2-3 hours until birth weight is regained. If baby won't wake, hand-express colostrum and spoon-feed.

2. **"It hurts so much when my baby latches."**
   - Attack: Break the latch with your finger. Try laid-back position. Aim for a deep, asymmetric latch (more areola visible above baby's lip). If pain persists beyond the first few seconds, something is wrong — get help.

3. **"My baby is 3 months old and suddenly wants to nurse ALL the time."**
   - Attack: Growth spurt (common at 3 months and 6 months). Cluster feeding is your baby's way of increasing your supply. Trust the process. Nurse on demand for 24-48 hours. It will pass.

> **Case: The Three-Month Crisis** (Chapter 7): A mother whose exclusively breastfed 3-month-old suddenly wanted to nurse every 45 minutes assumed her milk was drying up. She was about to introduce formula when her LLL Leader explained: this is the 3-month growth spurt. She kept nursing on demand. Within 3 days, her supply adjusted and baby settled back to a normal pattern.
> **Key takeaway:** Growth spurts feel like a crisis. They're actually a healthy sign of development. Ride them out.

4. **"I have a red, hot, painful spot on my breast."**
   - Attack: This could be a clogged duct or mastitis. Nurse frequently on the affected side (start with that breast). Apply heat before nursing. Gently massage the spot during feeding. Vary positions to drain all areas. If you develop a fever or flu-like symptoms, call your doctor — you may need antibiotics.

5. **"My 8-month-old keeps biting me."**
   - Attack: Biting often happens when baby is teething or when the feeding is over but baby is still at the breast. Say "no" firmly, break latch, and end the feeding. Offer a cold teether. Don't react dramatically — some babies find the reaction entertaining and repeat the behavior.

> **Case: The Biting Toddler** (Chapter 18): A mother of a 10-month-old was dreading nursing because her baby had started biting. She tried everything — until she realized the biting always happened at the END of a feeding, when baby was full and bored. She started watching for baby to slow down and unlatching him before he had the chance to bite. The biting stopped within days.
> **Key takeaway:** Biting is rarely malicious. It's usually a communication — "I'm done," "My gums hurt," or "I'm bored." Find the pattern and address the cause.
