# Anti-Patterns: Common Breastfeeding Mistakes

## ❌ Assuming Low Supply Too Quickly
The most common reason mothers stop breastfeeding: perceived low supply that isn't actually low. The book warns: "Don't trust your fears. Trust the diapers." If baby has 6+ wet diapers and 3+ dirty diapers per day after day 4, and is gaining weight, she is getting enough milk.

The book identifies several false signals that mothers mistake for low supply: baby fussing (normal), baby wanting to nurse frequently (cluster feeding is normal), breasts feeling softer (that's your supply regulating), baby taking a bottle after nursing (doesn't mean hungry — just means baby likes the fast flow).

> **Case: The Supplement Trap** (Chapter 18): A mother who thought her milk wasn't enough started supplementing with formula after every feeding. Baby was satisfied, but her milk supply dropped rapidly because the breast wasn't being drained enough. Within two weeks, she was mostly formula-feeding — not because her body couldn't produce milk, but because the supplement created a cycle of reduced demand.
> **Key takeaway:** Every bottle of formula is a signal to your body to make less milk. Before supplementing, check diaper output and get help.

## ❌ Scheduled Feeding
Putting a baby on a feeding schedule (every 3 hours, 15 minutes per side) ignores how breastfeeding actually works. Babies nurse in bursts, snack, cluster feed, and take breaks. Scheduled feeding can reduce milk supply (less demand) and cause baby to be hungry and frustrated.

> **Case: The Clock-Watching Mother** (Chapter 5): A new mother was told by a well-meaning friend to nurse "10 minutes on each side every 3 hours." Her baby lost weight. The baby was slow and sleepy at the breast — at 10 minutes, she'd barely gotten the high-fat hindmilk. Once the mother switched to feeding on demand (watching baby, not the clock), weight gain normalized immediately.
> **Key takeaway:** Nurse the baby, not the clock. Watch hunger cues (rooting, hand-to-mouth, smacking lips), not the time.

## ❌ Introducing a Bottle Too Early (or Too Late)
Introducing a bottle before breastfeeding is well-established (before 3-4 weeks) can cause "nipple confusion" — the baby prefers the fast, constant flow of a bottle and becomes frustrated at the breast. Waiting too long (past 8-12 weeks) can make bottle refusal more likely.

The book recommends: wait 3-4 weeks, then introduce one bottle feeding every 2-3 days using a slow-flow nipple and "paced bottle-feeding" technique.

## ❌ Poor Positioning That Causes Pain
The most common cause of nipple pain is not "tender nipples" — it's a shallow latch caused by poor positioning. The mother leaning over the baby (rather than bringing baby to breast), or holding the baby's head and pushing him onto the breast.

**The fix:** Recline. Bring baby to breast, not breast to baby. Let baby lead the latch. If it hurts, break suction and try again. Pain is not a normal part of breastfeeding.

## ❌ Not Getting Help Early Enough
The book's strongest warning: "Don't wait until you're in tears." Many breastfeeding problems that are simple to fix in week 1 become complex by week 3. Nipple damage, low supply due to ineffective transfer, engorgement, and mastitis all get worse with time.

**The fix:** Contact a La Leche League Leader or lactation consultant at the first sign of trouble — persistent pain, poor weight gain, signs of jaundice, or just a feeling that something isn't right.
