# Core Framework: The Breastfeeding Foundation System

## Laid-back Breastfeeding (The Primary Position)

The cornerstone of The Womanly Art of Breastfeeding is the laid-back position. Mother reclines comfortably (not flat), and baby is placed on her chest, tummy-to-tummy. Gravity helps the baby find the breast and latch using natural reflexes. This is not just one position — it's the foundation that all other positions build on.

Why it works: When a baby is placed on his mother's chest, his inborn feeding instincts kick in. He'll bob his head, open his mouth wide, and latch with the deepest possible attachment. The mother doesn't need to "put" the baby on — she just needs to create the conditions for baby to find his own way.

> **Case: The Premature Baby** (Chapter 4, "Latching and Attaching"): A mother of a 35-week preemie was told her baby was "too small" to breastfeed. Using laid-back positioning with lots of skin-to-skin contact, the baby began rooting and searching on his own within days — long before the hospital's expected timeline. He latched successfully without intervention.
> **Key takeaway:** Even premature babies have powerful feeding instincts. Create the conditions and trust the baby.

## The Three Keeps

When breastfeeding gets hard — and it often does in the early weeks — the book's core fallback is three simple rules:
1. **Keep baby close** — Skin-to-skin contact, co-sleeping (safely), wearing baby. Proximity regulates baby's temperature, heart rate, and feeding rhythms.
2. **Keep nursing** — Don't supplement unless medically necessary. Every bottle of formula is a message to your body to make less milk. Nurse through the difficulty.
3. **Keep getting help** — Call a La Leche League Leader, a lactation consultant, or a knowledgeable friend. Breastfeeding problems are solvable. You just need the right support.

> **Case: The Exhausted New Mother** (Chapter 5, "The First Few Days"): A first-time mother called her La Leche League Leader at 2 AM, crying, convinced her baby was starving. The Leader asked: "How many wet diapers today?" Answer: 6. "How many poopy diapers?" Answer: 4. "When was the last feeding?" Answer: "20 minutes ago, but he's still fussy." The Leader reassured her: baby was getting plenty of milk — the fussiness was normal newborn behavior, not hunger. The mother kept nursing, and by morning, baby was content.
> **Key takeaway:** Most "not enough milk" fears are actually "I don't know what normal looks like" fears. Diaper output is the real measure.

## Supply and Demand (The Biological Engine)

The single most important physiological concept: **milk production is driven by milk removal.** When baby nurses, nerve signals tell the brain to release prolactin and oxytocin, which produce and release milk. The more baby nurses, the more milk is made. The reverse is also true.

This means:
- Supplementing with formula reduces milk supply (less demand = less supply)
- Pumping can increase supply (extra demand = extra supply)
- Growth spurts (cluster feeding) are baby's way of increasing your supply for the next growth phase

**Key insight:** If you think you have low supply, the solution is almost always to nurse more, not to supplement. The breast is not a bottle that runs out — it's a factory that responds to orders.
