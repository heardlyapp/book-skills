# Techniques: Practical Breastfeeding Skills

## Technique 1: The Laid-back Latch
1. Recline comfortably (not flat) in a bed or chair.
2. Place baby skin-to-skin on your chest, tummy-to-tummy.
3. Let gravity nestle baby between your breasts.
4. Baby's natural reflexes will cause him to bob his head, open wide, and attach.
5. Support baby's head and neck — don't push or guide. Let baby lead.
6. If the latch hurts, break suction with your finger and try again.

**Key signs of a good latch:** Baby's mouth is open wide (like a yawn), lips are flanged out, chin is touching the breast, you see more areola above baby's lip than below, and nursing doesn't hurt (after the initial latch sensation).

> **Case: The Pain-Free Promise** (Chapter 4): A mother who had been told "breastfeeding just hurts for the first two weeks" discovered through laid-back positioning that pain-free nursing was possible from day one. Her previous baby had been positioned incorrectly (leaning forward, pushing baby on). The laid-back position changed everything.
> **Key takeaway:** Persistent pain is not normal. It's almost always a positioning problem. If it hurts beyond the initial latch, break the latch and try again.

## Technique 2: Milk Storage Guidelines
The book provides clear, evidence-based storage guidelines:
- **Room temperature** (up to 77°F/25°C): 4 hours (6-8 hours for very clean expressed milk)
- **Insulated cooler with ice pack:** 24 hours
- **Refrigerator** (39°F/4°C): 3-8 days (5 days is standard recommendation)
- **Freezer compartment inside refrigerator:** 2 weeks
- **Freezer with separate door:** 3-6 months
- **Deep freezer** (0°F/-18°C): 6-12 months

**Thawing:** Use oldest milk first. Thaw in refrigerator or warm water. Never microwave — it destroys nutrients and creates hot spots.

## Technique 3: Pumping for Success
- Pump when baby is most content (usually after a feeding or first thing in the morning when supply is highest)
- Practice with a hand pump before using an electric pump — it's quieter and less intimidating
- Build a freezer stash slowly: one extra pumping session per day yields 2-3 ounces
- "Power pumping" (pump 20 min, rest 10, pump 10, rest 10, pump 10) mimics cluster feeding and signals the body to increase supply

> **Case: The Working Mother** (Chapter 15, "Pumping and Storing"): A mother returning to work at 12 weeks wanted to maintain exclusive breastfeeding. She started pumping once daily at 6 weeks, storing 2-3 ounces each time. By 12 weeks, she had 100+ ounces in her freezer. She pumped twice during her workday (matching her baby's feeding pattern) and nursed directly when home. She continued for 14 months.
> **Key takeaway:** Start pumping early (3-4 weeks, once daily after a feeding). The slow buildup is less stressful than a last-minute scramble.

## Technique 4: The Diaper Output Test
The most reliable way to know baby is getting enough milk:
- **Day 1-2:** 1-2 wet diapers, 1-2 black/tarry meconium stools
- **Day 3-4:** 3-5 wet diapers, 3-4 greenish/yellow transitional stools
- **Day 5+:** 6-8 wet diapers (clear or pale urine), 3-5 yellow, seedy stools

If baby meets these targets, she is getting enough milk. Period. Don't be swayed by fussiness, short feeds, or the breast feeling softer.

## Technique 5: Dealing with Engorgement
When breasts become painfully full (usually day 3-5):
1. Nurse frequently (at least every 2 hours, or on demand)
2. Apply warm compresses *before* nursing to encourage let-down
3. Gently massage the breast while nursing
4. Apply cold compresses *after* nursing to reduce swelling
5. If baby can't latch due to fullness, hand-express a small amount to soften the areola

## Technique 6: The Crossover Check
For persistent nipple pain, do this systematic check:
1. Position: Is baby tummy-to-tummy with you? Ear, shoulder, and hip aligned?
2. Mouth: Wide open (like a yawn), not a small "pursed" opening?
3. Lips: Flanged out, not tucked in?
4. Chin: Touching breast, nose slightly away?
5. Pain: Sharp, pinching, or persistent? Break latch and try again.
