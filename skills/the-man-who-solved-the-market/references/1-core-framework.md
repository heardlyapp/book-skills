# 1. Core Framework — The Renaissance Formula

> Source: "The Man Who Solved the Market" by Gregory Zuckerman (2019). The formula that built the Medallion Fund.

## The Three Elements

Renaissance's success rested on three elements: brilliant people, massive data, and relentless iteration.

### Element 1: Brilliant Non-Finance People
Simons hired mathematicians, physicists, and code-breakers — people who knew nothing about Wall Street but everything about finding patterns.

> **Case: The Interview Process** (Chapters 5-6): Renaissance interviews were notorious. Candidates were given hard math problems — not finance questions. Simons wanted people who could think, not people who knew about stocks. One candidate was asked: "How would you model the stock market?" The answer "I wouldn't" was considered the right one.
> **Key takeaway**: Hire for cognitive ability, not domain knowledge. Smart people can learn finance; finance people cannot learn genius.

### Element 2: Massive Computing Power
Renaissance invested in computing before it was obvious to do so. They had access to the most powerful machines.

### Element 3: Relentless Iteration
The models were constantly tested, refined, and improved. Never satisfied. Always looking for the next edge.

> **Case: The Weekend Experiment** (Chapter 7): Early Renaissance traders noticed their models performed better on Mondays. The reason: data from Friday's close had two days to be processed. They realized the models weren't predicting the market — they were reacting to it. This insight led to a complete rethinking of their approach.
> **Key takeaway**: Sometimes your edge comes from noticing something everyone else overlooked.

## The Medallion Fund

Launched in 1988, the Medallion Fund was Renaissance's flagship. From 1988 to 2018, it averaged 66% annual returns before fees (39% after fees). No other fund in history comes close.

> **Case: The Fee Structure** (Chapter 10): Medallion charged 5% management fee and 44% performance fee — far higher than industry standard (2% and 20%). Employees didn't care because the returns were so extraordinary. The fee structure ensured that only serious investors participated.
> **Key takeaway**: If your returns are extraordinary, you can charge extraordinary fees. Value capture follows value creation.
