# 2. Principles — The Quant Mindset

> Source: "The Man Who Solved the Market" by Gregory Zuckerman (2019). Principles from Simons' approach.

## The 7 Principles

### Principle 1: Markets Have Patterns
Simons believed that financial markets are not random. They contain detectable patterns. This belief is the foundation of quantitative trading.

### Principle 2: Data Beats Intuition
Human traders rely on gut feelings. Quantitative models rely on data. Data wins.

### Principle 3: Small Edges Compound
Renaissance's trades had tiny edges — fractions of a cent. Over millions of trades, these edges compounded into enormous profits.

> **Case: The High-Frequency Edge** (Chapter 9): Renaissance made thousands of trades per day, each profiting fractions of a cent. Competitors couldn't understand how they could make money on such tiny moves. The answer was volume: a tiny edge applied millions of times is a fortune.
> **Key takeaway**: Scale transforms small advantages into large profits. A 51% edge × 10,000 trades = a certain winner.

### Principle 4: Secrecy Protects the Edge
If competitors knew Renaissance's models, the edge would disappear. Secrets are assets.

### Principle 5: The Best Problems Attract the Best Minds
Simons attracted brilliant people by offering interesting work, not just high pay.

### Principle 6: Never Stop Iterating
The models were never "done." Constant refinement was the only constant.

### Principle 7: Money Is a Byproduct
For Simons, the goal was solving the market. The money was a side effect.

> **Case: The Code-Breaking Connection** (Chapter 2): Before Renaissance, Simons worked as a code-breaker for the NSA during the Cold War. His job was to find patterns in Soviet communications. The skill set was directly transferable: find patterns in noisy data, exploit them before they disappear.
> **Key takeaway**: Skills are transferable. Code-breaking and quantitative trading use the same cognitive muscle: pattern recognition in noisy data.
