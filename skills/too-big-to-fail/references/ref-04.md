# Reference: The Key Players

## Overview
Sorkin's book is a narrative driven by personalities. The key figures in the crisis were extraordinary individuals making impossible decisions under unimaginable pressure.

## Case: Hank Paulson — The Reluctant Savior
Paulson was a reluctant Treasury Secretary who had wanted to be a conservationist. A former Goldman Sachs CEO, he believed in free markets and hated bailouts. He spent the first six months of 2008 insisting that Bear Stearns was an isolated case. By September, he was proposing $700 billion in government intervention. Sorkin shows Paulson on the verge of tears, physically ill from stress, and sleeping on his office couch. He was the right person for the crisis precisely because he understood both Wall Street and Washington — and trusted neither.

## Case: Ben Bernanke — The Academic in the Firestorm
Bernanke was a scholar of the Great Depression who had written his academic career on why central banks failed in the 1930s. His mantra during the crisis: "If we don't act, we'll have another Great Depression." Bernanke's academic authority gave him credibility with Congress that Paulson (a Wall Street CEO) lacked. His willingness to use every tool in the Fed's arsenal — including emergency lending powers that hadn't been used since the 1930s — prevented a complete banking collapse.

## Case: Jamie Dimon — The CEO Who Became the Government's Partner
JPMorgan Chase CEO Jamie Dimon was the only Wall Street CEO who emerged from the crisis with his reputation intact. He had avoided the worst subprime exposure, acquired Bear Stearns (with government backing) and Washington Mutual (for pennies), and organized industry rescues. Dimon was the CEO that Paulson and Geithner called when they needed a bank to take a failing institution or a CEO who could rally the industry. He was simultaneously competitor, regulator, and rescuer — a role no single banker should ever have to play.
