# Reference: TARP and the Bank Rescues

## Overview
The Troubled Asset Relief Program was Paulson's $700 billion request to Congress to buy toxic assets from banks. It passed after the most dramatic week in modern Congressional history.

## Case: The Three-Page Proposal
Paulson's initial TARP proposal was three pages. He walked into Speaker Pelosi's office and asked for $700 billion with no oversight, no judicial review, and no conditions. Pelosi told him: "Three pages? Hank, you must be on crack." The proposal was widely criticized as an attempt to bypass democratic accountability. The final bill (the Emergency Economic Stabilization Act) ran over 400 pages and included oversight provisions, limits on executive compensation, and warrants for taxpayer ownership.

## Case: The First Vote That Failed
The House voted on TARP on September 29, 2008. The vote failed 228-205. The Dow dropped 778 points immediately — the largest single-day point drop in history. Paulson stood in the White House Situation Room, watching the Dow collapse on television. He had to get the vote back. After adding a provision to increase FDIC deposit insurance from $100,000 to $250,000, the Senate passed the bill on October 1, and the House followed on October 3. The lesson: Congress votes for the system only when the alternative is visible collapse.

## Case: From Toxic Assets to Capital Injections
Paulson's original TARP plan was to buy mortgage-backed securities from banks. He quickly realized this was impractical (how do you price toxic assets?) and instead used the money to buy equity in banks — effectively partial nationalization. The first $125 billion went to the nine largest banks. Jamie Dimon and Lloyd Blankfein were told they were getting capital whether they wanted it or not. TARP's final cost to taxpayers was approximately zero — most recipients repaid with interest.
