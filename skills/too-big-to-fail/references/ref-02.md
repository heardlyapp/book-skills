# Reference: The AIG Rescue

## Overview
AIG was not a bank. It was the world's largest insurance company with $1 trillion in assets and operations in 130 countries. It was brought to the brink of collapse by a single division in London with 377 employees: AIG Financial Products.

## Case: The $400 Billion CDS Book
AIG FP had written $400 billion in credit default swaps on mortgage-backed securities. They sold insurance on bonds that they believed were safe, collecting premiums without reserving capital. When housing prices fell, the bonds defaulted, and AIG owed payouts it could not make. The collateral calls came in faster than AIG could raise cash. By September 15, 2008, AIG needed $85 billion in 48 hours or it would file for bankruptcy — which would have triggered cascading failures at Goldman Sachs, Deutsche Bank, and every other bank that had bought CDS from AIG.

## Case: The Government Reversal
Only 48 hours after letting Lehman fail, the government announced an $85 billion rescue of AIG. The decision contradicted everything Paulson had said about moral hazard. The difference: Lehman's failure was painful but not systemic. AIG's failure would have destroyed every major bank in the world — including European banks — because they all had billions in exposure to AIG's CDS. The government had learned the lesson of Lehman in 48 hours: the second shoe was bigger than the first.

## Case: The Bonuses Scandal
After the bailout, AIG paid $165 million in retention bonuses to the same employees in AIG FP whose trades had brought the company down. The public outrage was volcanic. The House voted 328-93 to tax the bonuses at 90%. Sorkin's account shows that the bonuses were legally required under contracts signed before the bailout, and that canceling them would have triggered a mass exodus of the only people who could unwind the toxic positions.
