# Reference: Systemic Risk Analysis

## Overview
The 2008 crisis was not a single failure but a cascade of failures across a deeply interconnected system. Understanding systemic risk requires understanding the plumbing that connects banks, insurers, and markets.

## Case: The Subprime Mortgage Chain
The chain began with mortgage brokers who originated loans with no documentation to borrowers with no income ("NINJA loans"). The loans were packaged into MBS by investment banks, then re-packaged into CDOs by the same banks. Rating agencies (paid by the banks) rated the CDOs AAA. Pension funds, insurance companies, and sovereign wealth funds bought the AAA paper. When subprime borrowers defaulted, the chain broke at every link. The problem was not any single link but the length of the chain — risk was distributed so widely that no one understood the total exposure.

## Case: Interconnectedness and Contagion
On September 15, 2008, when Lehman filed for bankruptcy, the Reserve Primary Fund (a money market fund with $62 billion in assets) "broke the buck" because it held Lehman commercial paper. This triggered a run on money market funds. Money market funds are the source of short-term funding for virtually every major corporation. Within 24 hours, the commercial paper market — which funds the daily operations of American business — had frozen. General Electric, which issues $100 billion in commercial paper annually, was unable to roll over its debt. This is the definition of systemic contagion: a bankruptcy at one investment bank paralyzing the funding of a manufacturing conglomerate.

## Case: The Regulatory Gap
No regulator had responsibility for the financial system as a whole. The SEC regulated investment banks (badly), the Federal Reserve regulated bank holding companies (partially), state regulators insured insurance companies (inconsistently), and the Office of Thrift Supervision regulated thrifts (ineptly). AIG's CDS business was regulated by... no one. The Office of Thrift Supervision theoretically oversaw AIG but had no expertise in derivatives. The crisis revealed that the most dangerous institutions were those that fell between regulatory jurisdictions.
