# Reference: The Lehman Collapse

## Overview
Lehman Brothers' bankruptcy on September 15, 2008, was the largest in US history and the event that turned the subprime crisis into a global financial panic. Sorkin's narrative follows the final 10 days in excruciating detail.

## Case: The Barclays Deal That Failed
By the second week of September, Lehman CEO Dick Fuld had negotiated a rescue by Barclays. The UK regulators (FSA) demanded that Barclays shareholders approve the deal, which would take weeks. The Bank of England refused to allow Barclays to assume Lehman's obligations without a shareholder vote. A Friday night phone call between Paulson and UK Chancellor Alistair Darling killed the deal. Paulson had no backup plan. He had told the room that morning that Lehman had a buyer. He was wrong.

## Case: Fuld's Refusal to Sell
Throughout 2007-2008, Dick Fuld received multiple offers to sell Lehman at a discount — to KKR, to the Korea Development Bank, to Warren Buffett. He refused every offer, believing the market would recover and Lehman would survive independently. When he finally agreed to sell in September 2008, no buyer remained. Fuld's refusal to accept reality — his belief that Lehman's stock would recover — destroyed a 158-year-old institution.

## Case: The Bankruptcy Filing
Lehman filed Chapter 11 at 1:45 AM on Monday, September 15. The filing was over 2,000 pages. The bankruptcy court was unprepared for a case this large. The bankruptcy estate's assets were $639 billion — more than 10 times the next largest bankruptcy. The chaos of the filing — unclear asset ownership, tangled derivatives contracts, international subsidiaries — demonstrated that no one had planned for a Lehman failure, including Lehman itself.
