# Core Framework — The Black Church

## Introduction: The Freedom Faith

Gates' thesis: The Black Church is "the oldest institution created and controlled by African Americans." It was not merely a spiritual refuge — it was a cultural cauldron, a political organizing base, a training ground for leaders, and the source of America's most distinctive music and oratory. "For a community disenfranchised and underserved by religious institutions established by and catering to the needs of white people, it served both secular and spiritual needs."

The seven historical Black denominations: AME, AME Zion, National Baptist Convention USA, National Baptist Convention of America, Progressive National Baptist Convention, CME, and COGIC. Roughly 80% of African Americans report that religion is "very important" in their lives.

## Chapter 1: The Freedom Faith — From Africa to the Invisible Institution

**African religious roots:** Enslaved Africans brought diverse beliefs — Yoruba, Kongo, Muslim, Catholic. An estimated 8-20% of enslaved Africans arriving in North America were Muslim. The Gullah Geechee of Sapelo Island, Georgia, preserved Islamic practices through generations, including Bilali Mohammed (c. 1760-1855), who was buried with his prayer rugs, beads, and Koran.

**Job Ben Solomon (Ayuba Suleiman Diallo):** An educated Fulbe Muslim captured in 1730. While enslaved in Maryland, he wrote a letter in Arabic to his father. It reached James Oglethorpe, who arranged his purchase by the Royal African Company. He met King George II and Queen Caroline, sat for a portrait by William Hoare, wrote himself out of slavery — then bought two horses and a slave of his own upon returning to Senegal.

**The "invisible institution":** Enslaved people worshipped in secret — "brush arbors" and "hush harbors" hidden from the master. Emma Tidwell (Arkansas): "We'd go out an put our mouths to de groun an pray low an de sound wud go up under de pot an ole boss couldn't hear us." Preaching was dangerous — "Black preachers couldn' preach tuh us. Ole boss would tie em tuh a tree an whoop em."

**The ring shout:** An African practice carried across the ocean. Enslaved people formed a circle, shuffling in rhythm, singing — the foundation of Black sacred music. "The body remembers how to worship."

## Chapter 2: A Nation Within a Nation — The Independent Black Church

**Richard Allen (1760-1831):** Born into slavery, Allen bought his freedom, became a Methodist preacher. In 1787, he led a walkout from St. George's Methodist Church in Philadelphia after being pulled from his knees during prayer and told to go to the gallery. He founded the African Methodist Episcopal (AME) Church in 1816. "A nation within a nation" — the first independent Black denomination in America.

**Absalom Jones (1746-1818):** Also born into slavery, purchased his wife's freedom. Became the first Black Episcopal priest in America. Preached a Thanksgiving sermon in 1808 commemorating the end of the slave trade: "Grant that this highly favoured country may continue to afford a safe retreat from war and slavery."

**Nat Turner's Rebellion (1831):** Turner, an enslaved preacher, used his biblical interpretation to justify violent revolt. "The text of the King James Bible being called upon to justify the revolutionary overthrow of the slave regime."

**Frederick Douglass:** Grounded in the AME Zion Church in New Bedford. His first public speeches were sermons. Forged his voice in the church before his famous 1841 Nantucket abolition speech.

**The spirituals (Sorrow Songs):** Du Bois: "The sorrow songs are the music of an unhappy people, of the children of disappointment; they tell of death and suffering and unvoiced longing toward a truer world." James Weldon Johnson: "The power to frame the poetic phrases that make the titles of so many of the Spirituals betokens the power to create the songs. Consider the sheer magic of 'Swing Low Sweet Chariot,' 'Steal Away to Jesus,' 'Deep River.'"

## Chapter 3: God Will Make a Way — The Civil Rights Era

**The Black Church as the engine of the movement:** Mass meetings were held in churches. Leaders were trained in the pulpit. The music of the movement — freedom songs — came directly from the spiritual and gospel tradition. "Without the role of the Black Church, the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the Voting Rights Act of 1965 would never have been enacted when they were."

**The 16th Street Baptist Church bombing (Birmingham, 1963):** Four little girls killed. The church as a target of white supremacist terrorism. "I was there that night, standing in the rubble," John Lewis recalled.

**Mahalia Jackson:** Dr. King's "sacred soul mate and private muse." She called out "Tell them about the dream, Martin!" during the March on Washington, spurring King into his most famous improvisation. She resisted lucrative offers to "go secular" and remained in gospel.

**Andrew Young, MLK, the SCLC:** All products of the Black preaching tradition. "The church is our foundation."

## Chapter 4: Crisis of Faith — The Black Church Today

**Declining membership:** A growing percentage of young Black Americans identify as "nones" (no religious affiliation). Roughly a third of African Americans now claim no religious affiliation. The church must adapt or fade.

**The prosperity gospel critique:** Televangelists who preach wealth as a sign of God's favor. T. D. Jakes, Creflo Dollar — controversial figures who represent a departure from the social gospel tradition.

**Sexism and homophobia:** The Black Church has historically marginalized women from leadership roles (though women like Vashti Murphy McKenzie and Yvette Flunder are breaking barriers). LGBT exclusion remains a deeply contested issue.

**Black Lives Matter and the new generation:** BLM was founded by three women — Patrisse Cullors, Alicia Garza, Opal Tometi — and represents a shift away from church-centered organizing. Yet many BLM activists are themselves products of the church.

**The COVID pandemic:** "Twin pandemics" — the virus disproportionately killing Black Americans and the police brutality exemplified by George Floyd's murder. The church adapted: online services, drive-in worship, pastoral care at a distance.

## Epilogue: On the Holy Ghost

Gates' personal confession: At age 12, he made a bargain with Jesus to save his mother's life. "I had no other language — no other framework — to make sense of the terror of losing her." He kept the deal. His mother lived.
