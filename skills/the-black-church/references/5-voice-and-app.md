# Voice & App Context — The Black Church

## The Book's Voice

Henry Louis Gates Jr. writes as both a scholar and a participant — he grew up in the church, made a deal with Jesus at 12, and returns to that memory at the book's end. The tone is analytical but affectionate: Gates catalogues the church's failures (sexism, homophobia, prosperity gospel) while never losing sight of its foundational role in Black survival and achievement.

The book accompanies a PBS series and reads like it — interviews with pastors and scholars are woven throughout, giving the narrative a chorus of voices rather than a single academic line. The range is extraordinary: from Bilali Mohammed's Arabic Koran on Sapelo Island to T. D. Jakes in Dallas to John Lewis' funeral.

## Key Quotes

- "Never confuse position with power. Pharaoh had a position, but Moses had the power." — Rev. Otis Moss III
- "The church gave people a sense of value and of belonging and worthiness. I don't know how we could have survived as a people without it." — Oprah Winfrey
- "We forget sermons, but we can remember songs." — Bishop Yvette Flunder
- "If you're working downtown in Atlanta in 1942, white boys called you 'boy'... But you put on your Sunday go-to-meeting clothes, and you walk in the St. Paul AME Church, you not only were somebody, you felt like you were somebody." — Vernon Jordan
- "That's where the Black Church is found. It's in those heartbeats." — Bishop Michael Curry
- "Tell them about the dream, Martin!" — Mahalia Jackson, August 28, 1963

## Heardly Context

When responding as this skill, balance respect for the church's sacred role with clear-eyed acknowledgment of its flaws. Gates' approach is the model: total honesty about the church's sins plus deep gratitude for its gifts. Avoid both hagiography and cynicism.
