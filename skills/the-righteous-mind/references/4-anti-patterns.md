## 6 Anti-Patterns of Moral Reasoning

### 1. "I Reasoned My Way to This Position"

You did not. You intuited, then your rider found reasons. Most people overestimate their rationality and underestimate their intuition.

### 2. "The Other Side Is Immoral"

The other side is not immoral — they are using different foundations. A conservative who values loyalty is not less moral than a liberal who values fairness. They are using a different part of the moral palate.

> **Case: The Politics of Purity** (Chapter 7): Haidt shows that liberals often dismiss the Sanctity foundation as irrational. But Sanctity evolved to protect against pathogens and contamination. It is not irrational — it is a response to a real adaptive challenge. Dismissing it as "backward" is a failure of understanding.
> **Key takeaway**: Every foundation has an evolutionary logic. Even the ones you do not share.

### 3. "Better Arguments Win"

In politics, better arguments rarely win. People process arguments through their moral intuitions. An argument that violates a person's core foundations will be rejected regardless of its logical strength.

### 4. "I Can Change Their Mind with Facts"

Facts alone rarely change moral intuitions. The elephant does not respond to data — it responds to stories, relationships, and experiences. The rider can be swayed by data, but the rider is not in charge.

### 5. "Openness to Both Sides Means Centrism"

Understanding both sides does not mean you take no position. It means you can see the moral logic of the other side while still holding your own convictions.

### 6. "The Hive Is Dangerous"

The hive switch can be dangerous (Nazi rallies, cults, nationalism). But it can also be beautiful (concerts, sports teams, religious communities, social movements). The hive is not evil — it is powerful. The question is how it is used.

> **Case: The Afghanistan War Argument** (Introduction): Haidt describes talking to a liberal friend about the Afghanistan war. The friend argued it was immoral because it killed civilians (Care violation). Haidt argued it was necessary for national security (Loyalty/Authority justification). Both were using different foundations. Neither could convince the other because they were speaking different moral languages.
> **Key takeaway**: Political arguments are often conflicts of moral foundations, not conflicts of facts. Finding common ground requires translating between foundations.
