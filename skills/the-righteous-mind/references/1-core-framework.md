## The Haidt Framework: Intuition, Foundations, and the Hive

Haidt's framework rests on three pillars: (1) intuition comes first (the rider and the elephant), (2) morality has six psychological foundations that evolved to solve different adaptive challenges, and (3) humans evolved the ability to lose themselves in groups (the hive switch).

**The Key Insight**: Political disagreements are not just about facts — they are about which moral foundations are active. When a liberal says "it's not fair" and a conservative says "it's not loyal," they are speaking different moral languages. Neither is wrong — they are using different parts of the moral palate.

> **Case: The Incest Study** (Chapter 2): Haidt describes a study where participants were told a story about a brother and sister who had consensual, protected sex using birth control. Participants said it was wrong — but could not explain why. They had a strong moral intuition (Sanctity/Degradation) but could not articulate it. This is "moral dumbfounding" — proof that intuition drives moral judgment, not reasoning.
> **Key takeaway**: If people cannot give reasons for their strongest moral convictions, then reasoning is not the source of morality. Intuition is. The rider (reasoning) was not making the decision — the elephant (intuition) was.

> **Case: The Flag Burning Study** (Chapter 4): Haidt compares reactions to flag burning. Americans (even those who defend free speech) often have a visceral negative reaction — a Loyalty/Betrayal intuition that flags should be treated with respect. People from other countries often do not share this intuition.
> **Key takeaway**: Different cultures emphasize different moral foundations. The question "Is flag burning wrong?" is not a test of logic — it is a test of which moral foundations are activated in your mind.
