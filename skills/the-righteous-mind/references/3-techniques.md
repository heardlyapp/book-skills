## Practical Techniques

### 1. The Foundation Translation

When you hear someone making a moral argument you disagree with, try to identify which foundation they are using. If they talk about "protecting our heritage," they are using Loyalty/Authority. If you only respond with Care/Fairness arguments, you will not connect.

### 2. The Elephant Whisperer

To change someone's mind, you must address their elephant (intuitions), not their rider (arguments). People change their moral positions through relationships, experiences, and stories — not through logic.

> **Case: The Gay Marriage Shift** (Chapter 10): Haidt explains why public opinion on gay marriage shifted so rapidly in the US. The primary driver was not rational argument — it was personal relationships. When people learned that someone they knew and loved was gay, their intuitions changed. The rider followed the elephant.
> **Key takeaway**: The fastest way to change someone's moral intuitions is through personal connection. Arguments harden positions; relationships soften them.

### 3. The Moral Matrix Check

Recognize that people from different political backgrounds live in different "moral matrices." Their values are not a mistake — they are a different configuration of the same basic foundations.

### 4. The Hive Activation

If you want to build team cohesion, activate the hive switch: shared meals, synchronized activities (singing, marching, dancing), collective goals, and sacred rituals.

> **Case: The Walking Group** (Chapter 9): Haidt describes a group that builds cohesion by hiking together in nature. The shared physical activity, the awe of nature, and the collective experience activate the hive switch. The group becomes more than a collection of individuals.
> **Key takeaway**: Team building is not about trust falls — it is about shared experiences that activate the hive. Find activities that make people feel part of something larger.
