# Key Principles

## 1. Mental Illness Is Biological, Not Moral

"For decades, parents — especially mothers — were blamed for causing schizophrenia." The "schizophrenogenic mother" theory held that cold, rejecting mothers caused their children's illness. The Galvin parents were blamed, shamed, and guilt-ridden. The science now proves: schizophrenia is a brain disease with strong genetic roots. Parenting does not cause it.

## 2. Genetics Is Not Destiny

"Having the genes for schizophrenia does not mean you will develop it." The Galvin family had 6 of 12 children affected — but 6 were NOT affected despite sharing the same genes and environment. This points to other factors — stress, trauma, developmental timing — that interact with genetic risk.

## 3. The Family System Suffers

"When one person has severe mental illness, everyone in the family is affected." The unaffected Galvin siblings describe growing up in chaos — violence, disruption, constant crisis. Some escaped. Others were consumed by guilt and responsibility.

> **Case: The Healthy Siblings** (Chapters 8, 14): Several Galvin siblings who did not develop schizophrenia still experienced profound trauma from growing up in a household with severe mental illness. Some struggled with their own mental health. Others dedicated their lives to caregiving. The "healthy" siblings were also wounded.
> **Key takeaway**: Mental illness in a family impacts everyone. The siblings who don't get the diagnosis also need support and healing.

> **Case: The Genetic Breakthrough** (Chapters 10-12): When researchers recruited the Galvin family for a genetic study, they discovered that the affected siblings shared specific rare genetic variants. But some unaffected siblings also had these variants. The study revealed that schizophrenia genetics is complex — the presence of risk genes does not guarantee illness.
>
> **Key takeaway**: The Galvin family's contribution to science was invaluable. Their DNA helped identify genes associated with schizophrenia — but the research also showed that genetics alone doesn't tell the whole story.

## 4. Progress Is Real but Incomplete

"From lobotomy and institutionalization to community treatment and antipsychotics — the arc bends toward better care." But deinstitutionalization, while well-intentioned, left many families without support. Jails have become the largest mental health facilities in many countries. Progress is real, but the system is still broken.

## 5. Stigma Kills

"The Galvins hid their sons' illness for years. Friends and neighbors didn't know. The secrecy amplified the suffering." Stigma prevents families from seeking help, talking openly, and finding community. Connection is the antidote to stigma.
