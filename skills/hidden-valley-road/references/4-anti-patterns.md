# Anti-Patterns

## 1. Blaming Parents

**The mistake**: Believing that bad parenting causes mental illness, especially the "schizophrenogenic mother" theory.

**Why it fails**: This theory was scientifically discredited. It added devastating guilt to already suffering families.

**Fix**: Mental illness is biological. Blaming parents is not only wrong — it's harmful.

> **Case: The Schizophrenogenic Mother Myth** (Chapter 6): For decades, psychiatrists told Mimi Galvin that she had caused her sons' schizophrenia by being a cold, demanding mother. She believed them. The guilt she carried for years was a second illness. The theory was later abandoned — it had no scientific basis.
> **Key takeaway**: Don't add guilt to the pain. Families of mentally ill individuals need support, not blame.

## 2. Secrecy and Shame

**The mistake**: Hiding mental illness from friends, family, and community.

**Why it fails**: Secrecy isolates the family, prevents others from helping, and delays treatment.

**Fix**: Talk about it. Not with everyone, but with people you trust. Stigma thrives in silence.

> **Case: The Galvins' Secret** (Chapter 3): For years, the Galvins hid their sons' hospitalizations from neighbors, friends, and even extended family. The isolation was crushing. When the story finally came out, other families came forward with their own stories — and the Galvins realized they were not alone.
> **Key takeaway**: The secret was heavier than the illness itself. Speaking openly about mental illness transforms shame into connection.

## 3. Caregiver Martyrdom

**The mistake**: Sacrificing everything — health, relationships, career — to care for a mentally ill family member.

**Why it fails**: The martyr caregiver eventually burns out and is no longer able to help anyone.

**Fix**: You cannot pour from an empty cup. Setting boundaries is not selfish — it's sustainable.

## 4. Expecting Cure Instead of Management

**The mistake**: Searching for a cure and dismissing treatments that manage symptoms.

**Fix**: Schizophrenia is not curable but is treatable. Medication and therapy can make a profound difference.
