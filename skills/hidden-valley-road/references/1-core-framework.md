# Core Framework: The Galvin Family Legacy

## The Family That Changed Science

The Galvin family of Colorado Springs had 12 children between 1945 and 1965. Six sons developed schizophrenia. Their story — documented through decades of research — became one of the most important case studies in the genetics of mental illness.

> **Case: Donald Galvin** (Chapters 1-2): Donald was the first Galvin child to show symptoms. At 16, he became violent, delusional, and paranoid. He was hospitalized repeatedly. His illness was the first sign that something was deeply wrong in the seemingly perfect family.
> **Key takeaway**: Mental illness often appears in adolescence or early adulthood. Donald's symptoms emerged at 16 — a typical age for schizophrenia onset.

> **Case: The Genetic Study** (Chapters 10-12): Researchers recruited the Galvin family for a landmark genetic study of schizophrenia. By studying the DNA of all 12 siblings — both affected and unaffected — scientists identified genes associated with the disorder. This research changed our understanding of mental illness heredity.
> **Key takeaway**: The Galvins' willingness to participate in research despite the stigma transformed psychiatric genetics. Science advances through families willing to share their stories.

## Key Scientific Insights

| Insight | What It Means |
|---------|---------------|
| Schizophrenia is highly heritable | Genetic factors account for ~80% of risk |
| But not purely genetic | Environment, stress, and timing are critical triggers |
| It's a brain development disorder | Symptoms typically emerge in late adolescence/early adulthood |
| Treatment is possible, not cure | Antipsychotics manage symptoms but don't reverse the disease |
| Stigma is the greatest barrier | Families hide the illness, delaying treatment and support |
