## Practical Techniques

### 1. The Interleaving Practice

Instead of practicing one skill until mastered (blocked practice), mix different skills together. Study math problems of different types in one session. Practice different tennis shots in the same drill. The struggle of switching between skills builds deeper learning.

### 2. The Analogy Exercise

When facing a problem, ask: "What is this LIKE?" Force yourself to find an analogy from a different domain. The further the distance, the more creative the potential solution.

> **Case: Gunpei Yokoi and the Game Boy** (Chapter 9): Nintendo engineer Gunpei Yokoi coined the phrase "lateral thinking with withered technology" — using cheap, widely available technology in creative ways rather than pushing the cutting edge. Yokoi had a background in industrial design, not video games. His broad range allowed him to see that the Game Boy's gray-scale screen — a limitation from the cutting-edge perspective — was actually an advantage (longer battery life, lower cost).
> **Key takeaway**: The outsider advantage is not just about seeing different solutions — it is about seeing different definitions of success. What looks like a limitation to an insider can be an opportunity to someone with range.

### 3. The Quitting Audit

Periodically ask: "If I started this today, knowing what I now know, would I choose it again?" If the answer is no, consider quitting — not because you lack grit, but because you have learned something about fit.

### 4. The Deliberate Amateur Hour

Spend regular time pursuing a field completely outside your expertise. This builds the cross-domain knowledge that drives creative breakthroughs.

> **Case: Johannes Kepler's Analogies** (Chapter 5): Discovering the laws of planetary motion, Kepler had no concept of gravity or momentum to work from. He drew analogies from light, heat, odor, magnetism, river currents, boatmen, balance scales, brooms, and more. He interrogated each one ruthlessly until he found the one that fit — gravity as a universal physical force. Galileo mocked him for suggesting the moon influenced tides.
> **Key takeaway**: When you have no direct path to a solution, analogies from distant domains are your most powerful tool. Kepler invented astrophysics by thinking outside his experience.
