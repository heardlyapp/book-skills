## The Range Framework: Breadth Beats Depth in Wicked Domains

Epstein's central framework divides all learning and problem-solving domains into two types:

**Kind Learning Environments** — Rules are clear, patterns repeat, feedback is immediate and accurate. Examples: golf, chess, classical music, surgery (for standard procedures). In these environments, deliberate practice and early specialization work exactly as advertised.

**Wicked Learning Environments** — Rules are unclear or changing, feedback is delayed or ambiguous, patterns may not repeat. Examples: business, investing, innovation, scientific research, policy-making, everyday life. In these environments, breadth — not depth — is the advantage.

> **Case: Tiger Woods vs. Roger Federer** (Introduction): Tiger started golf at 10 months, did deliberate practice from age 2, and his father engineered his entire upbringing around golf. Federer played soccer, basketball, handball, tennis, badminton, squash, skiing, swimming, and wrestling as a child. His parents were "pully" — they had to pull him away from sports. He didn't focus on tennis until his teens. Both became all-time greats — but Federer represents the far more common path: late specialization after broad sampling.
> **Key takeaway**: The Tiger Woods story is the exception, not the rule. The vast majority of elite performers — in sports and beyond — followed a Federer-like path of sampling before specializing.

> **Case: The Vinny Testa Study** (Chapter 1): Psychologist Vinny Testa studied Nobel laureates in science. He found that Nobel-winning scientists were far more likely to have broad artistic and creative interests outside their field (music, art, poetry, writing) compared to typical scientists. They were also more likely to have changed fields mid-career. The breadth of their interests — not the depth of a single focus — correlated with breakthrough achievement.
> **Key takeaway**: The most creative scientists are not the most narrowly focused. They are the ones with range.
