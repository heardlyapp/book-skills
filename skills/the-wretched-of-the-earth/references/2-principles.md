# 2 — Violence and Liberation

> Source: "The Wretched of the Earth" by Frantz Fanon

## The Argument

Fanon's most famous and controversial argument: violence against the colonizer is a "cleansing force." It frees the colonized from their inferiority complex, restores their sense of agency, and unifies the national struggle.

## The Colonizer's Violence

Fanon begins from the premise that colonialism is violence. The colonizer's original act and continued presence are violent. Decolonization is simply the colonized's response.

> **Case: The Birth of a Nation** (Chapter On Violence): "At the level of individuals, violence is a cleansing force. It rids the colonized of their inferiority complex, of their passive and despairing attitude. It makes them fearless and restores their self-respect." This is Fanon's most quoted passage.
> **Key takeaway**: Fanon is not glorifying violence. He is describing its psychological function for the colonized. In his clinical experience as a psychiatrist, he saw that colonized people who participated in liberation struggles had better psychological outcomes than those who accepted their oppression passively.

> **Case: The FLN in Algeria** (Chapter on Spontaneity): Fanon was a participant-observer in the Algerian War of Independence (1954-1962). He served as a psychiatrist and propagandist for the FLN. His analysis of violence was not theoretical — it was based on his experience of a real liberation struggle.
> **Key takeaway**: Fanon's theories were forged in the fire of actual revolution. He was not an armchair philosopher. His observations were clinical as well as political — he saw what colonialism did to people's minds.
