# Key Principles

## 1. Oil Wealth Is Political Power Without Accountability

"Unlike tax revenue, which requires citizen consent, oil revenue flows directly to the state. The state doesn't need citizens' permission to spend it. This breaks the democratic feedback loop."

## 2. The International System Enables Kleptocracy

"Western banks launder the money. Western law firms write the contracts. Western PR firms polish the image. The kleptocracy is not just a local problem — it's a global system."

## 3. Regulatory Capture Is the Silent Coup

"The oil industry doesn't need to overthrow democracy. It just needs to capture the regulators." Through lobbying, campaign contributions, and the revolving door, the industry ensures that regulation serves its interests.

> **Case: The Fracking Boom** (Chapters 9-10): The US fracking boom was celebrated as an energy independence success. But Maddow documents how fracking companies were granted exemptions from key environmental regulations, how they contaminated water supplies, and how regulators failed to enforce existing rules. The industry captured the regulatory process.
> **Key takeaway**: Even in a democracy with strong institutions, industry capture is possible. Citizen vigilance is the only defense.

## 4. The Environmental and Political Costs Are the Same Cost

"Climate change and kleptocracy are not separate problems — they are the same problem." The oil industry's business model produces both political corruption and environmental destruction. Addressing one requires addressing the other.

> **Case: The Nigerian Delta** (Chapters 11-12): Oil extraction in the Niger Delta devastated the environment and fueled corruption. The Nigerian government and international oil companies extracted wealth while local communities received pollution, poverty, and conflict.
> **Key takeaway**: The resource curse is not just about national politics — it destroys local communities and environments. The costs are always paid by someone.
