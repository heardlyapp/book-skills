# Techniques: Detecting and Countering Oil Industry Capture

## The Regulatory Capture Checklist

- [ ] Do regulators have recent experience working for the industry they regulate?
- [ ] Are enforcement actions rare and penalties small relative to profits?
- [ ] Are industry lobbyists invited to help write regulations?
- [ ] Are meetings between regulators and industry not publicly recorded?
- [ ] Are whistleblowers who report industry misconduct protected or punished?

> **Case: The Revolving Door** (Chapter 9): Multiple EPA administrators and energy department officials moved directly from oil industry executive roles to government positions — and back again. Each move brought industry-friendly policies. The revolving door is not corruption in the legal sense, but it produces the same outcome.
> **Key takeaway**: The revolving door is the primary mechanism of regulatory capture. When regulators come from and return to the industry they regulate, whose interests do they serve?

## Investigative Tools for Citizens

1. **Follow the money** — Who funds political campaigns? Who benefits from legislation?
2. **Track the revolving door** — Who moves between industry and government?
3. **Read the fine print** — Who wrote the regulations you're subject to?
4. **Support local journalism** — Investigative reporters need resources to follow these stories.
5. **Organize** — Individual action is limited. Collective action is powerful.

## How to Evaluate a Company's Ethics in Petrostates

- Do they publish their payments to governments? (Extractive Industries Transparency Initiative)
- Do they have a genuine human rights policy?
- Do they operate in countries with known corruption?
- Do their contracts benefit the local population or just the elite?
- Are they transparent about their environmental impact?

> **Case: The Transparency Solution** (Epilogue): Maddow points to the Extractive Industries Transparency Initiative (EITI) as a partial solution. Countries that publish what they receive from oil companies and companies that publish what they pay can begin to break the secrecy that enables corruption.
> **Key takeaway**: Transparency is not a cure-all, but it's a necessary first step. You can't fight what you can't see.
