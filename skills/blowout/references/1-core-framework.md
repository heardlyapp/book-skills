# Core Framework: The Resource Curse and Kleptocracy

## The Central Thesis

"The oil and gas industry is the richest, most destructive industry on Earth — not just environmentally but politically." Rachel Maddow's argument is that oil wealth systematically corrupts democracy, both in petrostates and in the democracies that do business with them.

> **Case: The Exxon-Rosneft Deal** (Chapters 3-4): ExxonMobil, led by Rex Tillerson, forged a partnership with Rosneft, Russia's state-owned oil company led by Vladimir Putin's ally Igor Sechin. The deal gave Exxon access to Russia's Arctic oil reserves. Tillerson received Russia's "Order of Friendship" award. Years later, when Russia invaded Crimea, the deal collapsed — but it showed how Western oil companies prioritized profits over geopolitical values.
> **Key takeaway**: When Western companies partner with authoritarian regimes, they lend legitimacy to those regimes and prioritize profits over democratic values.

## The Kleptocracy Cycle

1. **Resource discovery** — Oil or gas is found in a country with weak institutions
2. **Wealth concentration** — Ruling elites capture the revenue
3. **Institutional weakening** — Courts, media, and elections become tools of the elite
4. **Repression** — Dissent is crushed using oil-funded security forces
5. **International complicity** — Western companies, banks, and governments enable the system
6. **Environmental destruction** — Costs are externalized to communities and the planet

> **Case: Russia Under Putin** (Chapters 5-7): Putin transformed Russia from a struggling post-Soviet state into a global power by nationalizing oil and gas assets. The wealth funded military modernization, media control, and international influence operations. The oil price boom of the 2000s made it possible. The Russian people did not benefit — the elite did.
> **Key takeaway**: Oil wealth does not trickle down in kleptocracies. It flows up.
