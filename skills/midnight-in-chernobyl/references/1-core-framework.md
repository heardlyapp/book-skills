# Core Framework — Midnight in Chernobyl

## Prologue: 2,080 Roentgen

Senior Lieutenant Alexander Logachev, lead radiation reconnaissance officer, was speeding toward Chernobyl on April 26, 1986. His newly installed dosimeter read 2,080 roentgen per hour at the plant perimeter. "Logachev struggled to remain calm and remember the textbook; to conquer his fear. But his training failed him, and the lieutenant heard himself screaming in panic at the driver." His assessment: "If this thing dies, we'll all be corpses in fifteen minutes!"

## Chapter 1: The Soviet Prometheus (1970)

Viktor Brukhanov, 34, was appointed director of a project that would become "the greatest nuclear power station on earth." He knew little about nuclear power — his background was electrical engineering. "Knowledge and experience were regarded as less important qualifications for top management than loyalty and an ability to get things done." Construction began February 20, 1970, in a snow-covered field near the town of Chernobyl.

## The RBMK Reactor Design

The RBMK (Reaktor Bolshoy Moshchnosti Kanalnyy — High Power Channel-type Reactor) had fatal design flaws: a positive void coefficient (when water turned to steam, the reaction accelerated instead of slowing), graphite-tipped control rods that initially *increased* reactivity when inserted, and inadequate containment. It was designed to produce plutonium for weapons as well as electricity.

## Friday, April 25 — Saturday, April 26, 1986: The Experiment

Plant operators planned a test of the turbine generator's ability to provide emergency power during a station blackout. The test required the reactor to be running at low power — a dangerous state for the RBMK. As delays pushed the test to the night shift, a series of errors occurred: the reactor was stabilized at 200 MWt instead of the minimum 700 MWt required, and safety systems were disabled. At 1:23:04 AM, an emergency shutdown was initiated. The graphite-tipped control rods caused a power surge. The reactor exploded.

**The explosion:** "The thousand-ton cover plate of the reactor was lifted clean off, flipping in the air. A jet of plasma — superheated nuclear fuel — shot through the roof of the building." Graphite blocks, fuel assemblies, and burning debris rained down.

## The Firefighters

**Lieutenant Vladimir Pravik** and his crew arrived within minutes. They climbed onto the roof in short-sleeved shirts, kicking burning graphite blocks with their boots. None wore dosimeters. "They had no idea what they were dealing with." Within days, all of Pravik's men were dead from acute radiation syndrome.

**Sergeant Vasily Ignatenko** was Pravik's best friend. He was exposed to lethal radiation. His wife Lyudmilla was allowed to visit him in Hospital Number Six. She described his skin turning black, his hair falling out, his gums bleeding. "His body had become a nuclear reactor."

## The Cover-Up

Moscow's initial response: deny and minimize. Gorbachev wasn't told for three days. The Politburo sent a commission led by Boris Scherbina. The nearby city of Pripyat (50,000 residents) was evacuated 36 hours after the explosion. The world found out when Swedish monitoring stations detected elevated radiation. "The Soviet government tried to hide the truth — but the radiation cloud did not respect borders."

## The Liquidators

500,000 men were mobilized to clean up the reactor site. "Liquidators" — soldiers, miners, engineers, helicopter pilots — were sent into the most radioactive zones with minimal protection. Miners dug a tunnel under the reactor to prevent meltdown into groundwater. Helicopter pilots dumped 5,000 tons of sand, boron, clay, and lead into the open reactor. Many died or were permanently disabled.

## The Trial

Brukhanov, Fomin (chief engineer), and Dyatlov (deputy chief engineer) were put on trial. The trial was a Stalinist show — but it also revealed the truth: the reactor design was fundamentally unsafe. "The system was on trial, not just the men." All three were sentenced to ten years in labor camps.

## The Elephant's Foot

A mass of corium (melted nuclear fuel, concrete, and sand) had flowed into the basement of Unit Four. When discovered, it was emitting 10,000 roentgen per hour — lethal within seconds. "The Elephant's Foot" became the symbol of the disaster's enduring danger.

## Epilogue: Valery Legasov's Suicide

Valery Legasov, the Kurchatov Institute scientist who led the investigation, was broken by the experience. He testified truthfully about the disaster and the cover-up. Two years after the trial, he committed suicide. He was posthumously rehabilitated. His voice is heard narrating parts of the HBO miniseries.
