## The Preston Framework: Hot Zones and Level 4

Preston's framework is built on the idea that the earth harbors natural "hot zones" — areas where emerging viruses exist in animal reservoirs and occasionally cross into humans. The book traces three hot zone encounters: Charles Monet's Marburg infection, the Reston Ebola incident, and the search for Kitum Cave.

**The Key Insight**: The most dangerous pathogens are not the ones that are deadly — they are the ones that are deadly AND transmissible before symptoms appear. Ebola's saving grace: it kills faster than it spreads.

> **Case: Charles Monet's Flight** (Part I): Charles Monet, a French expat in Kenya, contracted Marburg virus. His symptoms began with a headache and progressed through vomiting, bleeding, and complete organ failure within days. In his final hours, Monet's liver was dissolving. He vomited black material — digested blood — and died in a hospital. The book opens with this story to establish the terror of filoviruses.
> **Key takeaway**: The incubation period of filoviruses is short. From first symptom to death can be less than a week. The speed is part of the terror.

> **Case: The Reston Containment** (Part III): In 1989, monkeys imported from the Philippines to Reston, Virginia began dying. Nancy Jaax of USAMRIID identified Ebola. The team entered Level 4 containment — fully sealed suits with internal air supplies — to euthanize the monkeys and decontaminate the facility. One soldier's suit was punctured. He was rushed to decontamination. He survived. The virus turned out to be a strain harmless to humans — but they did not know that at the time.
> **Key takeaway**: The Reston response was a dress rehearsal. The courage of the containment team was real even though the threat was not. The next time, the threat might be real.
