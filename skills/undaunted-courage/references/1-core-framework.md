# The Corps of Discovery — The Greatest Expedition in American History

> Source: Undaunted Courage, Part I-III

## Core Framework

The Lewis and Clark Expedition was not just a journey — it was a military expedition with scientific objectives, diplomatic goals, and immense political stakes.

### Key Facts

| Fact | Detail |
|------|--------|
| Duration | May 14, 1804 — September 23, 1806 (28 months) |
| Distance | ~8,000 miles round trip |
| Personnel | 33 permanent members (plus Lewis, Clark, Sacagawea, Jean Baptiste, York, and Charbonneau) |
| Boats | Keelboat (55 ft), two pirogues (open boats), later dugout canoes |
| Cost | $2,500 appropriated by Congress (about $50,000 in actual cost) |
| Fatalities | One death (Sergeant Floyd, ruptured appendix) — remarkably low for such a dangerous journey |

### The Route

1. St. Louis (May 1804) → 2. Missouri River → 3. Mandan villages (winter 1804-1805) → 4. Missouri River to Great Falls → 5. Rocky Mountains (Lemhi Pass) → 6. Columbia River → 7. Pacific Ocean (Fort Clatsop, winter 1805-1806)

> **Case: The Only Death — Sergeant Floyd** (Chapter 9)
> Charles Floyd was the only member of the Corps to die during the expedition. He suffered a ruptured appendix — a condition that was untreatable in 1804. He died on August 20, 1804, near present-day Sioux City, Iowa. The Corps buried him on a bluff overlooking the Missouri River, which they named Floyd's Bluff. The remarkable fact: 33 men traveled 8,000 miles through uncharted wilderness, facing grizzlies, rapids, starvation, and hostile tribes — and only one died from natural causes.
> **Key takeaway**: The survival rate of the Corps of Discovery is extraordinary. It testifies to Lewis's leadership, the men's toughness, and the indispensable help of Native American tribes. Modern expeditions with all their technology have worse survival records.

> **Case: The Winter at Fort Mandan** (Chapter 12)
> The Corps built Fort Mandan near present-day Washburn, North Dakota, and spent the winter of 1804-1805 with the Mandan and Hidatsa tribes. This was where they met Toussaint Charbonneau and his Shoshone wife, Sacagawea. Sacagawea was pregnant and gave birth to Jean Baptiste during the winter. The Mandan provided food, information about the route ahead, and diplomatic connections. Without this winter of preparation and alliance-building, the expedition would have failed.
> **Key takeaway**: The expedition's success depended on Native American knowledge and assistance. The Mandan winter was not a delay — it was essential preparation for the journey ahead. The Corps did not conquer the wilderness; they learned from those who already lived there.
