# Sacagawea and the Native American World

> Source: Undaunted Courage, Chapters 14-20

## The Corps and the Tribes

The Corps interacted with dozens of Native American tribes. Most encounters were peaceful. The expedition's success depended on good relations with the people who already lived in the West.

### Key Tribal Encounters

| Tribe | Interaction | Key Detail |
|-------|-------------|------------|
| Mandan | Alliance, winter quarters | Essential for survival of winter 1804-1805 |
| Teton Sioux | Hostile confrontation | Blocked the Missouri River; Corps prepared to fight but negotiated passage |
| Shoshone | Critical alliance | Provided horses for crossing the Rockies — Sacagawea's people |
| Nez Perce | Warm welcome | Fed the starving Corps, showed them how to make canoes for the Columbia |
| Chinook | Trade | Obtained food and information for the final push to the Pacific |

> **Case: Sacagawea's Meeting with the Shoshone** (Chapter 19)
> In August 1805, the Corps reached the headwaters of the Missouri, where they desperately needed horses to cross the Rocky Mountains. The only tribe that had horses was the Shoshone. Lewis went ahead with three men and found a Shoshone band. Their chief, Cameahwait, was suspicious. Lewis brought Sacagawea to interpret. When Sacagawea began interpreting, she recognized Cameahwait as her brother. They had been captured by the Hidatsa years before and had not seen each other since. The emotional reunion secured the alliance. The Shoshone provided horses that saved the expedition.
> **Key takeaway**: Sacagawea's reunion with her brother was one of the most dramatic moments in American history. It was pure luck — but it was luck that could only happen because the Corps had treated her with respect. She was not just an interpreter — she was the key to the expedition's most critical moment.

> **Case: The Nez Perce Rescue** (Chapter 21)
> By the time the Corps reached the Nez Perce in September 1805, they were starving. They had crossed the Rocky Mountains on foot, eating horse meat and candles. The Nez Perce fed them, showed them how to make dugout canoes, and guided them to the Columbia River. When the Corps returned in May 1806, the Nez Perce again helped them, providing food and guidance for the crossing back over the mountains. Without the Nez Perce, the Corps would have starved in the mountains.
> **Key takeaway**: The expedition's success was a team effort — and the team extended far beyond the 33 men of the Corps. The Nez Perce saved their lives twice. This is a story of cooperation, not conquest.
