# 4 — Media and Political Response

> Source: "Unmasked" by Andy Ngo

## Anti-Pattern: Minimization

The most common error is dismissing Antifa as a fabrication or overblown concern, despite extensive documented evidence of violence and organization.

## Anti-Pattern: Selective Coverage

Covering violence from the right extensively while downplaying or ignoring violence from the left.

> **Case: The "Scuffle" Description** (Chapter on Ngo's Assault): "I was attacked by Antifa in Portland in June 2019. A group of masked individuals punched and kicked me, hitting me with a metal object that caused a brain hemorrhage. The assault was captured on video. Some media outlets described it as a 'scuffle.'" Ngo argues that the choice of language minimizes the violence.
> **Key takeaway**: The way media describes ideologically different violence reveals bias. A brain-hemorrhage-inducing assault described as a "scuffle" is evidence of framing that minimizes left-wing violence.

> **Case: The Political Silence** (Chapter on Politics): "When local leaders in Portland refused to condemn the violence or even acknowledge Antifa's role, they created an environment where the violence could continue unchecked." Ngo documents how political leaders' refusal to name the perpetrators effectively enabled them.
> **Key takeaway**: Silence is a form of complicity. When political leaders refuse to condemn violence from their own side, they signal that it is acceptable. Consistent condemnation regardless of ideology is essential.
