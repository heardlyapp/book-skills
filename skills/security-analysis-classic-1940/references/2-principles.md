# Key Principles — Security Analysis: The Classic 1940 Edition

## I. Investment vs. Speculation

"An investment operation is one which, upon thorough analysis, promises safety of principal and an adequate return." If it doesn't meet these criteria, it's speculation. Case: Buying a stock without a margin of safety is not investment — it's gambling.

## II. The Margin of Safety

"The margin of safety is the central concept of investment." Buy at a significant discount to calculated intrinsic value. The margin protects against errors, bad luck, and market volatility. Case: A bond with $3 in earnings coverage per $1 of interest has a margin of safety that protects against downturns.

## III. Rigorous Financial Analysis

"Thorough analysis" is required — balance sheets, income accounts, earnings records, asset values. Not superficial ratios or market tips. Case: Graham's method of analyzing bond offerings required checking at least seven years of earnings data, current asset coverage, and specific protective covenants.

## IV. The Future Is Uncertain

"The times seem to call for caution in embracing any theory as to the future." The value investor doesn't predict the future — they buy assets at a discount that compensates for uncertainty. Case: The 1929 crash proved that all optimistic forecasts were worthless.

## V. Asset Values Matter

"Implications of asset values." Book value, current-asset value, and liquidating value provide a floor for the stock price. Case: A stock selling below its net current asset value is a classic Graham bargain.

## VI. Dividends Over Earnings

"The dividend factor in common stock analysis." Dividends are a more reliable indicator of value than earnings forecasts. A consistent dividend record signals financial health. Case: "A stock that has maintained its dividend through the depression is a better bet than one that pays no dividend but claims great future earnings."

## VII. Discrepancies Are Opportunities

"The entire purpose of security analysis is to find discrepancies between price and value." The market is not always rational. The analyst's job is to identify mispricings and act on them. Case: "Special situations" — mergers, liquidations, reorganizations — often create temporary price-value gaps that the analyst can exploit.
