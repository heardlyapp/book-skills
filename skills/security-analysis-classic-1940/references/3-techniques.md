# Techniques — Security Analysis: The Classic 1940 Edition

## How to Analyze Securities (Graham & Dodd's Method)

### Technique 1: The Bond Selection Framework
Seven tests for fixed-value investments: 1) Adequate earnings coverage of fixed charges, 2) Adequate asset coverage, 3) Strong market position, 4) Satisfactory earnings record (7+ years), 5) No serious adverse trends, 6) Protective covenants, 7) Margin of safety in coverage. Case: Railroad bonds failing just 1-2 of these tests were the ones that defaulted in the 1930s.

### Technique 2: The Net-Net Bargain
Find stocks selling at less than net current asset value (current assets minus total liabilities). Buy them. "These are the classic Graham bargains." Case: Companies trading below their cash alone — the investor essentially gets the business for free.

### Technique 3: Balance-Sheet Depth Analysis
"Book value, current-asset value, and liquidating value." Calculate each and compare to the market price. Case: A company with $50/book value trading at $20 is more interesting than one with $20/book value trading at $20.

### Technique 4: The Earnings Record Check
"Significance of the earnings record." At least 7 years of earnings data. Look for stability and coverage, not growth. Case: "A company that earned its dividend in every year of the depression has demonstrated earning power."

### Technique 5: The Dividend Record Test
"The dividend factor in common stock analysis." Companies with uninterrupted dividend records are more likely to be fairly valued. Case: "A corporation that has paid dividends for 20+ years demonstrates management's commitment to shareholders."

### Technique 6: The Comparative Analysis
"Comparative analysis of companies in the same field." Compare P/E ratios, yields, asset coverage, and earnings stability across competitors. Case: If Company A trades at 8x earnings and Company B at 5x, with similar balance sheets, Company B is likely undervalued.

### Technique 7: Special Situations
"Discrepancies between price and value." Mergers, liquidations, reorganizations, and spin-offs create temporary mispricings. Case: In a merger where the acquiring company's stock is undervalued, the arbitrage opportunity exists.
