# Core Framework — Security Analysis: The Classic 1940 Edition

## Introduction: Investment Policy

The 1940 edition opens by acknowledging that "the times seem to call for caution in embracing any theory as to the future and for flexible and open-minded investment policies." The book's framework is built on the recognition that financial markets are inherently unstable. Case: The three reference periods (1911-1913, 1923-1925, 1936-1938) show "progressive widening" of market fluctuations — instability is increasing over time.

## Part I: Survey and Approach

**Investment vs. Speculation.** "An investment operation is one which, upon thorough analysis, promises safety of principal and an adequate return. Operations not meeting these requirements are speculative." This definition underpins everything. Case: Buying stocks in 1929 at 30x earnings with no margin of safety was speculation, not investment.

## Part II: Fixed-Value Investments

The selection of bonds and preferred stocks. Stringent standards: adequate coverage of fixed charges, asset backing, and a margin of safety. "By sufficiently stringent standards of selection and reasonably frequent scrutiny, the investor should be able to escape most serious losses." Case: Railroad bonds — once thought "safe beyond question" — collapsed in the 1930s depression.

## Part III-V: Senior Securities, Common Stocks, Income Accounts

Covers privileged issues (convertible bonds, warrants), the theory of common stock investment, the dividend factor, and earnings analysis. Key insight: "The dividend factor in common stock analysis" — dividends are a more reliable indicator of value than earnings projections. Case: Companies with stable dividend records tend to have more stable market values.

## Part VI: Balance-Sheet Analysis

"Implications of asset values." Book value, current-asset value, liquidating value. The value investor looks at what a company actually owns. Case: Companies selling below net current asset value (the "net-net") are classic Graham bargains.

## Part VII: Discrepancies Between Price and Value

The core of the analyst's work: finding securities where market price diverges from intrinsic value. "Stock-option warrants," "cost of financing and management," "corporate pyramiding," and "comparative analysis of companies in the same field." Case: "Discrepancies between price and value" — the entire purpose of security analysis is to find and exploit these.
