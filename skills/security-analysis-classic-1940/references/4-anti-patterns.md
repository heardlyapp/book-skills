# Anti-Patterns — Security Analysis: The Classic 1940 Edition

## The Central Error: "This Time Is Different"

"The comfortable conviction of 1925 that we were moving steadily towards both greater stability and greater prosperity." Each generation believes the old rules don't apply. They're always wrong. Case: The 1929 "new era" boom — everyone believed stocks had reached a permanently higher plateau.

### Mistake 1: Buying Without a Margin of Safety
"It's a great company — just buy it." Even a great company, bought at too high a price, is a bad investment. Case: "A stock is not a good investment just because the company is good. It must be priced below its intrinsic value."

### Mistake 2: Confusing Speculation with Investment
"My stock went up 50% — I made a great investment." Price appreciation alone doesn't validate an investment. It was speculation that happened to work out. Case: "An investment operation must promise safety of principal based on thorough analysis."

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Asset Values
"This company has great earnings — who cares about book value?" Asset values provide the floor. When earnings disappoint, asset values are what's left. Case: "Companies that appeared strong on earnings alone collapsed when asset values were revealed to be overstated."

### Mistake 4: Chasing Growth at Any Price
"This company is going to grow 20% a year — it's worth any price." Growth projections are inherently uncertain. The margin of safety is more important than the growth rate. Case: "The newer canons of common stock investment" — Graham warns against paying excessive prices for growth expectations.

### Mistake 5: Over-Reliance on Future Forecasts
"The times seem to call for caution in embracing any theory as to the future." Past earnings records are facts. Future earnings are guesses. Base decisions on facts. Case: "Significance of the earnings record" — 7+ years of actual data matter more than any forecast.

### Mistake 6: Ignoring Protective Covenants
"It's a bond — it must be safe." Many bonds in the 1930s had weak protective covenants that left bondholders with no recourse when companies defaulted. Case: "Protective covenants and remedies of senior security holders" — without strong covenants, bondholders are at management's mercy.
