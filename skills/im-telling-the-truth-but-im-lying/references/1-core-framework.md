# 1 — The Bipolar Journey

> Source: "I'm Telling the Truth, but I'm Lying" by Bassey Ikpi

## The Framework: Diagnosis → Naming → Managing → Living

Ikpi spent years knowing something was wrong without having a name for it. The diagnosis of bipolar II gave her a framework — not a sentence, but a starting point.

### Diagnosis
The experience of being told you have bipolar is itself disorienting. A name for the chaos. Ike writes about the relief of having a word for what she experienced — and the fear of what that word means.

### Naming
Naming it doesn't fix it. But it makes it discussable, researchable, manageable. The opposite of unnamed terror is named condition.

### Managing
Medication, therapy, sleep, community. She's honest about the struggle: finding the right meds, the side effects, the temptation to go off them when you feel better.

### Living
Bipolar is not a life sentence. It's a condition that requires management. Ikpi built a life — a career, a family, a book — while managing bipolar.

> **Case: The Diagnosis Essay** (Chapter 5): Ikpi describes the moment of diagnosis not as a tragedy but as a revelation. "I had a name for the thing that had been chasing me my whole life." The name changed everything and nothing. She still had to do the work.
> **Key takeaway**: A diagnosis is a tool, not a verdict. It gives you a starting point for the work of healing. Without the name, you can't fight it.

> **Case: Managing Medication** (Chapter 7): Ikpi writes honestly about the struggle with medication. The side effects. The temptation to stop. The cycles of "I feel better, so I don't need this anymore" followed by relapse. "Every time I go off my meds, I end up back in the hospital. You'd think I'd learn."
> **Key takeaway**: Managing bipolar is a lifelong practice, not a one-time fix. The discipline of treatment is as important as the treatment itself.
