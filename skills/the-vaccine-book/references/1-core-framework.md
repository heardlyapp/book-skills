# 1 — Individual Vaccine Profiles

> Source: "The Vaccine Book" by Dr. Robert W. Sears

## How Each Chapter Is Structured (Chapters 1-12)

For each vaccine, Sears provides:
1. What the disease is
2. How common it is
3. How serious it is
4. How the vaccine is made
5. What the ingredients are
6. What the possible side effects are
7. Points parents should consider
8. His own summary of relative importance

> **Case: The Relative Importance Scale** (Preface and Chapter 1-12): Sears rates each vaccine on a relative importance scale. For example, HIB and pneumococcal are rated highly important because the diseases are most dangerous to infants, while rotavirus and chickenpox are rated lower because the diseases are generally less severe. "Not all vaccines are equally important. Your decision should reflect this."
> **Key takeaway**: Sears does not treat all vaccines as equal. He provides parents with a framework for prioritizing — some vaccines prevent life-threatening diseases, others prevent uncomfortable but rarely fatal ones.

> **Case: The DTaP Story** (Chapter 3): Sears describes the history of the pertussis (whooping cough) vaccine. The original whole-cell DTP vaccine had a higher rate of side effects including febrile seizures. The newer acellular DTaP has far fewer side effects but may wane faster. "Parents should know that even the best vaccine is not perfect, and that the disease itself is serious enough that vaccination is strongly recommended."
> **Key takeaway**: Vaccines evolve. The DTaP story shows that the current vaccine is safer than the previous version, but it also shows that vaccine development is ongoing. Parents should understand the history and the current state.
