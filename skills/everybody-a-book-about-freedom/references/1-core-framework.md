# Core Framework: The Body and Freedom

> Source: Everybody, Introduction and Chapter 1

## The Central Question

Can the body be free? Laing explores this through the life and work of Wilhelm Reich, who believed political and psychological freedom must be physically embodied.

> **Case: Laing's Own Body** (Introduction): Laing describes body psychotherapy. When her therapist touched her shoulder, she felt "energy streaming through my body, moving through my abdomen and down my legs." This bodily sensation — not intellectual insight — was the beginning of liberation from Section 28's legacy.

> **Key takeaway**: Freedom is not an abstract idea. It's a physical experience in the body. The book begins with Laing's own body as evidence.

## Reich's Central Insight

Repression is stored in the body as "character armor" — chronic muscle tension holding past trauma. Liberation requires releasing the body.

> **Case: The Working-Class Patients** (Chapter 1): In 1920s Vienna, Reich's working-class patients couldn't afford long analysis. He developed body techniques — pressure, breath, touch — that released emotional blockages quickly. This was revolutionary in psychoanalysis.

> **Case: The Orgone Box** (Chapter 1): Reich later invented the "orgone accumulator" — a box designed to concentrate universal life energy. He was prosecuted by the FDA, jailed, and died in prison. Laing uses this bizarre, tragic story to show how dangerous body freedom is to the state.

> **Key takeaway**: The body holds what the mind represses. Reich's story shows that trying to free bodies is a dangerous political act.
