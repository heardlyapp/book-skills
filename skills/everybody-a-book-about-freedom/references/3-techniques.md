# Techniques: Race, Protest, and Music

> Source: Everybody, Chapters 5-6

## A Radiant Net (Nina Simone)

Simone's body on stage — her hands on the piano, her voice rising in anger — was itself an act of freedom. Laing uses Simone to show that art is bodily protest.

> **Case: Nina Simone at the March on Washington** (Chapter 5): Simone performed "Mississippi Goddam" — a song about the murder of Medgar Evers and the bombing of a Birmingham church. Her body shook with rage and grief. This was not just music — it was a body refusing to be silent.

> **Key takeaway**: Protest is fundamentally bodily. Simone used her entire body — voice, hands, face — to demand Black freedom.

## Malcolm X

Malcolm's body was transformed multiple times. Each transformation involved discipline and reclamation.

> **Case: Malcolm in Prison** (Chapter 5): Prison was bodily confinement, but Malcolm used it for transformation. He educated himself, found faith, and emerged with a new body — disciplined, upright, purposeful. The state couldn't control his mind, and his body followed.

> **Case: Malcolm After Mecca** (Chapter 5): His pilgrimage transformed his body again — softer, more open. Laing notes that bodily transformation is spiritual transformation. Malcolm's changing body tracks his changing politics.

## Susan Sontag and Illness

Sontag wrote about illness as metaphor. Laing uses her to explore how the sick body is both vulnerable and political.

> **Case: Sontag's Cancer** (Chapter 5): Sontag refused to let her body be defined by cancer. She wrote about the metaphors that surround illness — TB as romantic, cancer as shameful — and argued for seeing illness as illness, not metaphor.

> **Key takeaway**: The body in protest is not only the marching body. It's also the singing body, the transforming body, and the sick body that refuses to be reduced to its diagnosis.
