# Principles: Sex Acts and Harm's Way

> Source: Everybody, Chapters 3-4

## Sexual Freedom

Sexuality is where the body is most regulated and most potentially free. Laing traces the fight from Reich through Stonewall.

> **Case: Stonewall** (Chapter 3): The 1969 Stonewall riots were a bodily rebellion. Gay and trans people, exhausted by police raids, fought back. Laing connects this to Reich: sexual repression is political repression. The body that refuses to be controlled is the body that demands freedom.

> **Case: Christopher Isherwood** (Chapter 3): Isherwood's life spans a century of gay experience — from 1920s Berlin to 1980s California. His body ages, desires, and ultimately faces mortality. Laing uses his story to show how gay men navigated bodily repression across a lifetime.

> **Key takeaway**: Sexual freedom is the core of bodily autonomy. A body that cannot love freely is not a free body.

## In Harm's Way

The body is vulnerable. Laing doesn't romanticize freedom — she shows its costs.

> **Case: The AIDS Crisis** (Chapter 4): The AIDS epidemic devastated gay communities. Bodies that had fought for sexual freedom were dying. The state's slow, callous response was another form of violence against gay bodies — letting them die.

> **Case: The PMRC Hearings** (Chapter 4): Tipper Gore's campaign to censor music in the 1980s. Laing uses this to show how the state tries to control the body through culture — what you can hear, see, and read.

> **Key takeaway**: Sexual freedom and bodily vulnerability are inseparable. Fighting for the body means protecting it from harm too.
