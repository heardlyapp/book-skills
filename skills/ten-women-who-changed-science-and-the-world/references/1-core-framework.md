# Core Framework: The Ten Profiles

## The Central Story

"These ten women were not exceptions — they were pioneers who overcame a system designed to exclude them." The book profiles ten women whose scientific discoveries changed the world, despite facing discrimination, lack of recognition, and systemic barriers.

> **Case: Rosalind Franklin's Photo 51** (Profile 7): Rosalind Franklin's X-ray crystallography image of DNA — Photo 51 — was the crucial evidence that revealed DNA's double helix structure. She showed the image to a colleague, who shared it with Watson and Crick without her knowledge. They used it to build their model, won the Nobel Prize, and Franklin died before receiving proper credit.
> **Key takeaway**: Franklin's story is the most famous case of a woman scientist not receiving credit for her work. Her data was essential to one of the greatest discoveries in biology, yet she was marginalized in the narrative.

| Rachel Carson | Environmental Science | Silent Spring, environmental movement |

> **Case: Marie Curie's Double Nobel** (Profile 1): Marie Curie won Nobel Prizes in two different sciences — Physics (1903) and Chemistry (1911). She discovered radium and polonium, pioneered research into radioactivity, and died from radiation exposure. She was the first woman to win a Nobel Prize and remains the only person to win Nobels in two sciences.
> **Key takeaway**: Curie's achievements are extraordinary by any standard. But even she faced barriers — the French Academy of Sciences refused to elect her as a member.
