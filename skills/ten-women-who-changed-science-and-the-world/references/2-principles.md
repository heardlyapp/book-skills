> Source: Ten Women Who Changed Science, Profiles 4-6

# Key Principles

## 1. Systemic Barriers Are Real, Not Personal

"The discrimination these women faced was not about their individual merits — it was about a system that excluded women by default." Understanding this helps avoid the trap of thinking "if they just tried harder" or "if I just try harder."

## 2. Persistence Is the Common Thread

"The one quality every woman in this book shares is the refusal to stop." Whether it was Curie pursuing radioactivity despite lacking lab space, or Levi-Montalcini continuing research in her bedroom during WWII, they all kept going.

> **Case: Levi-Montalcini's Bedroom Lab** (Profile 5): During World War II, Rita Levi-Montalcini was banned from her university position because she was Jewish. She set up a laboratory in her bedroom, using sewing needles as dissecting tools and homemade instruments. She discovered nerve growth factor in this makeshift lab.
> **Key takeaway**: When the system excludes you, find another way. Levi-Montalcini didn't have a formal lab, but she had her curiosity, her intelligence, and her determination. That was enough.

## 3. Credit Is Often Delayed or Denied

"Several of these women were denied credit for their discoveries during their lifetimes." Lise Meitner's contribution to nuclear fission was not recognized by the Nobel committee. Rosalind Franklin's role in DNA discovery was downplayed for decades.

## 4. Interdisciplinary Work Produces Breakthroughs

"The most important discoveries came at the intersection of fields." Dorothy Hodgkin combined physics (X-ray crystallography) with chemistry and biology. Rachel Carson combined biology with writing and public advocacy.

## 5. Science Serves Humanity

"Every woman in this book made discoveries that directly improved human life." From Curie's radiation therapy to Apgar's newborn scoring to Elion's drug development — their work saved lives.

> **Case: Gertrude Elion's Drug Breakthroughs** (Profile 8): Elion developed drugs that transformed medicine: the first treatment for leukemia, the first immunosuppressive drug for organ transplants, and the first antiviral drug (acyclovir). She won the Nobel Prize — but her work was done without a PhD, because she couldn't afford graduate school.
> **Key takeaway**: Formal credentials are not a prerequisite for world-changing work. Elion's lack of a PhD did not prevent her from developing drugs that saved millions of lives.
