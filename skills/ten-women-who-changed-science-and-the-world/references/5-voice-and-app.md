# Author's Voice + Key Scenarios

> **Case: The Authors' Purpose** (Introduction): Catherine Whitlock and Rhodri Evans wrote this book to make the stories of these women accessible to a general audience. Each chapter profiles one scientist, covering her life, her work, and her legacy.
> **Key takeaway**: The book is designed to educate and inspire — not to be an exhaustive academic history. It's a starting point for learning about these remarkable women.

> **Case: Rachel Carson's Legacy** (Profile 10): Rachel Carson's book Silent Spring launched the modern environmental movement. She was attacked by the chemical industry, called a "hysterical woman," and subjected to sexist criticism. She died of cancer shortly after publication — but her work changed the world.
> **Key takeaway**: Carson faced the same barriers as the other women — her scientific work was attacked not on its merits but because of her gender. She persisted, and the result was a global movement.

## Key Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Feeling Discouraged in STEM
Read one profile. These women faced far worse. Their persistence is the lesson.

### Scenario 2: Wanting Role Models
Each of the ten offers a different path. Curie's pure dedication. Franklin's precision. Carson's advocacy. Find the one that resonates.

### Scenario 3: Learning Scientific History
Start with the big names (Curie, Franklin, Carson) then explore the lesser-known (Wu, Levi-Montalcini, McClintock).

### Scenario 4: Advocating for Inclusion
Use these stories to demonstrate that talent is everywhere but opportunity is not. The barriers are real.

### Scenario 5: Mentoring Young Scientists
Share these stories. Representation matters. Young women need to see that they belong in science.
