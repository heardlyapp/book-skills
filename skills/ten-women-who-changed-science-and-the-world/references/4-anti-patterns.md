> Source: Ten Women Who Changed Science, Profiles 2, 6, 9

# Anti-Patterns

## 1. Believing Science Is Meritocratic

**The mistake**: Assuming scientific recognition is purely based on merit.

**Why it fails**: Lise Meitner was not awarded the Nobel Prize for nuclear fission despite doing the crucial work. Rosalind Franklin's data was taken without her knowledge. The system is not purely meritocratic.

**Fix**: Understand that recognition and contribution are not always aligned.

> **Case: The Meitner Oversight** (Profile 2): Lise Meitner worked with Otto Hahn on nuclear fission. She fled Nazi Germany in 1938. Hahn continued the work and published the discovery — without including Meitner as co-author. Hahn won the Nobel Prize. Meitner's contribution was essential but unrecognized.
> **Key takeaway**: The history of science is not a pure meritocracy. Women's contributions have been systematically overlooked. Fixing this requires active effort.

## 2. Thinking Individual Achievement Alone Is Enough

**The mistake**: Believing that individual brilliance overcomes all barriers.

**Fix**: Even Curie needed support — from her husband Pierre, from the University of Paris, from the Nobel committee. No one succeeds alone.

## 3. Equating Recognition with Impact

**The mistake**: Measuring success by awards and titles.

**Fix**: These women's impact is measured by what they discovered and how they changed the world, not by the prizes they won. Franklin died without a Nobel. Her work was essential.

## 4. Assuming Science Has "Caught Up" on Inclusion

**The mistake**: Believing the problems these women faced are entirely in the past.

**Fix**: While conditions have improved, women and other underrepresented groups still face barriers in STEM. The fight for inclusion continues.

> **Case: The Ongoing Struggle** (Conclusion): The book's final chapter notes that while progress has been made, women still make up a minority of Nobel laureates, hold fewer leadership positions in science, and face ongoing discrimination. The ten women's stories are not just history — they are ongoing inspiration.
> **Key takeaway**: The barriers these women faced are lower today but not gone. Their stories continue to inspire the next generation of women scientists.
