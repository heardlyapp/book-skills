# Reference: Fundamental Techniques

## Overview
Carnegie's three fundamental techniques for handling people are the foundation of everything else. They are counterintuitive and extremely effective.

## Case: Don't Criticize
Criticism is dangerous because it attacks the other person's pride and sense of importance. Carnegie tells the story of "Two Gun" Crowley, a murderer who saw himself as a defender of the downtrodden. Even the most violent criminals rarely see themselves as bad people. If they can justify their actions, the rest of us certainly can. Criticism never changes anyone's mind.

## Case: Honest Appreciation
The desire to feel important is the deepest human need. Appreciation satisfies this need more effectively than anything else. Carnegie contrast appreciation with flattery: appreciation is sincere and genuine; flattery is insincere and cheap. People can tell the difference.

## Case: Arouse an Eager Want
The only way to influence someone is to talk about what they want and show them how to get it. Carnegie tells the story of a salesman who needed a client to return a phone call. He called and said, "I wonder if you could help me solve a problem." The client called back immediately. He appealed to the desire to be helpful.

## Case: The Lincoln Technique
Abraham Lincoln used Carnegie's principles before Carnegie wrote them. When he wanted to change the behavior of a general, he would write a sharp letter full of criticism — and then put it aside. He rarely sent it. By writing the letter, he released his negative feelings. By not sending it, he preserved the relationship.
