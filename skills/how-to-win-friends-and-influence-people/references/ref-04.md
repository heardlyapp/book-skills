# Reference: Changing People

## Overview
Giving feedback that changes behavior without causing resentment is one of the most valuable leadership skills. Carnegie's approach is counterintuitive.

## Case: Begin with Praise
Always start with honest appreciation before you criticize. The contrast between positive and negative is softened. Carnegie uses the analogy of a dentist: they don't start with the drill. They start with a gentle cleaning. Then the drill is less painful.

## Case: Talk About Your Own Mistakes
Admitting your own mistakes before criticizing someone else creates an atmosphere where the other person can accept feedback. If you say, "I made a similar mistake in my first job," the other person is more open to hearing what they could have done better.

## Case: Saving Face
Never humiliate someone. Praise their strengths. Let them save face. Carnegie tells the story of a manager who needed to fire an employee. Instead of saying, "You're fired," the manager said, "I've been thinking about what else you could do with your talents. I think there's a better fit somewhere else." The employee left with dignity.

## Case: Ask Questions Instead of Orders
Carnegie says that asking questions instead of giving orders is more effective and preserves the other person's dignity. "What do you think about..." is better than "You should..." "Is there another way we could approach this?" is better than "Do it this way."

## Case: Give the Other Person a Fine Reputation to Live Up To
If you want someone to improve, give them a reputation to live up to. Tell them they are known for their integrity, and they will strive to live up to it. Tell them they are the most punctual person you know, and they will arrive on time.
