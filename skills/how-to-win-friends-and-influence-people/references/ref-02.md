# Reference: Making People Like You

## Overview
Carnegie's principles for making friends are simple, powerful, and based on genuine interest in others.

## Case: Six Ways to Make People Like You
The six principles: become genuinely interested in other people, smile, remember a person's name, be a good listener, talk about their interests, and make them feel important. Carnegie illustrates each with stories. A dog that wags its tail when you approach teaches us that enthusiasm and warmth are magnetic. A good listener is treasured everywhere.

## Case: The Name Principle
Carnegie tells the story of a politician who made people feel valued by remembering their names. He would write a person's name down, repeat it in conversation, and associate it with a visual image. The habit made him extraordinarily popular. A person's name is the sweetest sound in any language.

## Case: The Surgeon's Smile
A surgeon who was known as cold and distant decided to practice Carnegie's principles. He made an effort to smile at his patients and ask about their families. His relationships with patients and staff transformed. The simple act of smiling changed how people experienced him.

## Case: The Only Sure Way to Make People Like You
Carnegie says: "You can make more friends in two months by becoming interested in other people than you can in two years by trying to get other people interested in you." The secret to friendship is not trying to make others like you — it is becoming genuinely interested in them.

## Case: The Listener
One of Carnegie's students was a woman who was considered boring at parties. Carnegie advised her to ask questions and listen. At the next party, instead of trying to be interesting, she became interested. People loved her. The principle: the best conversationalist is the one who listens most.
