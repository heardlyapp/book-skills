# Anti-Patterns — Common Mistakes When Facing Death and Supporting the Dying

## The Central Fallacy

**False positivity is cruelty dressed as kindness.** Telling a dying person to "stay positive" or "fight harder" invalidates their suffering and isolates them in their pain.

> **Case: The Disappearing Patient** (Chapter 32): Irv writes about his therapy group for terminally ill patients. One patient, living with terminal cancer, stops coming to the group. When Irv calls, the patient's wife says he is "doing well" and "too busy." Irv knows the truth — the patient is avoiding the group because he can no longer bear hearing about others dying the way he will die. This is the anti-pattern in action: the patient isolates himself because his suffering has no safe place to land. The group was that safe place, but the fear of seeing his own future mirrored in others drives him away.
> **Key takeaway**: When someone withdraws during terminal illness, do not assume they are "doing well." They may be drowning alone because no one has given them permission to talk about what is actually happening.

> **Case: The Hospice Morphine Conflict** (Chapter 21): In Marilyn's final hours, the hospice nurse administers morphine and lorazepam. Irv experiences "irrational anger" at the nurse for giving too much, thus "depriving me of my last opportunity to speak with Marilyn." He recognizes this later as his own need, not hers. Marilyn needed comfort. Irv needed more time. Those two needs collided.
> **Key takeaway**: In the final moments, the patient's comfort must come before the caregiver's need for closure. If you feel angry at the medical team for easing their pain, ask yourself: "Who is this really for?"

> **Case: Irv Hiding the Lab Results** (Chapter 11): Irv secretly checks Marilyn's lab results using his own Stanford faculty ID. He misinterprets them — thinks they show no progress — and withholds the information from Marilyn. He spirals into despair. Later, Dr. M. emails directly to Marilyn with "cautiously optimistic" results. Irv's secret vigil was not only wrong — it was based on incompetent interpretation.
> **Key takeaway**: Do not filter information from the dying person, even with good intentions. They own their medical data. They are the ones dying — they deserve the truth, unmediated, even when you cannot bear to deliver it.

> **Case: The Portrait Facing the Wall** (Chapters 24, 29): Irv places Marilyn's portrait in the living room but turns it to face the wall because looking at it is too painful. For months he cannot bear to look at her face. When he finally turns it around, he is "staggered by her beauty." He weeps. Then turns it back to the wall. Nearly four months after her death, on her birthday, he still cannot look at it for long.
> **Key takeaway**: Avoiding reminders of the deceased is not a failure of grief. It is a sign that the wound is still fresh. Do not force yourself to "face the pain." Let yourself approach it at your own pace. The portrait will still be there when you are ready.
