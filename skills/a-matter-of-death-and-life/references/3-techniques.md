# Techniques — How to Live a Regret-Free Life and Die at the Right Time

## Practical Methods

The book offers specific techniques for facing death with serenity, drawn from Irv's decades of existential psychotherapy and tested in the most extreme laboratory.

> **Case: The Inventory of No Regrets** (Chapter 1): After a morning of reminiscing — Hawaii, London, Oxford, Paris, Bali, the Seychelles — Marilyn squeezes Irv's hand and says: "Irv, there is nothing I would change." Irv agrees. This is not about perfection. It is about intentionality. Marilyn tells her oncologist directly: "I am one of the lucky ones who will die with no regrets, thus should have an easier time facing death."
> **Key takeaway**: Take 30 minutes to ask yourself: "What would I need to have done to feel no regret at the end?" Then do that thing. Not eventually — now.

> **Case: Nietzsche's "Die at the Right Time"** (Chapters 1, 10): Irv returns repeatedly to a Nietzsche quote: "Many die too late, and some die too early. Die at the right time! What has become perfect, all that is ripe — wants to die. All that is unripe wants to live." Marilyn asks herself: "What is the right time for me to die? Does it make sense to prolong my life if it means living with so much physical wretchedness?" Her answer: "The right time to die is sooner rather than later." She begins giving away her possessions — a medieval manuscript page to Lily, a jacket to Alana, a silver necklace to Anisa. "More and more, I have to assimilate that, when I die, I shall have no consciousness and no say in the matter."
> **Key takeaway**: Ripeness is not biological age. It is a state of completion. Ask: "Is there urgent unfinished business in my life?" If yes, attend to it. If no, you may be riper than you think.

> **Case: The Salon Surprise — Tangible Evidence of the Ripple Effect** (Chapter 12): Marilyn's surprise salon visit includes a book of thirty handwritten letters from women she mentored over five decades. Each letter is evidence of the ripple effect in action — concrete proof that her influence outlasts her body. She reads them over and over.
> **Key takeaway**: Collect your ripple evidence. Ask friends, colleagues, former students to write down how you affected their lives. Read these when death anxiety surfaces. Keep them in a drawer you can reach from your deathbed.

> **Case: Reading Dickens Aloud — The Art of Presence** (Chapter 9): After Marilyn's first successful immunoglobulin treatment, Irv and Marilyn watch "Martin Chuzzlewit." Marilyn fetches the original 20-part Dickens edition and begins reading aloud. "I lean back in my chair, holding her free hand, I purr in ecstasy, listening to each word. This is sheer heaven: what a blessing to have a wife who delights in reading Dickens's prose aloud." They both know she is dying. The pleasure exists alongside the knowledge.
> **Key takeaway**: Presence does not require ignoring death. It requires being fully here — feeling the pleasure AND the knowledge of loss. These are not incompatible. They are the opposite of incompatible.
