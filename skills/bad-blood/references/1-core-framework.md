# Core Framework: The Theranos Pattern

## How Fraud Happens in Silicon Valley

The Bad Blood framework identifies a repeating pattern that made the Theranos fraud possible. Understanding it is the core tool of this book.

### The Seven-Stage Pattern

1. **A compelling personal story** — Holmes dropped out of Stanford at 19, had a fear of needles, and wanted to change the world. The story was irresistible to investors and press.
2. **An impossible claim** — A few drops of blood from a fingerprick could run 200+ tests. Any scientist would have questioned it, but the story swept technical doubts aside.
3. **Secrecy as strategy** — "Proprietary technology" and "trade secrets" were invoked to prevent anyone from examining the technology closely — including Theranos's own employees.
4. **Prestigious but passive board** — George Shultz, Henry Kissinger, Jim Mattis, and others lent credibility without technical oversight. None had medical laboratory expertise.
5. **Pressure on employees** — Engineers were told to hit impossible deadlines. Those who questioned the technology were marginalized or fired. The culture became fear-based.
6. **Whistleblowers emerge** — Employees who believed in the mission but couldn't watch it be corrupted came forward. Tyler Shultz (grandson of board member George Shultz) and Erika Cheung were among the first.
7. **Investigation brings truth to light** — WSJ reporter John Carreyrou's investigation, combined with whistleblower testimony and regulatory action, exposed the fraud.

> **Case: The Novartis Demo** (Prologue): In 2006, Holmes flew to Switzerland and demonstrated Theranos's system to Novartis executives. They were impressed. Inside the company, the demo was celebrated. But the system wasn't ready — it was a prototype held together by duct tape and charisma.
> **Key takeaway**: A successful demo is not a working product. Investors and customers often confuse a good presentation with a functional system.

## Key Red Flag Categories

| Category | Red Flag | What to Watch For |
|----------|----------|-------------------|
| Technology | Claims too advanced for industry norms | "Revolutionary" without peer-reviewed publication |
| Transparency | Secrecy about core technology | Can't visit lab, NDAs on basic operations |
| Board | Prestigious but no domain expertise | Diplomats, politicians, generals on a health-tech board |
| Culture | Fear of speaking up | No one asks questions in meetings, high turnover |
| Founder | Cult of personality | Founder is the only spokesperson, no one else can explain the tech |
| Data | No independent verification | All validation is internal, no third-party studies |
| Narrative | Story changes when challenged | "We also do traditional blood draws" after promising only fingerpricks |

> **Case: The Walgreens Deal** (Chapter 14): Walgreens signed a partnership with Theranos after being dazzled by Holmes's pitch and the company's board. They conducted almost no technical due diligence. When they finally tested the devices in stores, results were wildly inaccurate. Patients received incorrect test results. 
>
> **Key takeaway**: Even huge, established companies can be fooled when they trust charisma over verification. Walgreens' failure was not a failure of opportunity — it was a failure of process.
