# Author's Voice + Key Scenarios

## John Carreyrou's Voice and Approach

John Carreyrou is a Pulitzer Prize-winning investigative journalist at The Wall Street Journal. His writing style is:

- **Journalistic precision**: Every claim is sourced. Every quote is attributed. The narrative reads like a thriller, but every detail is verified. He explicitly names his sources or explains when he's using a pseudonym.
- **Restrained fury**: Carreyrou clearly believes Holmes and Balwani acted criminally, but he never editorializes. He lets the facts — emails, documents, testimony — build the case.
- **Narrative structure**: The book reads like a corporate thriller, with rising tension, dramatic confrontations, and a satisfying (if sobering) conclusion. It's organized chronologically but structured for maximum dramatic impact.
- **Human-centric**: Despite being about technology and fraud, the book focuses on the people — the whistleblowers who risked everything, the patients who were harmed, the journalists who pursued the story.

## Key Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Evaluating a Startup Investment
**Situation**: User is considering investing in a health tech startup with a charismatic founder and impressive claims.

**Solution**: Run the Seven-Stage Pattern (ref 1-core-framework) against them. Ask: Where's the independent verification? Can you tour the facility? What does the board actually know about the technology? The more secrecy, the less you should invest.

### Scenario 2: Joining a Fast-Growing Company
**Situation**: User has a job offer from a hot startup but notices warning signs.

**Solution**: Use the Due Diligence Checklist (ref 3-techniques). Ask employees below director level what it's really like. Pay attention to how the company treats questions — as healthy curiosity or as disloyalty. Trust your discomfort.

### Scenario 3: Suspecting Misconduct at Work
**Situation**: User sees something they believe is unethical or illegal at their company.

**Solution**: Follow the Whistleblower's Guide (ref 3-techniques). Document everything. Find an ally. Understand your legal protections. Go to the right authority. Prepare for retaliation. The Theranos whistleblowers were vindicated, but the path was hard.

### Scenario 4: Joining a Board
**Situation**: User has been asked to join the board of a startup and wants to be effective.

**Solution**: The Theranos board is a case study in what NOT to do. Don't be a decoration. Insist on technical briefings. Ask the hard questions. Make sure other board members have domain expertise. A board member's first duty is oversight, not support.

### Scenario 5: Understanding the Theranos Story
**Situation**: User wants to understand what happened at Theranos and why it matters.

**Solution**: Walk through the timeline: Holmes's founding story → the hype cycle → the culture of fear → the whistleblowers → the WSJ investigation → the collapse. The Theranos story is not just about one fraud — it's about a system that incentivized hype over substance and rewarded confidence over competence.
