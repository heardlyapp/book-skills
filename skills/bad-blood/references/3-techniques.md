# Techniques: Whistleblowing, Investigation, and Due Diligence

## Whistleblower's Guide

The book provides a powerful case study in how to come forward safely:

1. **Document everything** — Tyler Shultz saved emails, kept notes, and maintained a record of every concerning interaction. Documents are your best defense.
2. **Find allies** — Erika Cheung and Tyler Shultz found each other and corroborated each other's accounts. A single whistleblower is vulnerable; two are credible.
3. **Understand legal protections** — Regulatory agencies (FDA, CMS, SEC) have whistleblower programs. Know your rights before you speak.
4. **Go to the right authority** — Tyler and Erika went to regulators and journalists, not just internal HR (which was compromised at Theranos).
5. **Expect retaliation** — Theranos fought back aggressively. Whistleblowers were surveilled, threatened with lawsuits, and publicly attacked. Prepare mentally for this.
6. **Tell the truth, only the truth** — The power of the Theranos whistleblowers was their credibility. They stuck to facts. They never exaggerated. Their testimony held up under scrutiny.

> **Case: Tyler Shultz vs. His Grandfather** (Chapters 16, 20): Tyler Shultz worked at Theranos and discovered the technology didn't work. His grandfather, George Shultz, was on the board. Tyler faced an impossible choice: tell the truth and betray his grandfather's trust, or stay silent and let patients be harmed. He chose to tell the truth. The family relationship was damaged but the fraud was exposed.
> **Key takeaway**: Whistleblowing often comes at a personal cost. Tyler lost his relationship with his grandfather temporarily. The question isn't "will this be easy?" but "can I live with myself if I stay silent?"

> **Case: Erika Cheung's Stand** (Chapters 18-19): Erika Cheung was a young lab associate who discovered that Theranos was reporting inaccurate test results to patients. She documented everything, saved emails, and reported the company to regulators. Despite threats from Theranos's lawyers, she cooperated with investigators. Her testimony was crucial in shutting down the lab.
>
> **Key takeaway**: The most powerful whistleblowers are not disgruntled employees — they're people who believe in the mission and refuse to watch it be betrayed.

## Journalistic Investigation Techniques

John Carreyrou's investigative approach offers a playbook for fact-checking any company:

1. **Cultivate sources** — Carreyrou found former employees who were willing to talk. He protected their identities when needed
2. **Follow the document trail** — Internal emails, lab reports, regulatory filings
3. **Confirm denials** — Carreyrou always gave Theranos the chance to respond. Their responses (denials, threats, evasions) became evidence themselves
4. **Find independent experts** — He had scientists review Theranos's claims and patents
5. **Publish with corroboration** — The Wall Street Journal's story had multiple named sources and documented evidence

## The Due Diligence Checklist for Startups

Before investing in or joining a startup:

- [ ] Can I tour the facility and see the technology in operation?
- [ ] Are there independent, third-party validations of the core claims?
- [ ] Can I speak with engineers, not just the founder?
- [ ] Does the board have relevant technical expertise?
- [ ] What happens when employees question decisions?
- [ ] Is the company's financial information transparent?
- [ ] Are the company's claims consistent over time, or do they shift?
- [ ] Does the company publish research or keep everything "proprietary"?
- [ ] How does the company respond to criticism — with data or with attacks?
