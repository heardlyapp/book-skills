# Anti-Patterns — Calling All Minds

## The Central Error: "Just Follow the Instructions"

"If one of my projects failed, I would experiment for many hours until I got it to work." The opposite of the inventor's mindset is blind adherence to instructions. "Instructions are only guidelines."

### Mistake 1: "If the experiment didn't work, you did it wrong"
"Sometimes my students will come to me very upset because they followed the directions to an experiment perfectly, but it didn't produce the desired result." Failure is not a mistake — it's data. You "experiment with the experiment" to figure out what went wrong.

### Mistake 2: "You need to be well-rounded"
"Teachers and parents worry about the quirky kid who draws all day or the one who cares only about insects. They want kids to be well-rounded." Grandin argues the opposite: single-minded kids may grow up to create incredible things. Her own single-minded focus on animals and making things became the basis of her career.

### Mistake 3: "Only geniuses become inventors"
The book is full of counterexamples: Sam Houghton (age 5, two-broom design), Chester Greenwood (age 15, earmuffs), Becky Schroeder (age 10, Glo-sheet), Frank L. Gormley Sr. (orphan, no formal training, 25 patents). Invention is about persistence and curiosity, not IQ.

### Mistake 4: "Inventions are pure genius — they come in a flash"
"Genius is one percent inspiration and ninety-nine percent perspiration" (Edison). Grandin shows that most inventions are the result of trial and error, failure, and incremental improvement. Super Glue was discovered by accident. Velcro was inspired by burrs. Penicillin was a moldy petri dish.

### Mistake 5: "Inventing is about electronics and computers"
Grandin specifically pushes back: "You need to understand how a car works before you can invent a better one." The book focuses on paper, wood, string, pulleys, kites — physical things. "If we lose the ability to make things, we will lose a whole lot more."

### Mistake 6: "If you can't do it perfectly, don't try"
Grandin's father's toolbox had a folding yardstick with many hinges. "Partly opened, it looked like a fan, and fully opened, it became a sword." She didn't wait to be taught — she played, experimented, and learned through doing. The inventor's mindset starts with permission to try.
