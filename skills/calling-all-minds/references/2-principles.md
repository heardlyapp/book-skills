# Key Principles — Calling All Minds

## I. Make Things

"If I had to boil this book down, my message would be this: Make Things." Grandin's central thesis: hands-on making is the path to invention. "There is no substitute for real world experience and working with your hands." She wants kids to take things apart and put them back together.

## II. There Are No "Normal" Minds

Grandin describes herself as a visual thinker. Einstein thought in pictures too. Mathematicians see patterns. Musicians hear sequences. "Autism is not 'one size fits all.'" Different kinds of thinkers are essential for innovation. "There's no limit to the kinds of contributions we can make."

## III. Inventions Come From Connecting the Dots

"There is usually a fascinating story of how things get made by connecting the dots." Grandin traces paper from papyrus to Gutenberg to Fourdrinier machines to Linotype to Liquid Paper. Invention is building on what came before.

## IV. Patents Protect the Freedom to Invent

"The freedom to invent was linked with our country's founding principles." Grandin shows that patents preserve knowledge and protect inventors. The U.S. Patent Office is "a repository of knowledge" — anyone can look up any invention online.

## V. Necessity Is the Mother of Invention

Margaret Knight invented a paper bag machine because she saw a need. Sarah E. Goode invented the hideaway bed because city dwellers needed space. Richard Dudgeon invented the hydraulic jack to lift heavy machinery. "Where there is a need, there is an inventor."

## VI. Experiment With the Experiment

"Sometimes my students will come to me very upset because they followed the directions perfectly, but it didn't produce the desired result. I tell them: you have to experiment with the experiment!" Failure is part of the process. "If one of my projects failed, I would experiment for many hours until I got it to work."

## VII. Different Minds Are Essential for the Future

"We need all kinds of minds if we are going to figure out how to adapt." The future needs inventors who can cast iron, chemists creating new materials, storytellers, and people with autism who see the world differently. Grandin's squeeze machine — born from her autistic need for deep pressure — is the living proof.
