# Techniques — Calling All Minds

## How to Think Like an Inventor (Based on Temple Grandin)

### Technique 1: Think in Pictures
Grandin is a visual thinker. She can "test run equipment in my imagination" — seeing how machines work in 3D before building them. "If I can picture something, I can understand it. And then I can create it." Einstein thought the same way: "I very rarely think in words at all."

### Technique 2: Take Things Apart
Grace Murray Hopper took apart seven alarm clocks as a child. Grandin took apart her father's retractable measuring tape to understand the spring mechanism. "You need to understand how a car works before you can invent a better one."

### Technique 3: Read Patents
Grandin loves looking up patents online. "You can easily find any invention; just type in 'Google patent' and enter key words." She uses her grandfather's patent (US2383460A for autopilot) as an example. Patents are a repository of knowledge — study what came before.

### Technique 4: Connect the Dots Across History
Grandin traces paper from Egyptian papyrus → Gutenberg's press → Fourdrinier's continuous paper machine → Linotype → Liquid Paper. She shows how each invention builds on earlier ones. The technique: study the whole chain, not just the final product.

### Technique 5: The "Necessity" Test
Ask: "What problem needs solving?" Margaret Knight's paper bag machine solved the need for flat-bottomed bags. Sarah E. Goode's hideaway bed solved the need for space in cramped apartments. "Where there is a need, there is an inventor."

### Technique 6: Experiment With the Experiment
When a student's project fails: "I tell them the same thing: you have to experiment with the experiment!" Don't follow instructions blindly — test assumptions, try variations, and accept that failure is part of the process.

### Technique 7: Turn Your Difference Into Your Strength
Grandin's autism gave her the ability to see the world from an animal's perspective, leading to revolutionary cattle-handling systems. Her need for deep pressure led to the Squeeze Machine, which has helped thousands of autistic people. "There's no limit to what you can achieve if you have someone who believes in you."
