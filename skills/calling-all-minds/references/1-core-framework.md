# Core Framework — Calling All Minds

## Introduction: Temple Grandin's Story

Grandin introduces herself as a visual thinker and a person on the autism spectrum. "I organize the world through pictures, and my mind references words through series of visual images." She was a late talker, hated being hugged, and was teased as "tape recorder." But her mother and school headmaster encouraged her passion for animals and making things. Her grandfather John C. Purves, co-inventor of the autopilot for planes, was her greatest role model.

Her message: "Make Things." She wants kids to put down screens and build with their hands. "If I had to boil this book down, my message would be this: Make Things." She cites Grace Murray Hopper (inventor of COBOL) who took apart seven alarm clocks as a child — "That's usually a sign that a kid is headed for the laboratory."

## Chapter 1: Things Made of Paper

**History of paper and printing:** Paper comes from "papyrus" used in ancient Egypt. Johannes Gutenberg invented movable type in the 15th century — creating type from metal molds, an oil-based ink, and a press. Before Gutenberg: ~30,000 printed books in Europe. Fifty years later: 10-12 million. "Movable type was like the internet of its time."

**The Linotype machine:** Ottmar Mergenthaler (born 1854, apprenticed to a watchmaker) invented a machine that combined casting type and printing. "The operator typed our names into the machine, and it produced a warm metallic slab." 8,000 machines in use by 1901, 70,000 by 1954.

**The typewriter and QWERTY:** Christopher Sholes patented the "Type Writer Machine" in 1868. The QWERTY layout came from studying letter pairs to prevent jamming. Beulah Louise Henry ("Lady Edison") patented improvements. Bette Nesmith Graham invented correction fluid (Liquid Paper) in her kitchen, inspired by watching painters cover errors, sold it to Gillette for $40 million.

**Projects:** Handmade Paper (grind paper into slurry, dip splatter guards, dry), Paper Snowflake (fold and cut for symmetry), Crystal Snowflake (borax + boiling water + pipe cleaner), Kaleidoscope (Pringles can + acrylic mirrors + beads), Water Bomb (crayon-coated paper origami filled with water).

## Chapter 2: Levers and Pulleys

**Grandin's visual thinking:** She connected words with pictures from childhood. Einstein thought the same way: "I very rarely think in words at all." She uses this to design cattle-handling systems.

**The lever:** A simple machine that has existed since prehistoric times. Archimedes: "Give me a place to stand and a lever long enough and I will lift the world." Leverage = force + distance. Examples: seesaw, hammer, nutcracker, bottle opener.

**Patents and the first patent act:** The Founding Fathers passed the first patent act in 1790. Samuel Hopkins got the first U.S. patent for potash. Women couldn't own patents until the Married Women's Property Act of 1848. Mary Kies was the first woman granted a patent (weaving straw and silk). Only 20 patents awarded to women by 1940. Thomas Jennings (1821) was the first African American to receive a patent — for dry-scouring. Sarah E. Goode invented the "hideaway bed" (1885), inspiring the Murphy bed.

**Inventions through necessity:** Richard Dudgeon's hydraulic jack (1884). Frank L. Gormley Sr. — orphan, no formal training, patented 25 improvements on lifting jacks. "Often the best education is hands-on."

**Projects:** Jumping Jack (cardboard puppet with string — simplest form of leverage), Simple Wishing Well (bucket + pulley), Small Corral with Self-Closing Gate (a real problem Grandin solved on her farm).

## Chapter 3: Things Made of Wood

**Hands-on making:** Grandin's father's toolbox was more interesting than her mother's jewelry box. She loved retractable measuring tapes, folding yardsticks. "You need to understand how a car works before you can invent a better one."

**Inventions through accident:** Super Glue was discovered by accident in 1942. Velcro was inspired by burrs sticking to a dog's fur. Kevlar (Stephanie Kwolek) — lighter than metal, used in bulletproof vests.

**Projects:** Homemade Stilts, Sailboat (with weighted keel), Marionette (wooden puppet with strings), Papier-Mâché Puppet Head.

## Chapter 4: Things That Fly

**History of flight:** George Cayley (father of aviation) — understood lift and drag, built first glider. Wright brothers — wind tunnel testing. Philo Farnsworth invented television at 21, holding 130+ patents.

**Youngest inventors:** Chester Greenwood (age 15) — earmuffs. Louis Braille (age 15) — braille. Becky Schroeder (age 10) — Glo-sheet for writing in the dark. Sam Houghton (age 5) — two-broom sweeping device.

**Projects:** Simple Paper Airplane, Complex Paper Airplane, Kite (with bridle design), Simple Helicopter, Parachute.

## Chapter 5: Optical Illusions

**How the eye works:** "Your eyes take in light, convert it to information for your brain, and then your brain interprets the information." The brain fills in gaps and makes assumptions. This is why illusions work.

**Ames Trapezoidal Window:** A window-shaped object that appears to swing back and forth but is actually rotating. Invented by Adelbert Ames Jr. in 1934. "The brain assumes the window has right angles because we've seen thousands of windows with right angles."

**Stereoscope and 3-D vision:** Each eye sees a slightly different image; the brain combines them. Charles Wheatstone invented the stereoscope in 1838. Oliver Wendell Holmes (Oliver Wendell Holmes Sr.) created a popular version. View-Master (1939) brought 3-D to the public.

**Projects:** Ames Trapezoidal Window, Stereoscope, Ames Illusion Room, Solar System Diorama.

## Epilogue: The Squeeze Machine

Grandin's most personal invention. As an autistic child, she craved deep pressure but couldn't tolerate being hugged. She observed cattle being squeezed in a chute and realized it calmed them. At age 18, she built her own "squeeze machine" — two padded boards that applied pressure. "Being in it gives me a feeling of calm. It reduces my anxiety and helps me fall asleep." Her high school science teacher, Mr. Carlock, encouraged her to build it. "There's no limit to what you can achieve if you have someone who believes in you."

This chapter ties the book together: different minds create different solutions. Grandin's autism wasn't a limitation — it gave her a unique perspective that led to inventions no one else would have thought of.
