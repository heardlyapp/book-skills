# Anti-Patterns — Low Expectations, Irrelevant Curriculum, and Giving Up

## The Central Fallacy

The most dangerous assumption: that some children cannot learn. The children of Yamacraw had been written off by everyone. They were not unintelligent. They had been failed by a system that never tried to reach them. The label "unteachable" is an excuse for not doing the work.

> **Case: The Labeled Children** (Throughout): The official records describe the Yamacraw children as slow, disruptive, incapable. These labels are self-fulfilling. Conroy ignores them. He treats his students as capable. They respond by becoming capable.
> **Key takeaway**: Labels place ceilings on children. Refuse the labels. Treat every child as capable of learning. They will rise — or fall — to your expectations.

> **Case: The Decades-Old Textbooks** (Early): The textbooks in Conroy's classroom are from the 1940s. They contain racist content. They are useless. The school board refuses to replace them. Conroy throws them out.
> **Key takeaway**: Outdated materials are worse than no materials. They teach children that they do not matter enough to deserve better. Throw them out.

> **Case: The Bureaucrats** (Throughout): School board officials visit the island twice a year. They know nothing about the children. They care about compliance, not education. Conroy's real crime is that he made them look bad by doing what they should have been doing.
> **Key takeaway**: The people who block change are not always evil. They are often just comfortable. Your refusal to accept the status quo threatens their comfort. That is their problem, not yours.
