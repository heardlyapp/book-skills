# Core Framework: The Hedge Fund Structure and Evolution

> Source: More Money Than God, Introduction & Chapters 1–3

## The A.W. Jones Invention

Alfred Winslow Jones invented the first hedge fund in 1949, almost by accident. A former Marxist, State Department vice-consul, anti-Nazi operative, and Fortune journalist, Jones at age 48 raised $100,000 from four friends and put up $40,000 of his own to try his hand at investing. The result was the most remarkable investment record of the era: by 1968 he had racked up a cumulative return of 5,000 percent, meaning a $10,000 investment in 1949 was worth $480,000. In the five years to 1965 he returned 325 percent, dwarfing the 225 percent return on the hottest mutual fund, and in the ten years to 1965 he earned almost twice as much as his nearest competitor.

**Case: Jones's four-part innovation.** Jones combined four elements that had never been assembled together. First, a performance fee: he kept 20% of profits, sharpening incentives for his team. Second, regulatory flexibility: he avoided registration as an investment company, preserving the ability to shift strategies. Third, hedging: he balanced long positions in promising stocks with short sales of unpromising ones, insulating the portfolio from general market swings. Fourth, leverage: having hedged out market risk, he borrowed money to magnify his bets. Jones described this as "speculative means for conservative ends" — you could buy more good stocks without taking as much risk.

**Case: The segment manager model.** Jones pioneered an internal structure where individual managers ran separate "segments" of the portfolio. This allowed him to retain top talent by giving them autonomy and a share of the profits from their own segment. This model was later adopted and refined by Julian Robertson at Tiger Management and became the standard for multi-manager hedge funds.

## The Efficient Market Debate

From the mid-1960s to the mid-1980s, academic finance held that hedge funds succeeded mainly by luck. The efficient market hypothesis argued that all available information is already priced in, so beating the market consistently is impossible. As Michael Jensen of Columbia Business School argued in 1984, the few money managers who appear to defy the random walk are merely lucky coin flippers.

**Case: Buffett's coin-flipping rejoinder.** At a 1984 Columbia Business School debate, Warren Buffett offered a devastating counterargument. He asked the audience to imagine a national coin-flipping contest where 225 million Americans flip coins, and those who flip tails drop out. After 20 rounds, 215 people would have flipped 20 straight heads — and they'd write books about their technique. But if 40 of those 215 winners came from the same zoo, Buffett argued, something else was clearly going on. He then showed that all nine alumni of Ben Graham's value-investing "village" had beaten the market — not by luck, but by sharing a common approach.

**Case: The academic flip.** Starting in the 1980s, finance academics began acknowledging market inefficiencies. Scott Irwin discovered that commodity prices moved in trends — but a hedge fund called Commodities Corporation had reached the same conclusion twenty years earlier. The 1987 crash further undermined efficient-market dogma: when the market's valuation of corporate America changes by 20% in a single day, the theory is hard to defend. By the 1990s, academics were not only acknowledging hedge funds but joining them — Cliff Asness (Fama's student), Myron Scholes, Robert Merton, Andrei Shleifer all moved from academia to hedge funds.

## The Institutional Shift

The biggest effect of the new inefficient-market consensus was that institutional investors gained a license to allocate capital to hedge funds. Before 1987, most hedge fund capital came from wealthy individuals. After, endowments led the charge.

**Case: David Swensen and Yale.** Swensen, the Yale endowment chief, argued that systematic market patterns (like the value premium identified by Fama and French) could be exploited by hedge funds in a diversifying way. He invested in funds like Farallon, which in 2002 swooped into Indonesia to buy the country's largest bank despite a currency collapse, political revolution, and Islamist extremism. Swensen's approach was copied by endowments nationwide.

## The Leverage Double-Edged Sword

Leverage gives hedge funds the ammunition to trade in volume and render prices more efficient. But it also makes them vulnerable — when trades move against them, they can burn through capital at lightning speed.

**Case: The 1994 bond meltdown.** The Fed announced a tiny 0.25 percentage point interest rate rise, and the bond market went into a spin. Leveraged hedge funds had been wrong-footed and began dumping positions. The turmoil spread from the US to Japan, Europe, and emerging markets. Several hedge funds sank; for a few hours, Bankers Trust itself seemed threatened. This foreshadowed LTCM's collapse four years later and the 2008 crisis.
