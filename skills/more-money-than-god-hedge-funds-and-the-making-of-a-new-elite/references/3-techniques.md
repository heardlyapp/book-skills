# Techniques: Hedge Fund Strategies Across Eras

> Source: More Money Than God, Chapters 7–16

## 1. Long/Short Equity

The original A.W. Jones strategy. Buy promising stocks and simultaneously sell short unpromising ones. The long positions generate gains in bull markets; the short positions provide a hedge against general market declines. Net exposure can range from net long (bullish) to net short (bearish) to market-neutral (balanced).

**Case: Julian Robertson's stock picking.** Robertson made money on both sides. In 1984, when the S&P 500 rose just 6.3%, Tiger returned 20.2% — more than half from short investments. His analyst Patrick Duff identified Prime Motor Inns as a fraud, shorted it at $28, and watched it fall to $1. Robertson coaxed Wall Street analysts into divulging their best short ideas over the phone, since they were too afraid to publish sell recommendations: "I know you all consider these companies to be your children, but just call me with your least favorite child."

**Case: Cliff Asness and AQR.** Asness, a Fama protege from University of Chicago, applied academic factor research to hedge fund investing. His "value" strategy — buying cheap value stocks and shorting expensive growth stocks — systematically exploited the value premium that Fama and French had documented. By 2007, AQR ran $38 billion.

## 2. Global Macro

Betting on entire economies, currencies, interest rates, and commodities based on a top-down view of the world. Requires understanding of geopolitical dynamics, central bank behavior, and cross-market linkages.

**Case: Soros at the Plaza Accord.** Soros shorted the dollar in 1985 not because of economic data but because he understood the Reagan administration's political determination to weaken the dollar. He analyzed the banking system, interest rate trends, and the psychology of speculators already riding the dollar upward. After the Plaza Hotel meeting, when most traders would have cashed out, Soros recognized that the reflexive cycle had just begun — the dollar's initial fall would create momentum for further declines.

**Case: Soros and Druckenmiller's pound trade.** Stan Druckenmiller, Soros's lieutenant, convinced Soros in 1992 to bet £10 billion against the Bank of England. The trade combined fundamental analysis (the pound was overvalued in the ERM), political understanding (the UK government would prioritize domestic growth over currency stability), and an appreciation of reflexivity (devaluation would feed on itself). The British government's own interventions created liquidity for Soros to exit at excellent prices.

**Soros's method:** "Invest first, investigate later." He read the broad sweep of events, made a directional bet, and only then did the detailed analysis to confirm his thesis. He was willing to move on incomplete information — because by the time you're certain, the opportunity has passed.

## 3. Quantitative / Algorithmic Trading

Using mathematical models and computer programs to identify and exploit statistical patterns in market data. The strategy depends on data, computing power, and scientific talent.

**Case: The D.E. Shaw approach.** David Shaw, a computer scientist from Columbia, built a quant operation that grew to account for 5% of daily NYSE turnover by 1994. His team systematically cleaned financial data for errors — an academic would call to say they'd discovered a profitable anomaly, and Shaw's team would find it was caused by a misreported price (e.g., IBM at $16 instead of $61). Shaw's quants developed an options-pricing model superior to the Black-Scholes framework, exploited misalignments in Japanese equity derivatives, and became dominant in options market-making.

**The Bezos connection.** Jeffrey Bezos worked at D.E. Shaw in the early 1990s, where he was the firm's youngest senior vice president. He rolled out sleeping bags to stay overnight. The experience shaped his approach to data-driven decision-making at Amazon.

**Case: Tudor's Wadhwani system.** Paul Tudor Jones hired Sushil Wadhwani, a Bank of England economist, to build a trading system that mimicked the best discretionary traders. Wadhwani watched Jones and Mark Heffernan trade, interviewed them about their reasoning, and built models that replicated their eclectic thinking. The key insight: it wasn't enough to identify an anomaly — you also needed a "trigger" (an upcoming election, a chart pattern, a change in institutional behavior) that would cause the anomaly to correct.

## 4. Relative Value Arbitrage

Exploiting price discrepancies between related securities that should trade at similar prices. Requires patience and leverage since the mispricings are usually small.

**Case: LTCM's Royal Dutch/Shell trade.** Royal Dutch Petroleum (Netherlands) and Shell Transport (Britain) represented claims on the same cash flows, but the British shares traded at a discount to the Dutch ones. LTCM bought the cheap British shares and shorted the expensive Dutch ones, collecting dividends and indifferent to the direction of the overall market. They played this across Denmark, Japan, and other markets.

## 5. Event-Driven and Distressed

Betting on corporate events: mergers, bankruptcies, restructurings, or regulatory changes.

**Case: Robertson's merger plays.** Robertson targeted companies that were also attractive to corporate acquirers. In 1985, Tiger bought Empire Airlines at $9 and was taken out at $15; bought Aviall (aviation parts distributor) at $12.50 and was taken out at $25. That year Tiger was up 51.4% after fees.

## 6. Statistical Arbitrage

Modern evolution of Steinhardt's block-trading strategy. Computers monitor thousands of stock pairs (Ford/GM, American/United Airlines, International Paper/Georgia-Pacific) looking for temporary divergences. When one stock in a pair falls relative to its peer without any news, it's likely an institutional seller — buy the fallen stock, short the peer, profit when they reconverge.

**Case: Morgan Stanley's APT unit.** Morgan's secretive Analytical Proprietary Trading group pioneered pairs trading in the 1980s. David Shaw improved the approach by applying computer science techniques to search for anomalies more systematically, and built a business that his successors couldn't replicate because they lacked his computational sophistication.
