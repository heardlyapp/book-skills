# Voice and Application: Mallaby's Narrative and Lessons for Today's Investor

> Source: More Money Than God, Introduction, Chapters 5, 12, 16, Epilogue

## Mallaby's Narrative Voice

Sebastian Mallaby, a Council on Foreign Relations fellow, writes with the perspective of a political economist turned financial historian. His central argument is that hedge funds are misunderstood — they are neither the gods of finance that their most successful managers imagine themselves to be, nor the villains that politicians like Mahathir Mohamad denounce.

**The balanced view:** Mallaby presents hedge funds as vehicles for "loners and contrarians" whose independence gives them a structural advantage over banks. But he also documents their capacity for self-destruction. His verdict after the 2008 crisis: "The cataclysm has indeed shown that the financial system is broken, but it has not actually shown that hedge funds are the problem."

**Mallaby's technique:** He weaves biography into financial history. Each chapter focuses on a single manager or event, building character through telling details. A.W. Jones was born "in the ninth hour of the ninth day of the ninth month." Simons "took orders from nobody, seldom wore socks." Robertson spoke in a slow Carolina drawl: "Pah-wah-ful, Bob." These humanizing details make the technical financial content accessible.

**Case: The Paradox of Genius** — Mallaby explores a recurring theme: the best traders often cannot explain their own success. Paul Tudor Jones's analyst Peter Borish mapped the 1980s market against 1929 charts. Seeing the two lines looked similar, Jones predicted a crash. But Borish admitted to massaging the data to make the lines fit, and his prediction was for spring 1988 — the crash hit October 1987. If Jones had followed Borish's timing, he'd have been wiped out. Jones succeeded for reasons he couldn't articulate. Mallaby quotes tennis coach Vic Braden: "Out of all the research that we've done with top players, we haven't found a single player who is consistent in knowing and explaining exactly what he does." The same applies to top hedge fund managers — their edge often operates below conscious reasoning.

**Case: The Swensen Model for Institutional Investors** — David Swensen of Yale transformed endowment investing by applying hedge fund principles to institutional portfolio allocation. His approach: identify strategies with expected positive alpha through systematic patterns, emphasize diversification benefits from uncorrelated returns, and find managers with genuine edge beyond good marketing. Swensen invested in Farallon, which in 2002 bought Indonesia's largest bank despite a currency collapse, political revolution, and Islamist extremism scaring most Westerners out. The willingness to go where others wouldn't was the source of Farallon's edge. This "Yale model" was copied by endowments nationwide.

## Practical Application: Hedge Fund Thinking for Personal Investors

**1. Find your edge.** An edge is something you understand better than most people. It could be your industry knowledge, your local market, your professional network. Soros's edge was his theory of reflexivity; Robertson's was his network; Simons's was his mathematical talent. You need some version of this — an informational or analytical advantage.

**2. Size your bets according to conviction and risk.** Soros was willing to bet $720 million (more than his fund's equity) when he saw the dollar reversal approaching. But he also had stop-loss mentalities — he defined in advance at what point he'd abandon half the trade. The lesson: bet big when your edge is clear, but never bet so much that one loss wipes you out.

**3. Use high-water mark discipline.** Hedge fund managers don't get paid on recovered losses — they get paid only on new profits above the previous peak. Apply this to your own investing: don't increase risk to "make back" what you've lost. Reset your base and think about risk from the new level.

**4. Stay paranoid.** The common trait among the longest-surviving hedge fund managers (Soros, Robertson, Simons) was discomfort. Soros's back would seize up before a crisis. Robertson stayed on the phone constantly, testing every thesis. Simons hired the best mathematicians because he knew the market would find and destroy any weakness in their models.

**5. Understand correlation during crises.** Normal diversification is not crisis diversification. When markets panic, everything becomes correlated. The best hedge against this is not a wider range of assets — it's cash, deep understanding of your positions, and leverage that you can survive.

**6. Avoid the trap of overconfidence.** LTCM's partners were Nobel laureates who had created the options-pricing models that powered modern finance. They believed they were the smartest people in the room. This intellectual arrogance made them blind to the limitations of their models. As Mallaby notes: "The smarter the people, the more elaborate the rationalization for ignoring tail risk."

**Case: The 2008 Crisis Verdict** — Mallaby argues that hedge funds were not the problem in 2008. Banks that were "too big to fail" took risks because they expected bailouts. Wall Street traders had heads-I-win-tails-you-lose compensation. By contrast, hedge funds: received zero direct taxpayer assistance, had high-water marks requiring lost fees to be earned back before performance fees resume, kept managers' own capital at risk in the fund, and faced no government safety net — making them more paranoid about leverage. The average hedge fund borrowed 1-2x capital, while Goldman Sachs and Lehman were leveraged 30-to-1.

## Mallaby's Final Verdict

Hedge funds are not the problem with modern finance. The real systemic risks come from:
- Banks that are "too big to fail" and take risks because they expect bailouts
- Trader compensation that rewards upside without punishing downside
- Financial institutions that rely on short-term funding and government safety nets

Hedge funds, by contrast, have high-water marks, manager capital at risk, no government backstop, and lower leverage. Mallaby's policy prescription: regulate the largest, most leveraged hedge funds, but recognize that the vast majority are "safe to fail, even if they are not fail-safe."
