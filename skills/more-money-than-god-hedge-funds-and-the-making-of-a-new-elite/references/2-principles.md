# Key Principles: The Great Hedge Fund Managers and Their Philosophies

> Source: More Money Than God, Chapters 4–9

## George Soros: Reflexivity and the Macro Bet

George Soros arrived at the London School of Economics in 1949, a Jewish survivor of the Nazi occupation who had hidden with false Christian identity and seen corpses in the streets. He was inspired by Karl Popper's philosophy that humans cannot know absolute truth — we can only grope at it through trial and error. Soros applied this to finance, creating a theory he called "reflexivity."

**Soros's insight:** Investor perceptions don't just react to reality — they change reality. When investors are bullish about a stock, their buying drives the price up, which allows the company to raise capital cheaply, which improves its performance, which justifies more bullishness. This creates a feedback loop that drives prices away from equilibrium, producing boom-bust sequences.

**Case: The Plaza Accord (1985).** Soros spent $720 million shorting the U.S. dollar (more than his fund's entire equity). He agonized through a $20 million loss in September 1985, but held his conviction. When the five major powers signed the Plaza Accord on September 22, pledging to push the dollar down, Soros made $30 million overnight. Rather than take profits, he doubled down, calling brokers in Hong Kong to buy more yen. This single trade cemented his reputation.

**Case: Breaking the Bank of England (1992).** Soros spotted that the British pound was overvalued within the European Exchange Rate Mechanism. He borrowed and sold £10 billion worth of sterling, betting the Bank of England would be forced to devalue. When Black Wednesday arrived on September 16, 1992, the UK pulled out of the ERM and the pound crashed. Soros reportedly made £1 billion in profit. The British government lost £3.4 billion defending the currency.

**Soros's motto:** "Invest first, investigate later." He didn't believe in perfect information — he believed in developing a conceptual edge and acting decisively before all the evidence was in.

## Julian Robertson: The Tiger Network

Robertson launched Tiger Management in 1980 at age 48 after befriending A.W. Jones's son-in-law and learning the hedge fund mechanics over lunches. Between 1980 and 1998, Tiger earned an average of 31.7% per year after fees, trouncing the S&P 500's 12.7%.

**The Robertson method:** He was a stock picker who believed in deep fundamental analysis, but his real edge was his network. Robertson maintained a giant Rolodex (literally, two wagon-wheel-sized rotating card files) of CEOs, industry contacts, and Tiger investors. When an analyst pitched an idea, Robertson would call three contacts who worked at that company to test the thesis.

**Case: The charm offensive.** Robertson hired Michael Bills from Goldman Sachs by deploying Lew Lehrman (financier and philanthropist) and Tom Wolfe to call Bills. Wolfe and Bills bonded over Wolfe's book "The Right Stuff" and Bills's father's military pilot career. Within a 20-minute call, Goldman Sachs had lost its man.

**Case: The Tiger cubs.** Robertson's protégés spun off into their own funds — by 2008, 36 former Tiger employees managed $100 billion collectively as "Tiger cubs." These cubs (including Andreas Halvorsen's Viking Global, Lee Ainslie's Maverick Capital, Chase Coleman's Tiger Global) beat not only the market but also other hedge funds, proving Robertson's edge was transferable.

**Robertson's style:** Charismatic and intense. He conducted Tiger retreats in Idaho's Sawtooth Mountains where employees had to lash logs into rafts and plunge into icy water. The emotional payoffs were extreme: "That is the be-yest idea Ah ever saw" could make a young analyst's year; "That is the dumbest idea Ah ever heard" could shrivel a six-foot Wall Street alpha male.

## Michael Steinhardt: The Block Trader

Steinhardt made his fortune by exploiting a footnote in the efficient market hypothesis — the assumption of perfect liquidity. When a large pension fund wanted to dump a block of shares, it couldn't find a buyer without offering a discount. Steinhardt systematically milked these discounts.

**Case: The Ford trade.** When Ford stock dipped illogically after a large-block sale, Steinhardt bought it, knowing the discount reflected nothing but a pension fund's need for cash. He held until the price reverted. This was not insider trading — it was understanding that institutional constraints create predictable price distortions.

**Steinhardt's temperament:** Relentlessly aggressive. He reduced underlings to sobs. When one said "All I want to do is kill myself," Steinhardt responded "Can I watch?" This intensity was matched by a paranoid discipline about risk.

## Paul Tudor Jones: The Rock-and-Roll Cowboy

Jones came to prominence for calling the 1987 crash. His analyst Peter Borish had mapped the 1980s market against 1929 charts, but Mallaby notes the explanation was oversimplified — Borish predicted the crash for spring 1988, not October 1987. Jones succeeded through a combination of chart reading, macro instinct, and position sizing that he himself could not fully explain.

**Case: The Robin Hood Foundation.** Jones created one of the first "venture philanthropies" to fight poverty in New York. It identified innovative charities, set demanding benchmarks for progress, and paid for performance — applying hedge fund metrics to charity.

## Jim Simons and Renaissance Technologies: The Code Breakers

Simons was a mathematician and code breaker who had won the Oswald Veblen Prize (geometry's highest honor), worked at the Pentagon's Institute for Defense Analyses, and got fired for opposing the Vietnam War. Between 1989 and 2006, his Medallion Fund returned 39% per year on average.

**Case: The ghost-hunting method.** Simons hired mathematicians, physicists, astronomers — never economists. They applied code-breaking algorithms to hunt "ghost patterns" in market data — faint traces of structure in what appeared to be random noise. Henry Laufer discovered that in the period after a news release, a commodity or currency would jigger in patterns invisible to the naked eye but statistically significant across thousands of observations.

**Case: Medallion's resilience.** In 1994 (when Steinhardt lost billions in the bond crash), Medallion returned 71% after fees. In 2008 (the financial crisis), it returned 80% after fees and almost 160% before fees. The quant machine thrived on volatility that destroyed discretionary traders.

**Simons's rule:** "He took orders from nobody, seldom wore socks, and got fired from the Pentagon's code-cracking center after denouncing his bosses' Vietnam policy."
