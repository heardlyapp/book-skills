## Practical Techniques

### 1. The Second Opinion Rule

When experts disagree, seek a third. Garfield's doctors were unanimous in their approach — and they were unanimously wrong. Consensus is not a substitute for evidence.

### 2. The Question Authority Protocol

When an authority figure says "I know best" without showing their evidence, be suspicious. Expertise without transparency is dangerous.

> **Case: Dr. Bliss Refuses Help** (Chapter 9): Other doctors offered to consult. Bliss refused. He was in charge and would not share control. His ego prevented the collective expertise that might have saved the president.
> **Key takeaway**: When an expert refuses input from others, it is a sign of insecurity, not confidence.

### 3. The Hygiene Awareness Check

Garfield died because doctors did not wash their hands. The simplest protocols — checklists, basic hygiene, sterilization — save more lives than the most advanced treatments.

### 4. The Delusion Detection

Guiteau was clearly delusional — he believed God had told him to kill the president, and that the "Stalwart" Republicans would reward him. But in a system where every office seeker believed they were entitled, Guiteau was an extreme case of a common pathology.

> **Case: Guiteau's Trial** (Epilogue): Guiteau's defense was insanity. The prosecution argued he was simply evil. The trial was a circus — Guiteau repeatedly interrupted, shouted at the judge, and insisted he was a patriot. He was convicted and hanged. A modern evaluation would likely find him severely mentally ill.
> **Key takeaway**: The line between madness and evil is not always clear. History judged Guiteau as a murderer — but his actions were driven by a mind that could not perceive reality.
