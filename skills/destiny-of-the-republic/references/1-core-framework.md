## The Murder Framework: A Perfect Storm of Madness, Medicine, and Politics

Millard's book weaves three parallel stories that converge in Garfield's death: Charles Guiteau's delusional pursuit of a political appointment, the primitive state of medicine in 1880s America, and the tragedy of a good man killed by his doctors.

**The Core Framework: Garfield was shot but not killed by the bullet — he was killed by his doctors.** The bullet lodged in his back, missing all vital organs. Had the doctors left it alone, Garfield would likely have survived. Instead, Dr. Willard Bliss probed the wound with unwashed fingers and instruments, introducing the infection that killed him.

> **Case: The Shooting of President Garfield** (Prologue): On July 2, 1881, Charles Guiteau shot Garfield in a Washington D.C. train station. The bullet entered Garfield's back, fractured a rib, and lodged near his pancreas. Garfield was conscious and stable. He said to the crowd: "My God! What is this?" He was taken to the White House — expected to survive.
> **Key takeaway**: The assassination attempt itself was not immediately fatal. Garfield had a chance. The tragedy is what happened next.

> **Case: The Doctors Who Killed a President** (Chapter 8): Dr. Willard Bliss took charge of Garfield's care. He repeatedly probed the wound with unwashed fingers and instruments trying to find the bullet. Alexander Graham Bell invented a metal detector to find the bullet — but Bliss refused to use it properly. The wound became infected. Garfield died 79 days after being shot, not from the bullet but from sepsis caused by his doctors.
> **Key takeaway**: Garfield's death is a case study in the danger of overconfident doctors. Bliss was certain he was right. His certainty killed the president.
