# The Three Tragedies — Core Framework

The book is organized around three intersecting stories: a promising president, a delusional assassin, and a medical system that failed.

**Case: The President Who Should Have Lived.** James Garfield was shot in the back. The bullet lodged in his abdomen. But it did not hit his spine, major blood vessels, or any vital organ. By modern standards, he should have survived. His death was caused not by the bullet but by the treatment.

**Case: The Assassin Who Believed He Was God's Instrument.** Charles Guiteau was mentally ill. He believed God had chosen him to kill the president. He stalked Garfield for weeks before shooting him. After the shooting, he calmly hailed a cab and drove to a police station to announce his deed.

**Case: The Doctor Who Killed His Patient.** Dr. Willard Bliss was one of the most respected physicians in America. He believed he was saving the president. But his treatments — probing the wound with unwashed fingers, introducing infection — killed Garfield. Good intentions were not enough.

**Case: The Nation's Grief.** Garfield's body lay in state in the Capitol Rotunda. The funeral procession was the longest in American history. The nation mourned not just a president but a man who could have been great.

**Case: The Medical Lesson.** Garfield's death led to a national conversation about medical practices. Within a decade, antiseptic procedures became standard. Garfield's death saved countless lives.
