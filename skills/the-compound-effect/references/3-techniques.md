# Techniques — The Compound Effect

## How to Apply the Compound Effect (Based on Darren Hardy)

### Technique 1: The Scorecard
For one week, carry a notebook. Every choice — what you eat, how long you work, how you spend your evenings — write it down. Categorize: positive (+), negative (-), or neutral (=). At end of week, analyze the pattern. "You can't change what you don't measure." This is the foundational technique; everything else depends on it.

### Technique 2: The Replacement Strategy
"You can't just stop a bad habit. You have to replace it with a good one." Example: instead of "stop eating junk food," replace with "eat an apple when craving hits." Instead of "stop watching TV," replace with "read 10 pages of a book." Hardy: the empty space will be filled regardless — fill it intentionally.

### Technique 3: Bookend Your Days
Morning: wake up early, mindset preparation (10 pages of a book or 30 min audio), exercise. Evening: review the day, plan tomorrow, disconnect from screens. "How you start and end your day determines everything in between."

### Technique 4: The Pump Well
Accept that the first 80% of effort produces zero visible results. "You have to pump the well before the water comes." At first: nothing. Then: muddy water. Then: clear water. Most quit at the muddy stage. The technique: expect the delay. Plan for it. Don't let it surprise you.

### Technique 5: The Vice Check
List every bad habit honestly. Then rank them by impact. Kill the biggest one first — not all at once. Hardy: "Don't try to change everything. That's a recipe for failure. Pick one. Dominate it. Then pick the next."

### Technique 6: The Accountability Partner
Tell someone your goal. Report daily. Hardy: "Public declaration creates a powerful psychological commitment." The partner doesn't need to coach you — they just need to ask: "Did you do it?"

### Technique 7: The 30-Day Test
Commit to a new habit for 30 days — no exceptions. After 30 days, reassess. "Anyone can do anything for 30 days." The test removes the fear of "forever" and replaces it with a manageable experiment.
