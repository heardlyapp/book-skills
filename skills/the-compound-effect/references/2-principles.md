# Key Principles — The Compound Effect

## I. Small Actions + Consistency + Time = Radical Difference

The book's central formula. The magic penny: $10.7M on Day 31. The three friends: 125 cal/day difference = 67 lbs between them in 31 months. "The most challenging aspect of the Compound Effect is that we have to keep working away for a while, consistently and efficiently, before we can begin to see the payoff."

## II. Change Starts with 100% Ownership

"Everything in your life exists because you first made a choice about something." Not 99.9% ownership. Not 99.99%. 100%. "The admission of even a sliver of a percentage of lack of ownership is an invitation to get caught by any trap." Hardy's father's philosophy: "No excuses."

## III. You Can't Change What You Don't Measure

The scorecard: track every choice for a week. Soda, skipped workouts, Facebook minutes. Hardy: "What gets measured gets managed." The awareness alone shifts behavior. "Without a scorecard, you're playing blind."

## IV. Discipline Is Sacrificing Now for Later

The compound effect requires delayed gratification. Hardy's father: "Be the guy who says 'no.' It's no great achievement to go along with the crowd." The world pushes instant results — microwave, overnight shipping, lottery. Real success requires pushing back against that current.

## V. Habits Cannot Be Removed — Only Replaced

Hardy: "You can't just stop a bad habit. You have to replace it with a good one." The empty space created by removing a bad habit will be filled by something else — make sure it's intentional. Find the replacement behavior before you eliminate the old one.

## VI. You Are the Average of Your Environment

Jim Rohn: "You are the average of the five people you spend the most time with." Hardy extends this to your physical environment (cluttered desk), your media inputs (garbage in, garbage out), and your routines. "The influences you allow into your life determine the trajectory of your life."

## VII. Nothing Fails Like Success

"The restaurant was immaculate at first. Then success happened. They took it for granted. Eighteen months later, out of business." Complacency is the silent killer. "We need to recover our grandparents' work ethic." The moment you stop doing what made you successful is the moment decline begins.
