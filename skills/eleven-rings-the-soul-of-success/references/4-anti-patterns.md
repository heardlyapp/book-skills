## 6 Anti-Patterns of Conventional Coaching

### 1. "The Coach Must Control Everything"

Jackson did not micromanage. He empowered. The coach who tries to control every play, every practice, every player — burns out and alienates.

### 2. "The Best Player Must Have the Ball"

The most talented player wants the ball. But championship teams distribute the ball. The triangle offense is designed to prevent star dominance.

> **Case: Kobe's Learning Curve** (Chapter 11): Kobe Bryant arrived in the NBA believing he should shoot every time. Jackson had to teach him that passing was power. Kobe learned — and became a better player for it. But it took years.
> **Key takeaway**: The most talented person on the team is not always the solution. Often they are the problem that needs to be solved.

### 3. "More Practice = Better Results"

The NBA is 82 games plus playoffs. More practice does not produce better results — better-rested players do. Jackson's practice hours were among the lowest in the league.

### 4. "Treat Everyone the Same"

Jackson treated each player differently. Jordan got challenge. Pippen got respect. Rodman got freedom. Kobe got trust. Shaq got love. Fairness does not mean sameness.

### 5. "Emotion Must Be Suppressed"

Jackson allowed players to express anger, frustration, and sadness. He believed that suppressed emotion becomes poison. The 2004 Lakers imploded because Kobe and Shaq's conflict was never resolved.

> **Case: The Lakers' Dysfunction** (Chapter 18): The Kobe-Shaq feud destroyed the Lakers' 2004 season. Jackson could not mediate it because both players refused to engage. The lesson: conflicts must be addressed early, before they become unmanageable.
> **Key takeaway**: Ignoring conflict does not make it disappear. It makes it worse.

### 6. "Winning Justifies Everything"

Jackson won by building people, not by sacrificing them. He believed that how you win matters. A trophy won at the cost of human relationships is not worth having.
