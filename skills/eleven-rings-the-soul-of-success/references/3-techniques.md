## Practical Techniques

### 1. The Circle Practice

Before any group project, sit in a circle. Every member shares something personal. This is not a team-building exercise — it is the foundation of trust. Do it once and the dynamic changes.

### 2. The Reading Assignment

Give each team member a book that speaks to their personality and challenges. Jackson gave Jordan Zen books, Pippen books on leadership, Rodman books on self-discovery. Books are coaching tools.

> **Case: The Book for Rodman** (Chapter 12): Jackson gave Dennis Rodman a book about Native American spirituality. Rodman, who was struggling with identity and belonging, connected with it deeply. It was not a basketball book — it was a human book. Jackson coached Rodman through books when direct conversation was difficult.
> **Key takeaway**: Sometimes the most powerful coaching happens indirectly. A well-chosen book can reach a person in ways that direct conversation cannot.

### 3. The Silence Timeout

During timeouts, sometimes say nothing. Let players figure it out. Jackson would stand quietly, letting the players solve problems. This built ownership and self-reliance.

### 4. The Doing Nothing Practice

Schedule time with no agenda. No phone, no TV, no work. Jackson believed that boredom was productive — it allowed the mind to rest and creativity to surface.

> **Case: The Canceled Practice** (Chapter 16): During the Bulls' 72-10 season, Jackson canceled a practice the day after a loss. The media was outraged. The players were confused. But Jackson knew the team was exhausted. The day off restored their energy. They won the next 10 games.
> **Key takeaway**: The counterintuitive move — doing less when everyone expects more — is often the right one.
