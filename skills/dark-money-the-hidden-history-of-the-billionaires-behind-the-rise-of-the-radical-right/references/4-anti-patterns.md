# Anti-Patterns: Dark Money Traps

> Source: Dark Money, Chapters 11-14

## Thinking It's Both Sides Equally

Dark money is not symmetric. Mayer documents that the right's dark money infrastructure dwarfs the left's by an enormous margin.

> **Case: The Billionaire Gap** (Chapter 11): In the 2012 election, the top 100 conservative donors gave more than the top 100 liberal donors combined. The disparity is structural — the right is backed by industrial wealth, the left by smaller donors.

> **Case: The Koch Budget** (Chapter 11): The Koch network's spending in the 2012 cycle rivaled the Republican National Committee. They could outspend almost any opponent.

> **Key takeaway**: This is not a symmetric partisan game. One side has dramatically more anonymous money — and uses it systematically.

## Believing Disclosure Solves Everything

Transparency helps but unlimited spending is legal. The volume of money overwhelms the system.

> **Case: The Fundraising Trap** (Chapter 12): After Citizens United, even successful politicians spend 30-50% of their time fundraising. The system rewards those who can raise money over those who can govern.

## Ignoring State and Local Impact

Dark money's deepest influence may be at the state level — where ALEC drafts model legislation and Koch groups target state legislatures.

> **Case: The ALEC Machine** (Chapter 13): The American Legislative Exchange Council brings together corporate donors and state legislators to draft laws benefiting donors.

> **Key takeaway**: Dark money's most powerful impact is often invisible — shaping regulation, blocking legislation, and capturing state governments.
