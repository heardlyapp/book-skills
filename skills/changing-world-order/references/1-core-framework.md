## The Archetypal Big Cycle

Dalio identifies a repeating pattern across 500 years of empires. The cycle has three phases:

### Phase 1: The Rising

A new empire emerges. It has strong leadership, sound education, high innovation, growing competitiveness, low debt, and a rising share of world trade. Other empires are in decline. The rising empire accumulates wealth and military power. It eventually challenges the existing world order.

> **Case: The American Rise** (Chapter 2): The US rose from the early 1900s through WWII. It had high productivity, innovation, education, and a strong manufacturing base. It avoided the devastation of both world wars. By 1945, it was the dominant economic and military power, and the dollar became the world's reserve currency.

### Phase 2: The Peak

The empire is at its height. It has the world's reserve currency, the strongest military, and the most prosperous economy. But internally, signs of decline emerge: debt grows, wealth inequality increases, infrastructure decays, education quality declines.

> **Case: The British Peak** (Chapter 4): The British empire peaked in the late 1800s. London was the financial center of the world. The pound was the reserve currency. But debt from wars and an expensive empire, combined with rising competition from Germany and the US, began the decline.

### Phase 3: The Declining

The empire enters a self-reinforcing cycle of decline. High debt leads to more borrowing. Internal conflict divides the population. The empire overextends militarily. It loses its competitive edge. Eventually, it loses reserve currency status. The decline creates a new world order — and the cycle begins again.

> **Case: The Dutch Decline** (Chapter 3): The Dutch empire peaked around 1700. By the late 1700s, it had lost its competitive edge, its navy was outmatched by the British, and its financial system was weakened by debt. The guilder lost its reserve currency status. The British ascended.
> **Key takeaway**: The decline phase is the most dangerous for investors because it challenges every assumption about the existing order.

## The 8 Markers of Decline

Dalio identifies 8 measurable indicators that signal when an empire is in the decline phase: (1) High debt and deficits, (2) Large wealth and opportunity gaps, (3) Internal conflict around wealth and values, (4) Education and infrastructure decline, (5) External conflict, (6) Loss of reserve currency status, (7) Natural disasters and pandemics, (8) Leadership and governance decline.

> **Case: The US Scorecard** (Chapter 8): Dalio applies the 8 markers to the US today. The US scores poorly on debt, wealth gap, internal conflict, infrastructure, and education — but still strong on reserve currency status, military, and technology. Dalio's conclusion: the US is in the early-to-middle decline phase.
> **Key takeaway**: The US is not in sudden collapse — but it is following the same pattern as every previous empire in decline. The markers are visible. The question is how the transition will unfold.
