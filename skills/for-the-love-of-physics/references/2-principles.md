## 7 Principles of Physics Wonder

### 1. Wonder Comes First

Before the equation comes the awe. Lewin shows the phenomenon first — then explains it. The wonder is the hook.

### 2. Physics Is for Everyone

Lewin's MIT lectures were accessible to anyone — no prerequisites. Physics is not a secret language for specialists.

> **Case: The Van de Graaff Lecture** (Chapter 7): Lewin stands on a Van de Graaff generator — his hair stands on end. He explains electric fields. The demonstration is unforgettable. Anyone who saw it understood that electricity is real — not abstract.
> **Key takeaway**: Demonstrations make physics real. You do not need to understand the math to feel the force.

### 3. Energy Conservation Is the Deepest Principle

Energy cannot be created or destroyed. The pendulum ball cannot hit Lewin because energy is conserved. This is not a rule — it is the bedrock of physics.

### 4. Light Is a Wave — and a Particle

Light behaves as both a wave and a particle. This wave-particle duality is the strangest and most wonderful fact about the universe.

### 5. The Universe Is Knowable

The same laws that govern a falling apple govern the orbit of planets. Physics is universal. The cosmos follows rules we can discover.

### 6. Teaching Is Performance

Lewin treated each lecture as a performance — with demonstrations, drama, and humor. Physics teaching should be entertaining.

### 7. Equations Are Poetry

F = ma is not just a formula. It is a statement about the universe. The simplicity of the equation is the beauty.

> **Case: The Water Bucket** (Chapter 5): Lewin spins a bucket of water in a vertical circle fast enough that the water does not fall out. The demonstration teaches centripetal force. The audience gasps — and remembers.
> **Key takeaway**: Physics demonstrations are not just teaching tools — they are experiences. The audience felt centripetal force, not just understood it.
