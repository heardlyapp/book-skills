## Practical Techniques

### 1. The Rainbow Observation

Next time you see a rainbow, do not just admire it — analyze it. Red on top, blue inside. The colors are in order of their wavelength. Rainbows are physics in plain sight.

### 2. The Pendulum Demonstration

Tie a weight to a string. Set it swinging. Measure the period. It depends only on the length of the string. You have rediscovered one of the fundamental laws of physics.

> **Case: The Home Pendulum** (Observation): Lewin encourages readers to make a simple pendulum at home — a key on a string. Swing it. Time it. The period is the same regardless of how far it swings. You have discovered a law.
> **Key takeaway**: You do not need a laboratory to do physics. A key and a string are enough.

### 3. The Energy Audit

Watch a ball bounce. It never bounces higher than where it started. That is energy conservation. The lost energy becomes heat and sound.

### 4. The Cosmic Perspective

Look at the night sky. Every star is a sun. Many have planets. The laws that govern them are the same laws that govern a falling apple. You are part of the cosmos.

### 5. The "Why" Chain

When you see a phenomenon, ask "why?" — then ask again. And again. The fifth "why" usually leads to fundamental physics. A ball falls. Why? Gravity. Why? Mass curves space. Why? Because energy tells spacetime how to curve. The chain of questions leads to the deepest truths.
Physics is the art of asking why.

> **Case: The Falling Apple** (Chapter 2): Lewin's simplest demonstration — hold an apple, drop it. Why does it fall? The answer leads to Newton's law of universal gravitation. A child's question leads to fundamental physics.
> **Key takeaway**: The simplest questions lead to the deepest truths. A falling apple is physics.
