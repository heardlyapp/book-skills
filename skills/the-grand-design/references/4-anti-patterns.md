# Anti-Patterns

## 1. God of the Gaps

**The mistake**: Attributing phenomena science can't yet explain to God.

**Fix**: Every time science has filled a gap, God has retreated. There are no gaps left for the origin of the universe.

> **Case: The Newtonian God** (Chapter 1): Newton invoked God to explain the stability of the solar system. Laplace later proved the system is stable on its own. God was pushed out of that gap. The process has repeated throughout history.
> **Key takeaway**: The "God of the gaps" argument becomes weaker with every scientific advance. Eventually, there are no gaps left.

## 2. Confusing "We Don't Know" with "We'll Never Know"

**The mistake**: Assuming that what science cannot explain today, it will never explain.

**Fix**: Science has a track record of explaining the previously inexplicable. The origin of the universe is the next frontier.

## 3. Believing in a Single Theory of Everything

**The mistake**: Expecting a single elegant equation to explain all of reality.

**Fix**: Reality may require a network of theories (M-theory), not a single one.

> **Case: The String Theory Disappointment** (Chapter 8): Many physicists hoped string theory would provide a single theory of everything. Instead, it produced five different string theories. M-theory unified these five but introduced the multiverse. The search for simplicity revealed more complexity.
> **Key takeaway**: The universe may not be reducible to a single equation. A network of theories may be the best we can do.

## 4. Anthropic Reasoning Without Evidence

**The mistake**: Arguing that because the universe supports life, there must be a purpose.

**Fix**: In a multiverse, it's inevitable that at least one universe supports life. Our existence is not evidence of design — it's statistical necessity.

## 5. Treating Models as Reality

**The mistake**: Forgetting that our scientific theories are models, not ultimate truth.

**Fix**: Model-dependent realism reminds us: models are tools for prediction, not mirrors of reality.
