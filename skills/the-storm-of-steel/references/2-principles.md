# The Soldier's Code — Principles

Jünger's memoir reveals an unwritten code of conduct that governed the front-line soldier's life. These principles were never formally stated — they emerged from the conditions of trench warfare.

## I. Lead from the Front

Jünger became an officer at 19 and later commanded stormtroop units. His leadership principle was simple: do not ask your men to do anything you would not do yourself. Officers who hid in deep dugouts while their men held the line earned nothing but contempt.

**Case: The Wire-Cutting Patrol.** Jünger led his patrols personally, crawling through the mud and grass to within meters of the English wire. When a bomb nearly killed him, he did not send a replacement. He went out the next night. "Such moments on the prowl by night are unforgettable. Eyes and ears are stretched to their utmost." The men followed him because he was always where the danger was.

**Case: The Mortar Duet.** When English trench mortars began bombarding his sector, Jünger did not take cover. He manned the Lanz mortar himself, exchanging fire with the enemy. "We crouched on the sun-cracked ground in our little gun-pit, and sweating sent over bomb after bomb." When the English targeted his position, he fired one last round and then ran — but he ran after his men, not before them.

## II. The Routine of Survival

Trench life was an alternation between periods of grinding monotony and moments of sheer terror. The key to survival was not heroism but discipline: maintaining the routine even when every instinct screamed to flee.

**Case: The Daily Grind.** "The day began before dawn, when every man had to be standing-to. From 10 p.m. to 6 a.m. only two men from each section might be sleeping, which worked out at two hours sleep per man." The schedule was unrelenting: guard duty, patrols, trench repair, carrying ammunition, fetching food, filling sandbags. The war was not a series of battles separated by periods of rest. It was a continuous crisis.

**Case: The Cooker's Arrival.** "A ray of light in this scene of dreary monotony was the arrival of the cooker every evening at the corner of Hiller wood. When the lid was taken off the dixies a wonderful smell of beans-and-bacon or something else as delightful was wafted aboard." The small pleasures — hot food, a dry place to sleep, a bottle of wine shared with a fellow officer — became the things that made life bearable. Survival was not achieved in grand gestures. It was achieved in the accumulation of small comforts.

## III. Respect the Enemy

Jünger's attitude toward the British soldier is one of the most striking features of the book. He does not hate his enemy — he respects him. Hatred is a political emotion, not a soldier's emotion.

**Case: The English at Monchy.** "In our talks in the trenches, in the dugout, or on the fire-step, we often talked of the 'Tommy'; and, as any genuine soldier will easily understand, we spoke of him very much more respectfully than was commonly the case with the newspapers of those days." The newspapers dehumanized the enemy. The soldiers who faced him knew better.

**Case: The Duel at the Embrasure.** Jünger engaged in a personal duel with a British soldier 100 meters away: "a harebrained Englishman who showed his head over the parapet of a trench 100 metres away at the utmost, and got a succession of bull's-eyes on our loophole." After a prolonged exchange, Jünger hit the plate behind which the Englishman was hiding. He does not celebrate the hit. He describes the encounter as between two equals engaged in a deadly sport. "Warlike achievements are enhanced by the inherent worth of the enemy."

## IV. Hold the Line

When the barrage began, when the attack came, when every instinct said run — the soldier held. This was not about ideology or patriotism. It was about not letting down the men on either side of you.

**Case: The Night Before the Somme.** Throughout June 1916, the English bombardment intensified. Jünger's diary records the growing pressure: "I was not destined to-day, either, to have the refreshment of sleep. From early morning we were bombarded by trench-mortars with ever-lessening intervals." Sleep was impossible. The trenches were being destroyed around them. Yet the men held. "As soon as the fire ceased we made a brilliant display of light rockets, and every man hurried out of the deep dugouts to assure the enemy by a furious burst of rifle-fire that the spirit of the Hanover Fusiliers was not to be broken."

**Case: The Toffee-Apple Bombardment.** "The Englishmen were sending 'toffee-apples' over which, in spite of the moderate report they made when fired off, were of such weight that the splinters from them smashed the stout tree-trunk posts of the revetment clean through." Jünger's response was not to retreat but to counter-bombard: "We had recourse to this rigmarole in case the enemy might be listening in. Very soon there came the comforting reply." Holding the line was not a passive act. It was an active refusal to be intimidated.

## V. Gallows Humor as Survival

The men developed a dark humor that made the unbearable bearable. The mud-filled shell holes were called "men's baths." The desiccated vegetables were "wire entanglements." The officer who kept falling down the dugout stairs was the subject of endless jokes.

**Case: The S.O.S. Message.** When the English bombardment was particularly heavy, Jünger sent the following S.O.S. to his artillery: "Helen is spitting into our trenches. Nothing but great big bits. We want potatoes all sizes." The humor was deliberately coded (in case the English were listening) but also genuinely funny. The artillery replied: "the fat sergeant-major with the stiff moustache and a few of his younger brothers were coming forwards." In the middle of a bombardment, men who could joke were men who could survive.

**Case: The Bantam's Perch.** Sergeant-major H. had a tiny dugout that he called the "bantam's perch." When a mortar bomb scored a direct hit and destroyed it, Jünger wrote: "The 'bantam's perch' was disposed of by a direct hit. Its unfortunate owner had already bestowed himself in a deep dugout. Otherwise he would no doubt have seized the opportunity for a third fall downstairs." The humor is dark, but it is evidence of a functioning mind. The moment the jokes stop, the mind has broken.
