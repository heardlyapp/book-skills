# The Cost of War — Anti-Patterns

Jünger's memoir is a record of survival, but also a record of damage. These are the patterns of harm that war inflicts — not just on the body, but on the mind and the soul.

## Anti-Pattern 1: The Failure of Romanticism

Jünger and his generation volunteered with enthusiasm. "The war had entered into us like wine. We had set out in a rain of flowers to seek the death of heroes. The war was our dream of greatness, power, and glory." The romantic image of war was not an individual delusion — it was a cultural catastrophe.

**Case: The First Casualty.** When the first shell hit Orainville, Jünger saw a wounded man being carried on a stretcher, "streaming from blood whose limbs hung loose and who unceasingly gave a hoarse cry for help." He writes: "What was all this then? The war had shown its claws and torn off its pleasant mask." The romantic image shattered in a single afternoon. But the damage of that romanticism did not disappear. It left a generation of young men unprepared for what they would experience.

**Case: The Beer-Drinking Celebration.** Before the regiment left for the Somme, the men celebrated with "a tremendous beer-drinking in the huge barn." They were celebrating their departure from a quiet sector — heading into the largest battle in human history. The celebration was not courage. It was the inability to imagine what was coming. Romanticism is dangerous because it fills the mind with images that have no relation to reality.

## Anti-Pattern 2: The Numbing That Never Heals

The mind can only absorb so much horror. After a point, it stops feeling. Jünger describes this numbing process, and its permanent consequences.

**Case: The Casualty Ledger.** By the spring of 1916, Jünger's diary entries read like an accounting book: "Fusilier Schmidt II, on the left flank of my platoon, got a bad head wound. Fusilier Lohmann was killed near my dugout by a shot in the head. Fusilier Kramer was wounded in the head by the splinter of a bullet." The dead become items on a list. This is not coldness — it is the mind's only way to process an unprocessable quantity of loss. But the cost is that the ability to feel deeply about individual death is permanently damaged.

**Case: The Permanent Startle.** "Whether a train clattered by, or a book fell on the floor, or a cry rang out at night, the heart always stood still for a moment." Jünger reports this as a permanent condition that persisted throughout the war. The body's alarm system never turned off. The veteran who lived through four years of shelling never stopped hearing shells, even when there were none.

## Anti-Pattern 3: The "One More" Trap

The war created a mindset of endless extension: one more patrol, one more night, one more battle. The soldier told himself that if he could just survive this one, he would be safe. But there was always another.

**Case: The Endless Night.** "The trench life here was as exhausting as it well could be. The day began before dawn, when every man had to be standing-to. From 10 p.m. to 6 a.m. only two men from each section might be sleeping." The exhaustion was not a temporary condition lasting a few days. It lasted years. The "one more night" trap is that the nights never end.

**Case: The 14 Wounds.** Jünger was wounded 14 times. Each time, he recovered and returned to the front. Each time, he believed the wound would be his ticket home. Each time, he was wrong. The threat of death and the relief of survival formed a cycle that repeated endlessly. The mind adapted to the cycle — but at a terrible cost.

## Anti-Pattern 4: The Glorification of Suffering

Jünger's most controversial passages are those that seem to glorify the ordeal of war. "Hardened as scarcely another generation ever was in fire and flame, we could go into life as though from the anvil." This is a real feeling among survivors, but it leads to a dangerous conclusion: that suffering is valuable in itself.

**Case: The "Incomparable Schooling."** Jünger writes: "Time only strengthens my conviction that it was a good and strenuous life, and that the war, for all its destructiveness, was an incomparable schooling of the heart." This is the authentic voice of a survivor looking back. But it is also a trap — the belief that the war was worth it because it made him stronger. The problem is that millions died who never had the chance to become stronger. Their deaths cannot be justified by the growth of the survivors.

**Case: The Final Paragraph.** Jünger ends the book with a declaration of faith: "Germany lives and Germany shall never go under!" Written in 1920, this was neither prophecy nor fact. Germany would go under — into Nazism, into a second world war, into division. The belief that the sacrifice was worthwhile is a psychological necessity for survivors. But it is not an objective truth.

## Anti-Pattern 5: The Loss of Normalcy

The most insidious effect of war is not the horror of combat. It is the loss of the ability to live a normal life afterward. The veteran who has spent four years in the trenches does not know how to be a civilian.

**Case: The Hospital Train.** When Jünger was evacuated in September 1918, he writes: "Once again a German landscape flitted by me, tinged this time with the first dyes of autumn." The landscape was beautiful. But Jünger was no longer capable of seeing it the way a civilian would. He saw it as a man who had lived underground for four years, surfacing into a world that had changed while he was gone.

**Case: The Pour le Mérite.** The telegram awarding Jünger Germany's highest military honor arrived in the hospital. He celebrated with Wenzel, a fellow wounded officer. "As a doubt had been expressed whether we should soon be passed out fit for active service, Wenzel and I felt ourselves compelled to jump again and again over a large armchair. We came out of it, however, very badly. Wenzel broke his arm again, and I was kept in bed next morning with a temperature of 104." The celebration was tragicomic — two men so damaged that even celebrating their survival injured them further. That is the final truth of the book: the war does not end when the shooting stops.
