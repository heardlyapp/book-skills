# The Electromagnetic Framework

The book tells two interwoven stories: Faraday's experimental discoveries and Maxwell's mathematical synthesis.

**Case: Faraday's Lines of Force.** Faraday had no mathematics. He visualized lines of force filling space around magnets and currents. This was revolutionary — physicists of his time believed in action-at-a-distance, not fields.

**Case: The Induction Ring.** In 1831, Faraday wound two coils of wire on an iron ring. He connected one coil to a battery and the other to a galvanometer. When he switched the current on and off, the galvanometer moved. He had discovered electromagnetic induction — the principle of the electric generator.

**Case: Maxwell's Equations.** Maxwell translated Faraday's field concept into four equations. The equations showed that electricity and magnetism are two aspects of a single field. They predicted that light is an electromagnetic wave.

**Case: Hertz's Confirmation.** Twenty years after Maxwell's death, Heinrich Hertz generated and detected radio waves. He showed they moved at the speed of light and had the same properties. Maxwell was right.
