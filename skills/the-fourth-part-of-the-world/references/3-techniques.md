## Practical Techniques

### 1. The Source Audit

When evaluating any map (or any claim about the world), ask: "Where does this information come from?" Waldseemüller's map combined multiple sources — some reliable, some not. The same applies to any knowledge system.

### 2. The Lost Knowledge Awareness

The Waldseemüller map was lost for 400 years. Much of what we "know" could be lost or discovered again. Knowledge preservation is as important as knowledge creation.

> **Case: The Library of Prince Waldburg-Wolfegg** (Epilogue): The sole surviving copy of the Waldseemüller map was discovered in a library that had not been cataloged. It was sitting next to other maps, unnoticed. The librarian recognized what he had. One person's awareness saved the most valuable map in history.
> **Key takeaway**: Rediscovery requires awareness. The information was there — but no one had recognized its significance.

### 3. The Cognitive Map Check

We all carry mental maps — assumptions about how the world works. The Waldseemüller map challenged the medieval worldview. Ask yourself: "What maps am I carrying that may need to be updated?"

### 4. The Naming Power Awareness

Giving something a name gives it reality. Waldseemüller named America into existence. Be aware of the power of naming — in products, projects, and ideas. Names shape perception.

> **Case: The "New World" as Concept** (Chapter 10): Before it was called America, the continent was called the "New World" — a name that carried the assumption of newness, potential, and discovery. The name shaped how Europeans approached it.
> **Key takeaway**: Names are never neutral. The choice of "New World" versus "America" versus "the Indies" carried very different assumptions about what the place was and how it should be treated.
