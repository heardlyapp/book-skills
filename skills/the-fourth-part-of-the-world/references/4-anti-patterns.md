## 6 Anti-Patterns of Cartographic Misunderstanding

### 1. "Columbus Discovered America"

Columbus never knew he had discovered a new continent. He died believing he had reached Asia. The naming of America belongs to Vespucci, and the mapping belongs to Waldseemüller.

### 2. "Vespucci Was a Fraud"

Vespucci has been accused of fabricating voyages. The evidence is contested. But even if he exaggerated, his conceptual contribution — recognizing a new continent — stands independent of the details.

### 3. "Medieval People Thought the World Was Flat"

This is a myth. Educated people since the Greeks have known the earth is round. The Waldseemüller map is drawn on a globe projection. The flat-earth myth was invented in the 19th century.

> **Case: The Round Earth Myth** (Chapter 3): Lester shows that the debate was never about whether the earth was round — it was about its size and what lands existed beyond Europe. Columbus argued the earth was smaller than most scholars believed. He was wrong.
> **Key takeaway**: The "flat earth" is a modern invention used to contrast "backward" medieval thinking with "modern" enlightenment. It is historically false.

### 4. "Maps Are Objective"

Maps are never objective. They are selections of information made by people with purposes, biases, and limitations.

### 5. "One Map Settles the Question"

Waldseemüller himself changed his mind. His 1513 map dropped the name "America." The first map was not the final word — it was the beginning of a conversation.

### 6. "The Fourth Part Was the Final Part"

The fourth continent was not the last. The discovery of Australia and Antarctica would follow. The idea that the world has a fixed number of continents is itself a historical artifact.

> **Case: The 1513 Map Retraction** (Chapter 14): Waldseemüller's 1513 map removed the name "America" from the continent and showed South America labeled as "Terra Incognita" or "Paria." Historians debate whether he had doubts about Vespucci or was responding to political pressure. Either way, the name had already spread beyond his control.
> **Key takeaway**: Even the creator of a name cannot always control it. Once a name enters the world, it takes on a life of its own.
