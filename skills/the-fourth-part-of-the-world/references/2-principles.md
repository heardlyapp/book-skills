## 7 Principles of Cartographic History

### 1. Maps Are Political

Every map reflects the worldview of its makers. The Waldseemüller map showed Spain and Portugal's claims, the Pope's authority, and the European Christian worldview.

### 2. Knowledge Is Cumulative

Waldseemüller built on Ptolemy, the portolan charts, the explorers' reports, and the humanist scholarship of his time. No map is created in isolation.

> **Case: The Portolan Charts** (Chapter 7): Medieval Mediterranean sailors used portolan charts — remarkably accurate coastal maps that were passed down among navigators. These charts were kept secret by trading cities like Venice and Genoa. Waldseemüller had access to them — and his map benefited from their accuracy.
> **Key takeaway**: The best maps combine different kinds of knowledge: ancient theory, practical navigation, and explorers' reports.

### 3. The Name Creates the Thing

Before Waldseemüller's map, "America" did not exist. After it, the name spread faster than the knowledge of what it described. Naming is a form of creation.

### 4. The East-West Axis of History

For most of history, the world was defined along an East-West axis (Europe to Asia). The discovery of the Americas created a North-South axis that fundamentally changed the European worldview.

### 5. A Map Can Be Lost

The Waldseemüller map disappeared for 400 years. Knowledge is fragile. It depends not just on discovery but on preservation.

### 6. The Mapmaker Never Crossed the Ocean

Waldseemüller worked in the Vosges Mountains — landlocked, far from the sea. He had never been to America. His map was based entirely on reports from others.

> **Case: The Voyager Who Saw Clearly** (Chapter 11): Columbus died believing he had reached Asia. Vespucci understood the truth — because he was more interested in what was new than in what confirmed his expectations. The mapmaker, sitting in his study, saw the truth more clearly than the explorer who had been there.
> **Key takeaway**: Distance can provide clarity. Waldseemüller, removed from the politics and preconceptions of exploration, saw the New World more clearly than Columbus, who was trapped by his assumptions.

### 7. The Fourth Part Changed Everything

The addition of a fourth continent to the three known since antiquity (Europe, Asia, Africa) shattered the medieval worldview. If there could be a fourth part, the world was far larger and stranger than anyone had imagined.
