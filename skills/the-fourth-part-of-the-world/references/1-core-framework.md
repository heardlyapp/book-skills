## The Lester Framework: The Map as Worldview

Lester's framework is simple: the 1507 Waldseemüller map is not just a cartographic artifact — it is a snapshot of the European mind at a pivotal moment. The book traces the intellectual history that made the map possible: from ancient geography through medieval cosmology to Renaissance discovery.

**The Key Insight**: The map named America before anyone knew what America was. The act of naming was an act of creation — it brought the continent into existence in the European imagination.

> **Case: Vespucci's Letter That Changed History** (Chapter 10): In 1504, Amerigo Vespucci's letter "Mundus Novus" was published across Europe. He wrote: "We discovered a continent more densely populated and full of animals than our Europe, Asia, or Africa." This was the first clear statement that the lands across the Atlantic were not Asia — they were a new world. Waldseemüller read this letter and decided to name the new continent after Vespucci.
> **Key takeaway**: Vespucci's insight was not geographic — it was conceptual. He recognized a new continent because he was willing to see something that had not been seen before.

> **Case: The Rediscovery of Ptolemy** (Chapter 5): Ptolemy's Geography was lost to Europe for 1000 years. When it was rediscovered and translated into Latin in the early 1400s, it revolutionized European mapmaking. For the first time, maps were drawn using latitude and longitude. The Waldseemüller map is a direct descendant of this tradition.
> **Key takeaway**: The Waldseemüller map could not exist without Ptolemy. Knowledge is cumulative — every map stands on earlier maps.
