# Innovation Principles — Patient First, Rules Second

> Source: Fragile Lives, Chapters 4, 6, 7, 10, 11

## The Surgical Innovator's Playbook

Westaby was not a conformist. He was repeatedly told "it can't be done," "it's too risky," "the ethics committee won't approve." He did it anyway.

### Principles of Medical Innovation

1. **The patient in front of you is more important than the system behind you.** — When the choice is follow protocol and let the patient die or break protocol and maybe save them, the choice is clear.
2. **The system is designed to prevent mistakes, not to enable breakthroughs.** — Every surgical advance was initially opposed by the establishment.
3. **Protect yourself but not at the patient's expense.** — There are ways to take calculated risks. Document everything. Get second opinions. But don't let fear of litigation stop you from doing what the patient needs.
4. **Collaborate with engineers.** — Westaby worked closely with engineers to adapt the Jarvik heart for pediatric use. The best surgical innovations come from surgeon-engineer partnerships.
5. **Teach the next generation.** — Innovation is worthless if it dies with you. Westaby trained the surgeons who would take his techniques further.

> **Case: The Jarvik 2000 in a Child** (Chapter 10)
> A child was dying of heart failure. The only device that could save her was the Jarvik 2000 — designed for adults. The pump was too large for her chest cavity. Westaby worked with the engineers to implant it outside the heart but inside the body. The operation had never been done before. The ethics committee was skeptical. The risk of death was very high. Westaby did it anyway. The child survived.
> **Key takeaway**: "Impossible" in medicine often means "nobody has done it yet." When a patient is dying and there is no alternative, the surgeon's duty is not to accept impossibility but to attempt the unproven.

> **Case: The Girl with No Name** (Chapter 5)
> A young woman was admitted with a devastating infection of her heart valves. She was unconscious, and no one knew her identity — she was the "girl with no name." Without emergency surgery, she would die. But there was no consent, no next of kin, no legal authority to operate. Westaby operated anyway, without consent — because the alternative was letting her die. She survived. Later, her family was found and thanked him.
> **Key takeaway**: When the patient cannot consent and delay means death, act. The law was written to protect patients, not to kill them. Sometimes the ethical thing to do is the legally questionable thing.
