# Anti-Patterns — What's Wrong with Modern Medicine

> Source: Fragile Lives, Chapters 8, 12, 13, 15, Afterword

## The Five Medical Failures

### 1. The Committee Kills More Than It Saves
**Pattern**: Ethical review boards, hospital administration, and regulatory bodies say no to innovative treatments because the risk of being sued exceeds the reward of saving a life.
**Correction**: The system is designed to prevent bad outcomes — but the same system prevents good outcomes that involve risk. Westaby's view: committees never saved a life. Surgeons do.

### 2. The Hierarchy Trap
**Pattern**: Junior doctors see a problem but defer to senior consultants who are no longer on the cutting edge. Patients die while waiting for the "right" person to make the decision.
**Correction**: Good ideas come from anywhere. The most junior person on the team sometimes has the best insight. The rigid medical hierarchy kills patients.

### 3. The Golden Hour Syndrome
**Pattern**: Emergency surgery is delayed by paperwork, consent procedures, bed availability. Every minute of delay reduces the chance of survival.
**Correction**: The system treats the protocol as more important than the patient. When someone is dying, the only question should be: can we help them right now?

### 4. The Malpractice Defense
**Pattern**: Surgeons are trained to practice defensive medicine — do every test, document every decision, avoid every risk — not to save lives but to avoid lawsuits.
**Correction**: The safest surgeon is not the one who takes no risks but the one who takes calculated risks and documents them well. The legal system incentivizes inaction.

### 5. The "We've Always Done It This Way" Fallacy
**Pattern**: Surgical techniques and protocols persist long after they have been shown to be inferior because "that's how we were trained."
**Correction**: The best surgeons constantly question their own techniques. Assumption is the enemy of progress.

> **Case: The Corpse on the Operating Table** (Chapter 12)
> A patient was declared dead after a cardiac arrest. The team had stopped resuscitation. Westaby arrived, assessed, and believed there was still a chance. He opened the chest on a "corpse" — something that is almost never done. He massaged the heart directly, identified the problem, and repaired it. The patient walked out of the hospital. The system had given up. Westaby refused.
> **Key takeaway**: The definition of "dead" is not as fixed as most people think. The willingness to attempt heroic measures when others have stopped is the difference between a technician and a true surgeon. Westaby didn't accept the verdict.

> **Case: The Black Banana** (Chapter 8)
> Westaby describes a new surgical technique he developed that was dismissed by senior colleagues as too dangerous. Years later, the same technique became standard practice. The innovators were ridiculed; the establishment was wrong. The "black banana" — something that looks rotten but isn't — became a metaphor for ideas that are dismissed too quickly.
> **Key takeaway**: The history of medicine is the history of ridiculed innovators who were later proven right. If you are doing something truly new, you will be opposed. Accept it as a signal that you may be onto something important.
