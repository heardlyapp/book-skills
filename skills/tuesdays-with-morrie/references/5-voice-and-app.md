# Morrie Schwartz's Voice & Application Scenarios

> Source: Tuesdays With Morrie, all chapters

## Authorial Voice

Morrie Schwartz is the teacher. Mitch Albom is the narrator. The voice is:

- **Gentle and direct**: Morrie never yelled, never judged. He asked questions. He listened.
- **Profane and sacred**: Morrie swore like a sailor and quoted spiritual texts in the same breath.
- **Warm but clear**: He could hold your hand while telling you the truth you did not want to hear.
- **Dying but alive**: The central paradox — Morrie was dying, but he was more alive than anyone Mitch knew.

### Classic Morrie Quotes

- "The truth is, once you learn how to die, you learn how to live."
- "Love each other or perish."
- "If you don't have the support and love and caring and concern that you get from a family, you don't have much at all."
- "The culture we have does not make people feel good about themselves. We are teaching the wrong things."
- "As you get older, you learn more. The aging process is not decay — it is growth."

## Five Application Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Person Feeling the Rush of Life
You are overwhelmed by work, commitments, and the pressure to achieve.
**Lesson**: Morrie's message: you are running from something. Stop. Ask yourself: what would I do if I knew I had only one year to live? The answer will tell you what matters.

### Scenario 2: Someone with a Broken Family Relationship
There is someone you have not spoken to in years — a parent, sibling, or child.
**Lesson**: Morrie's lesson: forgive. Not because they deserve it — because you deserve peace. Say the words now, before it is too late.

### Scenario 3: A Person Afraid of Death
You lie awake thinking about mortality and feel terror.
**Lesson**: Morrie's wave-ocean metaphor. You are not separate. You are part of the ocean. Death is returning to the whole. Read the Sixth Tuesday chapter.

### Scenario 4: Someone Who Thinks Materialism Will Make Them Happy
You are chasing money, status, possessions.
**Lesson**: Morrie was dying in a small house with few possessions. He was the happiest dying man Mitch ever met. Money cannot buy what Morrie had.

### Scenario 5: A Person Who Needs to Say Goodbye
Someone you love is dying — or you are dying.
**Lesson**: Morrie's living funeral is the blueprint. Do not wait. Say it now. "I love you." "Thank you." "Forgive me." Say it all.

> **Case: Morrie's Dance** (The Syllabus)
> Morrie loved to dance. Even when he could no longer walk, he would tap his fingers to music. There is a story: before ALS, he went to a college dance night and shined. Everyone loved him. He was old, but he was alive. When he could no longer dance, he talked about dancing. When he could no longer talk, he remembered dancing. The dancing never stopped — it just took different forms.
> **Key takeaway:** Morrie's dancing is a metaphor for everything he taught. The things that matter — joy, love, connection — do not end when your body fails. They change form. They continue. The dance never ends.

> **Case: The Aphid Story** (The Audiovisual, Final Chapter)
> Morrie's son told Mitch a story. When Morrie was a child in the Bronx, a teacher brought in an aphid to show the class under a microscope. The children saw the aphid's heart beating. They saw the blood flowing. They saw life happening in a tiny creature. Morrie never forgot that. He spent his whole life trying to see life that clearly — to see the heart beating inside everything.
> **Key takeaway:** Morrie's entire philosophy can be traced to a single moment: seeing the heartbeat of an aphid under a microscope. His gift was the ability to see the life in everything — and to make you see it too.
