> **Case: The Living Funeral** (The Syllabus)
> Morrie read about someone who held a funeral while still alive. He thought it was brilliant. He called his friends and family and told them: "I'm having my funeral — and I want you to come." They gathered at his house. They cried. They laughed. They told stories. They read poems. They said everything they would have said at his grave — but he was there to hear it. Morrie called it the "best party I've ever been to." The living funeral became the foundation of his approach to dying: do not wait until it is too late to say what needs to be said.
> **Key takeaway:** The living funeral is one of the most powerful ideas in the book. Why wait until someone is dead to tell them you love them? Why save the eulogy for the grave? Morrie lived to hear his own eulogy — and it gave him peace.

> **Case: The Final Goodbye** (The Fourteenth Tuesday)
> On the last Tuesday, Morrie was too weak to talk for long. He could barely breathe. He told Mitch: "You are a good student." Mitch wept. Morrie said: "When you are crying like this, you are not thinking about anyone else. You are just feeling. That's what I want you to do. Feel it. Then let it go." Then he said: "Mitch, I love you." And Mitch said: "I love you too." It was their final conversation. Morrie died a few days later.
> **Key takeaway:** The final Tuesday was not about great wisdom — it was about love. At the end of his life, with nothing left to teach and barely any strength to speak, Morrie's last words were "I love you." This is the ultimate lesson: when everything else is stripped away, the only thing that remains is love.
