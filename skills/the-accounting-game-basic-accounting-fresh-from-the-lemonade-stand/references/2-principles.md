# Principles: The Income Statement

> Source: The Accounting Game, Chapters 2-3

## Revenue, COGS, and Gross Profit

Revenue from lemonade sales minus the cost of lemons and sugar (Cost of Goods Sold) = Gross Profit. This is the first key measure of business health. A business with strong gross profit can cover its expenses. Weak gross profit means the business model itself is flawed.

> **Case: First Day Sales** (Chapter 2): You sell 20 cups of lemonade at 25 cents each = $5 revenue. You used $1 worth of lemons and sugar. Gross Profit = $5 - $1 = $4. The income statement starts here. This 80% gross margin means your basic business model is sound.

> **Case: Raising Prices** (Chapter 2): You raise prices to 50 cents per cup. You sell 15 cups = $7.50 revenue. Same $1 COGS. Gross Profit = $6.50. Higher price but lower volume — the income statement shows whether the trade-off was worth it.

## Expenses and Net Profit

From Gross Profit, subtract operating expenses (sign materials, advertising, interest). What remains is Net Profit — the "bottom line."

> **Case: The Sign and Interest** (Chapter 2): You borrowed $10 from Dad to make a better sign. The sign cost $8. Interest on the loan is $1. Expenses = $8 (sign materials) + $1 (interest). Net Profit = Gross Profit $4 - Expenses $9 = -$5. You lost money despite having cash. The income statement reveals the truth.

## The Relationship Between Statements

Net Profit flows into Retained Earnings on the Balance Sheet. Profit increases equity; loss decreases equity.

> **Key takeaway**: The Income Statement tells you whether you're making money. The Balance Sheet tells you whether you're financially healthy. You need both to understand your business.
