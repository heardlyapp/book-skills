# Anti-Patterns: Common Accounting Mistakes

> Source: The Accounting Game, Chapters 5, 6, 8

## Confusing Cash and Profit

The most dangerous mistake. Profit is an accounting concept. Cash is real. A business that shows profit but has no cash will fail.

> **Case: The Profitable Lemonade Stand That Went Broke** (Chapter 4): You sell $100 of lemonade — $40 to customers who paid cash, $60 to a store on 30-day terms. Your income statement shows $100 revenue. But you only have $40 cash. You need $50 to buy more supplies. You can't. You're profitable and broke.

> **Key takeaway**: Always manage cash separately from profit. Monitor both.

## Confusing COGS with Expenses

Cost of Goods Sold varies with sales. Expenses are fixed. Mixing them up distorts your understanding of profitability.

> **Case: The Bad Business Decision** (Chapter 5): You think your lemonade stand isn't making money because expenses are too high. But expenses are fixed. The real problem is COGS is eating your margin. You need to raise prices or find cheaper suppliers.

## Using Cash Accounting When You Should Use Accrual

Cash accounting is simpler but can be misleading, especially if you sell on credit, buy inventory, or have prepaid expenses.

> **Case: The Store Order** (Chapter 4): A store orders $100 of lemonade and will pay you in 60 days. Under cash accounting, you show $0 revenue this month — even though you earned it. Under accrual, you show $100 revenue. Small businesses on cash basis often misrepresent their performance.

## Creative Accounting

The book warns about manipulating financial statements by timing transactions or changing methods. Honest accounting means applying the same rules consistently.
