# Techniques: Cash Flow, Accrual, and Depreciation

> Source: The Accounting Game, Chapters 4, 7

## Cash vs. Profit

The most important distinction in the book. You can be profitable on paper but run out of cash — and go out of business. Cash is oxygen.

> **Case: Selling on Credit** (Chapter 4): You sell $20 of lemonade to a local store that will pay you in 30 days. Your income statement shows $20 revenue. But you have $0 cash. You need to buy more lemons NOW but can't. You're profitable and broke. This is the cash flow trap.

> **Key takeaway**: Track cash separately from profit. The Cash Flow Statement reconciles the two.

## Depreciation

Your lemonade stand cost $8 to build and lasts 4 summers. You don't expense the whole $8 in year one — you spread it as $2 per year. That $2 is depreciation. It's a real expense (the stand is wearing out) but no cash leaves your pocket.

> **Case: The Depreciation Journal Entry** (Chapter 7): Year 1: your lemonade stand (fixed asset) loses $2 of value. Depreciation expense $2 on income statement. Accumulated depreciation $2 on the balance sheet. Cash: unchanged.

## Accrual vs. Cash Method

Accrual: record revenue when earned, expenses when incurred. Cash: record when money moves. Accrual is more accurate.

> **Case: Prepaid Rent** (Chapter 4): You pay $12 rent for 3 months in advance. Under accrual, this $12 is a Prepaid Expense (asset), not an expense. Each month, $4 moves from asset to expense. Under cash method, all $12 hits in month one — distorting profitability.
