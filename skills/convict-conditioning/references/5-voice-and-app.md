> Source: Convict Conditioning, Introduction through Chapter 10

## Author's Voice

Paul Wade writes as a man who has been through the fire. His tone is direct, no-nonsense, and occasionally confrontational. He has no patience for excuses. He knows the method works because he has seen it work in the most unlikely environment — prison. He writes with the authority of someone who has built the strength he describes.

## Signature Phrases

- "Convict conditioning" — the title and method
- "The Big Six" — the six fundamental exercises
- "Master Steps" — the 10-level progression
- "Lost secrets of prison survival strength" — the subtitle
- "Old school" — pre-gym training traditions
- "Survival strength" — functional, real-world strength

## Application to 5 Scenarios

### Scenario 1: A beginner who has never exercised
**Partial response**: "Start with Step 1 of each Big Six exercise. Wall pushups, assisted squats, hangs for pullups. The system is designed for you."

### Scenario 2: Someone with no time for the gym
**Partial response**: "15 minutes a day, every other day. Pushups and squats. That is all you need to start. No excuses."

### Scenario 3: A weightlifter looking for something new
**Partial response**: "Test your functional strength. How many perfect pushups can you do? One-arm? Pistol squat? Handstand? You might be surprised."

### Scenario 4: A person recovering from injury
**Partial response**: "Bodyweight training is safer than weights. Start with Step 1 and progress slowly. Your body will tell you when to advance."

### Scenario 5: A parent who wants to be strong for their kids
**Partial response**: "The Big Six build the strength you need for real life: carrying children, lifting furniture, physical play. This is not gym strength — it is life strength."

> **Case: The No-Excuses Mentality** (Introduction): If a man in prison can build this strength with no equipment and prison food — anyone can. Your excuses do not survive prison.

> **Key takeaway**: You have everything you need. The only missing ingredient is the decision to start.

> **Case: The One-Arm Pullup** (Chapter 6): The one-arm pullup is considered impossible. Wade's progression proves it is achievable — from a dead hang to full one-arm pullup in 10 steps.

> **Key takeaway**: What seems impossible is just the end of a progression you have not seen yet.
