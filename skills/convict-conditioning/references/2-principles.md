## 7 Principles of Prison Strength

### 1. Progressive Overload Without Weights

Make the exercise harder, not the weight heavier. Change the angle, the leverage, or the number of limbs. Your body provides all the resistance you need.

### 2. Master Step Before Progress

Do not move to Step 3 until you have mastered Step 2. 2 sets of 50 reps (or equivalent) is the standard. Rushing through steps builds weakness.

> **Case: The Injured Spotter** (Chapter 2): Wade describes a man who rushed through the pushup progression. He could do 30 two-arm pushups and attempted one-arm pushups too early. He injured his shoulder and could not train for months.
> **Key takeaway**: Patience in progression is not a suggestion — it is a requirement. Skip steps at your own risk.

### 3. Form Over Everything

One perfect rep is worth more than ten sloppy ones. Prison training emphasizes quality because quantity without quality is just damage.

### 4. Consistency Beats Motivation

Prisoners train daily because it is routine, not because they feel motivated. Build the habit first. Motivation follows consistency.

### 5. The Big Six Are Enough

You do not need dozens of exercises. Six movements — pushups, squats, pullups, leg raises, bridges, handstands — train every muscle group.

### 6. Train the Spine

The bridge progression strengthens the spine — the most important structure in the body. Modern training neglects spinal strength. Prison training does not.

### 7. There Is No Finish Line

Mastery of one exercise is not the end. There is always a harder movement. The journey is the point.

> **Case: The 50-Rep Standard** (Chapter 3): Wade explains that prison inmates aim for 50 reps per set not because they want endurance but because they want mastery. By the time you can do 50 perfect reps, you have mastered that movement. Then and only then do you advance.
> **Key takeaway**: The 50-rep standard is not arbitrary. It ensures that your technique is perfect and your strength is solid before you attempt harder movements.
