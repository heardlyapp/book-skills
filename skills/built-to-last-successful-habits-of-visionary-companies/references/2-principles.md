## Key Principles: Core Ideology, Continuity and Change, Home-Grown Leadership

### Principle 1: Core Ideology — More Than Profits

Visionary companies pursue a cluster of objectives, of which making money is only one — and not necessarily the primary one. Every visionary company is guided by a **core ideology** consisting of two parts:

- **Core Values**: A small set of timeless guiding principles that require no external justification. They are intrinsic — the company would keep them even if they became a competitive disadvantage.
- **Core Purpose**: The organization's reason for being beyond just making money. It is the idealistic motivation for doing the work — the "why" that never changes.

> **Case: Merck vs Pfizer** (Chapter 3): George Merck articulated in the 1920s: "Medicine is for the patient; not for the profits. The profits follow." This core ideology guided Merck's decision to develop and donate Mectizan to treat river blindness — a drug that would never be profitable. Pfizer, in contrast, has been consistently more profit-driven. Yet Merck outperformed Pfizer in long-term cumulative stock returns, proving that purpose-driven capitalism creates more value.
> **Key takeaway**: Core ideology is not a marketing statement. It must be backed by costly actions that prove its authenticity.

> **Case: Johnson & Johnson Credo** (Chapter 3): Written in 1943, the J&J Credo explicitly states that the company's first responsibility is to doctors, nurses, patients, mothers, and fathers — not to shareholders. When the Tylenol poisoning crisis hit in the 1980s, J&J's management relied on the Credo to guide their decision to recall 31 million bottles ($100M cost). The comparison company would likely have made a different calculation.
> **Key takeaway**: A well-articulated core ideology provides decision-making clarity during crises when the right choice is expensive.

**Core Values Discovery Process:**
Do not invent your core values — discover what you genuinely hold. Ask: "What values would we keep even if they became a competitive disadvantage? What would we stand for even if there were no reward for standing for it?"

### Principle 2: Preserve the Core / Stimulate Progress

This is the yin and yang of visionary companies. The core ideology stays fixed like a star on the horizon; everything else is open to change. The goal is to manage both **continuity** (preserving identity) and **change** (stimulating progress) simultaneously.

**What Never Changes** — Core values and core purpose
**What Always Changes** — Operating practices, business strategies, cultural norms, structures

> **Case: HP vs TI** (Introduction to Paperback Edition): HP executives constantly asked "What should never change?" — the HP Way of trust and respect for individuals was non-negotiable. Everything else — product lines, organizational structure, business models — evolved continuously. Texas Instruments, lacking this distinction, struggled to maintain identity through periods of change.
> **Key takeaway**: The gyroscope model — a stable inner wheel (core) combined with a freely pivoting outer frame (everything else) — describes how visionary companies navigate change.

### Principle 3: Home-Grown Management

In seventeen hundred years of combined life spans across the visionary companies, researchers found only four individual incidents of going outside for a CEO — and those in only two companies. Visionary companies develop and promote from within at a ratio of 6:1 compared to their comparison companies.

> **Case: 3M vs Norton** (Chapter 8): 3M has never hired an outside CEO since its founding in 1902. Every chief executive in 3M's history has been an insider who lived and breathed 3M's culture. Norton Corporation (3M's comparison) frequently brought in outsiders. 3M's continuity of leadership and culture is a direct result of this home-grown discipline.
> **Key takeaway**: Home-grown leaders preserve the core ideology naturally because they've lived it for decades.

**When to make an exception:** Only when the company has no viable internal candidates AND the company has time to onboard an outsider thoroughly. The evidence shows this is rare — and usually a sign of earlier failures in leadership development.
