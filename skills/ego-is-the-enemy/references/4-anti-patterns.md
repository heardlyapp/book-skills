# Anti-Patterns: What Ego Looks Like

## ❌ Talking Instead of Doing
The most common ego trap. You talk about your plans. People congratulate you. You feel accomplished. You never start. The talking has replaced the doing entirely.

## ❌ The Storytelling Addiction
Success makes you want to tell a heroic story about yourself. The self-made myth. This story disconnects you from reality and makes you vulnerable to the next fall.

> **Case: The CEO Who Wouldn't Listen** (Chapter 18): Holiday profiles a CEO who built a successful company and then became convinced of his own infallibility. He made increasingly reckless bets, ignored critics, and refused to adapt. The company collapsed. His ego had blinded him to reality.
> **Key takeaway:** Ego tells you that you're always right. Reality disagrees. The ability to question yourself is a superpower. The inability to do so is a fatal weakness.

## ❌ Entitlement
You feel like you deserve things you haven't earned. The antidote: earn everything, every time. Nothing is owed to you.

## ❌ Crediting Yourself for Others' Work
Success is rarely a solo achievement. The ego takes all the credit. Humility shares it. The best leaders know their success is built on the contributions of many.

## ❌ Resenting Criticism
The ego hates criticism. It makes excuses, blames others, lashes out. The path welcomes criticism as data. Use it to improve.

> **Case: The Painter Who Wouldn't Learn** (Chapter 3): Holiday tells of a painter who was talented but refused to take feedback. Every critique was met with an excuse. His work never evolved. He stayed average while less talented peers who accepted feedback surpassed him.
> **Key takeaway:** Criticism is the fastest path to improvement. The ego sees it as an attack. The path sees it as information. Choose the path.

Resenting criticism is like resenting the mirror for showing you a smudge on your face. The reflection is not the problem. The smudge is. Criticism reveals where you need to improve. Thank the person who gives it to you. They have done you a favor.

Comparison to others is a trap. There will always be someone ahead and someone behind. Both comparisons are toxic. One feeds arrogance; the other feeds despair. The only comparison that matters is you versus who you were yesterday.

The most insidious form of entitlement is the belief that you are entitled to success because you have worked hard. Hard work is the entry fee, not the guarantee. The market does not care how hard you worked. It cares about what you produced. Keep working. Keep producing. Let the results speak.
Resisting criticism is the quickest way to stop growing. Every critique is an opportunity to improve. The person who criticizes you is doing you a favor - they are showing you something you could not see yourself. Thank them, even when it hurts.
When you find yourself resenting criticism, pause and ask: what can I learn from this? The answer is always something. Even unfair criticism contains information about how others perceive you. Use it. Do not waste it.
