# Principles: Staying Grounded When Things Go Well

## Principle 1: Always Stay a Student
Success creates the illusion that you've arrived. You haven't. The moment you think you know enough is the moment you begin to decline. The most successful people are the most committed to continuous learning.

## Principle 2: Don't Tell Yourself a Story
Success tempts you to believe your own press. The narrative you construct about your greatness is usually a fiction. You got lucky. You had help. You benefited from circumstances beyond your control. Acknowledge this.

> **Case: The General's Myth** (Chapter 10): Holiday describes a military general who, after several victories, began to believe he was invincible. He stopped listening to advisors, ignored intelligence, and made reckless decisions. His next campaign was a disaster. The story he told himself about his genius had disconnected him from reality.
> **Key takeaway:** Success creates a bubble of self-delusion. Seek feedback and listen to criticism. The narrative you tell yourself is usually wrong.

## Principle 3: Beware the Disease of Me
Success can create entitlement, control, and paranoia. You feel you deserve everything. You need to control everything. You suspect everyone is out to get you. This is the disease of me, and it destroys leaders who could otherwise continue thriving.

## Principle 4: Maintain Your Sobriety
Sobriety here means clear-headedness, not abstinence. Don't let success intoxicate you. Stay disciplined. Stay humble. Stay focused on the work, not the rewards. The moment you relax your discipline is when you start your decline.

> **Case: The Entitled Athlete** (Chapter 11): Holiday tells of a talented athlete who, after signing a huge contract, stopped training hard. He felt he had "made it." His performance declined. He was traded. Within two years, he was out of the league. The success had gone to his head, and he forgot what had gotten him there.
> **Key takeaway:** Success is not a destination. It's a platform for continued effort. The moment you think you've arrived, you begin to leave.

The disease of me is particularly dangerous because it is invisible to the person who has it. Everyone around you can see it. You cannot. The antidote: seek honest feedback from people who will tell you the truth. When multiple people say you are changing, listen to them. They are probably right.

Entitlement is the belief that the world owes you something. It does not. The world does not care about your plans, your dreams, or your sense of deserving. The sooner you accept this, the sooner you can start earning what you want through effort and contribution.

Staying a student means actively seeking what you do not know. The more successful you become, the harder this is because people stop telling you hard truths. You must create systems to find them. Read books. Seek mentors. Ask for criticism. Never assume you have it figured out.
The most dangerous ego trap in success is thinking you have permanently "arrived." You have not. The moment you relax your guard, the ego reasserts itself. The practices of humility - reading, listening, serving - must be maintained forever, not just until you feel successful.
