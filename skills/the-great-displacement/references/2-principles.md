> Source: The Great Displacement, Chapters 4-7

# Key Principles

## 1. Climate Migration Is Already Here

"Millions of Americans have already been displaced by climate disasters. The numbers will only grow." This is not a future problem.

## 2. Inequality Determines Who Moves

"The wealthy can afford to rebuild or relocate. The poor are displaced permanently." After disasters, wealthy areas recover quickly. Poor communities often never return.

## 3. Insurance Is the Canary

"When insurance becomes unavailable or unaffordable, the market is telling you something." Insurance companies have better data on climate risk than most governments.

> **Case: The Florida Insurance Crisis** (Chapter 6): Florida homeowners are paying the highest insurance premiums in the nation. Multiple insurance companies have left the state. As sea levels rise and hurricanes intensify, insuring a Florida home becomes increasingly difficult. The insurance market is signaling that parts of Florida may become unlivable.
> **Key takeaway**: Follow the insurance industry. When they pull out of an area, it's not because they're being political — it's because they've calculated the risk is too high.

## 4. Managed Retreat Is the Only Long-Term Solution

"Some communities cannot be saved. The question is not whether they will move, but how." Managed retreat — planned relocation — is more humane and less costly than waiting for disaster.

## 5. The Government Is Behind

"Current disaster policy focuses on rebuilding in place, not relocating." Federal policy encourages rebuilding in high-risk areas rather than helping communities move to safer ground.

> **Case: The Buyout Program** (Chapter 7): The federal government has bought out thousands of flood-prone homes. But the process is slow, underfunded, and leaves communities fragmented. Buyouts often help individual homeowners but destroy the community fabric.
> **Key takeaway**: Current policies help individuals but don't preserve communities. A better approach would be to relocate entire communities together, preserving social bonds and local economies.
