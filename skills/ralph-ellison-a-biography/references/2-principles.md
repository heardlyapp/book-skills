# Key Principles: Ellison's Ideas and Relationships

## Principle 1: Invisibility Is Not a Metaphor for Victimhood

Ellison's most famous concept is widely misunderstood. The invisible man is not a powerless victim. He narrates his own story, he manipulates those who cannot see him, he survives and finally chooses how and when to be seen.

**The core idea:** Invisibility is a condition imposed by the refusal of others to see. The narrator: "I'm invisible simply because people refuse to see me." It is not a personal pathology but a social fact.

**Three dimensions of invisibility:**
1. **Social** — Whites literally do not see blacks as individuals; blacks are a "mass"
2. **Psychological** — The internal damage of being unseen, doubting one's own existence
3. **Existential** — The underground man chooses invisibility as a form of power; he lights his 1,369 lightbulbs to assert his own existence

**Application:** For Ellison, the novel's Prologue and Epilogue (added last) frame the story as a meditation on identity. The narrator emerges from his underground hole at the end, not because society has changed, but because he has changed — and he chooses engagement over withdrawal.

> **Key quote:** "I am an invisible man. No, I don't mean a spook nor one of these Hollywood movie ectoplasms. I am a man of substance, of flesh and bone, fiber and liquids — and I might even be said to possess a mind."

## Principle 2: The Blues as Cultural Theory

Ellison defined the blues as an aesthetic philosophy. Not sad music, but transcendence through art:

> "The blues is an impulse to keep the painful details and episodes of a brutal experience alive in one's aching consciousness, to finger its jagged grain, and to transcend it, not by the consolation of philosophy but by squeezing from it a near-tragic, near-comic lyricism."

**Why it matters:** The blues embodies the Ellisonian worldview. The tragic and comic coexist. Pain and laughter are not opposites but partners. This duality is the aesthetic engine of *Invisible Man*.

**Connection to the novel:** The narrator's voice — sardonic, wounded, witty, philosophical — is a blues voice. Louis Armstrong's "(What Did I Do to Be So) Black and Blue" is the novel's musical signature. "Perhaps I like Louis Armstrong because he's made poetry out of being invisible."

## Principle 3: The Intellectual Friendship with Stanley Hyman

Hyman and Ellison met at Harry Slochower's wedding (1942). They became the deepest intellectual friendship of Ellison's life.

**What Hyman gave Ellison:**
- Access to Kenneth Burke's circle
- The myth-and-symbol school of literary criticism (Lord Raglan, Jessie Weston)
- An example of confident, witty critical writing (Hyman at *The New Yorker*)
- Boundless enthusiasm for learning that matched Ellison's own autodidact drive

**What Ellison gave Hyman:**
- A window into black culture, jazz, the blues
- A model of the integrationist intellectual — "A Jew married to a Gentile, Hyman saw himself as a cosmopolitan intellectual — just as Ralph, labeled a Negro, saw himself as a citizen of the world"

> **Key moment:** In summer 1945, Hyman helped Ellison hammer out the outline for *Invisible Man* at the Hyman home in Bennington. Two intellectuals, one black one white, shaping American literature.

## Principle 4: Burke's Dramatism as Creative Engine

Kenneth Burke's "dramatistic" approach to literature became the structural architecture of *Invisible Man*.

**Burke's pentad:** Act, Scene, Agent, Agency, Purpose — the five terms for analyzing symbolic action.

**Purpose → Passion → Perception:** Ellison wrote this formula on his manuscript drafts as a mantra. It represents the tragic process: the hero has a purpose, suffers through passion (experience/ordeal), and achieves perception (wisdom). This maps directly onto the narrator's arc in *Invisible Man*.

**Burke's indirect influence:**
- Language as symbolic action, not mere description
- Literature as a form of knowledge, not propaganda
- Myth and ritual as structures underlying narrative
- The critic as synthesizer of all knowledge

> **Ellison to Burke:** "One of the most important things I learned was the possibility of creating depth and resonance in my fiction by taking the gambler's chance of alluding to things I'd read in the Bible, in literary classics, scientific works, folklore, or to anything else that might be conveyed through the written word."

## Principle 5: The Wright Mentorship and Break

Richard Wright was the single most important figure in Ellison's early literary development, and also the most complicated.

**What Wright gave Ellison:**
- A model of the serious black writer
- *New Masses* and *New Challenge* connections
- "Blueprint for Negro Writing" — the manifesto that shaped Ellison's early aesthetic
- Access to the Communist literary world
- Encouragement (at first) and brutal honesty ("This is my story, my style. You have copied my ideas, my words and my structure!")

**The break:**
- After Wright accused Ellison of copying his style, Ellison "never showed him another piece of fiction"
- Wright's *Black Boy* (1945) inspired Ellison's breakthrough essay "Richard Wright's Blues"
- Ellison's *Invisible Man* (1952) preempted Wright's continued dominance — the student surpassed the master
- Wright exiled to France in 1947. Ellison stayed in America. The geographical divide reflected an aesthetic divide: Wright remained a naturalist/protest writer; Ellison moved toward modernism and the novel of ideas.

> **Ellison on their differences:** "I had to rigidly control my thawing, allowing the liquid emotion to escape drop by drop through the trap doors of the things I write."

## Principle 6: The Master-Apprentice Method

Ellison taught himself to write by a systematic method of imitation.

**The technique:**
- He copied out passages by hand from Hemingway, Joyce, Stein, Malraux, and Faulkner
- Typed extended passages to absorb their rhythms and sentence structures
- Analyzed how they built scenes, dialogue, and narrative voice

**Why it worked:**
- Ellison came to writing late (first story at 24) and had no formal training
- His musical training gave him the discipline: musicians learn by transcribing the masters
- "I have considered the possibility that I might not be a novelist myself" — he admitted this to Wright. But he persisted.
- The method allowed him to internalize technique before finding his own voice

> **Key insight:** Unlike writers who fear influence, Ellison actively sought it. He believed you must absorb the tradition before you can transcend it.

## Principle 7: The University of the Streets

Ellison's real education happened outside classrooms.

**Sources of learning:**
- Deep Second (Oklahoma City's black district): jazz musicians, pool halls, barbershop talk
- The hobo journey: "I converted hoboing into a lark" — riding freight trains, sleeping in hobo jungles, learning from Charlie Miller
- Jim Randolph's drug store: delivery boy experience, exposure to business and white folks
- The YMCA in Harlem: meeting Langston Hughes, connecting to radical circles
- The Schomburg Collection: the Federal Writers' Project saturated him in black history

**Why the streets matter:**
- Ellison distrusted formal education (he never earned a degree)
- Book learning alone was insufficient — one needed vernacular knowledge
- The black vernacular (speech, music, folklore) contained sophisticated aesthetic and philosophical insights that academia could not match

## Principle 8: Integration Without Assimilation

Ellison was the most controversial integrationist of African-American letters.

**What he believed:**
- Blacks should claim the full American cultural heritage — Emerson, Melville, Whitman, Twain
- Black culture is American culture, not a separate or subordinate tradition
- The black writer should not be confined to "Negro themes"
- Racial chauvinism is as dangerous as white supremacy

**What he rejected:**
- Black nationalism/separation — "There is a school of thought that would have the American Negro a race culturally apart from the rest of America"
- White sociological definitions of black life — "The lady among the races" (Park & Burgess)
- Protest fiction that sacrificed artistic complexity for political message
- The idea that black writers should only write about race

**The critique of his position:**
- Black nationalists (including many after the 1960s) saw him as an Uncle Tom
- Some critics argued his integrationism prevented him from writing the second novel — he had nowhere to go artistically after *Invisible Man*
- But Ellison never wavered: he insisted his position was not about pleasing whites but about claiming his birthright as an American artist
