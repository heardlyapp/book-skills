# Core Framework: The Life Arc of Ralph Ellison

## Part 1: Oklahoma Boyhood (1913-1933)

### Key Events
- Born March 1, 1913 (Oklahoma City) to Lewis Alfred Ellison (ice/coal delivery man) and Ida Milsap Ellison (maid, janitor)
- **Father's death, 1916** — Lewis died when Ralph was 3. This wound defined Ellison's life. "What quality of love sustains us in our orphan's loneliness, and how much is thus required of fatherly love to give us strength for all our life thereafter?"
- Named after Ralph Waldo Emerson — his father's ambition, suggesting the suggestive powers of names and the magic involved in naming
- Raised by strong women: Ida (mother), Aunt Lucretia, Grandma and Grannie Randolph
- **J.D. Randolph** — "Grandpa" who loved books, storytelling, tall tales. Ellison: "That was my real college education because certainly I've done more with what I heard there than I'll ever do with my music."
- Zelia N. Page Breaux — music teacher who taught him to think of himself as an artist. "She was a sort of second mother"
- Started trumpet at 12, practiced obsessively — "I terrorized a good part of an entire city section"

### Key Influences
- **Deep Second (Deep Deuce)** — The vibrant black business and entertainment district of Oklahoma City. Jazz luminaries Duke Ellington, Louis Armstrong, Count Basie performed there.
- **Charlie Christian and Jimmy Rushing** — Two local musicians who became legends. Christian reinvented jazz guitar; Rushing became the finest blues singer of his era.
- **The Tulsa Race Riot of 1921** — Ralph and Ida passed through the destroyed Greenwood section. 68+ blacks killed, 1,000+ homes destroyed. Visceral lesson in white supremacy.
- **The Depression** — Ida lost jobs, the family moved, Ralph worked as shoeshine boy, waiter, delivery boy, janitor

## Part 2: Tuskegee (1933-1936)

### The Journey
- Hitchhiked + hoboed 1,000+ miles from Oklahoma City to Tuskegee, Alabama, in June 1933. Charlie Miller taught him the hobo's code. Nearly killed by railroad bulls in Decatur, Alabama (Scottsboro Boys territory)
- Arrived with almost no money. Worked bakery at 5am, practiced trumpet in afternoons, classes at night

### Key Figures at Tuskegee
- **W.L. Dawson** — The hero who became a disappointment. Ellison came to study with Dawson, who refused to mentor him. "My trip to Tuskegee was my journey into the 'heart of darkness.' My Kurtz was W.L. Dawson: the artist corrupted by his environment."
- **Walter Bowie Williams (librarian)** — Introduced Ellison to modern literature, poetry, aesthetic theory. Became his closest friend and surrogate father figure. Great influence but also a negative model — Williams was too despairing
- **Morteza Drexel Sprague (English)** — Taught the English novel. Under him, Ellison read *Crime and Punishment*, *Wuthering Heights*, *Jude the Obscure*, and encountered T.S. Eliot's *The Waste Land*
- **Hazel Lucille Harrison (piano)** — Her advice became a legend: "You must always play your best, even if it's only in the waiting room at Chehaw Station, because in this country there'll always be a little man hidden behind the stove whom you don't expect, and he'll know the music"
- **Dean Alvin Neely** — Homosexual dean who "hounded" Ellison out of school

### The Turning Point
- Encounter with *The Waste Land*: "Somehow its rhythms were often closer to jazz than were those of the Negro poets... Although I was then unaware of it, this was the real transition to writing."
- Left Tuskegee in 1936 without a degree. Headed to New York City with $100, planning to work for the summer and return. Never went back.

## Part 3: New York Radical (1936-1943)

### Arrival
- Reached Harlem July 5, 1936. Stayed at YMCA on West 135th Street
- **Met Langston Hughes in the YMCA lobby the next morning** — "meeting Langston Hughes would change his life forever"
- Met Augusta Savage (sculptor), became friends with Richmond Barthé
- Worked briefly as a receptionist, then lab assistant at a paint factory

### The Communist Years
- Joined radical circles through Hughes and Louise Thompson
- Became a close friend of **Richard Wright** — the most important relationship of his literary life
- Wrote reviews for *New Masses*, became a Stalinist. Defended Moscow show trials. Accepted Party line on Black Belt self-determination.
- Published first short story "Hymie's Bull" (1937), heavily influenced by Hemingway
- Mother Ida died October 1937 — misdiagnosed by a black doctor. This embittered Ellison against black bourgeois incompetence.
- Married **Rose Poindexter** (entertainer, Communist) in 1938
- Joined Federal Writers' Project — researched black history in New York. This saturated him in the raw materials of his future novel.

### Key Writing During This Period
- **"The Birthmark"** (1940) — story about a lynching survivor
- **"Mister Toussan"** (1941) — story about two black boys learning about Toussaint L'Ouverture. Most successful New Masses piece.
- **"The Way It Is"** (1942) — Last piece in *New Masses*. About a black mother's support for the war despite racism.
- **Managing Editor of *The Negro Quarterly*** (1942-1943) — Tried to create a black intellectual magazine of serious cultural criticism. Failed after a year due to funding.

### The Shift Away from Communism
- **Harry Slochower's influence** — Introduced psychological complexity, saw art as more than ideology
- **Stanley Edgar Hyman** — Met at Slochower's wedding. Hyman was Burke's disciple, writer at *The New Yorker*. Became one of Ellison's closest intellectual friends.
- **Kenneth Burke** — Ellison met Burke and adopted him as his guide. Burke's dramatism (Purpose → Passion → Perception) became the creative engine for *Invisible Man*.
- **The Nazi-Soviet Pact (1939)** and the war led Ellison to quietly exit the Party without a break.

## Part 4: Invisible Man (1945-1952)

### The Creation
- Wrote on a farm in Vermont, summer 1945. Reading Lord Raglan's *The Hero* and speculating on Negro leadership
- First sentence: "I am an invisible man. No, I don't mean a spook nor one of these Hollywood movie ectoplasms. I am a man of substance, of mass, bulk and liquids. I'm invisible simply because people refuse to see me."
- **7 years of writing.** Contract with Reynal & Hitchcock, then moved to Random House
- **Fanny McConnell** — Married 1946. Her steady job as executive secretary at the International Rescue Committee supported them throughout. "The rock of economic stability."

### Key Influences on the Novel
- **Burke's dramatism**: "Purpose → Passion → Perception" taped above his desk
- **Dostoyevsky's *Notes from Underground*** — The alienated, bitter narrator who refuses to be defined
- **Melville's *Moby-Dick*** — "To produce a mighty book, you must choose a mighty theme"
- **The blues** — Near-tragic, near-comic lyricism as narrative voice
- **Joyce's *Portrait of the Artist*** — Stephen Dedalus: "to forge in the smithy of my soul the uncreated conscience of my race"
- **Surrealism** — Especially from Kafka (Fanny was reading Kafka in 1945), and from jazz improvisation

### Publication and Triumph
- Published April 14, 1952. Won **National Book Award** (1953) — first black writer to win
- Also won *Russwurm Award* and *National Newspaper Publishers Association Award*
- Established Ellison as the preeminent black writer of his generation
- Preempted by *Richard Wright's* earlier dominance — Ellison had surpassed his mentor

## Part 5: Critic and Teacher (1952-1994)

### Later Career
- Published **Shadow and Act** (1964) — landmark collection of essays
- Published **Going to the Territory** (1986) — second essay collection
- Taught at NYU (1970-1980), Rutgers, Bard, Yale
- Elected to the American Academy of Arts and Letters
- Received the Presidential Medal of Freedom (1969) from Lyndon Johnson's administration

### The Second Novel
- Spent 40+ years trying to write a follow-up to *Invisible Man*
- Manuscript reached 2,000+ pages, was lost in a house fire in 1967, painstakingly reconstructed
- Published posthumously as **Juneteenth** (1999), edited by John F. Callahan
- **Three Days Before the Shooting…** (2010) — comprehensive edition of all surviving manuscript material

### Death
- Died April 16, 1994, of pancreatic cancer
- Buried at the Washington Heights neighborhood of upper Manhattan
- His work continues to shape American literary studies, African-American studies, and cultural theory

## Key Relationships

| Person | Relationship | Significance |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **Richard Wright** | Mentor, then rival | "Blueprint for Negro Writing" shaped Ellison's early aesthetics; later accused Ellison of copying his style. Became estranged after Wright's exile to France |
| **Langston Hughes** | Early patron, lifelong friend | Met in YMCA lobby July 1936. Shared politics, poetry, mutual respect. Ellison publicly criticized Hughes's autobiography for lacking radical seriousness |
| **Kenneth Burke** | Intellectual father | Took Ellison "as his guide through the world of ideas." Dramatism became the structural engine of *Invisible Man* |
| **Stanley Edgar Hyman** | Closest intellectual friend | Introduced Ellison to Burke's circle. Boundless enthusiasm for learning. "A Jew married to a Gentile, Hyman saw himself as a cosmopolitan intellectual — just as Ralph, labeled a Negro, saw himself as a citizen of the world." |
| **Fanny McConnell** | Second wife | The foundation. She supported him financially and emotionally for decades. "She became the rock of economic stability that sustained Ralph as he labored on his novel." |
| **Ida Ellison** | Mother | Died 1937 (misdiagnosed tuberculosis). "This is the end of childhood for us." Her death freed and haunted him. |
| **Albert Murray** | Tuskegee friend, lifelong intellectual companion | Fellow fighter for integrationist vision of black culture. They reinforced each other's ideas about the blues, jazz, and American identity. |
