# Techniques: Ellison's Writing Process and Key Works

## Technique 1: The 7-Year Novel

*Invisible Man* took Ellison 7 years (1945-1952). Here is how he did it.

### Phase 1: Conception (Summer 1945)
- Reading Lord Raglan's *The Hero* on a Vermont farm, speculating on Negro leadership
- First sentence came: "I am an invisible man. No, I don't mean a spook nor one of these Hollywood movie ectoplasms."
- **Key insight:** The narrative voice — sardonic, philosophical, wounded — was found in the first paragraph. The rest was elaboration.

### Phase 2: False Starts (1945-1946)
- Actually working on THREE novels: a Nazi prison camp story, a novel about a college teacher named Bard, and the invisible man
- The invisible man won. Ellison fused elements from the abandoned projects into the final novel.
- "I myself know only this: the invisible man will move upward through Negro life, coming into contact with its various forms and personality types"

### Phase 3: The Outline (Spring 1947)
- Finalized at Stanley Hyman's house in Vermont. Hyman helped hammer out the plot.
- **Purpose → Passion → Perception:** Burke's formula became the spine.
- The new outline convinced Random House to buy out his contract from Reynal & Hitchcock.

### Phase 4: The Sprint (1947-1952)
- Wrote in isolation: rented shack in Quogue, Long Island; cottages in Vermont; anywhere quiet
- "Harlem is killing me, I know that now. There my emotions are pulled in a thousand directions before I can have coffee in the morning."
- Fanny McConnell supported them with her salary from the International Rescue Committee
- The Prologue and Epilogue — the most philosophical sections — were written last, framing the entire narrative

> **Key lesson for writers:** The voice came first. The plot was discovered through writing and re-writing. The frame (Prologue/Epilogue) came last.

## Technique 2: The Plagiarism-As-Practice Method

> "I have considered the possibility that I might not be a novelist myself." — Ellison to Richard Wright

Ellison overcame this anxiety through systematic imitation:

1. **Hand-copy passages** from Hemingway, Joyce, Stein, Malraux, Faulkner
2. **Type them out** to feel the rhythm in his fingers
3. **Analyze structure:** how sentences build, how scenes transition, how dialogue reveals character
4. **Apply to own material:** write a scene in Hemingway's style, then rewrite in his own

**Why it works:**
- Writers block comes from not knowing HOW to write. Imitation gives you the how.
- "Trained as a musician to respect imitation as a necessary step toward mastery, Ralph had not hesitated to tread in Wright's footsteps."
- The goal is not to stay in imitation but to internalize technique until you find your own voice.

## Technique 3: The Mighty Theme Principle

Ellison took seriously Ishmael's advice in *Moby-Dick*: "To produce a mighty book, you must choose a mighty theme."

**His criteria for a mighty theme:**
1. Must encompass the full arc of black American experience — from slavery to freedom, South to North, folk to modern
2. Must be psychologically complex — not just sociology or protest
3. Must use American literary tradition (Melville, Twain, Emerson) as a resource, not a constraint
4. Must be shaped by formal experimentation — modernism, surrealism, myth
5. Must be funny and tragic at once — the blues principle

**Application in *Invisible Man*:**
- The narrator moves through every major institution of black life: segregated college, Northern factory, Communist organization, Harlem street life
- Each section is a satire of a different worldview: black bourgeois respectability, liberal charity, radical politics, black nationalism
- The novel is encyclopedic in its ambitions — like *Moby-Dick*, it tries to contain multitudes

## Technique 4: The Myth-and-Symbol Layer

Ellison layered his novel with mythological references that the casual reader might miss:

**Lord Raglan's *The Hero* — The pattern of the mythical hero:**
1. Hero's birth is obscure or miraculous → Ellison's narrator is born-again through his underground experience
2. Hero is driven from his kingdom → the narrator is expelled from college
3. Hero undergoes trials → the narrator's journey through North, factory, Brotherhood
4. Hero achieves apotheosis → the narrator's final descent into the manhole and his rebirth through writing

**Jessie Weston's *From Ritual to Romance* — The grail quest:**
- The narrator's search for identity parallels the grail knight's search for meaning
- Symbols of fertility and waste appear throughout: the college campus (artificial paradise), the factory (mechanical hell), the Harlem streets (chaos and vitality)

**The Waste Land (T.S. Eliot):**
- "I was unaware of it, this was the real transition to writing" — Ellison's encounter with *The Waste Land* at Tuskegee
- Eliot's discontinuities, changes of pace, and hidden system of organization became models for the novel's structure
- Ellison saw a link between Eliot's fragmented modern voice and Louis Armstrong's jazz improvisation

## Technique 5: The Surrealist Turn

> Case: **"King of the Bingo Game"** (1944)
> Ellison's breakthrough story. A desperate black man plays a bingo game in a movie theater. As the wheel spins, he hallucinates his life, his dead wife, his poverty. The line between reality and nightmare dissolves. This was Ellison's first fully realized surrealist fiction, written just before he began *Invisible Man*.

**How surrealism operates in *Invisible Man*:**
- The Prologue: The narrator smokes a joint, listens to Louis Armstrong, drifts into a multi-layered dream of an old slave woman, an auction block, and a preacher intoning "the blackness of blackness"
- The Battle Royal: Realistic yet nightmarish — blindfolded boys fighting each other for white entertainment
- The paint factory explosion: Symbolic overkill — black paint made whiter, machines that consume their operators
- The Harlem riot: The narrator moves through chaos like a figure in a fever dream

**Sources of Ellison's surrealism:**
- Kafka (Fanny was reading Kafka in summer 1945 — "Fanny is reading Kafka" appears in his notes)
- The jazz experience — bebop especially, with its disorienting improvisations
- The cumulative psychological pressure of racism — the real world is already surreal for black Americans
- Dostoyevsky's *Notes from Underground* — the underground as surreal space of transformed consciousness

## Key Works Summary

### *Invisible Man* (1952)
- **Length:** 581 pages (original edition)
- **Structure:** Prologue + 25 chapters + Epilogue
- **Narrative voice:** First-person, retrospective, from an underground hideout
- **Major awards:** National Book Award (1953), Russwurm Award
- **Sales:** Over 1 million copies by 1990
- **Legacy:** Routinely ranked among the 100 greatest novels of the 20th century

### *Shadow and Act* (1964)
- **Structure:** Collection of 22 essays, interviews, reviews
- **Three sections:** The Seer and the Seen (literary criticism), Sound and the Mainstream (music/culture), The Shadow and the Act (race and identity)
- **Key essays:** "Richard Wright's Blues," "Twentieth-Century Fiction and the Black Mask of Humanity," "The World and the Jug"
- **Legacy:** Established Ellison as one of America's finest literary critics

### *Going to the Territory* (1986)
- **Structure:** 17 essays
- **Key topics:** Flannery O'Connor, Huckleberry Finn, the blues, contemporary black writing
- **Legacy:** Deepened the aesthetic and cultural arguments of *Shadow and Act*

### *Juneteenth* (1999) [posthumous]
- **Editor:** John F. Callahan
- **Status:** Edited from ~2,000 pages of manuscript
- **Story:** A race-baiting senator (an African-American passing for white) is visited on his deathbed by the black minister who raised him
- **Critical reception:** Mixed — a remarkable fragment, not a finished novel

### *Three Days Before the Shooting…* (2010) [posthumous]
- **Editors:** John F. Callahan and Adam Bradley
- **Status:** Comprehensive edition of all surviving manuscript material for the second novel
- **Length:** Over 1,100 pages
- **Significance:** Reveals the scope and ambition of Ellison's unfinished masterpiece
