# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *Zucked* by Roger McNamee.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The Attention Audit

Track your phone usage for three days using built-in screen time tools. Identify: which apps consume the most time? What triggers checking (boredom, anxiety, transition moments)? How many times per day do you check without a specific purpose? McNamee's insight: awareness is the first step to regaining control. You cannot change what you do not measure.

> **Case: The Slot Machine Effect (Chapter 3):** Facebook uses variable ratio reinforcement — the same psychological mechanism as slot machines. You don't know how many likes, comments, or views a post will get until you check. The uncertainty creates a dopamine-driven compulsion loop. Checking becomes automatic because the reward is unpredictable.

### 2. The Privacy Cleanse

(1) Review Facebook's ad preferences — delete or hide interests Facebook has assigned to you. (2) Install browser extensions (Privacy Badger, uBlock Origin) to block tracking pixels. (3) Turn off location history for all apps. (4) Download your Facebook data (Settings → Your Facebook Information → Download Your Information). (5) Delete Facebook's off-Facebook activity tracking. (6) Use a password manager and enable two-factor authentication.

> **Case: The Facebook Pixel Surveillance Web (Chapter 7):** Facebook's tracking pixel is embedded on most major websites. Even if you never click a Facebook ad, Facebook knows what sites you visit, what you search for, and what you buy. This surveillance is not incidental — it is the foundation of Facebook's advertising business model.

### 3. The "Platform, Not Media" Refutation

When Facebook claims it is "just a platform, not a media company," McNamee shows why this is false: (1) Facebook's algorithm curates content — that is editorial judgment. (2) Facebook sells targeted advertising — that is a media business. (3) Facebook decides what to amplify and suppress — that is editorial power. The "platform" defense is a legal shield, not a factual description.

### 4. The Regulation Checklist

McNamee's five pillars of meaningful reform: (1) Privacy legislation (GDPR-style consent and data minimization). (2) Algorithmic transparency and independent auditing. (3) Antitrust action against monopolistic acquisitions (Instagram, WhatsApp). (4) Section 230 reform — platforms should not be immune from liability for systemic harms they engineer. (5) Independent oversight with enforcement power.

### 5. The Digital Boundaries Protocol

Delete social media apps from your phone (use the browser if needed). Turn off ALL push notifications except direct messages from real people. Set a daily time limit (most phones have this built in). Agree on phone-free times with family — meals, after 9 PM, first hour of the morning. McNamee does not advocate quitting entirely, but regaining conscious control over usage.
