# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *Zucked* by Roger McNamee.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The Attention Audit

Track your phone usage for three days. Which apps consume the most time? When do you check them (boredom, anxiety, transition moments)? What trigger prompts the check (notification, habit, impulse)? McNamee's insight: awareness is the first step to regaining control. You cannot change what you do not measure.

> **Case: The Slot Machine Effect (Chapter 3):** Facebook uses variable rewards — the same psychological mechanism as slot machines. You don't know how many likes you'll get on a post until you check. The uncertainty creates a dopamine loop. Checking becomes compulsive because the reward is unpredictable.
> **Key takeaway**: The addiction is not a personal failing. It is a designed feature. Understanding the design removes shame and enables change.

> **Case: McNamee's Memo to Zuck (Prologue):** In October 2016, McNamee sent Zuckerberg and Sandberg a two-page memo with specific examples of harm caused by Facebook's design. He offered to help fix it privately. They responded politely and delegated him to Dan Rose, who played the role of "let me make your concerns go away." The technique McNamee learned: if a company won't engage privately, go public.
> **Key takeaway**: Private engagement is always preferable. But when companies refuse to act, public pressure is the only remaining lever.

(1) Review Facebook ad preferences — delete interests Facebook has assigned you. (2) Use a browser extension (like Privacy Badger) to block tracking pixels. (3) Turn off location history. (4) Download your Facebook data (Settings → Your Facebook Information → Download Your Information). (5) Use a password manager and enable two-factor authentication.

### 3. The "Platform, Not Media" Refutation

When Facebook claims it's "just a platform, not a media company," McNamee shows why this is false: (1) Facebook's algorithm curates content (that's editorial judgment). (2) Facebook sells targeted advertising (that's media). (3) Facebook decides what to amplify and what to suppress (that's editorial power). The "platform" defense is a legal shield, not a factual description.

### 4. The Regulation Checklist

What effective regulation looks like: (1) Privacy: GDPR-style consent requirements and data minimization. (2) Transparency: algorithms must be auditable. (3) Antitrust: break up the monopoly (Facebook's acquisitions of Instagram and WhatsApp should have been blocked). (4) Accountability: modify Section 230 to require platforms to address systemic harms.

### 5. The Digital Detox Protocol

McNamee doesn't advocate quitting social media entirely — but recommends: (1) Delete the apps from your phone (use browser instead). (2) Turn off all notifications except direct messages from real people. (3) Set a daily time limit. (4) Replace scrolling time with an offline activity. (5) Support alternative platforms that are not advertising-based.
