# The Four Defaults

> Source: Clear Thinking, Part 1, Chapters 1.1-1.6

## Core Framework

### The Enemies of Clear Thinking

| Default | What It Does | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| Emotion | You react instead of think | Send the angry email |
| Ego | You defend instead of learn | Refuse to admit you were wrong |
| Social | You conform instead of evaluate | Follow the crowd into a bad decision |
| Inertia | You resist instead of adapt | Stick with a failing strategy |

> **Case: The Emotion Default (Chapter 1.2)**
> The emotion default is the most powerful and immediate. When you feel threatened, angry, or scared, your brain hijacks your decision-making. You react before you think. Parrish's advice: do not make decisions when you are emotional. Create space. Wait. The best decisions are rarely made in the heat of the moment. The pause is where judgment lives.
> **Key takeaway:** The emotion default is the most dangerous because it is the fastest. The solution is not to suppress emotions — it is to create space between the emotion and the response. One breath. One pause. That is enough.

> **Case: The Ego Default (Chapter 1.3)**
> The ego default makes you care more about looking right than being right. It defends your status, your identity, your past decisions. It prevents you from changing your mind even when evidence says you should. Parrish's framework: ask "what is true?" not "who is right?" The ego is the enemy of clear thinking because it prioritizes protection over accuracy.
> **Key takeaway:** The ego default is the hardest to recognize because it feels like conviction. The solution: separate your identity from your beliefs. Your beliefs can be wrong. You are not your beliefs.
