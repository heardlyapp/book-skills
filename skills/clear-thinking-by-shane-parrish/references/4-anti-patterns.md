# Anti-Patterns — Decision Mistakes

> Source: Clear Thinking, Part 4, Chapters 4.1-4.6

## The Five Errors

### 1. Rushing to a Solution
**Error**: Solve the problem before defining it.
**Correction**: Spend time on problem definition. A well-defined problem is half-solved.

### 2. Ignoring Alternatives
**Error**: Choose the first option that seems reasonable.
**Correction**: Generate multiple options before evaluating. The best option may not be the first one.

### 3. No Margin of Safety
**Error**: Assume everything will go right.
**Correction**: Assume things will go wrong. Leave room for error.

### 4. Confusing Activity with Progress
**Error**: Doing something feels better than deciding not to decide.
**Correction**: Sometimes the best decision is to do nothing. Inertia default is real — but so is action bias.

### 5. Not Learning from Decisions
**Error**: Move on to the next decision without review.
**Correction**: Review every decision. What did I learn? What would I do differently?

> **Case: The Margin of Safety (Chapter 4.5)**
> Parrish emphasizes the margin of safety: never push a decision to its limit. Leave room. If a project requires 10 days, budget 15. If an investment could lose 20%, prepare for 50%. The margin of safety is not pessimism — it is realism. Things go wrong. Plan for it. The best decisions are not the ones that maximize potential gain — they are the ones that minimize potential catastrophe.
> **Key takeaway:** The margin of safety is Parrish's most important practical concept. It shifts your focus from maximizing upside to surviving downside. The lesson: survive first. Thrive second.

> **Case: Defining the Problem (Chapter 4.1)**
> Most people rush to solutions. Parrish argues: spend 80% of your time on problem definition. If you define the problem correctly, the solution is often obvious. If you define it poorly, the best solution will still fail. The question "what problem are we solving?" is the most important question you can ask.
> **Key takeaway:** Problem definition is the most undervalued skill in decision-making. Every hour spent defining the problem saves ten hours of wasted solutions.
