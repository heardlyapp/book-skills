# Adams the Revolutionary — The Colossus of Independence

> Source: John Adams, Part I: Revolution

## Core Framework

Adams was not a natural revolutionary. He was a lawyer from Braintree, Massachusetts, a man who valued order and the rule of law. But when Britain violated the rights of the colonies, Adams became the most powerful voice for independence in the Continental Congress.

### The Road to Independence

| Year | Event | Adams's Role |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1765 | Stamp Act | Adams led protests in Massachusetts |
| 1770 | Boston Massacre | Adams defended the British soldiers — because they deserved a fair trial |
| 1774 | First Continental Congress | Adams traveled to Philadelphia as a delegate |
| 1775 | Bunker Hill | Adams nominated Washington as commander-in-chief |
| 1776 | Declaration of Independence | Adams was the "Colossus of Independence" — the leading voice for the break |

### The Defence of the Boston Massacre Soldiers

Adams's most remarkable act before independence: he defended the British soldiers who had fired into a crowd in Boston, killing five colonists. The city was furious. Adams knew he would be unpopular. He took the case because he believed every person deserved a fair trial. He won — most of the soldiers were acquitted. This act established his reputation for integrity.

> **Case: The Declaration Debate** (Chapter 3)
> In June 1776, the Continental Congress debated independence. Adams spoke for three days, delivering the most powerful argument for separation. He knew that a vote for independence was a vote to be hanged if the war was lost. He pushed anyway. When the Declaration was adopted on July 2 (not July 4 — that was the approval of the text), Adams wrote to Abigail: "The Second Day of July 1776 will be the most memorable Epocha in the History of America." He was wrong about the date but right about the significance.
> **Key takeaway**: Independence was not a foregone conclusion. It was a choice made by men who knew the cost. Adams's leadership in the Congress was as important as Washington's on the battlefield.

> **Case: The Nomination of Washington** (Chapter 2)
> In June 1775, the Congress needed to choose a commander-in-chief for the Continental Army. The obvious candidate from the South was George Washington. Adams nominated him — a politically essential move that bound Virginia to the New England cause. Adams later said that his most important service was not the Declaration but the appointment of Washington. Without Washington's leadership, the army would not have survived.
> **Key takeaway**: Knowing who to put in charge is as important as knowing what to do. Adams recognized that Washington's leadership was essential and sacrificed his own ambition for the good of the cause.
