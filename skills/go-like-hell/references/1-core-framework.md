# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *Go Like Hell* by A. J. Baime — Introduction, Parts I-IV.

## The Three Frameworks

### Framework A: The Ford-Ferrari Rivalry (Introduction, Part I)

In 1963, Henry Ford II tried to buy Ferrari. Enzo Ferrari agreed in principle — then walked away at the last minute, humiliating Ford. Ford's response: "We're going to beat Ferrari at Le Mans."

> **Case: The Deal That Never Was** (Part I): Ford sent a delegation to Maranello to negotiate the acquisition of Ferrari. The deal collapsed when Enzo realized he would lose control of his racing team. The Americans were furious. "Ford could not let this slight go unavenged. It was more than a business deal gone sour. It was a blow to the image of the company and the family."

> **Case: The GT40's First Two Failures** (Parts II-III): The Ford GT40 debuted at Le Mans in 1964. All three cars retired with mechanical failures. 1965: two cars retired, the third also failed. Total humiliation. The team went back to the drawing board. As Shelby said: "They failed because they had to. You can't go to a place like Le Mans and expect to win if you haven't been there before."

### Framework B: Carroll Shelby's Genius (Parts II-IV)

Shelby was a former chicken farmer and Le Mans winner (1959). He had never built a race car from scratch. But he had an intuitive genius for what made a car fast and reliable.

> **Case: The 427 Engine Decision** (Part III): Ford's engineers wanted their new 427 cubic inch engine in the GT40. Shelby said the 427 was too big and heavy — it would ruin the handling. He was overruled. The 427 GT40s failed. Reluctantly, Ford let Shelby use his preferred 289 engine. The car won Le Mans.

### Framework C: The 1966 Le Mans (Part IV)

Three Fords crossed the finish line together in a planned formation finish. The problem: Ken Miles, who had led the entire race, was told to slow down for the photo — and then was denied the official victory because his car started further back on the grid.

> **Case: The Photo Finish That Failed** (Part IV): "The plan was for cars 1, 2, and 3 to cross together, showing the world Ford's dominance. But when Miles slowed to wait for McLaren, the race officials ruled that McLaren had covered more distance because he started from a higher grid position." Miles lost the victory he had earned. Two months later, he died testing Ford's next-generation race car.
