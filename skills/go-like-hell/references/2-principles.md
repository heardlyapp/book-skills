# 2 — Key Principles

> Source: *Go Like Hell* by A. J. Baime.

## The 7 Principles

### 1. Rivalry Is A Fuel

Nothing drives innovation like the desire to beat someone who humiliated you. Ford's $10M bet was not about ROI — it was about pride.

### 2. Experience Cannot Be Bought

Ford spent millions hiring engineers and buying technology. They still failed twice before winning.

> **Case: The Failed Acquisition** (Introduction): Ford sent a team to Maranello to buy Ferrari. Enzo walked away at the last minute. "Ford Motor Company had been rejected. There was only one response: go to Le Mans and beat the red cars." The most expensive marketing decision in history was born from wounded pride.

> **Case: The 1964 Le Mans DNF** (Part II): Three GT40s entered. Zero finished. Brake failures, suspension failures, engine fires. "They had learned something important: the car was fast but fragile." The lessons of 1964 were applied in 1966.

### 3. Talent Over Titles

Carroll Shelby was a former chicken farmer. He had never built a production car. He was the right person anyway.

### 4. Your Biggest Asset Is Also Your Biggest Problem

Shelby was mercurial, politically incorrect, and disdainful of corporate hierarchy. Ford learned to protect him from their own bureaucracy.

### 5. Failure Is Tuition

> **Case: The 427 vs. 289 Engine** (Part III): Ford's engineers insisted on the 427 engine. Shelby said it was too big. The 427 cars failed. Shelby quietly built a 289 version. It won Le Mans. Sometimes the person in the field knows better than the experts at headquarters.

### 6. The Finish Line Is Also a Starting Line

Winning Le Mans didn't end the rivalry. Ford won four years straight. Ferrari won in 1963 and returned to win again in 1964 and 1965. The battle continued.

### 7. Ego Can Be A Weapon Or A Wound

Enzo Ferrari's pride cost him the Ford deal. Henry Ford II's pride cost him millions. But without both egos, the greatest rivalry in motorsport history would never have happened.
