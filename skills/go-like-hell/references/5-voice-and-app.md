# 5 — Voice & App

> Source: *Go Like Hell* by A. J. Baime.

## Voice

Baime writes like a sports journalist covering a war. The book is cinematic, fast-paced, and filled with larger-than-life characters. He makes you smell the gasoline and hear the roar of the V8s.

> **Case: The Ferrari Factory Visit** (Part I): The Ford delegation expected a formal corporate meeting. Enzo Ferrari met them in his courtyard, wearing a dark suit. "He was not looking at them as a business. He was looking at them as his children." The cultural clash was immediate. The deal collapsed.
> **Key takeaway**: When cultures clash over identity rather than economics, no amount of money can fix it.

> **Case: The Test Driver's Death** (Part III): A Ford test driver was killed at the proving grounds during GT40 development. "The car flipped, and the driver died instantly. There was no time to mourn. The program needed to continue." This was the real cost of racing in the 1960s.
> **Key takeaway**: High-stakes endeavors cost real sacrifices. Honor them by succeeding.

## Five Scenarios

### 1. The CEO Facing a Humiliating Setback

Ford's response to the failed Ferrari deal: "We'll beat them at Le Mans." A bold public response to a private humiliation. The bolder, the better.

### 2. The Leader Integrating an Acquisition

Ford tried to buy Ferrari and failed. If you succeed in an acquisition, don't make Ford's mistake — don't try to force them into your mold.

### 3. The Product Team That Needs to Catch Up

The GT40 failed in 1964 and 1965. The failures were tuition. Plan for at least two failures before success.

### 4. The Rebel Genius in a Corporate Job

Shelby needed Ford. Ford needed Shelby. If you're the rebel, find a sponsor. If you're the corporation, protect the rebel from your own bureaucracy.

### 5. The Champion Who Was Denied

Ken Miles drove 24 hours, led the race, was told to slow for the photo finish — and lost the victory. The system failed the right person. Fight for your people when the rules are unjust.
