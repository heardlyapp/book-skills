# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *Go Like Hell* by A. J. Baime.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The Rivalry Assessment

Don't panic after a competitor's move. Ford's mistake was assuming buying Ferrari would be easy. The real answer was to build something better.

### 2. The Cross-Culture Bridge

When two cultures clash: acknowledge the difference. Don't try to eliminate it. The best results come from combining both.

> **Case: The Ferrarization of Ford** (Part II): Ford was a bureaucratic corporation. Ferrari was a racing passion. Ford's people wore suits. Ferrari's people wore grease-stained coveralls. The program succeeded only when Ford stopped trying to make the racing team fit the corporate mold.

### 3. The Driver's Focus Protocol

Commit fully. Trust your training. Accept the risk. The best drivers at Le Mans pushed hardest at the right moments, not all the time.

> **Case: Ken Miles at Le Mans 1966** (Part IV): Miles drove 24 hours straight, pulling away from the Ferraris mile by mile. He was the fastest car on the track. But he was told to slow down for a photo finish that cost him the victory. He did what he was told. That cost him his win.

### 4. The Underdog Hiring Strategy

When you can't compete on resources, compete on talent. Hire misfits. Protect them from the machine.

### 5. The Two-Failure Rule

Plan for two failures before success. The GT40 failed in 1964 and 1965. By 1966, the team had learned enough to win. Each failure taught something specific. The 1964 brake failures led to redesigned brakes. The 1965 engine failures led to the switch to the 289. Failure you plan for is tuition. Failure you don't expect is a crisis.
