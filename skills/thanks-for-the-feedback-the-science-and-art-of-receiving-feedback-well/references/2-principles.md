# Principles: Understanding and Blind Spots

> Source: Thanks for the Feedback, Chapters 3-5

## Shift from "That's Wrong" to "Tell Me More"

The first move when facing feedback: get curious. Before evaluating, understand.

> **Case: The Surgeon's Mistake** (Chapter 3): A surgeon is told he's intimidating. His instinct: "I'm not intimidating — I'm just direct." Stone and Heen advise: instead of defending, ask "Can you tell me about a specific time I came across as intimidating?" This shifts from debate to learning.

> **Key takeaway**: "Tell me more" is the most powerful feedback phrase. It doesn't mean you agree — it means you're trying to understand.

## Blind Spots

The gap between your intent and your impact. You can't see your own blind spots — that's the whole point.

> **Case: The Helpful Colleague** (Chapter 4): A colleague repeatedly offers suggestions. She sees herself as helpful. Her colleagues experience her as condescending. Both perspectives are real. Her blind spot is that she's unaware of how her "help" lands.

> **Key takeaway**: Blind spots aren't your fault — but closing them is your responsibility.

## Switchtracking

When a feedback conversation derails into a different argument.

> **Case: The Late Report** (Chapter 5): A manager says "your report was late." The employee responds "but you didn't give me the data until Tuesday." Now they're arguing about Tuesday, not the late report. Switchtrack.

> **Key takeaway**: Blind spots are invisible by definition. That's why feedback is essential — it's the only way to see what you can't see.
