# Core Framework: The Three Triggers

> Source: Thanks for the Feedback, Introduction and Chapters 1-2

## Why Feedback Is Hard

Feedback triggers an automatic threat response. Stone and Heen identify three specific triggers, each requiring a different response.

> **Case: The Different Realities** (Introduction): Two people experience the same interaction and describe it completely differently. "You interrupted me three times." "I didn't interrupt." Both are telling their truth. This gap is where the truth trigger fires — and why the first skill is recognizing that your perspective is partial.

> **Key takeaway**: The first step is recognizing that your truth is not THE truth. Your perspective is limited. Understanding feedback requires curiosity, not defense.

## The Three Triggers

Truth trigger (the feedback is wrong), relationship trigger (you're not the one to tell me), identity trigger (this means I'm a bad person).

> **Case: The CEO and the Coach** (Chapter 2): A CEO receives feedback from an executive coach. Truth trigger: "that's not what happened." Relationship trigger: "you weren't even in the room." Identity trigger: "you're saying I'm a bad leader." All three fire at once, making it impossible to hear the feedback.

> **Case: The Unaware Parent** (Chapter 1): A mother is told her son finds her critical. Her truth trigger fires: "I'm not critical — I'm trying to help." Her identity trigger fires: "good mothers aren't critical." The feedback, intended to help, becomes a threat.

> **Key takeaway**: Most feedback activates all three triggers simultaneously. The skill is separating them and addressing each deliberately.
