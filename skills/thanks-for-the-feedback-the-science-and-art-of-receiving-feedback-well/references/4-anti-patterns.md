# Anti-Patterns: Feedback Traps

> Source: Thanks for the Feedback, throughout

## Defensive Escalation

When criticized, we escalate our defense. This makes everything worse.

> **Case: The Intensifying Argument** (Chapter 5): Manager: "You were late." Employee: "I'm never late." Manager: "You were late Tuesday." Employee: "Traffic was terrible." Manager: "Traffic is always bad." The original feedback is lost. Instead of "you were late", the argument is now about traffic.

> **Key takeaway**: Defensiveness escalates. The antidote: "I hear you. Tell me more." It's disarming and actually leads to understanding.

## Assuming Bad Intent

When we assume the giver means harm, the relationship trigger fires full-force.

> **Case: The Bad Delivery** (Chapter 6): A colleague gives blunt feedback. You assume they're being hostile. But they're actually trying to help — just incompetent at delivery. Your assumption of bad intent blocks any learning.

> **Key takeaway**: Assume good intent. Even when delivery is terrible, the intent is usually neutral or positive. This assumption makes feedback much easier to process.

## Letting Identity Collapse

One piece of feedback becomes a permanent verdict on your worth.

> **Case: The Failed Project** (Chapter 7): After one failed project, a team member concludes "I'm not cut out for this work." One negative feedback becomes a permanent identity. The feedback was about the project, but the collapse was about the person.

> **Key takeaway**: One data point is not a verdict. Your identity can absorb feedback without collapsing if you cultivate a growth perspective.
