> Source: The Boy in the Striped Pajamas, John Boyne (2006).

# Techniques — Seeing Through a Child's Eyes, the Fence as Meeting Place, and the Resistance of Innocence

## Practical Methods

> **Case: The Dinner with the Fury** (Chapter 7): Hitler comes to dinner at Bruno's house. Bruno does not understand who he is. He thinks the Führer is a boring dinner guest. He is unimpressed. The domesticity of the scene — a man in a uniform sitting at a table, eating soup, making small talk — is the novel's most effective technique for showing the banality of evil. Evil is not performed by monsters who look like monsters. It is performed by ordinary people who look like your neighbors.
> **Key takeaway**: When you imagine evil, you imagine something monstrous. The reality: evil is ordinary people doing ordinary things while extraordinary horrors happen with their consent.

> **Case: The Grandmother's Dissent** (Chapter 5): Bruno's grandmother is the only adult who speaks out against the regime. At a family dinner, she humiliates Bruno's father for his Nazi uniform. "I have not raised my son to be a — " She does not finish the sentence. She is never invited back. Her dissent costs her her relationship with her son. But she is the only one who speaks. The novel shows: speaking out is costly. Silence is complicity. There is no comfortable middle ground.
> **Key takeaway:** The person who speaks truth to power often pays a price. But the person who stays silent pays a different price — the loss of their own conscience.

> **Case: The Unanswered Questions** (Throughout): Bruno repeatedly asks questions that no one answers. "Why are all those people on the other side of the fence?" "What did they do?" "Why is it so different over there?" He never gets answers. This is the novel's technique for implicating the reader: the questions Bruno asks are the questions everyone should have asked. The answers were not given because the answers were unbearable.
> **Key takeaway**: Continue asking questions even when no one answers. The questions themselves are resistance. The refusal to stop asking is the refusal to accept that things must be the way they are.
