> Source: The Boy in the Striped Pajamas, John Boyne (2006).

# Key Principles — Friendship Across the Fence, Shared Humanity, and the Refusal to Learn Hatred

## Core Principles

> **Case: The Shared Birthday** (Chapter 10): Bruno and Shmuel discover they share the same birthday — April 15. They are both nine years old. Bruno asks: "How can we have the same birthday if we are on different sides of the fence?" Shmuel does not have an answer. Neither does the reader. The question cuts to the heart of the novel: if we are born on the same day, how can we be so different? The answer: we are not. The difference was created by adults. It is not real.
> **Key takeaway**: The most powerful argument against prejudice is not a theory. It is discovering that the person on the other side of the fence has the same birthday as you. Sameness is deeper than difference.

> **Case: The Chocolate** (Chapter 12): Bruno brings Shmuel food. He does not see it as charity. He sees it as sharing. For Shmuel, who is starving, the food is survival. For Bruno, it is what friends do. The sharing is natural. Bruno does not need to be taught to share. He needs to be taught not to. The novel suggests that generosity is our natural state. Prejudice is learned.
> **Key takeaway**: Children share naturally. They do not ask if the other person deserves food. They see hunger and respond. We learn to withhold as we grow up. Unlearn it.

> **Case: The Boy Without a Name** (Chapter 14): Shmuel tells Bruno he cannot remember his own last name. He has been in the camp so long that his identity has been erased. He has a number. He has striped pajamas. He does not have a name. Bruno is horrified. "Everyone has a name," he says. The dehumanization of the camp is complete when a person forgets who they are. Bruno's refusal to accept this is the novel's moral center.
> **Key takeaway**: Dehumanization begins with the erasure of names. The numbers tattooed on arms, the uniforms that replace clothing — these are tools of destruction. Remember people's names. It is the simplest act of resistance.
