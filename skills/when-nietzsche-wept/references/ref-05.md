# Reference: Nietzsche's Philosophy

## Overview
The novel brings Nietzsche's philosophy to life. The ideas are not abstract — they are the tools of healing.

## Case: Eternal Recurrence
The thought experiment: if you had to live your life again and again, exactly as it is, would you rejoice? Nietzsche challenges Breuer: if you had to live with Bertha forever, would you still want her?

## Case: Amor Fati
Love your fate. Not acceptance — active love. Breuer must learn to love his life as it is, not as he imagined it.

## Case: The Will to Power
The drive to become more, to overcome, to create. Breuer must overcome his obsession. Nietzsche must overcome his despair.

## Case: Becoming Who You Are
Nietzsche's most famous injunction. Not becoming someone else — becoming who you already are. The work is uncovering, not inventing.

## Case: The Abyss
When you stare into the abyss, the abyss stares back. Both men have stared into despair. They have not been destroyed. They have been transformed.

## Case: The Hammer
Nietzsche philosophized with a hammer. He broke the idols of morality, religion, and tradition. But he could not break his own despair. It took a friend to do that.

## Case: The Gift
Nietzsche gives Breuer the gift of eternal recurrence. Breuer gives Nietzsche the gift of connection. Each receives what they need.
Nietzsche's philosophy in the novel is not theory — it is survival. The ideas save his life because he finally has someone to share them with.
The philosopher who said God is dead found salvation in a friend.
