# Reference: Josef Breuer

## Overview
Josef Breuer is the novel's gateway character. A successful Viennese physician, he is in midlife crisis, obsessed with a former patient, unable to face his own mortality.

## Case: The Obsession
Breuer cannot stop thinking about Bertha Pappenheim (Anna O.). He remembers her voice, her body, her words. His wife notices. He is ashamed. He cannot stop.

## Case: The Talking Cure
Breuer discovered that patients get better by talking. Bertha called it "chimney sweeping." This is the foundation of psychoanalysis. But Breuer cannot apply it to himself.

## Case: The Meeting
Lou Salomé summons Breuer to Venice. She asks him to treat Nietzsche. Breuer is intrigued — and afraid. He senses this patient will be different.

## Case: The Pact
Breuer agrees to treat Nietzsche's migraines. Nietzsche agrees to treat Breuer's despair. Breuer thinks he is the doctor. He does not realize he is also the patient.

## Case: The Transformation
Through Nietzsche, Breuer faces his obsession. He admits his despair. He confronts his mortality. He returns to his wife. He is healed.

## Case: The Dream
Breuer has a dream about Bertha. He interprets it with Nietzsche's help. The dream reveals his fear of death and his longing for a life not lived. The interpretation is the turning point.

## Case: The Return
Breuer returns to Martha. He is present. He is no longer obsessed. He has faced his despair and chosen his life.
Breuer is the novel's proof that even the healer needs healing. His journey from doctor to patient — and back — is the arc that makes the novel work.
