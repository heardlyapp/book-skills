# Anti-Patterns: What Not to Do

## Overview

Axe identifies specific mental traps that keep people stuck. Each anti-pattern is rooted in a limiting belief that must be identified, challenged, and replaced.

## Anti-Pattern 1: Accepting Labels as Identity

When someone tells you who you are and you accept it without question.

**Case — Axe's teacher:** Mrs. Nobel's laugh nearly destroyed his medical career. The "not smart" label was reinforced by his GPA and ADHD diagnosis. **Case — IT Cosmetics investor:** Told Lima "no one would buy makeup from someone who looked like her." She chose to disbelieve it. L'Oréal bought the company for $1.2B.

## Anti-Pattern 2: Measuring Worth by Accomplishments

**Case — Lance Armstrong:** Seven Tour titles. Massive fame, wealth, influence. The doping scandal collapsed everything because character couldn't support the weight. **Case — Steve Jobs:** $10.2 billion, world-changing products, but told his biographer: "I wanted my kids to know me. I wasn't always there for them."

## Anti-Pattern 3: Avoiding Self-Awareness

**Case — Tasha Eurich's research:** 90% think they're self-aware; only 10-15% are. **Case — Axe's deadlift injury:** His wife showed him he was using poor form. His pride had prevented him from accepting her help.

## Anti-Pattern 4: Going It Alone

**Case — Axe's unfulfilled college years:** Before finding a mentor and community, he wasted time on parties and felt directionless. **Case — Dunbar's number:** Humans maintain 150 relationships max, only 5 in the core group. Many people try to go through life with none.

## Anti-Pattern 5: Living by Opinions Instead of Principles

**Case — SXSW panel:** Asked how to know what's true about nutrition, another doctor said "follow the latest science." Axe disagreed: "Follow principles." Research can be biased and outdated. Principles don't change.

## Anti-Pattern 6: Letting Fear Paralyze You

**Case — Axe's spinal infection:** Couldn't walk for a year, doctors warned of permanent disability. He chose hope over fear. **Case — Carol Dweck's fifth graders:** Children praised for intelligence gave up on difficult problems. Those praised for effort persisted.
