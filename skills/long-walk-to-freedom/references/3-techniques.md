# Reference 3: Key Techniques — Negotiation, Non-Violent Resistance, Endurance

> Source: Long Walk to Freedom, Chapters 51, 88-92, 104

## Principled Negotiation

Mandela's approach to negotiation was forged over four years of secret talks with the apartheid government, from his first meeting with Kobie Coetsee in 1985 to his release in 1990. He developed a framework that remains a masterclass in how to negotiate from a position of apparent weakness.

**Step 1: Establish minimum conditions before entering talks.** Mandela refused conditional release five times. *"Only free men can negotiate. Prisoners cannot enter into contracts."* This was not stubbornness — it was strategic positioning. By refusing to negotiate as a prisoner, he forced the government to accept that talks would happen on a more equal footing.

**Step 2: Use intermediaries.** The Eminent Persons Group (EPG) of the Commonwealth — led by General Olusegun Obasanjo and former Australian PM Malcolm Fraser — became a channel when direct talks were impossible. Mandela used the EPG to convey his positions to both the government and the ANC in exile, creating a feedback loop without committing either side.

**Step 3: Create a fait accompli.** When Mandela decided to begin talks, he did not seek permission from his colleagues in Lusaka. *"There are times when a leader must move out ahead of the flock, go off in a new direction, confident that he is leading his people the right way."* He knew that asking for approval would invite debate and delay. He acted first and explained later.

**Step 4: Prepare meticulously.** Mandela wrote a detailed memorandum to President P. W. Botha in 1989 laying out the ANC's views on every major issue: armed struggle, negotiations, the role of the Communist Party, minority rights, and majority rule. This document became the basis for all subsequent discussions. He did not improvise — he prepared.

**Step 5: Know what you want — and what they want.** Mandela understood that the government's primary fear was not Black majority rule but the loss of white identity and security. He addressed this directly: *"Whites are fellow South Africans, and we want them to feel safe and to know that we appreciate the contribution that they have made toward the development of this country."* He negotiated not to defeat the other side but to address their deepest anxieties while securing his own core demands.

> **Case:** When Mandela met President P. W. Botha in 1989, he wore a suit — not prison clothes — and treated Botha with the formal respect due a head of state. But he did not waver on substance. He refused to renounce violence unilaterally and insisted that the ANC's demands be met before talks could progress. The combination of personal courtesy and principled intransigence created the paradoxical dynamic that made negotiations possible.

## Non-Violent Resistance and the Turn to Arms

Mandela was committed to nonviolence until the government made it impossible. The ANC had pursued nonviolent protest for 50 years — petitions, delegations, boycotts, strikes, civil disobedience — with no result. The Sharpeville Massacre of 1960, in which 69 unarmed protesters were killed, was the turning point.

*"It was only when all other forms of resistance were no longer open to us that we turned to armed struggle."*

Mandela co-founded Umkhonto we Sizwe (MK, "Spear of the Nation") in 1961. But he insisted on a critical distinction: MK would target military and infrastructure, not civilians. *"I did not want to destroy the country before we freed it."* The decision to use violence was reluctant and strategic, not ideological.

**Key lesson:** Nonviolence is not a moral absolute — it is a tactic. When it stops working, leaders must adapt. But adaptation must be principled. MK's focus on sabotage rather than terrorism reflected Mandela's conviction that the struggle was political, not punitive.

> **Case:** The 1952 Defiance Campaign was Mandela's first mass action as a volunteer-in-chief. Thousands of volunteers deliberately broke apartheid laws by entering whites-only areas, using whites-only facilities, and defying curfews. The campaign resulted in 8,000 arrests, but it also unified the ANC and the Indian Congress and demonstrated that mass civil disobedience could mobilize the population. Mandela served as a defendant in the subsequent trial, which became a platform for articulating the ANC's demands.

## Endurance as a System

Mandela's 27-year imprisonment is often described as an ordeal of will. But it was more than that — it was a system of practices designed to preserve sanity and purpose.

**Physical system:** Exercise every morning without fail. Jogging in place, push-ups, sit-ups, fingertip press-ups. He called it "the exercise regimen." It was not optional — it was as essential as food.

**Mental system:** Study. He earned his LL.B. degree in prison. He read law, history, politics, and literature. He organized classes for fellow prisoners. *"Education is the most powerful weapon which you can use to change the world."*

**Emotional system:** Community. He resolved disputes, shared his food, and maintained morale among fellow prisoners. He never allowed himself to be isolated from the group emotionally, even when physically separated.

**Spiritual system:** Optimism as discipline. *"Part of being optimistic is keeping one's head pointed toward the sun, one's feet moving forward. There were many dark moments when my faith in humanity was sorely tested, but I would not and could not give myself up to despair. That way lay defeat and death."*

**Practical endurance hacks from Mandela:**
- Make a calendar on the wall. Losing track of time is losing your grip.
- Sprinkle sand in the corridor so you can hear the guard's footsteps.
- Keep your cell clean — order in your environment creates order in your mind.
- Sing while washing in cold water — it makes the water feel less icy.
- Never let them see your eagerness. When a letter arrives, take it slowly.
