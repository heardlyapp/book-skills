# 4 — Emotional Challenges and Shame

> Source: "ADHD an A-Z" by Leanne Maskell

## Anti-Pattern: Rejection Sensitivity as Character Flaw

Rejection Sensitivity Dysphoria (RSD) is one of the most painful and least understood aspects of ADHD. Many people mistake it for being "too sensitive" or "dramatic."

> **Case: R is for Rejection** (Chapter R): "Rejection Sensitivity Dysphoria is an intense, overwhelming emotional response to perceived rejection, criticism, or failure. It can feel like actual physical pain. A boss's neutral email can trigger hours of spiraling. A friend not texting back can feel like abandonment." Maskell validates this experience as a real ADHD symptom.
> **Key takeaway**: RSD is not being dramatic — it's a neurological symptom. The emotional intensity is real and overwhelming. The first step is recognizing it for what it is. The second is learning strategies to manage it: naming it, pausing, and grounding before reacting.

## Anti-Pattern: Shame Spirals and Burnout

Shame is a constant companion for many undiagnosed or late-diagnosed ADHDers. The cycle of failing to meet expectations, internalizing that as a character flaw, and then burning out from the effort of masking is devastating.

> **Case: B is for Burnout** (Chapter B): "ADHD burnout is not just being tired. It's a complete collapse — physical, mental, and emotional exhaustion from years of masking, overcompensating, and fighting your own brain. Recovery requires rest, not more effort." Maskell distinguishes ADHD burnout from ordinary tiredness and emphasizes that the solution is rest and rethinking your life.
> **Key takeaway**: Burnout is not a failure — it's what happens when you've been trying too hard for too long. The cure is not pushing through but stopping. Learning to recognize the signs of burnout before it hits is a crucial ADHD survival skill.
