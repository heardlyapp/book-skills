# 1 — Understanding ADHD and the A-to-Z Framework

> Source: "ADHD an A-Z" by Leanne Maskell

## The A-to-Z Approach

The book is structured alphabetically to make it accessible for ADHD brains — short chapters, clear topics, easy to dip in and out of. Each letter covers a key aspect of adult ADHD.

> **Case: A is for ADHD** (Chapter A): "ADHD is not a deficit of attention — it's a challenge with regulating it. The ADHD brain has different dopamine processing. This means you can hyperfocus on something that interests you but can't focus on something boring. It's not about lack of willpower — it's about brain chemistry." Maskell reframes the core understanding of ADHD from the first chapter.
> **Key takeaway**: ADHD is not about being unable to pay attention — it's about being unable to control where your attention goes. Hyperfocus and inattention are both symptoms of the same underlying challenge: attention regulation.

> **Case: E is for Executive Functioning** (Chapter E): "Executive function is the management system of the brain — planning, prioritizing, organizing, initiating tasks, impulse control, and working memory. ADHD affects every single one of these. When someone with ADHD says 'I just can't start,' they're not being lazy — their executive function system isn't giving them the green light." Maskell normalizes executive dysfunction as a core ADHD experience.
> **Key takeaway**: Executive dysfunction is not laziness. It's a neurological difference. Understanding this distinction is the first step toward developing strategies that actually work, rather than self-blame for not being able to "just do it."
