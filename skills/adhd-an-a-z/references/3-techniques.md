# 3 — Executive Function Strategies

> Source: "ADHD an A-Z" by Leanne Maskell

## Working With Your Brain

Executive dysfunction is the most pervasive ADHD challenge. The book provides practical, ADHD-friendly strategies — not neurotypical advice repackaged.

> **Case: O is for Organisation** (Chapter O): "Traditional organization advice — filing systems, color-coded planners, daily routines — rarely works for ADHD brains. What does work is externalizing executive function: visual timers, visible to-do lists, alarms, body doubling, and reducing the number of decisions you need to make." Maskell provides practical alternatives to neurotypical organizing systems.
> **Key takeaway**: Organization for ADHD means making things visible and reducing decision fatigue. Out of sight truly is out of mind for the ADHD brain. The goal is not to have a perfectly organized system — it's to have a system that you can actually use.

> **Case: P is for Procrastination** (Chapter P): "Procrastination is not laziness for the ADHD brain — it's a paralysis caused by overwhelm. The task feels too big, too boring, or too vague. The solution is to shrink the task until it feels doable. Not 'write a report' but 'open the document and write one sentence.'" Maskell reframes procrastination as an executive function problem.
> **Key takeaway**: ADHD procrastination requires a different approach. The standard advice "just start" doesn't work because the ADHD brain literally cannot initiate without sufficient dopamine or structure. The solution is micro-steps, time-boxing, and external accountability (body doubling, timers, deadlines).
