# Reference: Mergers & Acquisitions

## Overview
Berkshire Hathaway is both an operating company and an acquisition vehicle. Buffett's approach to M&A is distinctive: he buys companies and never sells them.

## Case: The Acquisition Criteria
Buffett has stated his acquisition criteria publicly for decades: (1) large purchases (at least $75 million, now $1 billion), (2) demonstrated consistent earning power, (3) good returns on equity with little or no debt, (4) capable management, (5) simple businesses, and (6) an offering price. He rarely finds companies that meet all criteria. When he does, he buys them with cash and never sells.

## Case: The Management Retention
When Berkshire acquires a company, Buffett keeps the existing management. He does not impose Berkshire's culture. He does not fire people. He trusts the managers who built the business. This is the opposite of most private equity or corporate acquirers. The result: managers of acquired companies are eager to sell to Berkshire because they know their jobs, their culture, and their autonomy will be preserved.

## Case: The GEICO Acquisition
Berkshire began buying GEICO stock in 1976. By 1996, Berkshire owned 51% and acquired the rest. GEICO is a wonderful business: low-cost auto insurer, direct-to-consumer (no agents), with a sustainable cost advantage. Berkshire has owned GEICO for decades and compounded its investment at an extraordinary rate.

## Case: The Burlington Northern Acquisition
In 2009, Berkshire acquired BNSF Railway for $44 billion — Berkshire's largest acquisition. BNSF is a capital-intensive business, unlike Buffett's usual preference for asset-light companies. But BNSF has a wide moat: the rail network is irreplaceable. The acquisition demonstrated Buffett's willingness to invest in the right business even when it requires significant ongoing capital expenditure.
