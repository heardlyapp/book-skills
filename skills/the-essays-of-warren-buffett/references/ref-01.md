# Reference: Buffett's Investment Philosophy

## Overview
Buffett's investment philosophy draws from Benjamin Graham (buy below intrinsic value) and Philip Fisher (buy wonderful businesses). His unique contribution is combining both into a framework that emphasizes temperament as much as analysis.

## Case: Mr. Market
The Mr. Market allegory appears in Buffett's 1987 letter. Imagine you own a share in a business and a manic-depressive partner offers to buy or sell his share every day. Some days he is euphoric and offers an absurdly high price. Other days he is terrified and offers a terribly low price. Your job is not to be influenced by his mood but to take advantage of his foolishness. This is the stock market. Price fluctuations are opportunities, not threats.

## Case: See's Candies
Berkshire bought See's Candies in 1972 for $25 million. Since then, See's has earned Berkshire over $2 billion in after-tax profits. The business had a simple competitive advantage: a beloved brand in the Western US, loyal customers (who buy chocolate as gifts), and pricing power. See's taught Buffett the value of a wonderful business with a durable competitive advantage. He has said that See's was the most important investment he ever made — not because of its size but because of the lesson it taught him.

## Case: The Coca-Cola Investment
Buffett began buying Coca-Cola stock in 1988, eventually acquiring 7% of the company for $1.3 billion. By 1998, Berkshire's stake was worth $13 billion. The investment was based on Buffett's understanding of Coca-Cola's competitive advantages: global brand, distribution network, and pricing power. The investment demonstrated the power of buying a wonderful business at a reasonable price and holding it through market fluctuations.
