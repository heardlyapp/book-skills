# Reference: Accounting & Finance

## Overview
Buffett is a critic of conventional accounting, which he believes obscures the true economic performance of businesses. He has developed his own metrics for evaluating what matters.

## Case: Owner Earnings
Buffett introduced the concept of "owner earnings" to address the limitations of reported net income. Owner earnings = net income + depreciation/amortization - capital expenditures. This metric tells the investor how much cash the business is generating that can be distributed to owners. Many businesses with rising reported earnings have negative owner earnings because they require enormous capital investment. Buffett prefers businesses with low capital requirements.

## Case: Goodwill and Intangible Assets
Buffett has written extensively about the accounting for acquisitions. When Berkshire buys a company, the purchase price above book value is recorded as goodwill. Buffett argues that the subsequent amortization of goodwill (required under old accounting rules) was economically meaningless. He advocated for the current accounting treatment where goodwill is tested for impairment rather than amortized.

## Case: Look-Through Earnings
Berkshire owns large stakes in publicly traded companies (Coca-Cola, American Express, Apple) but does not control them. Under accounting rules, Berkshire reports only the dividends it receives from these investments, not its share of their earnings. Buffett introduced "look-through earnings" to show Berkshire's proportional share of the retained earnings of its investee companies.
