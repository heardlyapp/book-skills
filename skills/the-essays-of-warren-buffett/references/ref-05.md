# Reference: Berkshire's Culture

## Overview
Berkshire Hathaway is unlike any other large corporation. Its unusual structure and culture are direct reflections of Buffett's philosophy.

## Case: Decentralization
Berkshire is composed of dozens of wholly owned subsidiaries that operate with complete autonomy. There is no corporate headquarters in the traditional sense. There is no central HR, no central marketing, no central strategy group. The operating managers run their businesses as they see fit. Buffett's role is capital allocation and culture setting. This structure attracts exceptional managers who value independence.

## Case: The Annual Meeting
The Berkshire Hathaway annual meeting is the largest in the world — 40,000+ shareholders gather in Omaha every May. Buffett and Munger answer questions for 5+ hours. The meeting is part investment seminar, part shareholder celebration, and part cultural event. It is a testament to the loyalty Berkshire's culture inspires.

## Case: Succession
Buffett has been preparing for succession for years. The planned structure: a CEO (Greg Abel) who runs the operating businesses and a CIO (Todd Combs or Ted Weschler) who handles investments. Buffett has said that Berkshire's culture is strong enough to survive his departure and that the most important thing is to have managers who are committed to the culture.

## Case: The Munger Influence
Charlie Munger, Buffett's vice chairman and partner, has been instrumental in shaping Berkshire's culture. Munger pushed Buffett away from Graham's "cigar butt" approach (buying cheap, bad businesses) toward buying wonderful businesses at fair prices. Munger's multi-disciplinary thinking — he calls it "mental models" — has influenced Buffett more than any other person. The partnership between Buffett and Munger is the longest and most successful in business history.
