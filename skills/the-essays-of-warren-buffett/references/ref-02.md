# Reference: Corporate Governance

## Overview
Buffett is an investor first and a corporate manager second. But his insights on corporate governance — the relationship between owners and managers — are among the most influential in business.

## Case: The Owner-Manager Relationship
Buffett believes that managers should act as if they own the business. He evaluates managers based on how they treat shareholders: Do they communicate honestly? Do they invest retained earnings wisely? Do they resist the temptation to manage earnings? Buffett himself, as CEO of Berkshire, writes a letter to shareholders that is unusually candid, admitting mistakes and explaining his thinking.

## Case: Compensation
Buffett is critical of CEO compensation that is tied to stock price or short-term earnings. He advocates for compensation based on the long-term economic performance of the business. At Berkshire, the compensation of operating managers is based on the performance of their own business unit, not Berkshire's stock price. Buffett believes that stock options reward managers for market conditions they did not create.

## Case: Board of Directors
Buffett believes that board directors should represent shareholders, not management. He is critical of boards that are too cozy with management. At Berkshire, the board is small, independent, and meets without management present. Buffett argues that the most important quality for a director is business judgment, not celebrity or political connections.
