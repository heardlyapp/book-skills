## Core Framework Quick Reference

- **Arrested 1985**: Hinton was convicted of two murders based on flawed forensic evidence (bullet matching). The "expert" testified the bullets came from his mother's gun.
- **Death Row**: 30 years on Alabama's death row. Most of it in a 5x7 cell for 23 hours a day.
- **Friendship**: Hinton formed a "book club" with other inmates. They became brothers. Some were executed.
- **Forgiveness**: Hinton chose not to hate. He forgave the prosecutor, the judge, the "expert" witness.
- **Exoneration 2015**: Bryan Stevenson and EJI proved the bullet evidence was junk science. Hinton walked free.
- **Today**: Hinton speaks about criminal justice reform. He remains joyful and forgiving.

## Key Principles

1. **Hope is a choice.** Hinton chose hope every day for 30 years.
2. **Forgiveness is freedom.** Hating his captors would have kept him in prison even after his release.
3. **The system is not fair.** Race, poverty, and bad lawyering determine outcomes more than guilt or innocence.
4. **Find your people.** Hinton's friends on death row saved his life.
5. **Your spirit can't be imprisoned.** They could lock up his body, not his mind or heart.
6. **Laughter is medicine.** Hinton kept his sense of humor. It kept him alive.
7. **Never stop fighting.** Hinton never gave up, even when he had no legal options left.

## Anti-Pattern Summary

The core mistake this book corrects: **the belief that those on death row are monsters beyond redemption — when Hinton's story shows that humanity exists in all of us, and that the death penalty is an irreversible punishment for a system that makes fatal mistakes.**
