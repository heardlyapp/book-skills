# Techniques — Dealing with Adjusters, Bad Faith Claims, and Hiring a Lawyer

## Practical Methods

> **Case: The Adjuster Interview** (Chapter 5): The claims adjuster will call you. They will sound friendly and reasonable. They will ask for a recorded statement. Feinman warns: do not give a recorded statement without understanding your rights. The adjuster is gathering evidence to use against you.
> **Key takeaway**: You have the right to decline a recorded statement. Say: "I will provide a written statement after I have reviewed my policy and consulted with my attorney if necessary."

> **Case: Bad Faith** (Chapter 3): Bad faith occurs when an insurer unreasonably denies or delays payment despite clear coverage. Feinman explains: every state has a law requiring insurers to act in good faith. Violations can result in punitive damages. But you must prove the insurer acted unreasonably.
> **Key takeaway**: Bad faith is a powerful legal claim. But you need evidence. Your documentation — every call log, every denial letter, every delay — is the evidence.

> **Case: When to Hire a Lawyer** (Chapter 11): For small claims (under $5,000), it may not be worth hiring a lawyer. For larger claims, especially those involving bad faith, a lawyer is essential. Most insurance bad faith lawyers work on contingency — they take a percentage of the settlement.
> **Key takeaway**: If your claim is significant, consult a lawyer. Most offer free initial consultations. You have nothing to lose by asking.
