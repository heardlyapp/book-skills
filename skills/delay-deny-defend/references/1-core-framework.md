# Core Framework — The Three D's and the McKinsey Revolution

## The Model

Feinman's framework: insurance companies have adopted a systematic strategy of Delay, Deny, and Defend to minimize claim payouts. This is not accidental. It is the result of a deliberate business decision driven by McKinsey consulting.

> **Case: The McKinsey Revolution** (Chapter 4): In the 1990s, McKinsey & Company was hired by major insurance companies to analyze their claims processes. McKinsey's conclusion: claims departments should be treated as profit centers. Every dollar not paid to a claimant is a dollar of profit. McKinsey recommended segmenting claims into categories — those worth fighting, those worth paying, and those worth delaying. This advice transformed the industry. Before McKinsey, claims were processed relatively straightforwardly. After McKinsey, claims became a battleground.
> **Key takeaway**: The Three D's are not a bug. They are a feature. The system was deliberately designed to minimize payouts. You are not imagining the resistance.

> **Case: The MIST Program** (Chapter 6): McKinsey developed a system called MIST for segmenting auto insurance claims. Claims were categorized by complexity. Simple claims were paid quickly. Complex claims were fought aggressively. The system was designed to identify which claims would be worth fighting — and to fight them as hard as possible.
> **Key takeaway**: Insurance companies analyze your claim to decide how hard to fight it. If your claim is significant, expect them to fight it.

> **Case: The Three D's in Action** (Chapters 1-2): Feinman opens with examples: a family whose house burned down, an accident victim with serious injuries, a business owner whose property was destroyed. Each had valid insurance. Each had to fight for years to get paid. The stories are not exceptions. They are the rule.
> **Key takeaway**: The system works exactly as designed. The more you understand the design, the better you can fight it.
