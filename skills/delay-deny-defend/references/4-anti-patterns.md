# Anti-Patterns — Giving Up, Accepting the First Offer, and Trusting the Adjuster

## The Central Fallacy

The most dangerous assumption: that the insurance company will process your claim fairly. The system is designed to minimize payouts. The company that delays your claim is not broken. It is working exactly as intended.

> **Case: The Lowball Offer** (Chapter 2): Insurance companies often make an initial offer that is much lower than the claim's actual value. They count on the claimant being desperate, impatient, or uninformed. Many people accept. Those who negotiate often get significantly more.
> **Key takeaway**: Do not accept the first offer. It is almost always lower than what you are entitled to. Negotiate.

> **Case: Giving Up** (Chapter 2): The majority of people who file claims accept whatever they are offered. They do not appeal. They do not complain. They do not fight. The system is designed to exploit this. The insurance company makes more money from the people who give up than from the people they defeat.
> **Key takeaway**: The system counts on you giving up. Do not give up. The people who fight get paid. The people who accept get less.

> **Case: Trusting the Adjuster** (Chapter 5): Adjusters are trained to be friendly, sympathetic, and helpful. They are also trained to minimize payouts. The two are not contradictory. The friendly adjuster who seems to be on your side is still working for the company that does not want to pay you.
> **Key takeaway**: Be polite. Be cooperative. But do not trust the adjuster. They are not your advocate. You are your own advocate.
