# 3 — Study, Preparation, and Education

> Source: "Across That Bridge" by John Lewis

## Knowledge as Activism

Study is not separate from action — it is a form of preparation that makes action effective. Lewis emphasizes learning history and nonviolent philosophy.

> **Case: The Nashville Workshops** (Chapter 3 — Study): "Before we ever sat at a lunch counter, we trained. James Lawson led workshops on nonviolence. We role-played. We practiced being insulted, spat on, and beaten without reacting. This training was essential. Without it, the movement would have collapsed." Lewis shows that study and preparation are not optional.
> **Key takeaway**: The most dramatic acts of the civil rights movement were the result of disciplined preparation. The sit-ins looked spontaneous to outsiders, but they were carefully trained. Study and practice are what turn conviction into effective action.

> **Case: Learning from History** (Chapter 3 — Study): "I studied Gandhi and Thoreau. I learned about the long history of resistance to oppression. I came to understand that our struggle was not new — it was part of a global movement for human dignity." Lewis connects the American civil rights movement to a broader tradition.
> **Key takeaway**: Knowing history connects you to a larger struggle. It gives you models, lessons, and hope. The civil rights movement did not invent nonviolence — it learned from Gandhi, who learned from Tolstoy, who was influenced by the early Christians. We stand on the shoulders of those who came before.
