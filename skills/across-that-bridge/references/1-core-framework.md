# 1 — Faith and the Framework for Change

> Source: "Across That Bridge" by John Lewis

## Faith as Foundation

Lewis begins with faith — not necessarily religious faith, but faith in the possibility of change. Without it, no movement can begin. Faith is what makes action possible.

> **Case: The March on Washington** (Chapter 1 — Faith): "I was 23 years old when I spoke at the March on Washington. I believed then, as I believe now, that the arc of the moral universe is long, but it bends toward justice. I did not know if I would see the Voting Rights Act passed in my lifetime. But I had faith that it would happen, and I acted on that faith." Lewis grounds his philosophy in lived experience.
> **Key takeaway**: Faith is not certainty about outcomes. It is the willingness to act despite uncertainty. Lewis did not know when or how justice would come. He knew only that it was worth fighting for. This is the kind of faith that sustains movements.

> **Case: The Edmund Pettus Bridge** (Chapter 1 — Faith): "On March 7, 1965, I stood on the Edmund Pettus Bridge in Selma, Alabama. I had faith that we would cross that bridge. But I was beaten unconscious before I made it to the other side. It is easy to have faith when the sun is shining. The test of faith is whether you keep it when you are bleeding on the ground." Lewis's most famous moment illustrates the cost of faith.
> **Key takeaway**: Faith is tested in the hard moments. Lewis was beaten nearly to death on that bridge. He kept marching. This is the lesson: faith is not hope that things will be easy. It is the conviction that the struggle is worth it, even when it costs everything.
