# 4 — Truth, Action, and Nonviolence

> Source: "Across That Bridge" by John Lewis

## Anti-Pattern: Acting Without Truth

A movement that abandons truth loses its moral authority. Lewis insists that the civil rights movement won because it was right, not because it was powerful.

> **Case: The Power of Truth** (Chapter 4 — Truth): "We did not need to exaggerate or lie about segregation. The truth was enough. When Bull Connor turned fire hoses on children, we did not need propaganda — the cameras showed the truth. The nation saw it, and the nation was changed." Lewis argues that truth is the movement's most powerful weapon.
> **Key takeaway**: In the age of disinformation, this lesson is more relevant than ever. A movement based on truth has a moral authority that cannot be defeated by propaganda. Lies may win in the short term, but truth has the power to transform.

## Anti-Pattern: Violence as a Tactic

Lewis is unequivocal: violence is both morally wrong and practically counterproductive. It destroys the possibility of reconciliation.

> **Case: The Choice of Nonviolence** (Chapter 5 — Act): "Some people called for violence. They said we should fight back. But we knew that violence would destroy the movement. If we had responded to the fire hoses and police dogs with violence, we would have lost the moral high ground. The nation would have seen a riot, not a movement." Lewis explains the strategic wisdom of nonviolence.
> **Key takeaway**: Nonviolence is not passive — it is the most strategic choice. It denies the oppressor the justification for repression. It wins the sympathy of the undecided. It preserves the possibility of reconciliation. Violence would have destroyed all of this.
