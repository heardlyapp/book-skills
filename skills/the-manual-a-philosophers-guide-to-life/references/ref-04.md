# Reference: Relationships and Society — How to Deal with Others, Criticism, and Social Obligations

> *"The power of choice cannot be abducted or taken away. It is a fortress no enemy can conquer."* — Epictetus, Chapter 14

## Overview

Stoicism is often caricatured as solitary and cold, but Epictetus gives extensive attention to social life. The Stoic is deeply engaged with others — but from a position of inner freedom. You cannot control what others do, say, or think. You can control your own character, your responses, and your choices in every interaction. This is the foundation of Stoic social philosophy.

## The Fundamental Principle in Social Context

The dichotomy of control applies to relationships: other people's opinions, actions, and feelings are outside your control. Their praise cannot make you better; their blame cannot make you worse. What you control is your own conduct — whether you act with kindness, honesty, patience, and courage.

This leads to a radical form of social freedom: you can engage fully with others without needing them to approve of you. You can be helpful without needing gratitude. You can love without needing to be loved in return.

## Case 1: The Horse's Pride — On Claiming Merits Not Your Own (Chapter 6)

Epictetus uses a brilliant analogy: "If a horse, in his pride, were to say, 'I am handsome,' his pride might be excusable. But if you boast, 'I have a handsome horse,' you are claiming a merit that is not yours."

The point applies to everything external. Your wealth, your family background, your physical appearance, your social status — none of these are truly yours. They are accidents of fortune, not reflections of your character.

**The extension to relationships**: When you are praised for your connections, your status, or your accomplishments that depend on others, remember that the praise is not for you — it is for something external. The only praise worth receiving is for your character, which is entirely your own doing.

**The reverse**: When you are insulted or criticized based on your external circumstances — your appearance, your background, your lack of status — remember that the criticism has no bearing on your true self. It is like someone criticizing you for the color of the borrowed horse.

**Application in daily life**: In any social situation where you feel pride or shame about your circumstances, ask: "Is this truly mine, or is it on loan?" If it's on loan, your character is not on the line.

## Case 2: Socrates on Death — Even Death Is Nothing to Fear in Itself (Chapter 5)

Epictetus invokes Socrates as the model for facing the ultimate social judgment — condemnation by society. Socrates was condemned to death by the Athenian jury. He did not beg for mercy, did not grovel, did not compromise his principles. Why? Because death itself is nothing to fear; only a base life is to be feared.

> "If death were truly something to be feared, Socrates would have run from it."

Death is outside our control. The opinion of the crowd is outside our control. What is within our control is how we face death — with dignity, integrity, and courage. Socrates chose virtue over life itself.

**The social implication**: The fear of social disapproval is a form of fear of death — we fear the "death" of our reputation, our standing, our place in the group. But this fear is based on an error: it treats the opinion of others as if it were within our control. It is not. Socrates could not control the jury's verdict. He could only control his own integrity.

**Application**: When facing criticism, rejection, or social pressure to compromise your values:
- Ask: "Would I sacrifice my integrity to avoid this judgment?"
- Ask: "Is this person's opinion within my control?"
- Remind yourself: "I would rather lose their approval than lose myself"

## How to Deal with Specific Social Situations

### Criticism and Insults
From Chapter 5: When someone criticizes you, the disturbance is not from the criticism but from your judgment that criticism is harmful. If the criticism is true, it is a gift — it shows you something to improve. If it is false, it has no bearing on you.

**The Stoic practice**: When insulted, pause. Do not react defensively. Ask: "Is there truth in this?" If yes, thank them. If no, let it pass like wind.

### Dealing with Difficult People
Epictetus's approach: People act according to their character. The rude person is rude because they lack education in what is good. The liar lies because they have not learned to value truth. The thief steals because they have not learned to value virtue.

Your response should be: **pity, not anger**. The person who wrongs you harms themselves more than they harm you — they damage their own character. You feel the external effect, but they suffer the internal corruption.

### Social Obligations and Duties
Being a Stoic does not mean withdrawing from society. Epictetus emphasizes fulfilling your roles: as a citizen, a family member, a friend, a colleague. You act — not because you will get a particular outcome, but because the role demands it and virtue requires it.

**The key distinction**: You perform the action because it is right, not because of what others will think. Your motivation is internal, not external.

### Friendship
Friendship in Stoicism is based on mutual virtue, not utility or pleasure. A true friend is someone who helps you grow in wisdom and character. You value the friend for their character, not for what they can give you. And you are ready to let them go when nature calls — because all relationships are on loan.

## The Fortress of Choice (Chapter 14)

The most powerful statement in the Manual on human freedom: "The power of choice cannot be abducted or taken away. It is a fortress no enemy can conquer."

**What this means in practice**:
- A tyrant can chain your body but not your mind
- A critic can slander your name but not your character
- An enemy can take your possessions but not your virtue
- Society can reject you but cannot compel your assent

Diogenes the Cynic is Epictetus's example of this principle. When confronted by the tyrant, Diogenes was asked what he feared. He replied that he feared nothing because the tyrant could not touch what was truly his — his power of choice. The tyrant could take his body, but not his soul.

## Conversation Starters

- "Am I trying to control what others think of me?"
- "Is my peace dependent on someone else's approval?"
- "Would Socrates have changed his answer to please the jury?"
- "Am I acting out of virtue or out of fear of judgment?"
- "If I truly believed no one's opinion could harm me, how would I act differently?"
- "Am I pitying the person who wrongs me, or am I angry at them?"
- "Whose character is being damaged by this interaction — theirs or mine?"
