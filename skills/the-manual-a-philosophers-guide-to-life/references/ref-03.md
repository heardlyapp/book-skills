# Reference: Daily Stoic Practices — How to Apply Stoicism to Everyday Life

> *"Lay aside alibis. It is better to die poor, while free from fear and grief, than to live surrounded by riches and filled with anxiety."* — Epictetus, Chapter 12

## Overview

Stoicism is not a theoretical philosophy — it is a practical discipline meant to be lived every hour of every day. Epictetus's Manual is filled with concrete practices, habits, and mental exercises designed to build character. This reference organizes the key daily practices from the text.

## The Morning Meditation: Preparing the Mind

Epictetus does not explicitly describe a "morning meditation" in a single chapter, but the practice permeates the Manual. Before you face the world, you must prepare your mind for what you will encounter.

### The Premeditation of Adversity (Praemeditatio Malorum)

From Chapter 4 (the public bath): Before entering any situation, prepare yourself for what might happen. The Stoic runs through potential difficulties calmly, in advance, so that when they occur, the reaction is not surprise or anger but prepared acceptance.

**The practice** — Each morning, before stepping out:
1. Review the day ahead: meetings, interactions, tasks
2. Identify what could go wrong that is outside your control
3. Say to yourself: "I may encounter rude people, delays, unexpected changes, criticism, or failure"
4. Remind yourself: "None of these can harm my character unless I allow them to"
5. Set your intention: "Today I will act with patience, integrity, and kindness regardless of circumstances"

This is not pessimism. It is mental armor. The difficulties you anticipate will either (a) not happen — in which case, you have lost nothing, or (b) happen — in which case, you are ready.

### Case 1: Sam Torode's Foreword — The Discovery of Stoicism in Crisis

Sam Torode's personal story in the Foreword illustrates the power of these practices. During the 2008 financial crisis, Torode found himself browsing the philosophy aisle of a bookstore, anxious about the economic collapse and his personal financial future. He picked up a copy of Epictetus's Enchiridion and read the first lines: "There are things within our power, and things outside our power."

For Torode, this was a revelation. The financial crisis — the bank failures, the housing market crash, the loss of jobs — all of it fell into the category of things outside his control. His anxiety was based on attachment to outcomes he could not influence. By accepting this, he found peace not by solving the crisis (which he could not), but by changing his relationship to it.

**The lesson**: Stoicism is not a luxury for calm times. It is most powerful precisely when everything is falling apart. The Manual is a crisis handbook.

## Daily Practices from the Manual

### 1. The Reframe of Loss (Chapter 11)

When something is taken — a broken possession, a cancelled plan, a friends's departure — practice the traveler-at-an-inn reframe:
- "I don't say 'I lost it,' I say 'I gave it back'"
- Everything is borrowed. Be grateful you had it at all.

### 2. The Questioning of Impressions (Chapter 5)

Every time you feel disturbed, pause and ask:
- "Is this disturbance caused by the event itself, or by my opinion about the event?"
- "What would this situation look like if I had no attachment to the outcome?"
- "If I cannot change what happened, can I change how I judge it?"

### 3. Small Beginnings (Chapter 12)

Epictetus emphasizes starting with small things: "Begin with little things. A bit of spilled oil or a little stolen wine: say to yourself, 'This is the price of my peace and tranquility.'"

**The practice**: When something trivial goes wrong — you drop your keys, you spill your coffee, you miss a turn while driving — use it as a training exercise. Say: "I accept this disturbance as the price of my peace." Each small victory builds the muscle for larger ones.

### Case 2: The Disciplined Athlete (Chapter 29)

Epictetus draws a powerful analogy between the philosopher and the disciplined athlete. An athlete who wants to win at the Olympic Games follows a strict regimen: they train, they diet, they follow a schedule, they abstain from pleasures that would weaken them. They do not complain about the difficulty — they chose it.

In the same way, the Stoic philosopher must accept the disciplines of the philosophical life:
- You cannot learn what you think you already know
- You cannot make progress while seeking comfort
- You cannot be free while serving your desires
- You cannot complain about the challenges — you chose this path

> "You request the crown of victory, and you must train accordingly. You must obey the trainer, eat according to the rules, abstain from pastries, train at the appointed hour, both in heat and cold. You must not drink cold water, nor wine at will. You must place yourself under a trainer as under a physician."

**Application**: The philosophical life requires the same commitment as elite athletics. If you are not willing to do the work, do not claim the title. The practice is daily, disciplined, and sometimes uncomfortable. That is how growth happens.

## The Evening Review

At the end of each day, reflect:
1. Did I let my opinions disturb me about things outside my control?
2. Did I desire what was not mine to have?
3. Did I avoid what was within my power to face?
4. Did I act with virtue (wisdom, justice, courage, temperance)?
5. What can I improve tomorrow?

## Chapter 33 — A Code for Daily Life

Epictetus offers a concrete behavioral code:
- **Live simply** — do not seek luxury and extravagance
- **Laugh without excess** — cheerful but not foolish
- **Avoid oaths** — let your yes be yes and your no be no
- **Don't talk too much** — especially about your own affairs
- **Accept favors without servility** — receive graciously without obligation
- **Don't wallow in past pleasures** — nostalgia for enjoyment is a form of desire that cannot be satisfied
- **Don't advertise your philosophy** — let your character, not your words, show who you are

## Conversation Starters

- "What small thing happened today that I can use as Stoic training?"
- "Did I get disturbed by something outside my control?"
- "Have I done my morning preparation?"
- "What would I do today if I were truly indifferent to praise and blame?"
- "Am I living the philosophy or just thinking about it?"
