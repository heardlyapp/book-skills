# 2. Principles — War and Technology

> Source: "The Gun" by C. J. Chivers (2010). Principles from the history of automatic weapons.

## The 7 Principles

### Principle 1: Technology Drives Tactics
The machine gun did not just change how soldiers fired — it changed how they moved, hid, and organized. Trench warfare was not a choice; it was a response to defensive firepower.

### Principle 2: Simplicity Is a Form of Genius
Kalashnikov's AK-47 succeeded because it was simple enough to be manufactured in factories with low precision and used by poorly trained soldiers. Complexity is fragility.

> **Case: The Loose Tolerances** (Chapter 5): The AK-47 was designed with larger clearances between moving parts than Western rifles. This meant it would fire when clogged with mud, sand, or carbon fouling. The same design feature made it less accurate — but reliability mattered more.
> **Key takeaway**: In many contexts, reliability is more important than precision. A weapon that always fires is better than one that fires perfectly — sometimes.

### Principle 3: Weapons Outlive the States That Create Them
The Soviet Union no longer exists. The AK-47 does. The rifle has been manufactured in dozens of countries and will likely be produced for another century.

### Principle 4: Proliferation Has Consequences
The USSR distributed AK-47s without considering what would happen when those weapons fell into unwanted hands. The consequences — armed insurgencies, terrorism, crime — have shaped the post-Cold War world.

> **Case: The Afghan Stingers** (Chapter 7): The US provided Stinger missiles to Afghan fighters during the Soviet war. After the war, the US tried to buy them back. Many had already spread to other conflicts. Arming proxies has long-term consequences.
> **Key takeaway**: Weapons provided for one purpose often end up serving another. The law of unintended consequences applies powerfully to arms transfers.

### Principle 5: Bureaucracy Can Kill
The M-16's early failures were caused by bureaucratic shortcuts — changing the ammunition propellant without redesigning the rifle, skipping proper testing.

### Principle 6: The AK-47 Changed Who Could Fight
Before the AK-47, automatic weapons required training and logistics. After it, a child could be trained in minutes. The AK-47 democratized violence.

### Principle 7: A Rifle Is a Political Statement
Kalashnikov's design reflected Soviet values: simple, rugged, mass-produced, ideologically committed to arming the proletariat. The M-16 reflected American values: high-tech, precise, expensive, requiring maintenance and training.
