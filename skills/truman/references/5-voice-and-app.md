# 5 — Voice & App

> Source: *Truman* by David McCullough.

## McCullough's Voice

McCullough is a narrative historian at his peak. He writes with love for his subject but without hagiography. He shows Truman's flaws — his quick temper, his loyalty to old friends, his occasional pettiness — as well as his greatness. The reader comes away feeling they know the man, not just the president.

> **Case: The "Give 'Em Hell, Harry" Moment** (Chapter 14): During the 1948 campaign, a supporter shouted "Give 'em hell, Harry!" Truman replied: "I never gave anybody hell. I just told the truth and they thought it was hell." This is the quintessential Truman quotation — and it perfectly captures the man's character.

> **Case: The Morning Walk** (Chapter 18): After leaving the White House, Truman took brisk morning walks through the streets of Independence, stopping to talk to neighbors. "He was just Harry again," one neighbor recalled. The most powerful man in the world had returned to his roots with no evident sense of loss.

## Five Scenarios

### 1. The Leader Facing Impossible Choices

Truman's model: gather facts, consult experts, decide, don't look back. The atomic bomb decision shows that some choices are never clean — but they must be made.

### 2. The Underdog Proving Everyone Wrong

The 1948 election: everyone said he'd lose. He campaigned harder, not smarter. He trusted the people when the experts dismissed them.

### 3. The Person Taking On a Powerful Adversary

The MacArthur firing: sometimes the right thing to do is also the least popular thing. Truman's approval rating collapsed. History proved him right.

### 4. The Public Servant Surrounded by Corruption

The Pendergast lesson: you can work with imperfect people without becoming them. Your integrity is not determined by your associations.

### 5. The Leader Leaving Office

Citizen Truman: the ex-president who refused to sell his influence. He paid his debts, wrote his memoirs, walked his dog, and let history judge him.
