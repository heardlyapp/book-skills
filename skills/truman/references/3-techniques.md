# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *Truman* by David McCullough.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The Decision Making Protocol

Assemble all the facts → Consult the experts (Stimson, Marshall, the Joint Chiefs) → Make the call → Never second-guess publicly. The atomic bomb was decided in days, not weeks.

### 2. The "Let 'er Go" Commitment

Once the facts are in and the advice is heard, make the decision cleanly. Truman didn't agonize publicly. He decided. That allowed the country to move forward.

> **Case: The Potsdam Orders** (Chapter 10): Truman wrote in his diary: "I have told the Sec. of War, Mr. Stimson, to use the bomb so that military objectives and soldiers and sailors are the target and not women and children." The bomb was dropped on Hiroshima on August 6, 1945. Three days later, Nagasaki. Truman's decision was made with conviction — and in private, with anguish.

### 3. The Whistle-Stop Campaign

When everyone said he'd lose, Truman traveled 21,000 miles by train, spoke directly to the people, and trusted them to decide. The more he spoke, the more people trusted him.

> **Case: The 1948 Whistle-Stop Tour** (Chapter 14): Truman made 356 speeches in 33 days, often from the back of his train at 3 a.m. to crowds of farmers and factory workers. When the Chicago Tribune printed "DEWEY DEFEATS TRUMAN," he held it up and smiled. He had won because he trusted the people over the experts.

### 4. The Pendergast Tightrope

Work with imperfect people without becoming them. Accept their support. Don't accept their corruption. Truman's rule: "Never take anything that isn't rightfully yours."

### 5. The Post-Presidency Dignity Framework

Truman's model: (1) Don't trade on your office, (2) Live within your means, (3) Return to your community, (4) Write your story yourself, (5) Let history be the judge.
