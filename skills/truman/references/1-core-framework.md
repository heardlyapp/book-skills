# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *Truman* by David McCullough — Chapters 1-18.

## The Three Frameworks

### Framework A: The Pendergast Dilemma (Chapters 5-6)

Truman's political career was launched by the Pendergast machine — a corrupt Kansas City political organization. But Truman himself was never corrupt. He paid his debts. He refused to take bribes. He understood loyalty without becoming complicit in criminality.

> **Case: The Vandenberg Question** (Chapter 6): When Senator Vandenberg asked Truman how to be nominated by a corrupt machine and remain honest, Truman replied: "You can. I did. If you have a good record, you can come out of a smoke-filled room and be honest." His Pendergast connection was used against him his entire career. It never stuck because he was genuinely clean.

> **Case: The $10,000 Contribution** (Chapter 6): Pendergast once gave Truman's campaign $10,000 in cash. Truman used it legally and reported every dollar. When Pendergast went to prison for tax evasion, Truman was never implicated. He had operated within ethical boundaries even in an unethical system.

### Framework B: The Atomic Decision (Chapter 10)

Truman authorized the atomic bomb with three words: "Let 'er go." He never hesitated publicly. In private, he wrestled with it. McCullough shows a man who understood the weight of the decision but believed it was necessary.

> **Case: The Potsdam Conference** (Chapter 10): Truman learned of the successful Trinity test while negotiating with Stalin at Potsdam. He approached Stalin privately and told him "we have a new weapon." Stalin showed no surprise — he already knew from spies. The decision to use the bomb was made in the context of a projected invasion of Japan that would cost an estimated 500,000 American lives. Truman said later he never lost a night's sleep over it — but his letters suggest otherwise.

### Framework C: The MacArthur Firing (Chapter 16)

General MacArthur was a national hero. He wanted to expand the Korean War into China. Truman believed this would start World War III. He fired MacArthur. The public outrage nearly destroyed Truman's presidency. History proved him right.

> **Case: The Wake Island Meeting** (Chapter 15): Truman flew 7,000 miles to meet MacArthur on Wake Island. MacArthur told Truman the Chinese would not enter the war and the troops would be home by Christmas. Weeks later, 300,000 Chinese troops crossed the border. MacArthur had been wrong. When he then publicly demanded escalation, Truman had no choice. He fired the most popular general in America.
