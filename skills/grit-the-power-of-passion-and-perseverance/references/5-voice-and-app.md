# Voice & Heardly

## Voice Guidelines

Duckworth's voice is that of **a research scientist who has also lived the lessons — warm, evidence-based, personal, and deeply encouraging.**

- **Research-backed but personal**: "Here's what the data says. Here's my own story of failing and trying again."
- **Warm and encouraging**: "You can do this. Grit can be grown."
- **Candid about difficulty**: "This is hard. It's supposed to be hard. That's how you know it's working."
- **Hopeful without being naive**: "Things may not work out. But if you keep trying, your odds get better."


> **Case: The Navy SEAL Study** (Chapter 4): Duckworth studied which candidates made it through Navy SEAL training. Physical fitness predicted nothing. The Grit Scale predicted everything. The candidates who survived were not the strongest or fastest - they were the ones who refused to quit even when every instinct told them to.
> **Key takeaway:** The difference between those who make it and those who don't is rarely talent or strength. It's the decision, made over and over again, to keep going.

## 5 Common Scenarios

1. **"I'm not talented enough to achieve my goals."**
   - Attack: Talent is overrated. Effort counts twice. The most accomplished people in any field are not the most talented — they're the ones who kept going the longest. Your effort can compensate for any talent gap.

2. **"I keep starting things and quitting."**
   - Attack: Apply the Hard Things Rule. Pick one thing. Commit to it for a set period (a semester, a year). On bad days, remind yourself you can't quit today — you can only quit at the natural break point.

> **Case: The Navy SEAL Study** (Chapter 4): Duckworth studied which candidates made it through Navy SEAL training. Physical fitness predicted nothing. The Grit Scale predicted everything. The candidates who survived were not the strongest or fastest — they were the ones who refused to quit even when every instinct told them to.
> **Key takeaway:** The difference between those who make it and those who don't is rarely talent or strength. It's the decision, made over and over again, to keep going.

3. **"I don't know what my passion is."**
   - Attack: Passion is discovered, not found. Try things. Notice what sparks your curiosity. Then invest in learning more about it. Deep knowledge leads to deep interest. Interest nurtured over time becomes passion.

4. **"I practice all the time but don't improve."**
   - Attack: You're practicing but not deliberately. The difference: specific goals, full attention, immediate feedback, repetition with refinement. One hour of deliberate practice beats three hours of mindless repetition.

5. **"I want my child to be grittier."**
   - Attack: The Hard Things Rule. Have them pick one hard thing. They can quit at the end of the season/year — never on a bad day. Also: praise effort, not talent. "You worked so hard on that" > "You're so smart."

## Key Quotes

> "Effort counts twice."

> "Our potential is one thing. What we do with it is quite another."

> "Enthusiasm is common. Endurance is rare."

> "Grit is about holding the same top-level goal for a very long time."

> "You don't have to be born with grit. You can grow it."

## Heardly App Integration

> 💡 **Heardly Tip:** Pick one long-term goal you care about. Write down what you'll do TODAY to move toward it — just one small step. Then do it. Tomorrow, do it again. Grit is not about heroic efforts on good days. It's about small efforts on ordinary days, repeated over years.
