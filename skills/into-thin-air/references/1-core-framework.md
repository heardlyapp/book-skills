# The 1996 Everest Disaster

> Source: Into Thin Air, Chapters 1, 11-20

## Core Framework

### The Chain of Events

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| March 29, 1996 | Krakauer arrives in Kathmandu |
| April-May | Acclimatization rotation up the mountain |
| May 6 | Final push begins from Base Camp |
| May 9 | Climbers reach Camp Four (South Col, 26,000 ft) |
| May 10, 12:00 AM | Summit push begins |
| ~1:00-4:00 PM | Summit reached by various climbers — too late |
| ~4:30-6:00 PM | Storm hits; climbers caught high on the mountain |
| May 11, all night | Impossibly barbaric conditions; people dying |
| May 11-12 | Rescue efforts; survivors found |

> **Case: Rob Hall's Last Hours** (Chapters 18-19)
> Rob Hall, the most experienced guide on the mountain, summited with client Doug Hansen around 3:40 PM — far too late. On the descent, Doug collapsed and could not move. Hall stayed with him. He could have descended alone to safety. He didn't. Over the next 18+ hours, he was in radio contact with Base Camp. His wife, Jan Arnold, who was seven months pregnant, spoke to him by satellite phone as he lay dying at 28,700 feet. He said: "I'm not going to make it. I'm not being selfish. I'm just being realistic." His body was found on May 23.
> **Key takeaway:** Hall's decision to stay with Doug Hansen was heroic — and fatal. The question the book asks: was it the right decision? There is no easy answer. It shows that even the best guides are human.

> **Case: Beck Weathers — The Man Who Came Back** (Chapters 19, Epilogue)
> Beck Weathers, a Dallas pathologist, was left for dead on the mountain. He lay in the open for 18+ hours at 26,000+ feet, unmoving — presumed dead. His face frozen, his right arm frozen solid. Rescue teams passed him and assumed he was a corpse. But Beck was alive. He stood up and walked into camp. He lost his right hand, all the fingers on his left hand, and his nose to frostbite. But he survived. His story became the most miraculous survival narrative of the disaster.
> **Key takeaway:** Beck's survival is the exception that proves the rule: at extreme altitude, the human body can sometimes endure the unendurable — but usually does not. Never assume someone is dead at high altitude unless you are certain.
