# Anti-Patterns — Summit Fever and the Cascade of Failure

> Source: Into Thin Air, Chapters 13, 15, 18, 21, Epilogue

## The Fatal Errors

### 1. The Turn-Around Time Was Ignored
**Error**: Rob Hall's own rule: "Turn around by 2:00 PM no matter what." He broke it.
**Consequence**: Climbers were caught on the summit ridge when the storm hit.

### 2. The Fixed Ropes Were Not Installed
**Error**: The Sherpas did not install fixed ropes on the upper mountain before the summit push.
**Consequence**: The Hillary Step bottleneck — an hour-long wait at 28,840 feet without oxygen.

### 3. The Guide Became Indistinguishable from the Client
**Error**: Rob Hall did not "guide" — he became a climber seeking the summit.
**Consequence**: He was not in a position to make objective, leader-level decisions.

### 4. The Client Deferred Judgment
**Error**: Krakauer and other clients sensed problems but deferred to their guides.
**Consequence**: The guides' bad decisions went unchecked by the group.

### 5. The Rainbows and Unicorns Briefing
**Error**: The pre-summit briefing by Rob Hall was relentlessly optimistic.
**Consequence**: Clients were not mentally prepared for the possibility of turning back or the reality of the death zone.

### 6. The Survivor's Guilt Trap
**Error**: Survivors compare their outcome to others' and conclude they did something wrong.
**Consequence**: Psychological damage persists long after physical recovery.

> **Case: Scott Fischer's Choice** (Chapters 12, 15)
> Scott Fischer, the charismatic leader of Mountain Madness, was also caught high on the mountain. He had been sick during the climb. He was running late. He made the decision to go to the summit anyway. On the descent he became incapacitated and died at approximately 28,000 feet. His body was later found. Friends noted that Fischer's ambition — to compete with Hall, to build his guiding business — may have clouded his judgment.
> **Key takeaway:** Fischer's death illustrates the danger of competing to be the best — when being the best means being the one who takes the most risks. The mountain does not care about your business plan.

> **Case: The Anatoli Boukreev Controversy** (Chapters 17, Epilogue)
> Anatoli Boukreev, the Russian guide on Fischer's team, descended before his clients on summit day. Krakauer criticized him for this in Outside and in the book. Boukreev responded in "The Climb": he descended to get oxygen and be ready for rescue. He went back into the storm that night to pull climbers to safety. He saved lives. But his decision to leave his clients on the mountain remains controversial. The lesson: even a hero can be criticized for choices made under impossible conditions.
> **Key takeaway:** The Boukreev controversy shows that there is no "right answer" in the death zone. Every decision is a trade-off. Armchair criticism is easy; decision-making at 29,000 feet is not.
