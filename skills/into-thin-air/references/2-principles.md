# Commercialization and Ethics

> Source: Into Thin Air, Chapters 4, 6, 9, 21, Epilogue

## The Guiding Business

### Two Models

| Model | Rob Hall (Adventure Consultants) | Scott Fischer (Mountain Madness) |
|-------|----------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| Price | $65,000/client | $65,000/client |
| Approach | Strict, conservative, guided group | Free-spirited, individualistic |
| Sherpa ratio | 1:1 guide/client | More clients per guide |
| Pre-disaster reputation | Gold standard | Rise-and-shine, ambitious |

> **Case: The Bottleneck at the Hillary Step** (Chapters 12-13)
> The Hillary Step — a 40-foot rock face at 28,840 feet — became a bottleneck. Three expeditions (Hall's, Fischer's, and a Taiwanese team) converged simultaneously. The fixed ropes had not been installed properly. Climbers waited in line for over an hour at nearly 29,000 feet without oxygen. This delay pushed the entire schedule back by critical minutes. When the summit was reached at 1:00-4:00 PM instead of 12:00 PM, the window of safety was lost.
> **Key takeaway:** The bottleneck was a direct consequence of commercialization. In the old days, one team at a time climbed Everest. In 1996, it was a traffic jam. More people meant more time — and more time at that altitude is deadly.

> **Case: The Sherpas and the Work** (Chapters 5, 8)
> Sherpas do the real work on Everest: fixing lines, carrying loads, setting camps, cooking, cleaning. They endure the same dangers with less pay, less glory, and less medical care. Ngawang Topche, a Sherpa on Fischer's team, developed HAPE at Camp Two. He was reluctant to admit he was sick because sick Sherpas lose jobs. He nearly died. Krakauer's portrayal of the Sherpas' role raises questions: who gets the fame, who gets the pay, and who takes the real risk?
> **Key takeaway:** The commercial Everest industry runs on Sherpa labor. The disparity between Sherpas and western climbers — in pay, respect, and medical care — is a moral problem the industry has not fully addressed.
