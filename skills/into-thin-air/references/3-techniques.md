# High Altitude Survival

> Source: Into Thin Air, Chapters 7, 11, 13, 19

## The Death Zone

Above 26,000 feet (8,000 meters), the human body cannot survive for long. Oxygen is ~30% of sea level. Cells die. The brain deteriorates. Judgment fails.

### Altitude Illnesses

| Condition | Cause | Treatment |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| AMS (Acute Mountain Sickness) | Rapid ascent without acclimatization | Rest, descent, medication |
| HAPE (Pulmonary Edema) | Fluid in the lungs | Immediate descent, oxygen, Gamow Bag |
| HACE (Cerebral Edema) | Brain swelling | Immediate descent, dexamethasone |

> **Case: The Oxygen Problem** (Chapters 13, 17)
> Krakauer ran out of oxygen on the descent. He had used most of his supply on the way up due to the delay at the Hillary Step. At the South Summit, guide Andy Harris insisted that all the canisters were empty — but they were not. Harris's regulator was likely iced up, giving a false reading. He could not see that there was still oxygen. Krakauer and Mike Groom could not convince him otherwise. Andy Harris died on the mountain.
> **Key takeaway:** The oxygen system was the critical piece of equipment — and it failed in multiple ways: bottles ran short, regulators malfunctioned, judgment was impaired. In the death zone, every piece of equipment must work perfectly. One failure can trigger a cascade.

> **Case: The Storm's Arrival** (Chapters 14-16)
> The storm that hit on May 10 was described as a "rogue storm" — sudden, violent, unexpected. But was it really unexpected? Climbing teams had weather forecasts. The storm was predicted. The guides chose to go anyway. The storm dropped visibility to near zero, reduced temperatures to -40°F, made navigation impossible. Climbers who were still on exposed ridges were trapped. Those who were in camp were mostly safe. The lesson: the decision to start climbing was made before the storm arrived — and could not be unmade once it hit.
> **Key takeaway:** Weather decisions in the mountains are binary: go or no-go. The no-go decision is always safer. The "go" decision at 3:00 AM on May 10 was the decision that set the disaster in motion — even though the storm did not arrive until 15 hours later.
