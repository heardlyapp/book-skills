# Core Framework: The Value Proposition Canvas

> Source: Value Proposition Design, Chapters 1-3

## The Two Sides

The Value Proposition Canvas has two sides. The Customer Profile (right) captures jobs, pains, and gains. The Value Map (left) describes products, pain relievers, and gain creators. When the map addresses the profile, you have a value proposition. When it doesn't, you have a product nobody wants.

> **Case: The Disconnected Product** (Chapter 1): Products fail when entrepreneurs design features without understanding real customer jobs, pains, and gains. The canvas makes the gap visible between assumption and reality.

> **Key takeaway**: Start with the Customer Profile. Understand before designing.

## Customer Profile Elements

Customer Jobs: functional, social, emotional tasks. Pains: risks, obstacles, negative outcomes. Gains: required, expected, desired, and unexpected benefits.

## Value Map Elements

Products & Services: what you offer. Pain Relievers: how you alleviate specific pains. Gain Creators: how you deliver specific gains.

## Three Types of Fit

Problem-Solution Fit: does your solution address a real problem? Product-Market Fit: does the market want it? Business Model Fit: can you profitably deliver it? Achieve them in order.

> **Case: The Value Proposition Assessment** (Chapter 3): Osterwalde recommends assessing every value proposition against three criteria: is it relevant (customers care), is it unique (different from alternatives), and is it sustainable (can you maintain advantage)?

> **Key takeaway**: Fit must be achieved in sequence. Each is a prerequisite for the next.
