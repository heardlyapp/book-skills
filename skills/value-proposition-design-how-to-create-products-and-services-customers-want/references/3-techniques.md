# Techniques: Testing and Iterating

> Source: Value Proposition Design, Chapters 7-9

## The Testing Ladder

Test assumptions from cheapest to most expensive. Start with customer interviews to explore jobs, pains, and gains. Then test demand with landing pages or fake door tests. Then prototype for usability. Only then build an MVP to test willingness to pay. At each stage, decide whether the evidence supports continuing.

> **Case: The Customer Interview** (Chapter 7): The best tool for understanding the Customer Profile. Ask about current behavior, not ideal scenarios. Past behavior predicts future actions better than stated preferences. Don't ask "would you buy this?" Ask "how do you currently solve this problem?" The answers reveal real pains and gains.

> **Key takeaway**: Test assumptions from cheapest to most expensive. Stop when you have enough evidence to make a decision. Speed is everything in early-stage product design.

## Pivot or Persevere

After gathering evidence: pivot means change something fundamental (customer segment, value proposition, or business model). Persevere means continue with more confidence.

> **Case: The Pivot Decision** (Chapter 8): If customers confirm the problem but don't want your solution, pivot the Value Map. If they don't confirm the problem at all, pivot your customer segment or assumptions.

## Measuring Fit

Use real behavioral evidence: repeat purchases, organic growth, willingness to pay, and referrals. Don't rely on opinions or stated intentions.

> **Key takeaway**: The market votes with its wallet. Listen to what it tells you, not what you hope it wants.
