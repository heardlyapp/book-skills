# Anti-Patterns: Design Mistakes

> Source: Value Proposition Design, Chapters 2, 8

## Solution in Search of a Problem

Building the product before identifying the customer need. The most common startup mistake — and the most costly.

> **Case: The Feature-First Error** (Chapter 2): Entrepreneurs who start with a product idea and go looking for customers almost always fail. The canvas forces starting with the Customer Profile before the Value Map.

> **Key takeaway**: If you haven't completed a Customer Profile, you don't have a value proposition. You have a guess.

## Assuming Without Testing

Believing you know customers without evidence. Every assumption is a hypothesis that must be tested.

> **Case: The Founder's Bias** (Chapter 8): Founders who are sure they understand customers often resist testing. The book shows experienced entrepreneurs get the Customer Profile wrong without real customer interviews.

## Confusing Features with Benefits

Describing what your product does (features) instead of how it helps customers (pain relievers, gain creators). Customers buy outcomes, not features.

> **Key takeaway**: Every feature must connect to a specific pain reliever or gain creator. If it doesn't, it's probably not valuable to the customer.

## Ignoring the Business Model

Building a great product with no way to make money. Value + business model = success. Value without business model = hobby.

> **Key takeaway**: The Value Proposition Canvas connects to the Business Model Canvas. Don't design one without the other.
