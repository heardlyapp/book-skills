# 4. Anti-Patterns — Critiques of Siddhartha

> Source: "Siddhartha" by Hermann Hesse (1922). Common criticisms of the novel.

## Critique 1: Orientalism — The Western Gaze

**What it looks like**: Hesse, a European, writes about Eastern spirituality through a Western lens. The novel is not authentic Indian philosophy but a European romanticization of "the East."

**Why it matters**: Siddhartha synthesizes Hindu, Buddhist, and Taoist ideas in a way no actual Indian teacher would recognize. The novel represents what the West wanted Eastern philosophy to be — not what it actually is.

> **Case: The Buddha's Portrayal** (Part One): The Buddha Gotama appears briefly in the novel. He is portrayed as serene, perfect, beyond suffering — almost inhuman. As a character he is an ideal, not a person. This is consistent with Hesse's mythic style but also demonstrates the Western tendency to flatten Eastern figures into symbols.
> **Key takeaway**: Siddhartha is a beautiful novel about spirituality — but it is Hesse's spirituality, not authentic Eastern philosophy.

## Critique 2: The Female Characters Are Devices

**What it looks like**: Kamala is the only significant female character. She is beautiful, wise in love, and dies conveniently once she has served her purpose in Siddhartha's education.

**Why it matters**: Kamala has no inner life of her own. She exists to teach Siddhartha about love and pleasure. Her conversion to Buddhism and subsequent death by snakebite feel like plot devices rather than meaningful character development.

## Critique 3: The Novel Is Too Abstract

**What it looks like**: The characters are archetypes, not people. The prose is beautiful but the novel lacks the messiness of real life.

**Why it matters**: Some readers find Siddhartha too polished, too perfect, too removed from the complexity of actual human experience. The novel's beauty can feel like a limitation.

> **Case: Kamaswami's Character** (Part Two): The merchant Kamaswami teaches Siddhartha business. But Kamaswami is not a fully realized character — he is a type: the worldly businessman. He exists to teach Siddhartha about commerce and then disappears. The novel is not interested in his inner life.
> **Key takeaway**: Archetypal characters are a feature, not a bug, in mythic fiction — but readers expecting psychological realism may be disappointed.

## Critique 4: The Individualist Message Can Be Narcissistic

**What it looks like**: Siddhartha leaves his family, his community, and eventually his son — all in pursuit of his own enlightenment.

**Why it matters**: The novel's emphasis on individual spiritual seeking can be read as a justification for abandoning responsibilities. Siddhartha's enlightenment comes at a cost borne by others.
