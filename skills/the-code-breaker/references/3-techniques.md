# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *The Code Breaker* by Walter Isaacson.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The CRISPR-Cas9 Workflow

Design Guide RNA → Build into plasmid → Deliver into cell (via viral vector, lipid nanoparticle, or electroporation) → Cas9 cuts target DNA → Cell repairs the cut (either disabling the gene or inserting new DNA).

### 2. The Collaboration-Competition Balance

Doudna's model: publish early, share reagents, collaborate with unlikely partners (Charpentier), but also protect your intellectual property and file patents.

> **Case: The Cold Spring Harbor Meeting** (Chapter 11): Doudna presented her breakthrough results at a conference before publication. Zhang was in the audience and immediately went back to his lab to replicate and extend the work. Doudna could have withheld. She chose to share.
> **Key takeaway**: Open science accelerates progress even when it enables your competitors.

> **Case: The Rainbow of Papers** (Chapter 14): After Doudna's 2012 paper, three labs independently showed CRISPR worked in human cells — Zhang's (Broad), Church's (Harvard), and Doudna's own follow-up. They published almost simultaneously in January 2013, creating a "rainbow" of journal covers. The field exploded.
> **Key takeaway**: Breakthroughs rarely happen in one lab. Multiple groups converge from different directions.

### 4. The He Jiankui Red Flag Checklist

Motivation (glory, profit vs. medical need), Oversight (ethics board? regulatory approval?), Safety (off-target effects?), Necessity (alternative treatments available?), Transparency (secret vs. peer-reviewed). He failed all.

### 5. The Crisis Pivot Protocol

When Doudna pivoted to COVID: (1) Formed an immediate team, (2) Built in redundancy (every task had a backup person), (3) Moved quickly but carefully, (4) Collaborated across institutions.
