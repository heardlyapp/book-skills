# 4 — Anti-Patterns

> Source: *The Code Breaker* by Walter Isaacson.

## Common Errors

### 1. The "It Works in a Dish" Overconfidence

**The trap:** "If it works in a test tube, it will work in humans."

**Why it fails:** Delivery, safety, and off-target effects are enormous hurdles. CRISPR was proven in a petri dish in 2012. The first clinical trial approval came 7 years later. The gap between discovery and therapy is measured in decades, not months.

> **Case: The Alma Rosa Testing** (Chapter 25, COVID response): Doudna's team developed a CRISPR-based COVID test that worked beautifully in the lab. Scaling it and getting regulatory approval took months. The gap between "it works" and "it works at scale" is where many promising technologies die.
> **Key takeaway**: Lab success ≠ clinical success. The translation gap is measured in years, not weeks.

> **Case: He Jiankui's Secret Embryo Experiment** (Chapter 27): He edited embryos without telling the ethics committee that he was planning to implant them. When the babies were born, he announced the result at a gene-editing conference before publication. The secret operation was enabled by regulatory gaps and personal ambition.
> **Key takeaway**: The anti-pattern is going it alone, in secret, without peer review or ethical oversight. Science requires transparency.

**Why it fails:** Doudna published early and reaped the benefits of being first in the scientific record. Zhang patented aggressively and "won" the patent dispute. But without open publication, the field would have developed much slower.

### 3. The "Chinese Lab" Demonization

**The trap:** "He Jiankui's experiment was uniquely evil."

**Why it fails:** He was reckless. But many Western researchers had considered similar experiments. The difference was not morality — it was regulation. Weak enforcement, not bad intent, enabled He.

### 4. The Designer Baby Panic

**The trap:** "CRISPR means everyone will design perfect babies."

**Why it fails:** Germline editing is technically difficult, ethically controversial, and globally condemned. It will not happen casually. But it requires active governance, not just condemnation.

### 5. The Lone Genius Myth

Doudna did not discover CRISPR alone. She stood on the shoulders of decades of basic research by hundreds of scientists.
