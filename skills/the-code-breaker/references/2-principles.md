# 2 — Key Principles

> Source: *The Code Breaker* by Walter Isaacson — Chapters 1-33.

## The 7 Principles

### 1. Basic Research Precedes Applied Breakthroughs

Doudna was not trying to cure disease. She was curious about how bacteria fight viruses. The curiosity-driven discovery led to a tool that may cure hundreds of genetic diseases.

### 2. Science Is Both a Race and a Relay

Doudna and Zhang competed. They also built on each other's work. "In science, there is no single inventor. There is a web of discovery."

### 3. Patent Races Distort Scientific Progress

The Doudna/Zhang patent fight cost millions and delayed applications. "The patent system, which was designed to encourage innovation, can sometimes slow it down."

> **Case: Zhang's Expedited Filing** (Chapter 18): Zhang's employer, the Broad Institute, paid $100,000 for expedited patent review. Doudna could have done the same but chose not to. The difference in strategy determined who "won" the key patent.

### 4. Germline Editing Requires Global Governance — and We Don't Have It

He Jiankui's experiment was enabled by regulatory gaps. China had regulations, but enforcement was weak. The scientific community condemned him — but could not stop him.

### 5. The Public Must Be Part of the Conversation

"Science is too important to be left to scientists." The ethics of gene editing must involve everyone.

### 6. Women Face Unique Barriers in Science

> **Case: Doudna's Graduate School Advisor** (Chapter 3): Doudna's PhD advisor told her that women did not belong in science. "You will never succeed as a scientist. You should find something else to do." She considered quitting. Instead, she proved him wrong and used the experience to fight for other women.

### 7. Crises Accelerate Innovation

Doudna's pivot to COVID testing happened within days of the lockdown. The urgency of the pandemic compressed years of work into months.
