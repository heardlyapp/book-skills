# 5 — Voice & App

> Source: *The Code Breaker* by Walter Isaacson — voice and scenarios.

## Isaacson's Voice

Isaacson writes biography as a narrative of innovation. Like his books on Steve Jobs, Einstein, and Leonardo da Vinci, he tells the story through the lens of a creative genius. But unlike Jobs, Doudna is a collaborator, not a lone visionary. The book reflects that: more about teams, partnerships, and communities than individual brilliance.

> **Example: The Berkeley Campus Shutdown** (Introduction): "Berkeley, the university where she was a superstar for her role in inventing the gene-editing technology known as CRISPR, had just shut down its campus because of the fast-spreading coronavirus pandemic." Isaacson starts with COVID to show the immediate relevance of the discovery.

> **Example: The Mentor Who Said She Couldn't** (Chapter 3): Doudna's PhD advisor told her "women do not belong in science." Isaacson uses this moment to show her resilience — and to frame her career as a continuous response to gender barriers. "She considered leaving science. Instead, she proved him wrong."

## Five Scenarios

### 1. The Student Considering a Science Career

Doudna's path: from reading Watson's *The Double Helix* in high school to winning the Nobel Prize. Her story is proof that curiosity can carry you to the highest levels of science.

### 2. The Researcher in a Patent Race

The Doudna/Zhang fight shows: publish early, file broadly, collaborate across disciplines, and be prepared for the patent system to not reward the "right" person.

### 3. The Bioethicist

The central question: who decides where the line is on germline editing? He Jiankui showed that individual scientists will cross it. We need global governance, not just condemnation.

### 4. The Patient with a Genetic Disease

CRISPR cures are coming. Sickle cell disease is the first proof point. Clinical trials are underway. The gap between "promising" and "proven" is real, but narrowing.

### 5. The Citizen Trying to Understand Gene Editing

The CRISPR mechanism is elegant. Read Chapter 8 (the "Casper the Ghost" moment). It is the clearest explanation of CRISPR you will find.
