# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *The Code Breaker* by Walter Isaacson — Chapters 1-13 (Part I-CRISPR).

## The Two Frameworks

### Framework A: The CRISPR Mechanism (Chapters 5-8)

CRISPR is a bacterial immune system. When a virus attacks a bacterium, the bacterium captures a piece of the virus's DNA and stores it in its own genome — a "wanted poster." If the virus attacks again, the bacterium produces RNA copies of the stored DNA, which guide the Cas9 protein to the matching viral DNA and cut it. Doudna and Charpentier realized that this system could be reprogrammed: give it a guide RNA targeting any DNA sequence, and Cas9 will cut there.

> **Case: The E. coli Experiment** (Chapter 11): In June 2012, Doudna and Charpentier's team published the experiment that changed everything. They programmed a guide RNA to target the DNA sequence of a green fluorescent protein, "in vitro" (in a test tube). Cas9 cut precisely where directed. "When Doudna saw the results of the experiment, she stopped and caught her breath. 'It was one of those moments,' she later said, 'when you realize that you are seeing something that nobody else has ever seen before.'"

### Framework B: The Discovery Race (Chapters 9-12)

The race to turn CRISPR into a gene-editing tool was between Doudna/Charpentier (UC Berkeley) and Feng Zhang (Broad Institute/MIT). Both succeeded. Both deserve credit. The patent dispute was messy.

> **Case: The Patent Fight** (Chapter 18): Doudna and Charpentier filed their patent application in May 2012. Zhang filed in December 2012 but paid for expedited review, which Doudna did not. The patent office awarded the key patent (for use in eukaryotic cells) to Zhang. "The patent fight had no clear heroes. Doudna and Charpentier made the initial discovery; Zhang made the crucial advance of showing it worked in human cells." The dispute lasted years and cost millions. In the end, both sides were awarded overlapping patents.

> **Case: Emmanuelle Charpentier** (Chapter 7): The French microbiologist who co-discovered CRISPR-Cas9 with Doudna. Charpentier had been studying CRISPR for years, trying to understand how it works. She teamed up with Doudna in 2011 — a collaboration that was "unlikely" given their different backgrounds (microbiology vs. structural biology). Their partnership produced the key experiment. Charpentier won the Nobel Prize in Chemistry with Doudna in 2020 — but is often left out of popular accounts of the discovery.
