# Reference: The American Dream

## Overview
The play's central theme is the failure of the American Dream. Willy believed that success was guaranteed. He was wrong.

## Case: The Promise
Willy was told that being well-liked, hard-working, and personable would guarantee success. He believed it completely. He based his life on it.

## Case: The Betrayal
Willy is fired by Howard Wagner, the son of the man who founded the company. Willy helped name Howard. He is discarded without sentiment.

## Case: The Contrast
Bernard (Charley's son) succeeds through hard work and education. Biff (Willy's son) fails because he was taught that charm was enough.

## Case: Uncle Ben
Ben "walked into the jungle at 17 and came out rich." He represents the myth of sudden wealth. He is not a model — he is a fantasy.

## Case: The Lie
The American Dream is not a ladder. It is a lottery. Willy played by the rules and lost. The play asks: what do we owe those who lose?

## Case: The Obligation
At Willy's funeral, Charley speaks the truth: "A salesman is got to dream, boy. It comes with the territory." Willy was not allowed to stop dreaming, even when the dream was killing him.

## Case: The System
The play is not just about one man. It is about the system that creates Willys. The corporate structure that consumes and discards its workers.
Miller's critique is not of failure but of a society that has no place for those who fail. The American Dream is not a promise. It is a bet. And most people lose.
