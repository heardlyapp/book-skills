# Reference: The Requiem

## Overview
The requiem is the final scene. Willy's funeral. Only his family attends.

## Case: The Funeral
Charley, Bernard, Linda, Biff, and Happy are at the grave. No one from Willy's company. No customers. No friends. Willy's life amounted to nothing that the world noticed.

## Case: Charley's Eulogy
"He was a salesman." Charley speaks the truth: "A salesman is got to dream, boy. It comes with the territory." Willy's profession required him to live in delusion.

## Case: Biff's Epiphany
Biff understands his father: "He had the wrong dreams. All, all, wrong." Biff will not repeat his father's mistakes. He will live in reality.

## Case: Happy's Denial
Happy insists: "I'm gonna win it for him." Happy will continue the dream. He has learned nothing. The tragedy will repeat.

## Case: Linda's Goodbye
"I can't cry. I don't know what it is." Linda has been unable to cry. She has spent all her tears. She paid the last payment on the house. Willy can finally rest.

## Case: The Flute
The flute music returns at the end. Willy's father. The wilderness. The open road. The freedom that Willy never had. The music that haunts the play.

## Case: The Legacy
Willy's legacy is not money or property. The house is paid for — empty. The insurance money will go nowhere. What remains is Biff's understanding: "I know who I am."
The requiem is not sentimental. It is devastating. The play does not offer comfort. It offers truth.
And truth, Miller argues, is the only thing worth having.
