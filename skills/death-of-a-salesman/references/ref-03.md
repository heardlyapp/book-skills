# Reference: Biff and Happy

## Overview
Willy's sons are his hope and his heartbreak. Biff was the golden boy. Happy is the shadow.

## Case: Biff the Golden Boy
Biff was a high school football star with scholarship offers. Willy lived through him. Willy believed Biff would be a great success.

## Case: The Discovery
Biff goes to Boston to ask Willy for help. He finds Willy with another woman. His faith is destroyed. He never recovers.

## Case: Biff's Wander
As an adult, Biff works on ranches out West. He steals. He drifts. He cannot settle. He is trying to find himself.

## Case: The Reckoning
Biff confronts Willy in the play's climax. He forces Willy to face the truth: "I am not a dime a dozen! I am Willy Loman!" Then: "Pop, I'm nothing."

## Case: Happy's Tragedy
Happy is the younger son who never got Willy's attention. He is becoming his father: womanizing, lying, chasing the dream that does not exist.

## Case: The Comparison
Bernard, Charley's son, was a bookish child whom Biff mocked. As an adult, Bernard is a successful lawyer arguing a case before the Supreme Court. Biff is nothing.

## Case: The Final Moment
Biff weeps at Willy's grave. Happy refuses to give up. Happy says he will stay and win. Happy has learned nothing. The cycle continues.
Biff's journey is the only hopeful thread in the play. He finally accepts who he is. He breaks free of his father's dream. He will not be Willy.
Happy will continue the cycle. Biff has a chance to break it. The play ends with both possibilities.
