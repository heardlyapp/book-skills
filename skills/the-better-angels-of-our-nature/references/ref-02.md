# Reference: The Six Trends

## Overview
Pinker organizes the decline of violence into six major historical trends, each covering a different period and type of violence.

## Case: The Pacification Process (10,000 BCE → present)
The transition from hunter-gatherer bands to agricultural states was the first and largest reduction in violence. Archaeological evidence shows that hunter-gatherers and tribal societies had homicide rates of 10-60% of all deaths. States reduced violence by establishing a monopoly on force — the Leviathan. This is the most dramatic decline in violence in human history, and it occurred before written records.

## Case: The Humanitarian Revolution (1700s-1800s)
The Enlightenment brought a wave of moral reforms: the abolition of slavery, the end of torture, the abolition of cruel punishments (drawing and quartering, burning at the stake), the reduction of religious persecution, and the end of dueling. For the first time in history, people argued that cruelty was wrong regardless of whether the victim deserved punishment. This revolution was driven by literacy, cosmopolitanism, and the application of reason to moral questions.

## Case: The Long Peace (1945-present)
The great powers have not fought each other directly since 1945. This period of great-power peace is unprecedented in history. Pinker attributes the Long Peace to the nuclear deterrent (the "delicate balance of terror"), the growth of international institutions (the UN, NATO), the spread of democracy, and the economic incentives of trade and globalization.
