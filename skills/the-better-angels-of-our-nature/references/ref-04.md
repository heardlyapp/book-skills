# Reference: The Better Angels

## Overview
If humans have inner demons, we also have "better angels" — psychological faculties that reduce violence. Pinker identifies four.

## Case: Empathy
Empathy — the ability to feel what others feel — is the most obvious check on violence. If you feel someone's pain, you are less likely to inflict it. The expansion of empathy over time (from extended family to tribe to nation to all humanity) is one of the central stories of moral progress. The Humanitarian Revolution was driven in part by people imagining the suffering of slaves, prisoners, and animals.

## Case: Self-Control
Self-control is the ability to inhibit violent impulses. It is a cognitive faculty, located in the prefrontal cortex, that can be strengthened through practice. The civilizing process gradually strengthened norms of self-control: people learned to suppress their immediate aggressive impulses in favor of socially acceptable behavior. Pinker links the decline of violence to the Flynn effect (rising IQ scores), noting that higher cognitive ability is associated with better impulse control.

## Case: Reason
Reason is the most powerful of the better angels. When people reason about moral questions, they discover contradictions in their own positions and are forced to adopt more universal perspectives. The "escalator of reason" — the logical pressure to apply moral principles consistently to all people — drove the Humanitarian Revolution and continues to drive the Rights Revolutions. Reason, Pinker argues, is a force for peace.
