# Reference: The Five Inner Demons

## Overview
Pinker identifies five psychological systems that can lead to violence. Understanding them is the first step to managing them.

## Case: Predatory Violence
Predatory violence is instrumental — violence used to achieve a goal (food, money, territory). It is the most rational form of violence and the most common in the animal kingdom. In human societies, predatory violence is most visible in organized crime, pillaging, and conquest. The decline of predatory violence is largely the story of the Leviathan: states made predation unprofitable.

## Case: Dominance
Dominance is the drive to establish and maintain status through intimidation and aggression. It is common in male-male competition for status and mating opportunities. The civilizing process reduced dominance violence by creating alternative paths to status (wealth, education, professional achievement) and by stigmatizing physical aggression as low-status.

## Case: Ideology
Ideology is the most dangerous form of violence because it can override the other better angels. When a belief system identifies a group as evil, subhuman, or an obstacle to utopia, it can justify unlimited violence. Pinker argues that the decline of ideology-driven violence is partly due to the failure of utopian ideologies (communism, fascism) and partly due to the spread of skeptical, evidence-based reasoning.

## Case: Revenge and Sadism
Revenge is the urge to retaliate for perceived wrongs. It drives feuds, vendettas, and cycles of violence. Sadism is the pleasure of inflicting pain. Most people find the idea of enjoying cruelty incomprehensible, but historical evidence (Roman arenas, public executions, torture) shows that sadism was once more openly expressed. The decline of sadistic violence is a triumph of civilization — we have learned to suppress or redirect this impulse.
