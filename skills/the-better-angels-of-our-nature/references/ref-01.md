# Reference: The Historical Decline

## Overview
Pinker argues that violence has declined across human history by nearly every measure. The evidence spans archaeology, history, criminology, and international relations.

## Case: Homicide Rates in Medieval Europe
In medieval Europe, homicide rates were 30-50x higher than today. London in the 14th century had a homicide rate of approximately 50 per 100,000 per year. In 21st century London, the rate is below 2 per 100,000. This is not a result of better policing alone — it reflects a fundamental shift in social norms around violence. People in medieval Europe settled disputes with violence far more readily than modern people do.

## Case: The Decline of War
Interstate war has declined dramatically since 1945. The great powers have not fought each other directly since the Korean War (1950-53). Armed conflicts have shifted from wars between states to civil wars within states, and even those have declined since their peak in the 1990s. The per capita rate of war deaths is lower today than at almost any time in human history.

## Case: Genocide and Mass Killing
Genocide and mass killing have also declined. The worst episodes of the 20th century (the Holocaust, the Soviet Gulag, the Maoist famines) have no parallel in the post-Cold War era. The Rwandan genocide (1994) was the last major genocide. While atrocities continue, the scale and frequency have decreased. Pinker shows that the idea that the 20th century was an unusually violent time is based on absolute numbers — but per capita, past centuries were far more violent.
