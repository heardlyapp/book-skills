# Anti-Patterns — The Dogmas of Delhi

> Source: The India Way, Chapters 4, 5

## The Five Dogmas

### 1. Non-Alignment as an Identity
**Error**: "India must not align with any power."
**Correction**: Multi-alignment is the modern approach — align with multiple powers without being dependent on any.

### 2. Anti-Americanism
**Error**: "The US is inherently suspect."
**Correction**: The US-India partnership is India's most important strategic relationship.

### 3. Socialist Economic Orthodoxy
**Error**: "Globalization benefits the rich, not India."
**Correction**: India's economic growth came from opening up. The lesson: engage the world economy.

### 4. Strategic Romanticism
**Error**: "We can succeed through moral posture alone."
**Correction**: Moral authority without power is irrelevant.

### 5. The Nuclear Hesitation
**Error**: "India should not pursue nuclear weapons."
**Correction**: The delay in nuclear testing cost India strategic leverage for decades.

> **Case: The Dogmas of Delhi** (Chapter 4)
> Jaishankar lists the assumptions that constrained Indian foreign policy: that non-alignment was permanent, that the US was untrustworthy, that socialism was the path, that China could be managed through friendship, that nuclear weapons were immoral. These dogmas cost India decades. The 1991 economic reforms and the 1998 nuclear tests broke the mold. The lesson: question your assumptions before circumstances force you to.
> **Key takeaway:** Dogmas are comfortable — that is why they are dangerous. India's strategic failures (1962, slow economic growth, delayed nuclear status) were failures of imagination, not capability. The lesson: frictionless consensus is a warning sign.

> **Case: Of Mandarins and Masses** (Chapter 5)
> Jaishankar examines the gap between Indian elites (who tend to be Western-oriented, liberal, globalist) and Indian masses (who are more nationalist, pragmatic, and suspicious of global institutions). Foreign policy is no longer the domain of experts — it is shaped by public opinion. India's leaders must bridge this gap: foreign policy must be explained to the public, and the public's instincts must inform foreign policy.
> **Key takeaway:** The gap between mandarins and masses is a challenge — and an opportunity. Indian foreign policy has become democratic. The lesson: in a democracy, strategic clarity requires public consent.
