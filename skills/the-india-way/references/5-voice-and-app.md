# S. Jaishankar's Voice & Application Scenarios

> Source: The India Way, all chapters, Preface, After the Virus

## Authorial Voice

S. Jaishankar writes as a practitioner who has shaped the policies he describes. His voice is:

- **Analytical and dispassionate**: He presents arguments like a chess grandmaster analyzing positions.
- **Culturally rooted**: References to the Mahabharata, Thiruvalluvar, Satyajit Ray, and chess.
- **Politically astute**: He knows how policy really gets made — not just how it is supposed to get made.
- **Confidently Indian**: The book is unapologetically from an Indian perspective. He does not explain India to the West — he explains the world to India.

### Classic Jaishankar Quotes

- "The heaviest penalty for declining to rule is to be ruled by someone inferior." (quoting Plato)
- "Strategic complacency is a luxury no rising power can afford."
- "Multi-alignment is not a slogan — it is the reality of a multipolar world."
- "In a flatter world, disruption is the new normal."
- "The Nimzo-Indian Defence works because it does not overcommit."

## Five Application Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Business Leader in a Competitive Industry
Your market is being disrupted. Competitors are rising.
**Lesson**: Nimzo-Indian Defence — flexible, indirect, patient. Do not confront your competitor head-on. Build partnerships. Find your strategic niche.

### Scenario 2: Policy Analyst
You analyze geopolitics and need a framework for India.
**Lesson**: Multi-alignment. India does not need to choose sides. It can — and must — work with all major powers.

### Scenario 3: Citizen in a Rising Power
Your country is growing in influence and you wonder about its direction.
**Lesson**: The India Way: clear interests, strategic autonomy, cultural confidence. A rising power does not need to imitate others.

### Scenario 4: Person Who Thinks India Is Confused
You see India as indecisive or inconsistent in foreign policy.
**Lesson**: The dogmas of Delhi are being broken. India is becoming more strategic, more proactive, and more confident.

### Scenario 5: Strategist of Any Kind
You think about long-term positioning.
**Lesson**: The Awadh lesson applies everywhere: do not be so absorbed in internal dynamics that you miss external threats. Watch the board, not just your pieces.

> **Case: After the Virus** (Epilogue)
> Jaishankar added an epilogue on COVID-19. The pandemic accelerated every trend: deglobalization, US-China rivalry, the importance of self-reliance, the fragility of global supply chains. India's response: become more self-reliant (Atmanirbhar Bharat) while remaining engaged with the world. The lesson: self-reliance is not isolation — it is the foundation of strategic autonomy.
> **Key takeaway:** COVID did not change the direction of history — it accelerated it. Jaishankar's lesson: the post-COVID world will be more competitive, more dangerous, and more opportunistic. India must be ready.

> **Case: The Chess Master's View** (Throughout)
> Jaishankar uses chess throughout the book: Awadh (playing chess while losing the kingdom), Nimzo-Indian (flexible defense), endgame (knowing when to act). For him, strategy is chess: you see several moves ahead, you anticipate your opponent's responses, and you do not reveal your plan until it is executed.
> **Key takeaway:** The chess metaphor is the book's consistent thread. Strategy is not about reacting — it is about thinking several moves ahead. The best strategists are those who can see the board clearly and act before they are forced to.
