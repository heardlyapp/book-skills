# Strategic Tools

> Source: The India Way, Chapters 3, 5, 7, 8

## India's Strategic Toolkit

### Key Concepts

| Concept | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| Krishna's Choice | Knowing what role to play — not always the hero |
| Economic Statecraft | Trade, investment, technology as strategic tools |
| Maritime Strategy | Indian Ocean dominance, Indo-Pacific outreach |
| Public Diplomacy | Shaping global opinion about India |
| Strategic Autonomy | Making choices based on interests, not pressures |

> **Case: Krishna's Choice** (Chapter 3)
> In the Mahabharata, Krishna could have fought in the great war. Instead, he chose to be Arjuna's charioteer. His wisdom lay in knowing his role. Jaishankar argues India must make similar choices: India is not the US, not China. It must find its own role — not imitating great powers, but playing to its unique strengths. The lesson: strategic wisdom is knowing not just when to act — but when to enable others to act.
> **Key takeaway:** Krishna's Choice is a profound lesson in strategic humility. India does not need to be the world's policeman or the factory of the world. India needs to be India: a civilization-state that enables, connects, and balances.

> **Case: The Pacific Indian** (Chapter 8)
> India has historically been seen as a land power focused on South Asia. Jaishankar argues India must become a maritime power — from the Indian Ocean to the Pacific. The Indian Ocean is India's natural sphere of influence. But India's interests extend to the South China Sea, the Pacific Islands, and beyond. The "Pacific Indian" is a new identity: a maritime nation engaged across the Indo-Pacific.
> **Key takeaway:** The Pacific Indian is Jaishankar's vision for India's future: outward-looking, maritime, connected to the global economy. India cannot be a great power if it only looks inward. The ocean is the highway to India's future.
