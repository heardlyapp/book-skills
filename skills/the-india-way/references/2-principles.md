# Major Power Relations

> Source: The India Way, Chapters 2, 6, 7, 8

## Multi-Alignment

### India's Approach to the Great Powers

| Power | Relationship | Framework |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| United States | Strategic partnership, converging interests | Quad, defense deals, technology |
| China | Competitive coexistence, managed rivalry | Nimzo-Indian Defence |
| Russia | Old friend, new limits | Defense supplies, energy |
| Japan | Natural partner, Asian balance | Free and Open Indo-Pacific |
| Europe | Economic partner, values alignment | Trade, climate, technology |

> **Case: The Art of the Disruption** (Chapter 2)
> Jaishankar analyzes the US under Trump and Biden. The US is in a period of disruption: questioning alliances, withdrawing from global leadership, rethinking trade. India must navigate this disruption without being caught in the US-China crossfire. The key: understand US domestic politics (Trump was not an aberration — he reflected deep trends) and make the case for US-India partnership based on shared interests, not shared values.
> **Key takeaway:** The US is India's most important strategic partner — but the US is not reliable in the old sense. India must build a relationship based on concrete interests, not emotional attachment. The era of automatic US leadership is over.

> **Case: The Nimzo-Indian Defence** (Chapter 6)
> A chess opening: black does not confront white directly but builds a flexible, resilient position. Jaishankar argues India should adopt this approach to China: not confrontation, not capitulation — but flexible counterplay. Engage economically where it serves India's interests. Compete strategically where necessary. Build countervailing power through partnerships (Quad, Japan, ASEAN, US). The Nimzo-Indian Defence is the strategic framework for managing China's rise.
> **Key takeaway:** The Nimzo-Indian Defence is Jaishankar's most memorable framework. It captures the paradox: India must compete with China while not being consumed by that competition. The key: flexibility, patience, and building multiple strong positions rather than a single frontal confrontation.
