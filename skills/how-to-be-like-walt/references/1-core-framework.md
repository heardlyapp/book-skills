# Core Framework — How to Be Like Walt

## Part 1: The Boy (Chapters 1-2)

### Marceline Farm (1901-1917)
Walter Elias Disney born December 5, 1901 in Chicago. Youngest of four sons. Father Elias was a stern, humorless, religious man who believed "spare the rod and spoil the child." Once Walt snatched a hammer from Elias' hand and held his hands — "He cried. He never touched me after that."

Key memories: 45-acre farm near Marceline, Missouri. Walt greeted barnyard animals by name each morning. A fat piglet named Skinny followed him around. First theatrical play: Peter Pan starring Maude Adams. First motion picture: a depiction of the crucifixion.

At age 9, created flip-book animation to amuse his sister Ruth with measles. Doc Sherwood paid him 25 cents to draw his prize Morgan stallion — first money earned from art.

Fourth-grade teacher scolded him for drawing flowers with faces: "Flowers do not have faces!"

### Kansas City (1909-1917)
Elias sold farm due to illness, moved family to KC, bought newspaper distributorship. Walt and Roy delivered papers at 3:30 AM through blizzards. Elias kept the money. Walt had recurring nightmares about trudging through snow.

At Benton Grammar School, Walt dressed as Lincoln on his birthday and recited the Gettysburg Address in every classroom. Performed with friend Walt Pfeiffer as "The Two Walts" at amateur nights (sneaking out his window to avoid father's disapproval).

Walt was shaped by two KC amusement parks: Fairmount Park (peered through fence, couldn't afford admission — his sister called it "fairyland") and Electric Park (100,000 bulbs, steam train circling, "the imagination and feeling of happy excitement I knew when I was a kid").

### Birth of an Animator (1918-1923)
At 16, lied about age with false mustache to get post office job. Forged age (1900 instead of 1901) to join Red Cross Ambulance Corps. Served in France — decorated ambulance with cartoons, painted German helmets to look like souvenirs.

Returned determined to be newspaper cartoonist. Father: "You can't make a living drawing pictures." Walt: "I'll get a real job — as a newspaper cartoonist."

Applied at Kansas City Star (too young). Then too old (in uniform). Landed at Pesmen-Rubin Commercial Art Studio ($50/month). Met Ubbe Iwwerks (Ub Iwerks). They formed Iwerks-Disney Commercial Artists.

At Kansas City Film Ad Company, discovered animation. Found two library books: Muybridge's The Human Figure in Motion and Carl Lutz's Animated Cartoons. "Finding that book was one of the most important and useful events in my life."

Made first cartoons: Newman Laugh-O-grams (shown in local theater). Company failed. Walt sold his blood for $25 to eat. Then saved $40, bought train ticket to Hollywood.

## Part 2: The Creator (Chapters 3-5)

### Alice Comedies and Oswald (1923-1927)
Walt arrived in Hollywood with $40, a suitcase, and a finished print of "Alice's Wonderland." Rented space from his uncle Robert. Approached Margaret Winkler (New York distributor) — she bought the Alice series. Hired Roy to handle finances and business. Hired Ub Iwerks.

"The Two Walts" became "Ub and Walt." Alice Comedies ran until 1927; made 56 shorts. Then Universal's Charles Mintz offered a new series: Oswald the Lucky Rabbit. Walt created the character (Mintz owned the rights). Oswald became a hit.

In 1928, Walt traveled to New York to renegotiate. Mintz demanded a lower per-cartoon price and revealed he had signed away most of Walt's animation staff. Mintz: "Either you accept my terms or I'll take over your studio." Walt: "You know, you've got me over a barrel. But I won't do it." Walt called Roy: "The boys have left us." Roy said: "They haven't left us. They're still here — they're drawing our cartoons."

### The Birth of Mickey Mouse (1928)
On the train back from New York, Walt sketched a mouse with circular ears. Named him "Mortimer" — Lillian hated it. She suggested "Mickey."

Ub Iwerks animated the first two Mickey shorts (Plane Crazy, Gallopin' Gaucho) almost single-handedly — 700 drawings per day. Walt and Ub made the third short, Steamboat Willie, with synchronized sound. It premiered November 18, 1928 at the Colony Theater in New York. The first cartoon with synchronized sound was a sensation.

Hub Iwerks became the first cartoon superstar — his drawing was that good. But in 1930, Pat Powers lured Ub away with a better offer. Walt was devastated. "He was like a brother to me."

### The Silly Symphonies and Color (1930-1934)
Walt continued with a new team. Created Flowers and Trees (first Technicolor, won Oscar). The Three Little Pigs (1933) — "Who's Afraid of the Big Bad Wolf?" became a Depression-era anthem. Won Oscar.

Hired Don Graham to open an art school at the studio. "Walt began to realize that in order to compete with a genius like Iwerks, he'd have to create a staff full of geniuses."

### Snow White (1934-1939)
In 1934, Walt gathered his top artists and performed the entire story of Snow White for 4 hours. He wanted a feature-length cartoon — everyone told him it was impossible. "Disney's Folly."

Budget ballooned from $250,000 to $1.5M (eventually $1.7M). Bank of America was worried. Roy was terrified. Walt mortgaged everything — his house, his insurance, his salary.

Premiere December 21, 1937 at the Carthay Circle Theater. John Barrymore was "bouncing up and down in his seat." $8.5M in initial release. Paid off all debt in 6 months. Walt received 1 standard Oscar + 7 smaller statuettes.

## Part 3: The Leader (Chapters 6-9)

### Tragedy (Chapter 6)
November 26, 1938: Walt's mother Flora died from gas asphyxiation from a leaky furnace in the house Walt and Roy had bought for their parents. Walt blamed himself. He couldn't talk about it for years. "I don't want that ever mentioned in this office again."

Pinocchio ($2.6M) and Fantasia ($2.3M) both lost money due to WWII closing European markets. The studio nearly went bankrupt.

The Strike of 1941: Walt fired Art Babbitt for union organizing. 300 employees walked out. Walt felt betrayed. "I can remember about the second day of the strike... Walt was standing there, kind of smiling at the guys on the line. He said, 'Aw, they'll be back in a couple of days.' He didn't really realize the true situation." The strike ended with union victory.

### The Plus Factor (Chapter 7)
Walt's word for his obsession with quality: "plussing." Every scene had to be better than it was. Not just good enough — better than necessary.

Dumbo: cheapest feature ($812,000, 64 minutes). Grossed $2.5M. "Sometimes constraints produce the best work."

Saludos Amigos and The Three Caballeros came from a government-funded goodwill tour to South America. Walt's first clue that there was a world beyond cartoons.

### Stick-to-it-ivity (Chapter 8)
Walt had a made-up word for perseverance. Examples: the multiplane camera took years to perfect. Storyboarding was invented because Walt kept sketching scenes on notecards and pinning them up.

Song of the South took 10 years from idea to release. Walt didn't give up on ideas; he let them marinate.

### A Sponge for Ideas (Chapter 9)
Walt traveled constantly. Read everything. Built an extensive library at the studio. "Walt had this great curiosity. He was very excited about what he was doing. He lived and breathed it, and finally it rubbed off on you."

During Fantasia, Walt didn't know classical music. He steeped himself in it. "I never liked this stuff. Honest, I just couldn't listen to it. But I can listen to it now."

## Part 4: The Dreamer (Chapters 10-12)

### Disneyland (Chapter 10)
Walt conceived Disneyland while sitting on a bench in Griffith Park, watching his daughters ride the carousel. "They needed a place where parents and children could have fun together."

Art Linkletter on seeing the construction site: "I looked around and saw nothing but a cow pasture. I thought, My poor, deluded friend! He's going to put a bunch of merry-go-rounds out here, 45 minutes from L.A. He'll go broke!"

Walt: "There's a fortune to be made here. If you buy up all the property around Disneyland, in a year or two it'll be worth twenty times what you paid for it." Linkletter didn't buy — "by being so 'smart,' I passed up a chance to make millions!"

Opening Day, July 17, 1955: "Black Sunday." Rides broke down. Restaurants ran out of food. The asphalt was still soft — women's high heels sank in. Plumbers' strike meant limited water fountains. Counterfeit tickets let in twice as many people. Walt was furious.

Walt fixed everything and opened two days later for the official private premiere. It was perfect.

### The Man of Singular Focus (Chapter 12)
Walt was obsessed with work. His daughter Diane: "If he wasn't at the studio, he was at home working on some project in the workshop, or lying in bed reading scripts or storyboards. We had to learn to get his attention. If we wanted to ask him something, we'd sometimes have to stand in front of the TV and block his view."

Roy: "The only way I could get Walt to approve a budget was to nail him at home on Sunday. He would never sign anything at the studio — he was too busy."

## Part 5: The Legacy (Chapters 13-15)

### The Show Must Go On (Chapter 13)
Walt was diagnosed with lung cancer in 1966. He had been a 3-pack-a-day smoker since his Red Cross days in France.

His last project: EPCOT (Experimental Prototype Community of Tomorrow). Walt envisioned a real city where people would live, work, and test new technologies.

Walt died December 15, 1966 at age 65. His last words were reportedly to an attendant who asked how he was feeling: "I'm fine — just tired."

The last thing Walt ever wrote was about EPCOT: "I believe in the power of the future. I believe in progress. I believe there is no limit to what we can achieve if we work together."

### The Real Walt Disney (Chapter 14)
Walt was complicated: "He had this idea of how a father should be. He decided he was going to be the dad that he never had. He played with his girls a lot — after all, he was a kid at heart. He'd turn somersaults in the front yard with us and all the neighborhood kids."

But he was also demanding: A young artist once proudly showed Walt his drawing of an apple. Walt took the pencil, drew an X where the highlight should be, and said "Why would you turn it in without the highlight? The drawing isn't finished until the highlight is in." The artist never forgot — and never turned in incomplete work again.

Walt's favorite word: "Can you top it?" He was always pushing. Never satisfied. "Walt would say, 'That's not good enough. Let's do it again.' And we would. And then he'd say it again. And we'd do it again. And again. And again."

### Walt Lives! (Chapter 15)
The legacy: Disneyland expanded to Florida (Walt Disney World), Tokyo, Paris, Hong Kong, Shanghai. Disney movies: The Little Mermaid, Beauty and the Beast, The Lion King, Frozen.

Roy O. Disney on the day he died: "I think my brother would have wanted it this way. He always said he didn't want to be remembered as a man who made a lot of money, but as a man who made a lot of people happy."
