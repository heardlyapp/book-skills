# Key Principles — How to Be Like Walt

## I. Optimism Is a Choice, Not a Personality Trait

Walt had a deeply difficult childhood: a father who beat him, forced him to work without pay, and told him he'd never make a living as an artist. Yet Walt chose optimism. "He spoke with heartbreaking candor about having nightmares throughout his life, yet he never let childhood pain darken his optimism."

Ray Bradbury: "Everything Walt achieved in his life was something he was told he couldn't do. His father told him he could never make a living by drawing cartoons. He spent his entire career proving the doubters wrong. And he had a wonderful time doing it."

## II. Quality Is Not a Goal — It Is an Obsession

Walt's made-up word: "plussing." Every scene had to be better than the last. Not just good enough — better than necessary. A young artist once showed Walt a drawing of an apple. Walt drew an X where the highlight should be: "Why would you turn it in without the highlight? The drawing isn't finished until the highlight is in."

The multiplane camera could have been "good enough." Walt demanded depth. The result: Snow White's forest scenes, Bambi's backgrounds, Pinocchio's village — works of art.

Storyboard innovation: Walt was the first to pin drawings on a board so the entire story could be seen at once. When he didn't like a scene, he'd walk up, unpin it, draw a new one on the spot. "Can you top it?" was his favorite question.

## III. Risk Is Calculated, Not Reckless

"Some financial people considered Walt a dreamer who devised grandiose plans, then told his brother, 'Here — find the money.' In fact, Walt never entered any project without meticulous planning."

Snow White looked like a crazy bet. But Walt: storyboarded every scene. Tested with sneak previews. Hired the best. Wrote and rewrote. "From an artistic perspective, nothing was left to chance."

For Disneyland, he hired Stanford Research Institute to analyze location, costs, and feasibility. "Walt was willing to bet millions on a dream — but he planned out his dreams to the last decimal place."

## IV. Great Ideas Come from Being a Sponge

Walt was not a trained fine artist. He made himself an expert. During the Fantasia period, he steeped himself in classical music. "I never liked this stuff. Honest, I just couldn't listen to it. But I can listen to it now."

He built a massive library at the studio. He traveled constantly and absorbed everything — the Electric Park 100,000 lights became Disneyland's Main Street; the Colombian river boat became the Jungle Cruise.

John Hench: "Walt had this great curiosity. He was very excited about what he was doing. He lived and breathed it, and finally it rubbed off on you."

## V. Perseverance Means Not Giving Up — Even When It's Over

After losing Oswald and most of his staff, Walt created Mickey Mouse on a train ride home. After Ub Iwerks left, Walt built a new team and taught them to be better. After the strike, Walt found a new way to work. After Pinocchio and Fantasia failed at the box office, Walt made Dumbo and kept going.

Walt's made-up word: "stick-to-it-ivity." Song of the South took 10 years from idea to release. The multiplane camera took years to perfect. Walt didn't give up on ideas — he let them marinate and waited until the timing was right.

## VI. Death Is Inevitable — What You Build Is Not

Walt's mother died in a tragic accident in 1938. He blamed himself. "I don't want that ever mentioned in this office again." His daughter Diane found a newspaper clipping about it in his drawer years later.

Walt died of lung cancer at 65. His last written words were about EPCOT: "I believe in the power of the future. I believe in progress. I believe there is no limit to what we can achieve if we work together."

Roy, on the day Walt died: "I think my brother would have wanted it this way. He always said he didn't want to be remembered as a man who made a lot of money, but as a man who made a lot of people happy."

## VII. Great Leadership Is About Creating the Conditions for Greatness

Walt didn't draw the cartoons — he hired people who could draw better than him. He built the art school, the library, the custom desks with northern exposure. He paid below-cost meals. He gave artists freedom from quotas.

"The only thing I demand is quality, not quantity." This was the deal: do your best work, and I'll give you the tools, the time, and the space to do it. The result was a staff that created works of art — Snow White, Pinocchio, Bambi, Fantasia — under impossible deadlines and budget constraints.
