# Techniques — How to Be Like Walt

## How to Cultivate the Disney Mindset

### Technique 1: "Plus" Everything, Every Time

Walt's word for his approach: "plussing." A scene is good enough? Make it better. A drawing is complete? Add the highlight. A ride is fun? Add one more surprise.

Examples: Snow White's forest wasn't just a background — seven layers of glass in the multiplane camera created depth. The Jungle Cruise has a joke around every corner. Main Street USA windows bear the names of real Disney employees. "Walt would say, 'That's not good enough. Let's do it again.' And we would. And then he'd say it again. And we'd do it again. And again. And again."

### Technique 2: Storyboard Everything

Walt invented the storyboard: pinning drawings on a cork board so everyone could see the entire story at once. He did this because he couldn't hold more than one scene in his head at a time. The storyboard solved that.

"Walt would walk up to the storyboard, unpin a scene he didn't like, draw a new one on the spot, and pin it back up. The whole team could see what he wanted."

### Technique 3: Kill Your Darlings — Even Expensive Ones

Five months into Pinocchio, Walt realized the story didn't work. He scrapped most of what had been done and started over. In Collodi's original, Pinocchio was a juvenile delinquent who killed the cricket with a mallet. Walt reshaped him into a naive but well-intentioned lad and made Jiminy Cricket his conscience.

The result: a $2.6 million film. But it became an artistic masterpiece.

During Song of the South, Walt spent 10 years working on the story before he was satisfied. He never rushed a concept. "Walt didn't give up on ideas; he let them marinate."

### Technique 4: Hire People Better Than You, Then Trust Them

Walt did not draw the cartoons. He hired Ub Iwerks (who could produce 700 drawings per day), Don Graham (who ran the art school), Ward Kimball (who created Jiminy Cricket), the Nine Old Men (who defined Disney animation). He paid them well. He gave them freedom.

"Jenna Fischer's Jet Ski story became canon on The Office. Walt's approach was the same: listen to the people doing the work."

### Technique 5: Use the Body, Not Just the Brain

Walt didn't just describe scenes — he acted them out. For Snow White, he spent 4 hours performing the entire story for his artists: voices, gestures, timing, tears. For Pinocchio, he told the story to his 8-year-old nephew, acting out every part. "Each time he told the story, he watched how his listeners reacted."

He used the same approach for Disneyland: he walked the empty orange grove and saw — physically saw — where the train, the castle, the Jungle Cruise would go. Art Linkletter saw a cow pasture. Walt saw a Magic Kingdom.

### Technique 6: Turn Constraints into Creativity

Dumbo was the cheapest Disney feature ever ($812,000, 64 minutes). It was also one of the most emotionally affecting. Walt couldn't afford epic sequences, so he focused on character and emotion. "Sometimes constraints produce the best work."

After being stabbed in the back by his animation staff in 1928, Walt didn't give up — he created Mickey Mouse. After Ub left in 1930, Walt didn't give up — he built a better team. After the strike in 1941, Walt didn't give up — he went to South America and made Saludos Amigos.

### Technique 7: Leave Something for the Next Generation

Walt's driving question was not "How do I make more money?" It was "What will I leave for my grandchildren?"

His last project: EPCOT — not a theme park, but a real city of the future. He was working on it until his final days. Roy completed Walt Disney World as a tribute. The Disney Company came close to bankruptcy multiple times in the 1970s and survived because of what Walt built.

"Don't just build for today. Build for the generation that comes after you. Walt lived for the next generation."
