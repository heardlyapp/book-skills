## The Morton Framework: Halloween as Palimpsest

Morton treats Halloween as a layered historical artifact — each era added its own traditions without fully erasing the previous ones. The Celtic Samhain survives in the bonfires and the belief in supernatural visitors. The Christian All Hallows survives in the name and the prayers for the dead. The American secular holiday survives in the costumes and candy.

**The Key Insight**: Halloween is the most successful pagan survival in Western culture — not because it fought Christianity, but because it adapted.

> **Case: The Turnip Lantern** (Chapter 3): Irish children traditionally carved turnips into lanterns for Samhain. When Irish immigrants came to America, they discovered pumpkins — larger, easier to carve, and native to the New World. The jack-o-lantern was born.
> **Key takeaway**: Halloween traditions adapt to available materials. The switch from turnip to pumpkin is a perfect example of how the holiday evolves.

> **Case: The Souling Tradition** (Chapter 5): In medieval England, poor people ("soulers") went door-to-door on All Hallows' Eve asking for "soul cakes" in exchange for prayers for the dead. This practice evolved into trick-or-treating. The costume aspect came from the Irish tradition of dressing as spirits.
> **Key takeaway**: Trick-or-treating has deep roots — going door-to-door for food has been part of Halloween for over 1000 years.
