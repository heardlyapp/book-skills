## Practical Techniques

### 1. The Holiday Audit

Look at any holiday and ask: what are its layered histories? The oldest layer (pagan), the middle layer (religious adaptation), the newest layer (commercial). Understanding the layers helps you see what the holiday really means.

### 2. The Tradition Preservation

When you practice a tradition, ask: How old is this? Where did it come from? The jack-o-lantern is a 2000-year-old custom that changed materials. Knowing the history deepens the practice.

> **Case: The Costume Origins** (Chapter 4): The tradition of wearing costumes on Halloween dates back to the Celts, who dressed as spirits to confuse the wandering dead. The modern costume industry has transformed this — but the core impulse (to become someone else for a night) remains.
> **Key takeaway**: Even the most commercialized traditions have ancient roots. The meaning is still there — it is just buried.

### 3. The Moral Panic Check

When society panics about a holiday or tradition, check the evidence. The Halloween panic was real in its effects (parents inspecting candy, schools regulating costumes) but not in its causes. Moral panics reveal social fears more than actual dangers.

### 4. The Cultural Borrowing Awareness

Halloween is the product of centuries of cultural borrowing — Celtic, Roman, Christian, Irish, American. No culture owns it. It belongs to everyone.

> **Case: The Candy Industry's Role** (Chapter 7): Morton documents how the candy industry promoted Halloween trick-or-treating to increase sales. In the 1950s, candy companies created pre-packaged "fun size" candies specifically for Halloween. The industry gave parents a safe, convenient option — and transformed Halloween into a $10 billion holiday.
> **Key takeaway**: Commercial interests shape holidays. Understanding the candy industry's role explains why Halloween is what it is today.
