# Anti-Patterns: The System's Failures

> Source: Serpico, throughout

## Believing Internal Channels Work

The biggest mistake: thinking that reporting corruption through official channels will produce results. Serpico tried every channel — precinct captain, district commander, Internal Affairs. None worked.

> **Case: The Lost Report** (Part II): Serpico writes detailed reports about corruption. The reports disappear. Superiors claim they never received them. The system had mechanisms to absorb and neutralize complaints.

> **Key takeaway:** When the system itself is corrupt, internal reporting channels are traps. They give the illusion of accountability while protecting the guilty.

## The "Bad Apples" Myth

The NYPD's public position: corruption was the work of a few dishonest officers. The Knapp Commission proved otherwise.

> **Case: The Commission's Findings** (Part II): The Knapp Commission concluded that corruption was not isolated — it was "widespread" and "systematic." It extended from patrol officers to high-ranking commanders. It was not a few bad apples. The barrel itself was rotten.

> **Key takeaway:** "Bad apples" is a myth that protects the system. When the system rewards and protects corruption, the problem is not individual officers.

## Expecting Gratitude

Serpico expected no reward. But he also didn't expect to be betrayed by the people he trusted most.

> **Case: The Betrayed Partner** (Part II): Serpico's own partner, someone he worked with daily, testified against him during the commission hearings. The person closest to Serpico was part of the cover-up.

> **Key takeaway:** Whistleblowers should expect no gratitude from the institution. The system will turn on you. The only question is how badly.
