# Principles: The Whistleblower's Journey

> Source: Serpico, Part I and early Part II

## Reporting Up the Chain

Serpico tried to report corruption through official channels. He went to his precinct commander, then district HQ, then the NYPD's Internal Affairs. Nothing happened.

> **Case: The Internal Affairs Meeting** (Part II): Serpico eventually gets a meeting with the NYPD's Internal Affairs division. The officers listen politely. Nothing changes. They tell him to collect more evidence. He realizes Internal Affairs is part of the problem — they're protecting the system, not investigating it.

> **Key takeaway:** The system created to investigate corruption was itself compromised. Whistleblowers had no safe channel.

## The Isolation

Serpico's colleagues stopped talking to him. He was threatened. His life was in danger every day.

> **Case: The Dinner Table** (Part II): Serpico's girlfriend at the time — a woman who loved him — eventually left him because the pressure was too much. He was alone. His own partner, with whom he worked every day, could not be trusted.

> **Key takeaway:** The psychological toll of whistleblowing is immense. Serpico lost relationships, friendships, and his sense of safety.

## The Knapp Commission

Serpico's testimony finally reached the right people — the Knapp Commission, a city investigation into police corruption.

> **Case: The Testimony** (Part II): Serpico testifies before the Knapp Commission. He names names. He describes the payoff system in detail. His testimony makes headlines. The commission finds that corruption is "widespread" and "systematic."

> **Key takeaway:** Serpico's testimony was the turning point. The commission's report was a bombshell. But it came three years after he first tried to report the corruption.
