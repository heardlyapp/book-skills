# Principles: Imperialism and Race-Thinking

> Source: The Origins of Totalitarianism, Part Two

## The Boer Experience and Race-Thinking

Arendt traces race-thinking to the colonial experience. In Africa, Europeans treated natives as subhuman — a complete denial of shared humanity.

> **Case: The Boer Farmers** (Part Two): Boers in South Africa developed a worldview where native Africans were not just inferior but belonged to a different species. This race-thinking from the colonies returned to Europe and was adopted by totalitarian movements.

> **Key takeaway**: Imperialism exported the idea that some people don't deserve rights. That idea didn't stay in the colonies — it came back.

## Bureaucracy as Rule by Nobody

Imperial administration created a new form: bureaucracy where no one is responsible.

> **Case: The Colonial Administrator** (Part Two): Colonial officials who committed atrocities were "following orders." No individual felt personal responsibility. This model of rule by Nobody became the template for totalitarian administration.

> **Key takeaway**: Bureaucracy makes atrocity possible by diffusing individual responsibility. "Nobody did it" becomes the excuse.

## The Bourgeoisie and Surplus Capital

The bourgeoisie, driven by surplus capital that couldn't be invested domestically, pushed for imperial expansion. Economic expansion required political domination.

> **Case: The Export of Violence** (Part Two): The techniques of colonial violence — concentration camps in South Africa, massacres in the Congo — were perfected abroad and later applied in Europe.

> **Key takeaway**: Race-thinking, bureaucracy, and the techniques of violence were all developed in the colonies and returned to Europe as tools of totalitarianism.
