# 4 Anti-Patterns: Dataism and the Mistakes We Make About the Future

The most dangerous mistake in thinking about the future is assuming that humans will remain central to it. Dataism, the emerging religion of the 21st century, offers a radically different view: organisms are algorithms, the universe is data flows, and humanity is just a transitional stage toward something greater.

## What Dataism Actually Claims

Dataism is not a fringe theory. It has already conquered the scientific establishment. It rests on two pillars:

1. **The life sciences see organisms as biochemical algorithms.** A yeast cell, a mouse, and a human are all data-processing systems. Their behavior can be modeled computationally.
2. **Computer science builds electronic algorithms.** The same mathematical laws apply to both biochemical and electronic algorithms. With enough data and computing power, electronic algorithms will eventually outperform biochemical ones.

The conflation of these two insights produces a powerful worldview: there is no fundamental difference between a human brain and a computer. Both process data. Both make decisions. The one that does it better should be trusted more.

> **Case: The Capitalist Data-Processing System** (Chapter 11): Harari reframes the Cold War as a competition between two data-processing systems. Capitalism uses distributed processing — millions of independent processors (consumers, producers, investors) connected through markets. Communism uses centralized processing — all decisions made by a single authority in Moscow. Capitalism won not because it was more ethical but because distributed processing works better with accelerating technological change. When Mikhail Gorbachev's aide asked to meet "the person in charge of supplying bread to London," the answer was: nobody is in charge. That is the secret.
> **Key takeaway:** Economic and political systems are not just ideologies — they are data-processing architectures. The system that processes data most efficiently wins. This reframes everything from elections to corporate strategy.

## The Great Anti-Pattern: Assuming Humans Are Special

The most persistent error in our thinking about the future is exceptionalism — the assumption that humans occupy a special, irreplaceable position in the order of things. We have done this before. We assumed Earth was the center of the universe (Copernicus proved us wrong). We assumed humans were a separate creation (Darwin proved us wrong). We assumed we had rational free will (neuroscience is proving us wrong).

Dataism makes one more claim: there is nothing special about human consciousness. It is just a particular way of processing data — and not a very efficient one at that.

## The Four Stages of Misunderstanding History

Harari offers a Dataist reinterpretation of human history as a data-processing system evolving through four stages. Understanding these stages helps avoid the anti-pattern of linear thinking about the future:

1. **Cognitive Revolution**: Sapiens gain the ability to connect unlimited numbers of individuals into a single network. This gives them a decisive advantage over all other species. But as they spread globally, they lose connectivity — different cultures evolve in isolation.
2. **Agricultural Revolution**: Population grows, dense local networks form, but without writing or money, global unification is impossible. Tribes remain isolated.
3. **Writing and Money**: These technologies finally enable supra-local networks — cities, kingdoms, empires. For the first time, humans consciously dream of uniting the entire globe.
4. **Global Unification (1492-present)**: Explorers, conquerors, and traders weave the first global threads. Information increasingly flows freely. Democracy and free markets win because they improve data flow.

> **Case: The Soviet Data Processing Failure** (Chapter 11): The Soviet Union tried to run an entire economy through centralized data processing. Every decision about bread prices, wheat cultivation, and bakery output was made in Moscow. The system collapsed because it could not process the rapidly changing data of the late 20th century. Meanwhile, the distributed system of global capitalism produced Apple and Wikipedia. The lesson is not about ideology — it is about information architecture.
> **Key takeaway:** The future belongs to systems that process data efficiently, not to systems that protect human values. If democracy cannot keep up with the speed of data, democracy will be replaced.

## The Great Sins of Dataism's Critics

Critics of Dataism make several predictable mistakes:

1. **"Data is just a tool"** — This assumes that humans remain in control. But if algorithms make better decisions than humans, delegating control is not a choice but an inevitability. You cannot compete with a system that processes the entire universe's data.
2. **"Consciousness will protect us"** — This assumes that consciousness has survival value. If unconscious algorithms can run hospitals, drive cars, manage companies, and fight wars more effectively than conscious humans, consciousness becomes a luxury with no evolutionary advantage. It will not be preserved.
3. **"Humans will always find meaning"** — This assumes that meaning is intrinsic to human existence. Under Dataism, meaning is just a particular pattern of data flow. When more efficient patterns emerge, the old patterns are discarded.
4. **"We can hit the brakes"** — Even if we wanted to stop technological development, we cannot. The modern economy requires constant growth. No one knows where the brakes are. And even if we found them, the country that applies them would be economically crushed by the countries that do not.

> **Case: The Aaron Swartz Martyrdom** (Chapter 11): Aaron Swartz, a 26-year-old hacker, believed that information wants to be free. He downloaded hundreds of thousands of scientific papers from JSTOR to release them to the public. He was arrested, faced trial, and hanged himself in 2013. Dataism had its first martyr. The conflict between information freedom (a Dataist value) and intellectual property rights (a humanist value) will define our legal landscape for decades.
> **Key takeaway:** Dataism is not just a theory — it already has moral weight. People are willing to die for it. The conflict between humanist values (individual rights, privacy, ownership) and Dataist values (information flow, connectivity, optimization) will be one of the defining struggles of the 21st century.

## The Freedom of Information vs. Freedom of Expression

Dataism's most novel contribution is a genuinely new value: freedom of information. This is distinct from the old liberal ideal of freedom of expression. Freedom of expression protects humans' right to speak their minds. Freedom of information protects information's right to circulate freely — even if that means overriding human claims of ownership.

This new value will come into direct conflict with traditional rights. Privacy becomes an obstacle to data flow. Intellectual property becomes a barrier to optimization. If information can save lives by being shared, what right do we have to keep it locked up? The Dataist answer is clear: no right at all.
