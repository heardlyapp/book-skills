# 2 Principles: The Liberal Crisis and the Collapse of Self

The most unsettling argument in Homo Deus is not about what technology will do to us — it is about what science is doing to our self-understanding. Harari argues that the liberal worldview, which has dominated the West for centuries and shaped human rights, democracy, and the free market, is being systematically dismantled by the very sciences it nurtured.

## The Factual Claims of Liberalism

Liberalism is not just a set of ethical preferences. It is based on what it believes to be factual statements about human nature:

1. **Humans have free will.** Voters and customers make genuine choices that are neither deterministic nor random. This is why their decisions matter.
2. **Humans have a unified self.** There is a core identity — the "I" — that persists over time, owns experiences, and can be held responsible.
3. **The individual knows best.** No outsider can know your feelings or predict your choices better than you can. This justifies individual liberty and distrust of authority.

These are factual claims. They can be tested. And according to the best science we have, they are all false.

## Free Will: The Empty Term

Neuroscience has revealed that what we call "free will" is an illusion. The electrochemical brain processes that result in decisions are either deterministic (caused by prior events) or random (the result of chance at the quantum level). Neither amounts to freedom.

> **Case: The Brain Scanner Experiment** (Chapter 8): Scientists place people in brain scanners and ask them to press one of two switches whenever they feel like it. The researchers observe neural activity in the brain that predicts which switch the person will press — from a few hundred milliseconds to several seconds before the person is even aware of their own intention. The decision happens before the person "decides."
> **Key takeaway:** Our feeling of free choice is a post-hoc interpretation of neural events we do not control. We do not choose our desires. We feel them and act accordingly.

The theory of evolution adds another nail in freedom's coffin. Natural selection works on behavior. If animals could freely choose their actions regardless of their genes, selection would have nothing to select. The entire edifice of evolution depends on behavior being shaped by genetic inheritance.

Harari does not claim that we should stop acting as if we have free will in daily life. He claims that the concept, as used by liberals to justify political freedom and moral responsibility, has no scientific foundation.

## The Self: A Stream, Not a Substance

Buddhist philosophy and modern neuroscience converge on a startling conclusion: there is no unified self. What we experience as "I" is actually a stream of consciousness — a constant flow of thoughts, sensations, and emotions with no permanent owner.

> **Case: The Robo-Rat** (Chapter 8): Scientists implant electrodes into the sensory and reward centers of a rat's brain, allowing them to control the rat by remote control. The rat wants to turn left, so it turns left. The rat wants to climb a ladder, so it climbs a ladder. The rat's desires are just patterns of firing neurons. It makes no difference whether those neurons fire due to other neurons or due to Professor Talwar's remote control. The rat would tell you it has free will.
> **Key takeaway:** If the sense of self and free will can be generated in a rat by electrodes, what does that tell us about our own sense of self? The feeling of being a unified agent controlling our actions is a construction, not a discovery.

## The Humanist Revolution: A Brief History

Harari traces how humanism became the dominant global religion. Humanism places Homo sapiens at the center of the universe and sanctifies human life, human happiness, and human power. It has three main branches:

1. **Liberal humanism**: The individual is sacred. Everyone has a unique inner core that must be protected from collectivist trampling.
2. **Socialist humanism**: Humanity as a whole is sacred. Individual fulfillment comes through collective action and equality.
3. **Evolutionary humanism**: The human species must be perfected. This branch, which inspired Nazism and eugenics, has been discredited but its underlying logic remains alive in transhumanist thinking.

The Humanist Revolution of the last few centuries was a triumph. It gave us human rights, democracy, and unprecedented prosperity. But the same scientific method that made humanism possible is now proving that its factual foundations are fiction.

## The Coming Collapse

If humans do not have free will, do not have a unified self, and are just biochemical algorithms, then the political and ethical systems built on these concepts face a crisis. Why should democratic votes matter if voters cannot choose freely? Why should individual liberty be sacred if there is no individual? Why should human life be valued above all else if humans are just data processors that less efficient algorithms than computers?

Harari does not celebrate this collapse. He warns about it. The liberal package has been the best system humanity has created for ensuring peace, prosperity, and dignity. If it is intellectually bankrupt, we need to find something to replace it — and fast. Because the alternatives may not be better.

> **Case: The End of Humanism as a Unifying Force** (Chapter 7): For most of history, humanity justified its supremacy over animals by claiming humans had souls, free will, or unique consciousness. Science has undercut all these claims. If humans are just algorithms, then there is no moral basis for privileging human interests over animal interests — or over AI interests.
> **Key takeaway:** The crisis of liberalism is not just political. It is existential. If humans are not special, what happens to human rights? If data processing is what matters, what happens when computers process data better than we do?
