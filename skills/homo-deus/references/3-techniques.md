# 3 Techniques: Consciousness, Intelligence, and the Great Decoupling

One of Harari's most crucial contributions is the radical distinction between consciousness and intelligence. These two concepts are routinely conflated in popular discourse about AI and the future of humanity. Harari insists they are not just different — they may be headed in completely opposite directions.

## What Consciousness Actually Is

Consciousness is not thinking, reasoning, or problem-solving. It is subjective experience — the raw feeling of what it is like to be something. The pain of a burn. The taste of chocolate. The boredom of a long meeting. The joy of seeing a friend. These are not computational processes. They are experiences.

Harari points out that we have no scientific explanation for how physical processes in the brain produce subjective experience. This is the "hard problem" of consciousness, and science has barely touched it. We can map neural activity associated with pain, but the map is not the pain. The map is a pattern of data. The pain is what it feels like to be that pattern.

> **Case: The Traffic Jam Analogy** (Chapter 10): Harari asks us to imagine the traffic in London. Drivers, cars, roads, and traffic lights interact in complex patterns. A traffic engineer could model this system perfectly — predicting flow, congestion, and bottlenecks. But nowhere in this system does a "London traffic consciousness" emerge. The system does not feel congested. It just is congested. Why then do we assume that complex data processing in the brain produces consciousness? We have no reason to believe that more complex information processing automatically generates subjective experience.
> **Key takeaway:** Consciousness is not a byproduct of complexity. It is a separate phenomenon that we do not understand. We should not assume that super-intelligent AI will be conscious just because it is complex.

## Intelligence Is Leaving Consciousness Behind

Historically, intelligence and consciousness were bound together because the only intelligent entities we knew were biological organisms with nervous systems. Every human who solved a problem also felt something. The two seemed inseparable.

That is no longer true. AI systems can now play Go better than any human, translate between languages, recognize faces, recommend products, and generate art — all without a trace of consciousness. A Go-playing AI does not feel satisfaction when it wins. A translation AI does not feel the beauty of a poem. A facial recognition AI does not feel the strangeness of seeing a familiar face.

> **Case: The Autonomous Car** (Chapter 10): A self-driving car processes sensory data, makes decisions, and navigates traffic better than most humans. It is intelligent. But there is nothing it is like to be that car. It does not feel the wind, worry about the destination, or enjoy the journey. It is a pure information-processing system with zero subjective experience. Harari asks: why should we think that adding more processing power will somehow conjure consciousness into existence?
> **Key takeaway:** The autonomous car is a perfect example of intelligence without consciousness. This is not a limitation of current technology — it may be a fundamental feature of non-organic intelligence. The two phenomena are separate.

## The Great Decoupling

Harari describes the 21st century as the era of the Great Decoupling. Intelligence and consciousness are being pulled apart. Algorithms — both biochemical (in human brains) and electronic (in computers) — are increasingly understood through the same mathematical framework. But electronic algorithms are getting exponentially faster, while biochemical algorithms (our brains) are stuck at Neolithic speeds.

This creates three terrifying possibilities:

1. **Algorithms will outperform humans at everything.** Not just calculation, but emotion, creativity, and social intelligence. There is nothing special about human intelligence that cannot, in principle, be replicated computationally.
2. **Consciousness will become irrelevant.** If the system values data processing above all else, and algorithms process data better than conscious beings, then consciousness becomes a bug, not a feature.
3. **Humans will be economically useless.** Not just factory workers — doctors, lawyers, artists, and CEOs. The Great Decoupling means the value of human labor, human judgment, and human intuition may drop to zero.

## The Unconscious Mind: What We Don't Know We Don't Know

Harari also explores the implications of our growing understanding of the unconscious mind. The decisions we think we are making consciously are actually made by neural processes we cannot access. We invent post-hoc rationalizations that feel true but are fabrications.

> **Case: The Split-Brain Patient** (Chapter 10): In split-brain experiments, the connection between the left and right hemispheres is severed. Information shown only to the right hemisphere cannot be verbalized (because language is in the left hemisphere). Yet the left hemisphere will invent a plausible explanation for behavior it does not understand. If a command "walk" is shown to the right hemisphere, the person walks. When asked why, the left hemisphere fabricates: "I wanted to get a drink."
> **Key takeaway:** We are not the CEOs of our own minds. We are the PR department. Our conscious experience is a story we tell ourselves after the fact, not the source of our decisions.

## The Practical Technique: Questioning the Self

Harari offers a practical technique derived from these insights: train the mind to observe without identifying. This is the Buddhist meditation tradition, which Harari personally practices (he credits his teacher S. N. Goenka in the dedication of the book).

The technique is simple: sit and observe your breath. Watch thoughts arise and pass without engaging them. Notice how the sense of "I" is not a fixed entity but a construction that appears and disappears moment by moment. This is not philosophy — it is empirical investigation of consciousness from the inside.

The implications are profound. If there is no self, then the fear of death — the fear of the self ceasing to exist — loses its foundation. What is there to fear when there was never a permanent self to begin with? The Buddhist path and the Dataist path both deny the self, but they lead in very different directions.
