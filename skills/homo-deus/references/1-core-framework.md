> Source: Homo Deus, Chapter 1 "The New Human Agenda" and Chapter 2 "The Anthropocene"

# 1 Core Framework: The Three Projects of the 21st Century

The central thesis of Homo Deus is that humanity has conquered its three traditional enemies — famine, plague, and war — and is now turning its attention to three new projects: immortality, happiness, and divinity. These are not fringe fantasies. They are the logical consequence of our deepest values combined with our technological capabilities.

## The Old Agenda: Famine, Plague, and War

For thousands of years, these were incomprehensible and uncontrollable forces of nature. People prayed to gods for deliverance. They accepted suffering as an integral part of existence. Today, we treat them as manageable challenges. When famine strikes, we convene commissions of inquiry. When a plague emerges, we expect science to respond. When war threatens, we believe peace is the default.

The numbers back this up. More people today die from eating too much than from eating too little. More die from old age than from infectious diseases. More commit suicide than are killed by soldiers, terrorists, and criminals combined. In 2012, sugar killed 1.5 million people through diabetes; war killed 120,000. The Law of the Jungle has been broken — not everywhere, but enough to change the human agenda.

This victory creates a vacuum. History does not tolerate a vacuum. Something must fill it.

## Project One: Immortality (The Gilgamesh Project)

The first great project is the fight against death itself. Modern culture sanctifies human life above all else. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights makes "the right to life" the most fundamental value. Death violates this right. Therefore, death is a crime against humanity, and we must wage total war against it.

Harari reframes death as a technical problem. There is nothing metaphysical about it. The heart stops pumping — that is a mechanical failure. Cancerous cells spread — that is a genetic glitch. Germs multiply in the lungs — that is a biological infection. Every technical problem has a potential technical solution.

> **Case: Google's Calico** (Chapter 1): In 2013, Google launched Calico, a sub-company whose stated mission is "to solve death." Bill Maris, who led Google Ventures, said, "If you ask me today, is it possible to live to be 500, the answer is yes." Silicon Valley is investing billions in life-extending technologies. The Gilgamesh Project is not science fiction — it is venture capital.
> **Key takeaway:** The war against death will be the flagship project of the 21st century because our values demand it, our economy supports it, and our deepest fears fuel it.

The implications of even doubling life expectancy to 150 would be revolutionary. Marriages would need to last 110 years. Careers would span a century. Political leaders could rule for decades. The physicist Max Planck said science advances one funeral at a time — what happens when funerals stop happening?

## Project Two: Happiness (The Biochemical Revolution)

The second project is finding the key to happiness. Harari demonstrates that material progress does not reliably translate into greater contentment. American subjective well-being levels in the 1990s were roughly the same as in the 1950s, despite GDP doubling. Japanese happiness levels barely budged between 1958 and 1987, even as real income quintupled.

> **Case: The Happiness Glass Ceiling** (Chapter 1): Harari argues that happiness is held in place by two pillars — psychological expectations and biological biochemistry. On the psychological side, as conditions improve, expectations balloon. On the biological side, evolution designed our pleasure systems to drive survival and reproduction, not contentment. The biochemical system gives us fleeting rewards, then takes them away so we must seek more.
> **Key takeaway:** Getting what you want does not make you happy for long. The system is rigged against lasting contentment. The only way out is either biochemical manipulation (drugs, genetic engineering, brain stimulation) or the Buddhist path of reducing craving.

If science is right that happiness is determined by biochemistry, the logical conclusion is that we must re-engineer our biochemistry. This is already happening. Psychiatric drugs are becoming routine. Children take Ritalin to perform better in school. Soldiers take antidepressants to handle combat stress. The biochemical pursuit of happiness is the number one driver of the global drug trade.

The Buddhist view offers a different path: reduce craving rather than increase pleasure. But Harari notes that the capitalist juggernaut prefers the biochemical solution. Happiness is pleasure. Period. And pleasure must be engineered.

## Project Three: Divinity (Upgrading into Homo Deus)

The third project subsumes the first two. To achieve immortality and happiness, humans must acquire godlike control over their biological substratum. Harari outlines three paths to divinity:

1. **Biological engineering**: Rewriting genetic code, rewiring brain circuits, altering biochemical balance at the organic level.
2. **Cyborg engineering**: Merging organic bodies with non-organic devices — bionic hands, artificial eyes, nano-robots in the bloodstream.
3. **Non-organic engineering**: Creating intelligent software completely independent of biology, free from the limitations of organic chemistry.

> **Case: The Three-Parent Embryo** (Chapter 1): In 2000, Sharon Saarinen gave birth to Alana, a baby whose nuclear DNA came from her parents but whose mitochondrial DNA came from a third person. The UK Parliament legalized this treatment in 2015. What began as a cure for mitochondrial disease opens the door to embryo selection, gene replacement, and eventually genetic enhancement. Healing becomes upgrading. It is a slippery slope, and we are already on it.
> **Key takeaway:** There is no clear line between therapy and enhancement. Every upgrade begins as a cure. The transition from healing to divinity will happen in baby steps, each one justified by compassion.

## The Paradox of Knowledge

Harari warns of a crucial paradox: the more we know, the less we can predict. Complex systems react to predictions. If experts forecast an economic crash, banks change their behavior and a crash may be averted. If we predict that humanity will pursue immortality, we may choose differently. The purpose of prediction is not to be right — it is to provoke discussion and choice.

The three projects are not inevitable. They are what our current values and technologies point toward. If we do not like where we are heading, we can choose differently. But first we must understand where we are.
