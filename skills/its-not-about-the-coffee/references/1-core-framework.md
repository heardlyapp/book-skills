# Core Framework — It's Not About the Coffee

## The Premise

"We're in the people business serving coffee, not the coffee business serving people." Howard Behar joined Starbucks in 1989 as the 4th senior executive (with Howard Schultz and Orin Smith, forming "H2O"). He spent 13 years as president of Starbucks North America and 6 years as founding president of Starbucks International, growing from 28 stores to over 4,000.

The book contains 10 principles of personal leadership, each grounded in specific stories from Behar's career.

## Principle 1: Know Who You Are — Wear One Hat

**Core idea:** Be consistent with yourself. What you see is what you get. One-hat leadership is synonymous with honesty, clarity, passion, and being truly engaged.

**The furniture story:** In his 20s, running a chain of 20 furniture stores, a CEO told Behar: "You just can't go around wearing your feelings on your sleeve!" He began putting on different "hats" for different situations — work hat, father hat, husband hat. Result: he became a nervous wreck, bending paper clips, tearing paper. A friend asked: "What happened to Howard?" He realized you must find ONE hat that fits everywhere.

**"What do you love? Furniture or people?"** Behar's mentor asked this question when offering him a new job. After 20 years in furniture, he realized: "I didn't love furniture. I loved the selection and the selling and the whole idea of helping people realize their dreams." He took the job. His one hat had a name: serving people.

**The BHAG (Big Hairy Audacious Goal):** In 1996, Starbucks defined: "To be one of the most well-known and respected organizations in the world — known for nurturing and inspiring the human spirit." Behar: "How the heck do you measure that? It's not a numerical goal. But it's our legacy goal, which is bigger than any of us."

## Principle 2: Know Why You're Here — Do It Because It's Right, Not Because It's Right for Your Résumé

**Core idea:** When someone brings their passion to work, aligned with the organization, success is the natural outcome.

**The "me" culture:** In 2001, Behar returned from international to find Starbucks North America had become "me-oriented." Every department was talking about itself. Financials stagnated. "People felt overlooked." Behar formed a cross-functional team and declared: "We need to be one team. Forget about divisions."

**100 in 3:** Behar pulled a number out of thin air: grow average new store volume by $100K in 3 years. Marketing complained: "Isn't your big idea for us to talk about we?" Behar: "Friendly competition in the service of the greater purpose doesn't take away from the individual good." Result: $150K in 3 years — under Jim Donald, they blew the goal away.

**The people who wouldn't join international:** When Behar started Starbucks International, he had a team of 0. The first person he tried to recruit for finance demanded a raise and VP title. The second person — who had a sense of purpose — took the risk. He became one of the most senior finance people at Starbucks.

## Principle 3: Think Independently — The Person Who Sweeps the Floor Should Choose the Broom

**Core idea:** "Most of us have jobs that are too small for our spirit." People are not assets. Give people freedom and they'll exceed expectations.

**The "BE NICE. BE FAST. BE CLEAN." sign:** Behar walked into a store and saw this sign. His response: "What about a sign that says BE HUMAN?" Rule books disempower people. Recipes — guidelines based on outcomes — empower them.

**Toronto café lease crisis:** Starbucks leased a site in Toronto, but the existing tenant didn't want to leave. Local leader Roly Morris placed a $25K full-page open letter in the largest newspaper, then another $25K follow-up. Result: reputation stayed intact. Local management made the call, not Seattle.

**The barista who gave a refund + new drink:** A customer didn't like his drink. The barista gave a full cash refund AND made a new drink. Economically, you'd say that's the wrong call. But it was "an honest, care-filled exchange" that demonstrated human service.

## Principle 4: Build Trust — Care, Like You Really Mean It

**Core idea:** "People don't care how much you know. They want to know how much you care." You can't fake it.

**The three customer letters:** Behar returned from a trip to find three letters complaining about service. One: "I love your coffee... but I want to be treated like a human being who matters." Behar invited the customers and store managers to meet together. The result: "The human connection won out." This was the moment Starbucks realized: "Good service is hands and feet, but great service is heart."

**Picking up cigarette butts:** Behar's example of real caring: if you see a Starbucks cup on the ground, pick it up. And the McDonald's ones too. If there's a mistake, "Stand between the bullet and the person it's aimed at." That kind of caring drives an organization.

## Principle 5: Listen for the Truth — The Walls Talk

**Core idea:** Spend time listening. Amazing results follow when you hear what people are really saying.

**The Green Apron Book:** Behar's way to capture what mattered to everyone at Starbucks — "what we can do, not what we must or can't do." Simple, barely a booklet. The guidelines serve as reminders of values, not rules.

**H2O Monday dinners:** Schultz, Behar, and Orin Smith met weekly for dinner. "I remember laughing, crying, talking quietly, and arguing loudly." This created "an almost nonverbal level of understanding that drove so much of what we wanted to do." Deidra Wager called it "H2O alchemy."

## Principle 6: Be Accountable — Only the Truth Sounds Like the Truth

**Core idea:** No secrets. No lies of omission. Say what needs to be said, with care and respect.

**The wage miscalculation:** Behar fought to raise partner wages above minimum wage. His analysis said it would cost 1% of sales. Implementation cost turned out to be 2%. "I had to suck it up and admit I made a huge mistake on the numbers." But they never considered rolling back wages. Values mattered more.

**The protest beard:** When Behar was failing as president of Thousand Trails (his pre-Starbucks role), he didn't speak up. Instead, he "grew my protest beard... in hopes that I would get fired." Not mature. But it taught him: "If something is in conflict with your values, own up to it and do something about it."

## Principle 7: Take Action — Think Like a Person of Action, Act Like a Person of Thought

**Core idea:** Find the sweet spot of passion, purpose, and persistence. People serve people — it's not an idea, it's an action.

**The Chicago store turnaround:** In late 1989, Starbucks was failing to show investors the concept wasn't local. Behar told Schultz: "I will move to Chicago and I will not leave until we've gotten it right." He packed up and moved. The Chicago stores turned around.

**100 in 3 execution:** Behar didn't just talk vision. He set specific stretch goals, aligned every department, and got it done. "I have never felt that friendly competition in the service of the greater purpose takes away from the individual or greater good."

## Principle 8: Face Challenge — We Are Human Beings First

**Core idea:** Use all the principles to guide you during the hardest times. Put people first.

**High-deductive health insurance:** Behar pushed for Starbucks to offer health insurance even to part-time partners, "deductive high" to be affordable. It was controversial among investors. But: "If our people are better human beings, they'll be better partners of the company." Starbucks became known for this benefit.

**The semi-automated espresso machines:** Howard Schultz hated them — "they took away from the romance." But the data said: carpal tunnel syndrome among partners, speed issues, inconsistency. The decision became obvious. "We were no longer stuck in our underwear struggling with our individual problems."

## Principle 9: Practice Leadership — The Big Noise and the Still, Small Voice

**Core idea:** Leaders are just ordinary human beings. Don't let the noise crowd out the truth. Let quiet be your guide.

**Throwing his glasses:** When Behar struggled with returning to North America, he told Schultz: "I'm not getting this. I'm not being successful." He threw his glasses across the room. That night his wife Lynn reminded him: "You can only be who you are." He had to set himself free to do what he knew.

## Principle 10: Dare to Dream — Say Yes

**Core idea:** Say yes. The most powerful word in the world.

**The Hundredth Monkey effect:** Behar uses this story to show how one person doing the right thing can spread through an organization. Frappuccino, in-store music, nonfat milk — all started with one person who said yes.
