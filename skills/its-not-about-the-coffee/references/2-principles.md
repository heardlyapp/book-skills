# Key Principles — It's Not About the Coffee

## I. Put People First, Always

"We're in the people business serving coffee, not the coffee business serving people." This is the book's central thesis. Every principle flows from it. Behar fought to raise partner wages above minimum wage — cost turned out to be double the estimate, but they never considered rolling back. Starbucks offered health insurance to part-time partners when it was almost unheard of in retail. The three customer complaint letters taught them: "Good service is hands and feet, but great service is heart."

## II. Authenticity Is the Foundation of Leadership

Behar's "one hat" philosophy: you cannot be a different person at work, at home, and with friends. When he tried — putting on different hats for different situations — he became "a nervous wreck." A friend asked "What happened to Howard?" The lesson: "People who feel good about themselves produce good results." Faking it kills your effectiveness.

## III. Rules Disempower; Recipes Empower

"The person who sweeps the floor should choose the broom." Instead of rule books that tell people how to be, give them guidelines — recipes — that explain what needs to be accomplished and why. The "BE NICE. BE FAST. BE CLEAN." sign was the opposite of this — it dehumanized. The Toronto café lease crisis was the opposite: local leadership made the call to place $50K worth of newspaper ads to do the right thing.

## IV. Trust Is Built by Caring, and Caring Can't Be Faked

"You can't fake it." A manager at Starbucks was brilliant but hard to work with. Behar asked his team: does he care about the business and about you, even if he doesn't show it the way you want? They said yes. They chose to look past style and toward intent. But that only works if the caring is real. "The whole world knows the difference."

## V. Purpose Trumps Résumé

"Do it because it's right, not because it's right for your résumé." The person who demanded a VP title and raise to join international — passed over. The person who took the risk with no title — became a senior VP. Behar himself had no college degree, no MBA, no white shirt. Starbucks took a chance on him because he shared their dream: nurturing and inspiring the human spirit.

## VI. Say Yes — the Most Powerful Word

"Say yes, and enjoy all that you are doing, and help others to do the same." Big dreams require big goals, big hopes, big joys. Frappuccino, in-store music, nonfat milk — all started with one person saying yes. The Hundredth Monkey effect: one person doing the right thing creates a critical mass.

## VII. Leadership Is for Everyone, Not Just Executives

"We are all responsible for leading ourselves to realize our potential." Behar distinguishes between "Leaders with a capital L" (official titles) and "leaders with a small l" (all of us). The person who sweeps the floor should choose the broom. The barista who gave a refund AND remade the drink was practicing leadership. "People are not assets. You own assets. You don't own people."
