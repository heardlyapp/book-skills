# Techniques — It's Not About the Coffee

## How to Build a People-First Organization

### Technique 1: Find Your One Hat

Behar's method for self-discovery: ask "What do you love? [Your industry] or people?" He spent 20 years in furniture before realizing he didn't love furniture — he loved the people and the service. The question his mentor asked became his compass.

To find your hat: ask yourself "What makes you feel most deeply and vitally alive?" and "If there was no praise or criticism in the world, then who would you be?" Behar: "When you can answer that question, you are 'there.'"

### Technique 2: Say "No" to Title Chasers

When recruiting, do not hire people who are "résumé-ing their way to the top." Look for people who share the organization's purpose. Behar's test: the first international recruit demanded a raise and VP title. He passed. The second — who "seemed to have a sense of purpose in his life, and I knew he was passionate about Starbucks" — took the risk. He became a senior VP.

### Technique 3: Replace Rules with Recipes

Ask yourself for every policy: does this rule explain what we're trying to achieve (a recipe), or does it dictate every tiny step (a rule)? Recipes like Starbucks drink standards — "a double tall vanilla latte has to taste the same in Tokyo as in Baton Rouge" — are necessary. Rules like "BE NICE. BE FAST. BE CLEAN." are dehumanizing.

### Technique 4: Stand in Front of the Bullet

Behar: "The leader needs to stand between the bullet and the person it's aimed at." When a mistake happens, the boss who protects their team — who says "we made this mistake together" instead of "who did this?" — earns loyalty that drives an organization for years.

### Technique 5: Use Small Gestures to Stay Big

Behar sent birthday and anniversary cards — 60 per month at first, 2,500 per month by retirement. It became Starbucks lore. He also used helium balloons to celebrate peers. "Getting big and staying small" is the result of intentional, personal gestures at scale.

### Technique 6: Call the Problem Out Loud

Behar's moment of vulnerability: "I'm not getting this. I'm not being successful. I don't know how to do this." He threw his glasses across the room. Then went home and talked to his wife. Then came back and fixed it. The technique: when you're failing, say it out loud. To your boss. To your partner. To yourself. "When you're in a hole, quit digging."

### Technique 7: Make the Dream Tangible

"100 in 3" was a made-up number — "I had no idea how we'd meet this target." But Behar made it concrete: signs everywhere, questions from every department. The dream became real because it had a name, a deadline, and a visible presence. "Think like a person of action, and act like a person of thought."
