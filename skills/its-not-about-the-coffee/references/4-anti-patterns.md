# Anti-Patterns — It's Not About the Coffee

## The Central Error: Treating People as Assets, Not Humans

"Contrary to common business-speak, people are not assets. You own assets. You don't own people. Assets are buildings and trucks and supplies. Assets always give us what we expect. People never quite give us what we expect. People surprise us."

### Mistake 1: "Business is just about the numbers"
When Behar returned to North America, financials were stagnating. The instinct was to cut costs, add rules, tighten control. Behar did the opposite: he brought people together, addressed the "me" culture, set a stretch goal. The financial turnaround followed the people turnaround, not the other way around.

### Mistake 2: "Caring is soft, leadership is hard"
Behar: "Most corporations are biased against showing care in the workplace. We seem to admire the stoic, the person with a great poker face, the stone-faced negotiator." But he proved that caring — sending 2,500 cards per month, standing in front of bullets, genuinely listening — was the hardest, most effective leadership tool.

### Mistake 3: "Following the rules is always safe"
The Toronto lease situation could have been handled by Seattle with a legal threat. Instead, local leadership broke the "rules" and spent $50K on newspaper ads. Result: reputation saved. "Rules drive me crazy. When things are rule bound, people stop pleasantly surprising you."

### Mistake 4: "If you can do the job, the title doesn't matter"
Behar found that many people wouldn't transfer to Starbucks International because it was too small and had no fancy titles. Those people missed the growth opportunity of a lifetime. "You are bigger than your job title." The person who demands the VP title before doing the work is not a leader — they're a résumé builder.

### Mistake 5: "Once you're failing, keep your head down"
Behar's protest beard was a monument to this mistake. Instead of speaking up about the job that didn't fit, he hoped someone would notice and fire him. "If you need to leave a job, it doesn't mean you are a quitter." Own up to what's wrong, and do something about it.

### Mistake 6: "Persistence always pays"
"Persistence only pays when your hat fits." Behar stayed in a bad role at Thousand Trails for two years because he kept believing persistence would save him. It didn't. The right move was to leave. The lesson: persistence is a virtue — but not when it's keeping you in a place that violates your values.

### Mistake 7: "Great work cultures just happen"
The H2O Monday dinners were deliberate. The birthday cards were a system. The "100 in 3" signs were everywhere. The three customer letters led to a face-to-face meeting. None of this happened by accident. "We must always find tangible ways to show we care about one another. If it's not tangible, it's not real."
