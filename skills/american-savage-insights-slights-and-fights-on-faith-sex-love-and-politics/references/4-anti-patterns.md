# Anti-Patterns: Misunderstanding Savage

> Source: American Savage, throughout

## Confusing "Sex Positive" with "Anything Goes"

Savage is often accused of moral relativism. In fact, he's highly ethical — he insists on honesty, consent, and communication above all else.

> **Case: The GGG Abuser** (Chapter 4): A reader thinks GGG means she must do anything her partner wants. Savage corrects her firmly: GGG is mutual. Game means "within reason." Giving means your partner gives too. It's not a license for exploitation.

> **Case: The Unethical Non-Monogamist** (Chapter 2): A man in an open relationship who lies about his other partners. Savage calls him out: non-monogamy demands more honesty, not less. Without truth, there's no ethics.

> **Key takeaway**: GGG is a two-way street. Ethical non-monogamy requires more communication, less deception.

## The It Gets Better Misunderstanding

Critics say it tells youth to passively wait for improvement. False.

> **Case: The Critic's Response** (Chapter 16): Savage defends the project against charges of passivity. The full message is "it gets better, and we adults are working to make it better." The project mobilized thousands of adults to become visible allies.

## Assuming Savage Hates Religion

Savage is critical of institutions, not individual faith. His father was a deacon. His mother was a lay minister. He crosses himself on airplanes despite being an agnostic.

> **Key takeaway**: Savage attacks hypocrisy, not belief. He understands faith and family intimately.
