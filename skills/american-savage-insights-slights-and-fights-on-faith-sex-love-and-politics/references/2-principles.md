# Principles: Sex, Ethics, and Communication

> Source: American Savage, Chapters 2, 4-5

## It's Never Okay to Cheat (Except When It Is)

Savage argues cheating — lying, breaking agreements — is wrong. But some relationships survive infidelity and some even become stronger.

> **Case: The Four-Year Affair** (Chapter 2): A reader had a four-year extramarital affair without getting caught. Savage's nuanced response: the affair itself didn't harm anyone because it was never discovered, but the deception was the real problem. If you need non-monogamy, negotiate it upfront with your partner.

> **Case: The Open Marriage** (Chapter 2): A couple who opened their marriage after one partner had an affair. They rebuilt trust through complete honesty. Savage's point: the betrayal was the lie, not the sex. The same act with honesty would not have been cheating.

> **Key takeaway**: The sin is the lie, not the act. Ethical non-monogamy requires complete honesty.

## Sex Dread

Many people carry shame about sex from religious upbringing or trauma. Savage argues for active unlearning.

> **Case: The Religious Shame** (Chapter 3): A reader raised in a strict religious household can't enjoy sex without guilt. Savage advises: you have to practice unlearning the shame. Therapy, honest conversations, and patient partners help.

## The Choicer Challenge (Abortion)

Savage's pro-choice position is deeply personal. He argues abortion access is essential for women's equality.

> **Key takeaway**: Sexual ethics are about consent, communication, and responsibility. Shame and guilt are obstacles, not virtues.
