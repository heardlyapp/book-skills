# Anti-Patterns

## 1. Thinking Oil Is Just Another Commodity

**The mistake**: Treating oil like wheat or steel.

**Fix**: Oil is the only commodity that nations go to war over.

> **Case: Japan's Oil Gamble** (Part 2): Japan attacked Pearl Harbor because the U.S. embargo cut off 80% of its oil supply. Without oil, Japan's navy and army were paralyzed. They chose war over economic strangulation.
> **Key takeaway**: Oil is so vital that nations risk total war to secure it. No other commodity provokes such decisions.

## 2. Believing Oil Producers Have All the Power

**The mistake**: Thinking OPEC controls oil prices completely.

**Fix**: OPEC's power is limited by internal divisions, non-OPEC production, and demand changes.

> **Case: The 1986 Counter-Shock** (Part 6): OPEC's discipline fractured in the mid-1980s. Saudi Arabia grew tired of cutting production while others cheated. They flooded the market. Prices collapsed from $30 to below $10. OPEC's power, it turned out, depended entirely on Saudi cooperation.
> **Key takeaway**: OPEC is not a monolithic cartel. Its members have competing interests. The cartel's power rises and falls with Saudi willingness to play the role of swing producer.

## 3. Assuming the 1970s Crisis Is Behind Us

**The mistake**: Believing oil shocks are a thing of the past.

**Fix**: The world remains vulnerable. Disruptions anywhere affect everyone.

> **Case: The Gulf War** (Part 7): When Iraq invaded Kuwait in 1990, it threatened 20% of global oil reserves. The U.S. went to war — to protect oil supplies. The vulnerability never goes away.
> **Key takeaway**: Every decade has its oil crisis. Security of oil supply justifies military action even in the post-Cold War era.

## 4. Overestimating Energy Independence

**The mistake**: Believing any nation can be truly energy independent.

**Fix**: Oil is a global market. Disruption anywhere affects prices everywhere.
