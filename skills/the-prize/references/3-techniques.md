# Techniques: Understanding the Oil Economy

## Key Events in Oil History

| Year | Event | Impact |
|------|-------|--------|
| 1859 | First oil well (Pennsylvania) | Birth of the oil industry |
| 1911 | Standard Oil breakup | Created Exxon, Mobil, Chevron |
| 1953 | Iran coup | Oil-driven regime change |
| 1960 | OPEC founded | Producer cartel formed |
| 1973 | Arab oil embargo | Prices quadruple, global recession |
| 1979 | Iranian revolution | Prices triple, second oil shock |
| 1990 | Gulf War | Oil at center of conflict |

> **Case: The Oil Shock of 1979** (Part 6): The Iranian Revolution cut off 5% of global oil supply. Panic buying drove prices from $13 to $39 per barrel. Long lines at gas stations. Auto sales crashed. The world entered a severe recession. This second oil shock crushed hopes that 1973 was a one-time event.
> **Key takeaway**: Oil shocks are not isolated events. Small supply disruptions have outsized effects because of panic, speculation, and the inelastic nature of oil demand.

## How Oil Prices Work

- Supply + demand + geopolitics + speculation = oil price
- Small supply changes can cause large price swings (because demand is inelastic)
- OPEC has significant but not total control over prices
- Futures markets and speculation amplify volatility

> **Case: The Counter-Shock** (Part 6): After the 1979 crisis, high prices spurred conservation and new production. By 1986, oil prices collapsed below $10. The Saudi strategy of flooding the market broke OPEC's pricing power — temporarily.
> **Key takeaway**: High prices cure themselves by spurring alternatives and conservation. Low prices cure themselves by reducing investment and production. The cycle repeats endlessly.
