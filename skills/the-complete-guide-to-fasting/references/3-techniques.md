## Practical Techniques

### 1. The 16:8 Start

Skip breakfast. Eat lunch between 12-2 PM. Dinner between 6-8 PM. No food outside the window. Drink water, black coffee, or tea during the fast. Do this for 2 weeks before trying longer fasts.

### 2. The Hunger Wave Protocol

When hunger hits, note the time. Set a timer for 15 minutes. Do something (walk, work, read). Most hunger waves pass within 20 minutes. If the hunger is still there after 20 minutes, you may actually need to eat.

> **Case: The 24-Hour Fast Experience** (Chapter 9): Fung describes patients who fear a 24-hour fast but find it surprisingly easy. The hunger comes around habitual meal times (breakfast, lunch) and passes. By dinner time, most people are not hungry — they were hungry because of habit, not need.
> **Key takeaway**: Hunger is largely conditioned. Breaking the conditioning takes 2-3 fasts. After that, fasting becomes easier.

### 3. The Electrolyte Strategy

On extended fasts (36+ hours), add salt (sodium) to water. Sea salt or pink salt — 1/4 tsp per liter. This prevents headaches and fatigue. Magnesium and potassium can also help. "Fasting headaches" are usually electrolyte-related.

### 4. The Refeed Protocol

When breaking a long fast (48+ hours), start small. Bone broth, a few nuts, a small salad. Wait 30 minutes before eating a normal meal. The longer the fast, the gentler the refeed should be.

> **Case: The Refeeding Mishap** (Chapter 12): Fung describes a patient who broke a 5-day fast with a large pizza. The result: severe digestive distress, blood sugar spike, and loss of the post-fast mental clarity. The refeed is as important as the fast.
> **Key takeaway**: The longer the fast, the more careful the refeed. Start with low-carb, low-volume food. Let the digestive system wake up.
