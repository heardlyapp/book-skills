# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *Make Change* by Shaun King — Introduction, Chapters 1-12.

## The Three Frameworks

### Framework A: The Dip and the Choice (Chapters 1, 2)

King argues we are in a historical "dip" — a period of profound crisis comparable to other dark eras. The danger is that people inside a dip do not recognize it. Genocide, fascism, and oppression creep gradually — not all at once. The first step to making change is recognizing that we are in a dip and making a conscious choice to act. King's own turning point was watching the video of Eric Garner being killed by police in 2014. He chose to stop watching and start organizing.

> **Case: The Eric Garner Decision (Introduction):** "Watching Eric Garner served as a defibrillator to my conscience, propelling me to stop what I was doing and to fight against this profound injustice." King had been an activist in his youth but had stepped back. The video of Garner's death — and the national response — pulled him back in. He made a concrete choice: leave his job, use his platform, and organize for justice.

> **Case: The Anne Frank Connection (Introduction):** After the ICE raids in Mississippi (2019), King was struck by the words of Anne Frank: "Terrible things are happening outside... Families are torn apart; men, women and children are separated." He writes: "The brutal truth is that Magdalena Gomez Gregorio could've written them herself." The connection across history shows that the dip is repeating — and we must recognize it to fight it.

### Framework B: The Three Ingredients of Movements (Chapters 7, 8, 9)

Every successful movement requires three things working together: energized people (passion, anger, hope), organized people (structure, roles, meetings), and sophisticated plans (strategy, targets, timelines). Missing any one ingredient causes the movement to fail. Passion without structure burns out. Structure without passion becomes bureaucracy. Plans without people are fantasies.

> **Case: The Ferguson Uprising (Chapters 6, 7):** The protests in Ferguson after Michael Brown's killing had enormous energy — thousands of people in the streets, national media attention, a hashtag that spread worldwide. But the movement initially lacked organization and a sophisticated plan. King describes the raw energy as essential but insufficient. The most effective organizations combined that energy with clear demands, leadership structures, and strategic targets.

### Framework C: Failure and Revolutionary Self-Care (Chapters 11, 12)

King is unusually honest about his own failures. He describes years of organizing that produced little change. He talks about the emotional toll of supporting families grieving police violence — the burnout, the despair, the temptation to quit. His conclusion: failure is data, not a verdict. Burnout is not a moral failing. Revolutionary self-care — rest, boundaries, community — is part of the strategy, not an indulgence.

> **Case: The January 2015 Gloom (Chapter 1):** King describes a period of deep despair: "I found myself in an unfamiliar place of gloom and dread." After months of nonstop activism — writing, organizing, supporting families — with little justice to show for it, he was spent. "It was brutal, grueling, emotionally draining work." He does not offer a quick fix. He offers the recognition that this is normal and sustainable only with intentional self-care.
