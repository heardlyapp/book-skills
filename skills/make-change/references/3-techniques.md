# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *Make Change* by Shaun King.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The One-Action Starter

Choose one issue. Choose one gift you have. Take one action this week. King's model: you do not need to solve racism tomorrow. You need to write one post, make one call, attend one meeting, donate one amount. The action itself generates momentum. "I wrote this book not just to tell you about my experiences. I wrote this book because I want you to join me."

> **Case: The August 2019 Snapshot (Introduction):** King presents a 48-hour window: El Paso mass shooting (22 dead), Dayton mass shooting (9 dead), ICE raids in Mississippi (680 detained, children left alone). "Trying to capture the impact of this moment feels somewhat impossible, as every day brings its own unique horrors." His point: you cannot respond to everything. Choose where you will act.
> **Key takeaway**: The world's problems are overwhelming. You cannot fix them all. Choose one and act on it.

> **Case: The Magdalena Gomez Gregorio Story (Introduction):** An 11-year-old girl whose father was taken in the ICE raids looked into a camera and said she had no idea how she would eat or get school supplies. King writes: "The tears of these children had already broken me, but the courage of the young, grief-stricken Magdalena ripped me to shreds." Rather than despair, he used the story to fuel organizing.
> **Key takeaway**: Individual stories are more powerful than statistics. Use stories to energize people — but always follow energy with organization and a plan.

Before launching any organizing effort, assess: (1) Are people energized? (2) Are they organized into structures with clear roles? (3) Do we have a sophisticated plan with specific targets and timelines? Honestly identify which ingredient is weakest and strengthen it.

### 3. The Gift Inventory

Identify your contribution: Can you write? Speak? Organize? Fund? Create art? Connect people? Analyze data? Plan strategy? Everyone has at least one gift. If you genuinely cannot identify one, start with the gift of showing up — then pay attention to what you naturally do that others appreciate.

### 4. The Failure Postmortem

When an action fails: (1) What was the goal? (2) What happened instead? (3) What did we learn about our strategy? (4) What did we learn about our opponents? (5) What will we do differently next time? Failure without analysis is just failure. Failure with analysis is learning.

### 5. The Revolutionary Self-Care Protocol

Schedule rest as intentionally as you schedule actions. Set boundaries on your availability. Build community that supports you, not just the cause. King's insight: "I don't get down often, but in January of 2015, I found myself in an unfamiliar place of gloom and dread." He learned that sustainability requires intentional care, not heroic endurance.
