# 2 — Medieval and Catholic Philosophy

> Source: "A History of Western Philosophy" by Bertrand Russell

## The Synthesis of Faith and Reason

Medieval philosophy was a multi-century effort to reconcile Greek philosophy, especially Aristotle, with Christian theology. Russell treats this period with respect but also sharp criticism.

> **Case: Saint Augustine** (Book II, Chapter IV — Saint Augustine): "Augustine was the greatest of the Church Fathers. He synthesized Neoplatonism with Christianity, creating a philosophical framework that dominated Western thought for a thousand years. His concepts — the City of God, original sin, the Trinity — are as much philosophical as theological." Russell covers Augustine's intellectual journey from Manichaeism to Neoplatonism to Christianity.
> **Key takeaway**: Russell has a high regard for Augustine as a philosopher, not just a theologian. His ideas about time, memory, and the self anticipate modern philosophy of mind. But Russell is also critical of Augustine's influence on doctrines of sin and predestination.

> **Case: Thomas Aquinas** (Book II, Chapter XIV — Saint Thomas Aquinas): "Aquinas's achievement was monumental: he made Aristotle safe for Christianity. His five proofs of God's existence, his natural law theory, and his systematic theology represent the peak of scholasticism." Russell gives Aquinas his due as a system-builder but criticizes the scholastic method for being overly deductive and insufficiently empirical.
> **Key takeaway**: Russell's treatment of Aquinas is typical of his approach: he acknowledges the intellectual achievement while criticizing the method. Aquinas brilliantly systematizes what he already believes — the conclusions are given, and reason is used to justify them. This is the opposite of the scientific method.
