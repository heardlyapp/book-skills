# 3 — Modern Philosophy

> Source: "A History of Western Philosophy" by Bertrand Russell

## The Scientific Revolution and Its Philosophical Consequences

Modern philosophy begins with Descartes and his response to the collapse of the medieval worldview. The Scientific Revolution — Copernicus, Galileo, Newton — forced philosophy to rethink the nature of knowledge, reality, and the self.

> **Case: Descartes** (Book III, Chapter I — Descartes): "Descartes is the founder of modern philosophy. His method of radical doubt — doubting everything that could be doubted — led him to the one indubitable truth: 'I think, therefore I am.' From this single certainty, he attempted to rebuild all knowledge." Russell admires Descartes's ambition and clarity but finds his dualism (mind vs. matter) deeply problematic.
> **Key takeaway**: Descartes's Cogito is the foundational moment of modern philosophy. It shifted the starting point from the world (what is reality?) to the self (what can I know?). This subjective turn defined the next 300 years of philosophy.

> **Case: Hume** (Book III, Chapter on Hume): "David Hume is the most important of British empiricists. He argued that all knowledge comes from experience and that there is no rational basis for causation, induction, or the self. His skepticism was so radical that Kant said it woke him from his 'dogmatic slumber.'" Russell has enormous admiration for Hume, calling him "a master of philosophy."
> **Key takeaway**: Hume's challenge — that causation is just habitual association, that we have no direct knowledge of the self, that induction has no rational foundation — has never been fully answered. Every serious philosopher since has had to grapple with Hume. Russell ranks him among the very greatest philosophers.
