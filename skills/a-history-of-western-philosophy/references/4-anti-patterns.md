# 4 — 19th-20th Century and Russell's Critiques

> Source: "A History of Western Philosophy" by Bertrand Russell

## Anti-Pattern: Romanticism and Irrationalism

Russell is sharply critical of philosophical movements that reject reason and science. He sees Romanticism and its philosophical offshoots as dangerous — aesthetically appealing but intellectually and politically harmful.

> **Case: Nietzsche** (Book III, Chapter on Nietzsche): "Nietzsche is a critic of Christianity and a prophet of the Übermensch (superman). His philosophy is aesthetically compelling but ethically dangerous. He celebrates power, rejects conventional morality, and despises democracy and socialism." Russell acknowledges Nietzsche's literary brilliance but condemns his philosophy as "a megalomaniac's daydream."
> **Key takeaway**: Russell's treatment of Nietzsche reveals his own philosophical commitments: he values reason, democracy, and empiricism. For Russell, Nietzsche is the opposite — a romantic irrationalist whose ideas, taken seriously, lead to political disaster. This is Russell being honest about his own biases.

## Anti-Pattern: The Overreach of System-Building

Russell is skeptical of philosophers who build grand systems that claim to explain everything — especially when those systems ignore empirical evidence.

> **Case: Hegel** (Book III, Chapter on Hegel): "Hegel's dialectic — thesis, antithesis, synthesis — is presented as the essential pattern of all reality. But Russell finds the dialectic logically flawed. Hegel deduces the nature of reality from pure thought, ignoring empirical evidence entirely. His system is magnificent but rests on sand." Russell's critique of Hegel is one of the most extended and pointed in the book.
> **Key takeaway**: Russell's criticism of Hegel is not just philosophical — it's methodological. Philosophy must respect empirical science. Any system that claims to deduce the nature of the universe from pure thought alone is, for Russell, guilty of intellectual hubris.
