# 1 — Ancient Philosophy

> Source: "A History of Western Philosophy" by Bertrand Russell

## The Greek Beginning

Western philosophy began with the pre-Socratics, who asked what the world was made of. Socrates shifted the focus to ethics. Plato and Aristotle built the foundation for all subsequent Western thought.

> **Case: Pythagoras** (Book I, Chapter III — Pythagoras): "Pythagoras was the most influential of all the pre-Socratics. His combination of mathematics and mysticism — the belief that numbers are the ultimate reality — influenced Plato profoundly and, through Plato, all of Western philosophy." Russell devotes a full chapter to Pythagoras, calling him "intellectually one of the most important men that ever lived."
> **Key takeaway**: Russell's treatment of Pythagoras illustrates his method: he connects ideas (mathematics → mysticism → Plato's Forms) across centuries. Pythagoras is not a historical curiosity — he set a trajectory that runs through the entire Western tradition.

> **Case: Plato and Aristotle** (Book I, Chapters on Plato and Aristotle): "Plato's theory of Forms — the idea that the material world is an imperfect copy of a perfect, eternal reality — is the foundation of idealism. Aristotle rejected this, insisting that universal forms exist only in particular things. Their disagreement defined the central debate of medieval and modern philosophy." Russell presents Plato and Aristotle as the two poles around which Western philosophy revolves.
> **Key takeaway**: Russell does not hide his preferences. He admires Plato's literary genius and metaphysical ambition but finds Aristotle's systematic, empirical approach more productive. The Plato-Aristotle tension is not just ancient history — it reappears throughout the book.
