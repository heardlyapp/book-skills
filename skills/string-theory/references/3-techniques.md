# David Foster Wallace's Literary Craft

> Source: String Theory, all essays; Introduction by John Jeremiah Sullivan

## DFW's Writing Style

David Foster Wallace was perhaps the greatest American writer of his generation. His tennis essays show all his characteristic strengths.

### Key Stylistic Features

| Feature | Description | Example in String Theory |
|---------|-------------|-------------------------|
| Footnotes | Digressions, asides, jokes that add texture | The etymology of "tennis" in the introduction |
| Technical precision | Exact terminology for physical movements | Federer's grip, footwork, shot selection described with precision |
| High-low synthesis | Mixing philosophy and pop culture | Comparing Federer to Mozart, then to a video game character |
| Humor and sincerity | Switching between hilarious and deeply serious | Laugh-out-loud observations about tennis parents, then profound meditations on excellence |
| Self-referential honesty | Acknowledging his own limitations, biases, and struggles | His own history as a junior player, his awareness of writing about sport as a literary outsider |
| Metaphorical richness | Sporting action described in terms of art, philosophy, and everyday life | The forehand as "a thing of beauty" that "makes you feel something" |

### Writing About Sport

DFW's approach to sportswriting: take the sport seriously without taking yourself too seriously. Write about the game with the same intellectual depth you would bring to any other subject. Don't condescend to the reader or the athlete. Assume that sport matters because it matters to the people who play it and watch it.

> **Case: The Federer Essay's Opening Gambit** ("Federer Both Flesh and Not")
> The essay opens with DFW not writing about Federer at all but about the experience of watching him. He describes the physical sensation of seeing Federer play — the way the body responds to beauty. This is brilliantly indirect: by writing about the observer's experience, he tells us more about Federer than any direct description could. This is DFW's signature move: approaching the subject sideways, through its effects on the observer.
> **Key takeaway**: To describe something transcendent, sometimes you describe not the thing itself but what it does to people. The beauty of Federer is best captured not by analyzing his technique but by describing the experience of watching him.

> **Case: The Tennis History in the Introduction**
> The book's introduction by John Jeremiah Sullivan traces the word "tennis" through French, Italian, and English — from the French "Tenez!" (Take it!) to Petrarch to Shakespeare. By the time you reach DFW's essays, tennis has been established not as a sport but as a cultural and literary phenomenon. This framing is crucial: it tells the reader that we are about to read writing about tennis that takes the game as seriously as any other human endeavor.
> **Key takeaway**: Great writing establishes context — it shows that the subject has history, depth, and meaning before engaging with it directly. The introductory material is not decoration; it is the foundation.
