# Core Framework: The Vagabonding Ethic

Vagabonding is not a travel technique — it's a way of being in the world. Potts distinguishes it from tourism, backpacking, and vacationing by its core attitude: **extended travel as a practice of life, not an escape from it.**

## The Antisabbatical

Most people think of extended travel as something you do once or twice in a lifetime — between college and career, between jobs, or after retirement. Potts argues for the "antisabbatical": instead of taking one big break every 7 years, build regular, shorter travel time into your life. The goal is to make vagabonding a recurring practice, not a one-time event.

This reframes everything. You don't need to quit your job forever. You need to arrange your life so that travel is a regular part of it — even if it's 2-4 weeks a year, done mindfully and slowly.

> **Case: The Riverboat Epiphany** (Introduction): Potts describes reading Dale Carnegie's How to Win Friends and Influence People on a slow riverboat in Burma. He realized that travel advice books were missing something — they told you where to go but not how to think about travel itself. The "how to think" is vagabonding. The epiphany: long-term travel is not about logistics; it's about attitude.
> **Key takeaway:** The most important travel preparation isn't packing or planning — it's getting your mind right. Attitude determines experience.

## Fear as Fuel

Potts argues that fear of extended travel — fear of the unknown, of loneliness, of running out of money, of losing your career trajectory — is not a signal to stop. It's a signal that you're about to grow. The fear is proportional to the potential transformation. Use it as fuel, not as a brake.

> **Case: The First Trip** (Chapter 1, "Declare Your Independence"): Potts recounts his own first vagabonding trip — a year-long journey through Asia after college. He was terrified. He had no clear plan, limited savings, and no idea what he was doing. He went anyway. That trip changed the course of his life and led to his career as a travel writer. The fear he felt before leaving was the same energy that made the trip transformative.
> **Key takeaway:** The fear you feel before a big trip is not a warning — it's the initiation. Everyone feels it. The difference between those who go and those who don't is action despite fear.

## Simplify to Travel

Potts's core financial insight: every possession you own is a claim on your future time. You work to buy things. You store and maintain things. You insure things. The more you own, the more you need to earn, and the less freedom you have. Simplifying your life — owning less, needing less, spending less — is the most direct path to travel freedom.

The math is simple: if you reduce your monthly expenses by $500, that's $6,000/year — enough for months of travel in most parts of the world. Every expense you eliminate is a day of freedom you buy.
