# Anti-Patterns — The Controversy

> Source: American Dirt, Author's Note, critical reviews

## The Central Debate

### Who Gets to Tell This Story?

| Argument | Source |
|----------|--------|
| "White author writing Mexican experience = cultural appropriation" | Latinx critics, authors |
| "Stories build empathy regardless of author identity" | Supporters, publishers |
| "Publishing industry favors white authors over Latinx voices" | Industry critics |
| "The book sensationalizes migrant suffering for profit" | Some reviewers |

> **Case: The Author's Note** (Author's Note)
> Cummins wrote: "I wished someone slightly browner than me would write it." She acknowledged that she is a US-born white woman writing about an experience she has never lived. She said she wrote the book because the story demanded to be told — and no one else was telling it. The note was intended as humility. Critics called it performative: if you know you are not the right person, why write it at all?
> **Key takeaway:** The Author's Note is a case study in narrative ethics. Cummins tried to acknowledge her limitations — but the acknowledgment itself became part of the controversy. The lesson: good intentions do not guarantee the right to tell someone else's story.

> **Case: The Publishing Machine** (Critical Context)
> American Dirt was acquired by Flatiron Books for a reported seven-figure advance. Oprah chose it for her book club. Stephen King praised it. The publisher marketed it as "the next great American novel." Latinx critics noted: Latinx authors writing similar stories for years had received nowhere near this level of support. The controversy was not just about the book — it was about the publishing industry's pattern of elevating white authors while ignoring authors of color writing about their own communities.
> **Key takeaway:** The controversy around American Dirt is not just about one book. It is about structural inequality in publishing. The book became a symbol of a larger problem: who gets paid, who gets promoted, who gets to tell the story. The book's success and its criticism are two sides of the same coin.
