# 4. Anti-Patterns — Budgeting Mistakes

> Source: "You Need a Budget" by Jesse Mecham (2017). Common budgeting failures.

## Mistake 1: The Unrealistic Budget

**What it looks like**: Setting category amounts based on what you wish you spent, not what you actually spend.

**Why it fails**: An unrealistic budget is abandoned within weeks. Start with your actual spending, then adjust gradually.

> **Case: The Food Budget** (Chapter 4): Mecham describes a user who set her food budget at $200/month — half of what she actually spent. She overspent by $200 in the first week and gave up entirely. A realistic budget starts with what is, not what should be.
> **Key takeaway**: Budget for the person you are, not the person you wish you were. You can change over time.

## Mistake 2: Ignoring True Expenses

**What it looks like**: Not budgeting for irregular expenses (car repairs, Christmas, annual insurance).

**Why it fails**: When the expense hits, it becomes a "crisis" that blows up your budget.

## Mistake 3: The All-or-Nothing Mindset

**What it looks like**: Missing one day of tracking, then giving up entirely.

**Why it fails**: Perfection is not required. Consistency is. Roll with the punches. Missed a day? Start again tomorrow.

## Mistake 4: Budgeting Alone

**What it looks like**: One partner controls the budget; the other resents it.

**Why it fails**: Budgeting requires buy-in from everyone who spends money. Couples who budget together stay together — financially.

> **Case: The Couple Budget** (Chapter 6): Mecham describes a couple who fought about money every month. The solution: a weekly "budget date" where they review together. The joint review transformed their relationship — money became a team sport, not a battlefield.
> **Key takeaway**: Budgeting with a partner requires communication, not control. Regular check-ins prevent resentment.
