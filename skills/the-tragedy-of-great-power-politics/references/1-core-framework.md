# Core Framework: Offensive Realism

## The Central Thesis

"The international system creates powerful incentives for great powers to pursue hegemony. It's not that they want to — it's that the structure of the system forces them to. States that don't maximize power get crushed."

> **Case: The Rise and Fall of Germany** (Chapter 1): Germany unified in 1871 and rapidly became the dominant power in Europe. Its rise alarmed Britain, France, and Russia. They formed a balancing coalition. Two world wars resulted. Mearsheimer's point: Germany's rise was not the result of evil leaders but of systemic pressures.
> **Key takeaway**: Leaders come and go, but the logic of great power competition remains. Germany was not uniquely aggressive — it was responding to the same pressures any rising power faces.

## Key Concepts

| Concept | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| Anarchy | No world government to enforce rules |
| Security Dilemma | One state's security threatens others |
| Relative Power | What matters is power compared to rivals, not absolute |
| Offensive Realism | Great powers seek to maximize power |
| Defensive Realism | States seek security, not necessarily more power |

> **Case: The Peloponnesian War** (Chapter 2): Thucydides wrote: "It was the rise of Athens and the fear this caused in Sparta that made war inevitable." This is the classic statement of offensive realism — 2,400 years before Mearsheimer.
> **Key takeaway**: The logic of great power competition hasn't changed since ancient Greece. The names of the powers change, but the dynamics remain the same.
