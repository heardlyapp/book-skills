# Key Principles

## 1. Anarchy Is the Starting Point

"There is no world government. Each state is responsible for its own survival. This simple fact drives everything."

## 2. Intentions Cannot Be Known

"No state can ever be certain of another state's intentions. Today's ally could be tomorrow's enemy. This creates perpetual mistrust."

> **Case: The Soviet Dilemma** (Chapter 4): After WWII, the Soviet Union wanted a buffer zone in Eastern Europe for security. But the US saw this as aggressive expansion. The Soviet desire for security created the very threat it feared. This is the security dilemma in action.
> **Key takeaway**: One state's defensive measures look offensive to others. The security dilemma is a structural feature of anarchy, not a result of particular leaders.

## 3. Relative Power Matters More Than Absolute Power

"What matters is not how much power you have but how much you have compared to your rivals."

## 4. Great Powers Are Revisionist

"All great powers want to become hegemons. They are not satisfied with the status quo. They seek to change it in their favor."

## 5. Geography Constrains Ambition

"The stopping power of water makes global hegemony impossible." You can dominate your region but projecting power across oceans is extremely difficult.

> **Case: The Napoleonic Failure** (Chapter 5): Napoleon conquered most of Europe but could not cross the English Channel to defeat Britain. Britain survived and built coalitions that eventually defeated France. The "stopping power of water" prevented French hegemony.
> **Key takeaway**: Water is the ultimate defense. No power has ever achieved global hegemony because oceans prevent the projection and sustainment of military power over long distances.
