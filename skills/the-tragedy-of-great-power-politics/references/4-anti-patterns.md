# Anti-Patterns

## 1. Believing Democracy Prevents War

**The mistake**: Thinking that if all states were democracies, there would be no war.

**Fix**: The structure of the international system, not regime type, drives conflict. Anarchical systems create security competition regardless of domestic politics.

> **Case: The Russo-Ukrainian War** (Chapter 9 prediction): Mearsheimer predicted in the 1990s that NATO expansion would lead to war with Russia. Critics said democracies don't fight. But Russia is not a democracy, and the structural pressure of NATO expansion into Russia's sphere of influence triggered the conflict. The system, not regime type, was the driver.
> **Key takeaway**: Foreign policy is shaped by the international system, not domestic politics. A state's location and relative power matter more than whether it holds elections.

## 2. Believing Interdependence Prevents Conflict

**The mistake**: Thinking trade ties make war impossible.

**Fix**: Economic interdependence can create vulnerability, increasing rather than decreasing the incentive to conflict.

## 3. Blaming Leaders Instead of Systems

**The mistake**: Thinking wars happen because of bad leaders.

**Fix**: Leaders matter at the margins, but systemic pressures constrain them. Different leaders facing the same structural pressures often make similar choices.

> **Case: The Kaiser vs Hitler** (Chapter 2): Both Wilhelmine Germany and Nazi Germany pursued European hegemony. The leaders were very different, but the structural logic was the same: a powerful state in the center of Europe must dominate or be contained.
> **Key takeaway**: The system constrains leaders more than leaders constrain the system. Blaming individuals misses the deeper structural drivers of international conflict.
