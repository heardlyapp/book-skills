# Techniques: Analyzing Great Power Competition

## Mearsheimer's Five Propositions

1. Great powers fear each other (perpetual security competition)
2. Great powers maximize relative power (offensive realism)
3. Great powers are rational actors (they think strategically)
4. Great powers cannot trust each other (information problem)
5. Survival is the primary goal (everything else is secondary)

> **Case: The Cold War Balance** (Chapter 7): The US-Soviet bipolar system was remarkably stable. Both superpowers understood the costs of direct conflict. They competed through proxies but never fought directly. Bipolarity (two superpowers) is more stable than multipolarity (many great powers).
> **Key takeaway**: Stability doesn't mean peace — it means no great power war. The Cold War was dangerous but the structure of bipolarity made direct conflict less likely than in the multipolar world before WWII.

## Power Measurement

| Dimension | Metric | 
|-----------|--------|
| Military | Size, technology, readiness, nuclear capability |
| Economic | GDP, industrial base, technology |
| Population | Size, education, health |
| Geography | Size, location, natural defenses |
| Political | Stability, national unity, institutions |

> **Case: Japan as a Military Power?** (Chapter 8): Japan is an economic giant but geographically constrained. It cannot project power across its region without amphibious capability. Mearsheimer argues Japan will remain limited militarily because geography constrains it, not because of its constitution.
> **Key takeaway**: Geography is destiny in Mearsheimer's framework. A state's power projection capability is fundamentally shaped by its location and physical geography.
