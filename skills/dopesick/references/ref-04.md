# Reference: The Fight for Treatment

## Overview
For decades, addiction was treated as a crime, not a disease. The fight to change how we treat opioid addiction has been led by activists, doctors, and families who refused to accept that their loved ones were "junkies."

## Case: Medication-Assisted Treatment
MAT (medication-assisted treatment) is the gold standard for opioid addiction. Methadone has been used since the 1960s. Buprenorphine (Suboxone) was approved in 2002. These medications reduce cravings and prevent withdrawal without producing a high. Patients on MAT are 50% less likely to die from an overdose. Despite the evidence, MAT has been controversial and difficult to access. Only a fraction of people who need MAT receive it.

## Case: The Suboxone Barrier
Suboxone (buprenorphine/naloxone) is the most effective medication for opioid addiction. But doctors who prescribe it must obtain a special waiver from the DEA. For years, the waiver limit was capped at 30 patients, then 100, then 275. This restriction dramatically limited access to treatment. In rural areas, there might be no waivered doctor within 100 miles. The result: effective treatment exists but is not available to most who need it.

## Case: The Harm Reduction Movement
Harm reduction — meeting people where they are rather than demanding abstinence — has been controversial but effective. Needle exchange programs reduce HIV and hepatitis C transmission. Naloxone distribution has saved thousands of lives. Supervised consumption sites prevent overdose deaths. Beth Macy profiles activists who fought for these programs against political opposition. Their work has been essential in reducing the death toll.
