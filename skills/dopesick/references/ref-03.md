# Reference: Community Devastation

## Overview
The opioid epidemic has devastated communities across America, especially in rural areas and small towns. The human cost goes far beyond overdose statistics.

## Case: Appalachian Communities
The epidemic hit Appalachian communities hardest. Coal mining towns in West Virginia, Kentucky, and Ohio had high rates of workplace injuries (leading to painkiller prescriptions) and limited economic opportunities (leading to despair). The combination was devastating. Entire communities were decimated: grandparents raising grandchildren, funeral homes overwhelmed, schools struggling with students whose parents were addicted or dead.

## Case: The Foster Care Crisis
As parents became addicted, children entered the foster care system in unprecedented numbers. States like West Virginia saw foster care placements increase 60%+ between 2010 and 2017. The system was not designed for this volume. Children were placed in group homes, separated from siblings, and moved repeatedly. The opioid epidemic created a generation of children who lost their parents to addiction — either to death or to the criminal justice system.

## Case: The Heroin and Fentanyl Transition
When prescription opioids became harder to obtain (due to prescription monitoring programs), many users switched to heroin and then to fentanyl. Fentanyl is 50-100 times more potent than morphine. Illicit manufacturers add it to heroin and other drugs to increase potency. The result: overdose deaths accelerated. In 2021, over 100,000 Americans died of drug overdoses — most involving fentanyl. The death toll was higher than any year of the AIDS epidemic.
