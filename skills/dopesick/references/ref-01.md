# Reference: The OxyContin Story

## Overview
Purdue Pharma's OxyContin was approved by the FDA in 1995. It was promoted as a breakthrough painkiller with a low risk of addiction due to its time-release formulation. The marketing campaign was one of the most aggressive and misleading in pharmaceutical history.

## Case: The Marketing Machine
Purdue launched OxyContin with an army of sales representatives who made over 30,000 visits to doctors in the first year. The sales pitch: OxyContin was "non-addicting" because of its time-release mechanism. Doctors were given free samples, branded merchandise, and invitations to all-expenses-paid conferences at resorts. The marketing budget for OxyContin exceeded $200 million per year. Sales reps were trained to downplay addiction risk and to tell doctors that less than 1% of patients became addicted — a statistic that had no basis in evidence.

## Case: Pain as the Fifth Vital Sign
In the 1990s, the Veterans Health Administration and The Joint Commission launched a campaign to make pain the "fifth vital sign" — to be measured and treated as aggressively as blood pressure, heart rate, and temperature. Doctors were told that untreated pain was a public health crisis. They were evaluated on patient satisfaction scores that included pain management. The campaign, well-intentioned, created enormous pressure to prescribe opioids. Purdue funded materials for the campaign and positioned OxyContin as the solution.

## Case: The Sackler Family
Purdue Pharma was owned by the Sackler family, one of the wealthiest families in America. Members of the family served on Purdue's board and received billions in dividends. Despite knowing about OxyContin's abuse potential (internal memos and emails would later show the company was aware by 1996), the Sacklers continued to push for aggressive sales targets. The family has been described as "the most evil family in modern American history."
