# Reference: The Science of Addiction

## Overview
Addiction is a chronic brain disease, not a moral failing. Understanding the neuroscience of addiction is essential to understanding why the opioid epidemic has been so devastating and why punishment does not work.

## Case: How Opioids Hijack the Brain
Opioids bind to mu-opioid receptors in the brain, triggering a massive release of dopamine. The user experiences intense pleasure and pain relief. With repeated use, the brain adapts: it reduces its own dopamine production and becomes dependent on the drug to function normally. The user now needs the drug just to feel normal, not to feel high. This is the transition from physical dependence to addiction.

## Case: The Vicious Cycle of Withdrawal
When someone dependent on opioids stops taking them, withdrawal symptoms begin within hours: anxiety, muscle aches, insomnia, diarrhea, vomiting, and intense cravings. The symptoms are not just uncomfortable — they are physically painful and psychologically overwhelming. Many people return to opioids not to get high but to escape withdrawal. This cycle — use, withdrawal, relief, use — is extremely difficult to break without medical help.

## Case: Tolerance and the Escalation to Heroin
Over time, the user needs higher doses of opioids to achieve the same effect. Prescription pills become too expensive. Heroin is cheaper and often easier to obtain. The user transitions from legal pills to illegal drugs. The heroin supply is then contaminated with fentanyl, which is exponentially more potent and deadlier.
