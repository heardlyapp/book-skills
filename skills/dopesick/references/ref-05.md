# Reference: The Legal Aftermath

## Overview
The legal response to the opioid epidemic has been unprecedented. Thousands of lawsuits have been filed against Purdue Pharma, the Sackler family, and other pharmaceutical companies.

## Case: The Purdue Bankruptcy
Purdue Pharma filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy in 2019, facing thousands of lawsuits from states, counties, and cities. The bankruptcy process was an attempt to resolve all claims in a single proceeding. A 2021 settlement required the Sackler family to contribute $4.5 billion to a trust fund for addiction treatment and compensated victims. In exchange, the Sacklers received immunity from future lawsuits. The deal was controversial: critics argued it let the Sacklers off too easily.

## Case: The State Lawsuits
Nearly every state sued Purdue Pharma and the Sackler family. West Virginia, Ohio, and Kentucky — states hit hardest by the epidemic — were among the plaintiffs. The lawsuits alleged that Purdue misled doctors and patients about OxyContin's addiction risk. Evidence included internal memos showing the company knew about the addiction problem years before it was publicly acknowledged. The settlements have directed billions of dollars to addiction treatment, though critics argue the amount is far less than the damage caused.

## Case: Criminal Prosecutions
Criminal cases have been rare. Purdue executives were indicted in 2007 and pleaded guilty to misbranding OxyContin — a misdemeanor. No senior executive went to prison. In 2020, Purdue pleaded guilty to conspiracy to defraud the United States and agreed to pay $8 billion (most of which was forgiven due to bankruptcy). The Sackler family members have not been criminally charged, though they have been called to testify before Congress. The lack of criminal accountability remains a source of anger for many affected communities.
