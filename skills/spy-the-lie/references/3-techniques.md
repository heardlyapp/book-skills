## Practical Techniques

### 1. The Baseline Interview

For 2-3 minutes, ask neutral questions about topics the person has no reason to lie about. Observe their normal verbal, non-verbal, and para-linguistic behavior.

### 2. The Reverse Timeline

After a person recounts events forward, ask them to recount backward. "Start at the end and work backward." Liars struggle.

### 3. The Bait Question

Ask a question that includes a specific detail only the truth-teller would know — or that only the liar would know. Watch for the reaction.

> **Case: The Bait Question in Action** (Chapter 9): An investigator asked: "Did you meet with John on Tuesday?" The suspect did not know John had been seen. His reaction — a pause, followed by "No, I did not" — revealed that he was processing the knowledge, not recalling the memory.
> **Key takeaway**: Truth-tellers respond immediately. Liars pause to process. The pause is a para-linguistic cue.

### 4. The "Oops" Word Detection

Listen for information that the person should not know. If they reveal a detail that only a participant would know, you have found a deception indicator.

### 5. The Cluster Assessment

After the interview, list all the behavioral deviations from baseline. If you have 3+ in the same category (verbal, non-verbal, para-linguistic), you have a cluster.

### 6. The "Just the Facts" Reset

If the person is over-explaining or adding unnecessary detail, ask: "Just tell me the facts. What happened?" This increases cognitive load on someone fabricating details.

> **Case: The Over-Explainer** (Chapter 8): The executive who over-explained every transaction — when asked to "just give me the facts," he could not. His narrative collapsed.
> **Key takeaway**: Over-explanation is a verbal cue. Asking for only the facts strips away the fabrication.
