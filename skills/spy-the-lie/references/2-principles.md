## 7 Principles of Deception Detection

### 1. There Is No Pinocchio Effect

No single cue reliably indicates deception. Anyone who claims otherwise is selling something.

### 2. Baseline Is Everything

You cannot detect deception until you know what "normal" looks like for that person. Always establish baseline first.

> **Case: The Nervous Truth-Teller** (Chapter 3): A man accused of a crime was sweating, avoiding eye contact, and stammering. He was telling the truth. He was merely nervous.
> **Key takeaway**: Truth-tellers can look guilty. Guilty people can look innocent. The baseline is the only reliable reference.

### 3. Clusters, Not Singles

Three behaviors together are meaningful. One behavior is noise. Never accuse based on a single indicator.

### 4. The Right Questions Create Pressure

Deception appears when you ask the right questions. Passive observation will not detect lies.

### 5. Cognitive Load Is the Key

Liars are cognitively overloaded. Ask questions that increase that load — and the lie will break.

### 6. The Reverse Timeline

Ask for events in reverse order. Truth-tellers can do this. Liars cannot.

### 7. Avoid False Positives

The greatest danger is accusing an innocent person. When in doubt, gather more data. Do not jump to conclusions.

> **Case: The Corporate Fraud** (Chapter 8): An executive was questioned about financial irregularities. He provided too much detail — over-explaining everything. The over-explanation was a verbal cue of deception. Truth-tellers answer the question. Liars explain why they are not lying.
> **Key takeaway**: Over-explaining is a deception cue. Truth-tellers answer directly. Liars feel the need to prove their innocence.
