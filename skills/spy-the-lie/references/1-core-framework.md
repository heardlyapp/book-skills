## The Houston/Floyd Framework: Baseline, Cluster, Question

The CIA framework is three-step: (1) Establish a baseline of normal behavior. (2) Ask questions that create cognitive load for liars. (3) Look for clusters of behavioral changes that deviate from baseline.

**The Key Insight**: Liars are not nervous because they are lying — they are cognitively overloaded. Maintaining a lie takes mental effort. That effort leaks out through behavioral changes.

> **Case: The CIA Recruit** (Chapter 1): A CIA recruit was suspected of lying about his background. During questioning, he became unusually still — a departure from his normal animated behavior. The stilling, combined with vague answers and delayed responses, formed a cluster that indicated deception.
> **Key takeaway**: Stillness can be as suspicious as fidgeting. The key is deviation from baseline — not the specific behavior.

> **Case: The Reverse Timeline** (Chapter 7): An officer asked a suspect to recount his activities in reverse order. Truth-tellers can do this. Liars struggle — they have to invent both the events and the order simultaneously.
> **Key takeaway**: The reverse timeline test is powerful because it increases cognitive load. Liars cannot maintain the lie under the pressure of backward narration.
The framework is built on the premise that deception is detectable — but only if you use the right method.
 The right method: baseline, cluster, effective questioning.
