## 6 Anti-Patterns of Deception Detection Myths

### 1. "Liars Avoid Eye Contact"

Liars may avoid eye contact — or they may stare intensely to seem truthful. There is no reliable pattern.

### 2. "Nervous People Are Lying"

Nervousness is not deception. Innocent people can be terrified of being accused. Establish baseline first.

> **Case: The Falsely Accused** (Chapter 3): A man accused of theft was sweating, stammering, and acting guilty. He was innocent. His nervousness was fear of being disbelieved. His baseline was calm. The deviation was caused by fear, not guilt.
> **Key takeaway**: Innocent people may look more guilty than guilty people. Nervousness is not evidence without a baseline.

### 3. "One Behavior Confirms Deception"

A single behavior is meaningless. Touching the nose, crossing arms, looking away — none alone indicate deception.

### 4. "You Can Detect Lies from Observation Alone"

Passive observation is not enough. You must ask questions that create pressure. Deception is detected through interaction.

### 5. "Experienced Liars Cannot Be Caught"

The CIA method works on experienced liars because it targets cognitive load, not nervousness. Everyone has a cognitive limit.

### 6. "Deception Detection Is 100% Accurate"

No method is perfect. False positives and false negatives are inevitable. The goal is to increase accuracy, not achieve perfection.

> **Case: The Trained Liar** (Chapter 10): A suspect who had been trained to lie (an intelligence operative) was caught using the reverse timeline. His training helped him lie forward. He could not lie backward.
> **Key takeaway**: Even trained liars have limits. The reverse timeline exploits a cognitive vulnerability that training cannot overcome.
