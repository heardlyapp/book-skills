# Techniques: Building Grit

## Technique 1: The Hard Things Rule
Duckworth's family rule: everyone must do one hard thing every day — something that requires deliberate practice. You can quit, but only at a natural stopping point (end of semester, season, year), never on a bad day.

**Why it works:** It teaches that hard things are normal, quitting is allowed but not impulsive, and sticking with something long enough to see progress builds confidence.

> **Case: The Piano Lessons** (Chapter 10): Duckworth's daughter took piano lessons. She wanted to quit many times. The Hard Things Rule meant she could quit at the end of the year, not in the middle of a frustrating week. Each time she almost quit, she'd work through the frustration, improve, and decide to continue. Over years, she developed genuine skill and enjoyment. The rule kept her going through the plateaus.
> **Key takeaway:** Most quitting happens on bad days. If you make yourself wait until a natural break point, you often discover that you want to continue.

## Technique 2: The Grit Scale Assessment
Take the 10-item Grit Scale. Score yourself on passion (consistency of interest) and perseverance (effort over time). Identify which dimension is weaker. If your passion score is low, work on deepening interest. If your perseverance score is low, work on daily habits and the Hard Things Rule.

## Technique 3: The Goal Hierarchy Map
Write your top-level life goal at the top of a page. Below it, write 3-5 mid-level goals that support it. Below each mid-level goal, write 2-3 daily or weekly actions. If your daily actions don't clearly connect to your top goal, you have a misalignment.

## Technique 4: The Purpose Reframe
When your daily work feels meaningless, ask: "Who does this help?" and "Why does this matter?" Even mundane tasks can connect to a larger purpose. The janitor at NASA helped put a man on the moon. Reframe your work.

## Technique 5: The Deliberate Practice Log
Each practice session, write down: (1) What specific skill am I improving? (2) What's my goal for today? (3) What feedback did I get? (4) What will I adjust tomorrow? Without a log, practice becomes repetition. With a log, it becomes deliberate.

The Hard Things Rule is Duckworth's most practical tool. Everyone must do one hard thing every day that requires deliberate practice. You can quit, but only at a natural stopping point - never on a bad day. This rule builds the habit of pushing through difficulty without forcing you to commit forever.

> **Case: The Piano Lessons** (Chapter 10): Duckworth's daughter wanted to quit piano many times. The rule meant she could quit at year-end, not mid-frustration. Each time she almost quit, she'd push through, improve, and decide to continue. Over years, she developed genuine skill.
> **Key takeaway:** Most quitting happens on bad days. If you wait until a natural break point, you often discover you want to continue.
