# Reference: The Five Focusing Steps

## Overview
The five focusing steps are the practical method for implementing TOC. They provide a universal process that can be applied to any system.

## Case: Step 1 — Identify the Constraint
At Alex Rogo's plant (UniCo), the constraint was the NCX-10 machine. This was the slowest machine in the plant, and it determined the plant's total output. All work piled up before the NCX-10, and machines after it were starved. Every manager knew the NCX-10 was a bottleneck, but the company's cost accounting system made it invisible. The NCX-10 was treated as just another machine. Identifying it as the constraint was the first step toward improving it.

## Case: Step 2 — Exploit the Constraint
Once the NCX-10 was identified as the constraint, the team implemented changes to get the most out of it. They ensured the NCX-10 was never idle. Workers took lunch breaks while the machine ran. Maintenance was scheduled during off-hours. Quality inspection was moved before the NCX-10 so defective parts did not waste its time. These changes increased NCX-10 throughput by 30% without any capital investment.

## Case: Step 5 — Repeat
After the NCX-10 was elevated (a new machine was purchased), the constraint moved to the market — the plant could produce more than it could sell. The five steps then had to be reapplied to the new constraint. Goldratt emphasizes that the constraint is always moving. Improvement is a continuous process, not a one-time event.

## Case: Step 3-4 — Subordinate and Elevate
Step 3 (Subordinate) meant running every other machine at the pace of the NCX-10, not at full capacity. This was counterintuitive — it meant intentionally running non-bottleneck machines slower than they could go. Step 4 (Elevate) meant buying a second NCX-10 to increase bottleneck capacity. The subordination step was the hardest: managers had to overcome their conditioning that every machine must run at full capacity.
