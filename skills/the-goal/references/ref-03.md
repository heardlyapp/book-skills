# Reference: Throughput Accounting

## Overview
Goldratt argues that conventional cost accounting is fundamentally flawed and that companies should instead use throughput accounting — focusing on throughput, inventory, and operating expense.

## Case: The Cost Accounting Trap
Under standard cost accounting, a machine that is not running is "losing money" because it is not absorbing overhead. This creates pressure to run every machine at full capacity, even when there is no demand for the output. Alex Rogo's plant was evaluated on machine utilization; managers ran machines to keep utilization numbers high, even if the products could not be sold. The plant was producing inventory it did not need, which tied up cash and hidden inefficiencies.

## Case: The Three Measures
Goldratt defines three global measures: Throughput (the rate at which the system generates money through sales), Inventory (all the money the system has invested in things it intends to sell), and Operating Expense (all the money spent to turn inventory into throughput). The goal is to increase throughput while simultaneously reducing inventory and operating expense. Cost accounting focuses on local measures (machine utilization, labor efficiency); throughput accounting focuses on global measures.

## Case: The Product Mix Decision
A key insight in the book is that when a plant has a bottleneck, the product mix must be optimized based on throughput per constraint hour, not on standard margin. A product with a higher margin per unit may have low throughput per constraint hour. Producing it consumes constraint capacity that could be better used to produce other products. The product mix decision is one of the most powerful applications of TOC.
