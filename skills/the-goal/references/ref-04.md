# Reference: Jonah's Lessons

## Overview
Jonah is the mysterious consultant in the novel who teaches Alex Rogo how to think. His Socratic questioning method — asking questions rather than giving answers — is the pedagogical core of the book.

## Case: The Robot Trap
UniCo bought an expensive robot that was supposed to improve productivity. The robot replaced several workers and was a technological marvel. But after installation, the plant's performance did not improve. Jonah asked: "Did you reduce operating expense?" The workers were reassigned, not fired. "Did you increase throughput?" No. "Did you reduce inventory?" No. The robot was a waste of money. The lesson: technology that does not improve throughput, reduce inventory, or decrease operating expense is not an improvement.

## Case: The Inventory Questions
Jonah repeatedly asks Alex: "What is the goal?" Alex gives various answers — produce high-quality products, satisfy customers, provide employment, produce goods efficiently. Jonah rejects them all. The answer: the goal is to make money. Everything else is a means to that end. The inventory debate is central: accounting treats inventory as an asset. Goldratt treats it as money the system has invested and not yet recovered.

## Case: The Socratic Method
Jonah never tells Alex what to do. He asks questions that force Alex to discover the answers himself. "If you were running a plant and you wanted to increase throughput, what would you do?" "What is preventing you from doing that?" "If you could do that, what would happen?" The method teaches the reader how to think about any problem, not just the specific problems in the book. This is Goldratt's greatest contribution: not the answers but the way of thinking.
