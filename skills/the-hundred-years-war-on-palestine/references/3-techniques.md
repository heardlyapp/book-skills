# Techniques — The Hundred Years' War on Palestine

## How to Understand the Conflict (Based on Rashid Khalidi)

### Technique 1: Read the Sources from Both Sides
Khalidi relies on Ottoman, Palestinian, Israeli, and Western sources — military archives, private letters, newspapers in Arabic/English/French/Hebrew. He repeatedly shows how Zionist sources themselves confirm the colonial nature of the project (Jabotinsky's "iron wall," Herzl's diary). The technique: don't just read one side's history. Look for what each side's own documents reveal.

### Technique 2: The Family Archive Method
Khalidi uses his own family's papers — the Khalidi Library, Yusuf Diya's letters, personal correspondence spanning 150 years — to ground the history in lived experience. The technique: personal archives reveal what official documents erase. "Although members of my family have been involved in events in Palestine for years, as have I, our experiences are not unique."

### Technique 3: Test the "Land Without a People" Claim
Khalidi presents census data, traveler accounts, agricultural records, and tax rolls that show Palestine was densely populated and economically active before Zionist colonization. The technique: when someone says a land was "empty" or "barren," check the primary sources.

### Technique 4: Follow the External Power
Every phase of the conflict was enabled by an external power: Britain (Balfour, Mandate, crushing the 1936 revolt), the UN (partition plan 1947), and the US (post-1967 diplomatic/military support). The technique: understand the conflict as shaped by great-power politics, not just local dynamics.

### Technique 5: Track the Settlement Growth
Settlement construction in the occupied territories accelerated under every Israeli government, including those supposedly committed to peace. The technique: judge Israeli governments by their actions on settlements, not their rhetoric about peace.

### Technique 6: The "Two Narratives" Test
Khalidi acknowledges that Israelis and Palestinians have fundamentally different narratives of the same events. 1948 is either the War of Independence or the Nakba. The technique: don't try to pick one "true" narrative — understand both, and understand that each validates the other side's fears and grievances.

### Technique 7: Watch for What Was Erased
Or as scholar Darryl Pinckney put it, "If a people cannot imagine a future, its culture will die." Palestine before 1948 had newspapers, universities, an elected assembly, a vibrant intellectual life. Most of this was physically destroyed or rendered invisible. The technique: look for what was lost, not just what was built.
