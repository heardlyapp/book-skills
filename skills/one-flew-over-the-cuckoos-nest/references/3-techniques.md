# 3. Techniques — Kesey's Narrative Craft

> Source: "One Flew Over the Cuckoo's Nest" by Ken Kesey (1962). Techniques drawn from Kesey's choice of narrator and prose style.

## The Unreliable First-Person Narrator

Kesey's most radical choice: narrating the novel through the consciousness of a diagnosed schizophrenic. Bromden's perception is unreliable — he hallucinates, sees machines controlling people, hears things that aren't there. But his distorted vision reveals truths that a "sane" narrator could not.

> **Case: The Fog Machine** (Throughout): Bromden believes the hospital uses a fog machine to control patients. There is no fog machine. But the metaphor captures how psychiatric medication and institutional routines produce a fog of confusion and compliance in patients.
> **Key takeaway**: Metaphor can be more true than fact. The fog machine doesn't exist — but it's real.

## The Magical Realist Mode

Kesey blends realistic description with hallucinatory elements seamlessly. This is magical realism before the term was widely used in English.

> **Case: The Combine** (Part 1): Bromden describes the hospital as a factory where the Combine (a vast societal machine) processes people into compliant components. This is clearly a hallucination — and yet it describes the actual function of total institutions with perfect accuracy.
> **Key takeaway**: The most powerful political criticism can come through what appears to be madness.

## Dialogue as Power Struggle

Every conversation in the novel is a negotiation for status. McMurphy and Ratched's exchanges are battles disguised as conversations.

> **Case: The Group Therapy Sessions** (Throughout): The "therapy" sessions are rituals of shaming. Ratched encourages patients to expose each other's weaknesses. McMurphy's strategy is to refuse to participate in the shame economy — he treats the disclosures as jokes.
> **Key takeaway**: Dialogue in institutional settings is never neutral. Every word is a move in the power game.
