# Core Framework: The Political vs. Racial Identity Distinction

The central framework of Gilio-Whitaker's book is that Native American identity is fundamentally a **political status**, not a racial or ethnic one. This distinction is critical for understanding everything from ethnic fraud to disenrollment to cultural appropriation.

## Political Identity

Native Americans are citizens of sovereign tribal nations. This is a political relationship — akin to being a citizen of the United States, Canada, or Japan. Tribal nations have the inherent right to determine their own citizenship criteria, just as any sovereign nation does. These criteria vary: some use blood quantum (a minimum percentage of Native ancestry), others use lineal descent (direct descendant of an enrolled member), and still others use community recognition.

**Key insight:** No external authority — not the US government, not DNA tests, not family lore — gets to determine who is a citizen of a sovereign tribal nation. Only the nation itself decides.

> **Case: The Cherokee Nation** (Chapter 2): The Cherokee Nation, one of the largest tribal nations in the US, requires lineal descent from an enrolled ancestor listed on the Dawes Rolls (compiled 1898-1914). This means someone with 1/32 Cherokee ancestry who can document their lineage can be a citizen, while someone with 1/2 Cherokee ancestry who can't trace to the Dawes Rolls cannot. It's not about blood — it's about documentation and the nation's sovereign right to set its own criteria.
> **Key takeaway:** Each tribe sets its own citizenship rules. There is no universal standard for who "counts" as Native.

## Racial Identity vs. Political Citizenship

Many non-Natives assume that Native identity works like race — if you have Native ancestry, you're Native. This confusion is a direct legacy of colonialism. The US government historically used blood quantum (a racial classification system) to define "Indianness," with the explicit goal of eliminating Native peoples as political entities. Over time, this racial framing became embedded in how Americans think about Native identity.

The reality: Native identity is membership in a political community. Someone with no Native ancestry can in theory be adopted into a tribe. Someone with 100% Native ancestry who is not enrolled in any tribe is not a tribal citizen.

> **Case: The Racialization of Indianness** (Chapter 2): Gilio-Whitaker traces how the US government systematically imposed racial categories on Native peoples to undermine tribal sovereignty. Blood quantum was introduced as a tool to limit the number of people who could claim tribal citizenship, with the goal that over generations, Native peoples would be "assimilated" out of existence entirely. The framework persists today even within many tribal enrollment criteria.
> **Key takeaway:** Blood quantum is a colonial invention designed to eliminate Native peoples. Using it as the measure of "Indianness" reproduces colonial logic.


## The Two Sides of the Same Coin
Gilio-Whitaker argues that pretendianism (false claims to Indianness) and disenrollment (tribes stripping citizenship) are two sides of the same settler colonial coin. Both commodify Indianness. Both are driven by capitalist incentives. Both cause profound harm to Native communities from opposite directions.
