# Techniques: Engaging Respectfully

## Technique 1: The Deference Protocol
Before forming or expressing an opinion about Native identity, tribal politics, or cultural practices: find actual Native voices on the topic. Read what they've written. Watch what they've said. Defer to their expertise. If you're not Native and you disagree with a Native person about Native issues, the most likely explanation is that you don't know what you're talking about.

## Technique 2: The Ancestry Story Check
If you've heard your family has a Native ancestor: investigate before claiming. Ask: Which nation? What's the documentation? Is there an enrollment number? Most family stories turn out to be myths — but even if true, ancestry doesn't equate to citizenship.

**Key question:** "Whose story is this — mine, or the Native person/community I'm referencing?"

## Technique 3: The Appropriation Test
When unsure whether something is cultural appropriation:
1. Who made this? (Native artisan or mass-produced by non-Natives?)
2. Who is this for? (Made for Native ceremonial use or for the tourist market?)
3. Who benefits? (Does the Native community benefit, or is the profit extracted?)
4. What's the context? (Worn in ceremony or as a fashion statement?)

## Technique 4: Learning the Vocabulary
Understanding key terms enables respectful conversation:
- **Tribal sovereignty** — The inherent right of tribal nations to self-govern
- **Enrolled member** — A citizen of a federally recognized tribe
- **Blood quantum** — The colonial measurement system for "Indian blood"
- **Indian Country** — The collective term for Native communities and nations
- **NDN** — Slang abbreviation for "Indian" used within Native communities

> **Case: The Hippie Shaman** (Chapter 4): Gilio-Whitaker describes the phenomenon of non-Native "plastic shamans" who adopt Native spiritual practices without understanding or respecting their cultural context. These individuals sell sweat lodges, vision quests, and other ceremonies to non-Natives, often for profit. In 2009, a non-Native "shaman" named James Arthur Ray led a sweat lodge ceremony in Sedona that killed three people. Ray had no connection to any Native spiritual tradition.
> **Key takeaway:** The commodification of Native spirituality is not just offensive — it's dangerous. Sacred ceremonies require cultural context and accountability that non-Native appropriators cannot provide.

## Technique 5: The Amplify Method
When you encounter Native issues being discussed: amplify Native voices rather than speaking for them. Share links to Native writers. Cite Native scholars. When someone asks a question about Native issues that you could answer, consider instead: "Let me share what a Native writer says about this." Your platform is most valuable when you pass it to those who need it.


> **Case: The Indian Arts and Crafts Act** (Chapter 3): The Indian Arts and Crafts Act of 1990 makes it illegal to sell products as "Native American" if they are not made by a registered tribal member or certified Native artisan. Despite the law, enforcement is minimal. A 2019 investigation found that over 60% of "Native American" jewelry sold in the Southwest was actually manufactured overseas. The law exists but the will to enforce it is weak.
> **Key takeaway:** Even legal protections for Native cultural property are poorly enforced. Buyer beware.
