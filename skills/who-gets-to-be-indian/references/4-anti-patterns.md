# Anti-Patterns: What NOT to Do

## ❌ Treating DNA Tests as Identity
"I took a DNA test and it says I'm 2% Native American." DNA tests can show Indigenous genetic markers — but they cannot determine tribal citizenship. They also cannot tell you which nation you belong to, cannot tell you if your ancestors were enrolled members, and cannot grant you the political status that defines Native identity. DNA tests are ancestry tools, not identity documents.

## ❌ Claiming Cherokee Princess Ancestry
This is the most common false identity claim in America — and it is almost always a myth. The "Cherokee princess" story is a family legend repeated across generations. Cherokee citizenship is documented. If you can't provide a Dawes Roll number for your ancestor, you're almost certainly not a Cherokee citizen.

> **Case: The Family Legend** (Conclusion): Gilio-Whitaker recounts how many people she meets share versions of the same story: "My grandmother was a Cherokee princess." This specific myth is so pervasive that the Cherokee Nation has officially addressed it multiple times. The reality: the Cherokee Nation had no "princesses." The role didn't exist in Cherokee governance. The story is a white American folk tale, not a Cherokee tradition.
> **Key takeaway:** The "Cherokee princess" is a myth. If your family tells this story, approach it with healthy skepticism. It almost certainly reflects a family desire for Native identity, not actual Cherokee citizenship.

## ❌ Demanding Proof from Native People
"Prove you're Native." "Show me your card." "What's your blood quantum?" These questions are invasive and rooted in the very colonial logic that has oppressed Native peoples for centuries. You would never demand to see someone's birth certificate before accepting their stated ethnicity. Don't do it to Native people.

## ❌ Buying "Native" Souvenirs
Much of what is sold as "Native American" art, jewelry, and crafts is mass-produced in factories — often in China. This not only exploits Native cultural symbols but also undercuts actual Native artisans. The Indian Arts and Crafts Act of 1990 makes it illegal to sell fake Native art, but enforcement is minimal.

## ❌ Cultural Appropriation Disguised as Appreciation
"I just love Native culture." "I feel a deep spiritual connection to the land." "Native wisdom has so much to teach us." These sentiments may be sincere — but when they lead to adopting Native spiritual practices without context, wearing sacred regalia as fashion, or profiting from Native cultural symbols, they become appropriation regardless of intent.

> **Case: The Neo-Indian Movement** (Chapter 4): California became the epicenter of "neo-Indianism" — non-Native people adopting Native identities and practices, often more zealously than actual Native people. These individuals form "tribes" that have no connection to any recognized Native nation. They perform ceremonies, sell spiritual services, and claim Native identity despite having no legitimate tribal affiliation.
> **Key takeaway:** Enthusiasm for Native culture does not confer Native identity. Cultural engagement must be grounded in accountability to actual Native communities, not personal spiritual seeking.
