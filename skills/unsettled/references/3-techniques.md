# Techniques — Unsettled

## How to Think Clearly About Climate Science (Based on Koonin)

### Technique 1: Read the Source, Not the Summary
Koonin's complaint: "Very few people actually read the assessment summaries, let alone the reports." He provides concrete examples — the NCA report creates different impressions in the full text vs. the Summary for Policymakers vs. the press release. The technique: go one level deeper than the headline. Read the IPCC SPM. Check the actual data sources.

### Technique 2: Distinguish Weather from Climate
"Climate is what you expect; weather is what you get." A single hurricane, heat wave, or flood proves nothing about climate change. The technique: don't extrapolate from individual extreme events. Look at 30+ year trends.

### Technique 3: Apply Feynman's "Wesson Oil" Test
Feynman: "The fact that should be added is that no oils soak through food, if operated at a certain temperature." The technique: when you hear a climate claim, ask "what essential context is being withheld?" Record highs up? — Check if they control for the number of stations and the time period.

### Technique 4: Use the IPCC Likelihood Scale
The IPCC has calibrated terms: virtually certain (99%), very likely (90%), likely (66%), about as likely as not (50%), unlikely (33%), etc. The technique: when someone says "the science says," ask "at what confidence level?" The answer is often "likely" (2/3 chance) — not "virtually certain."

### Technique 5: Know the Three Climate Questions
"Influence → Response → Impact." Each depends on the previous one, so uncertainty compounds. The technique: when evaluating a claim, ask "which of the three questions does this answer?" Most public discussion conflates them.

### Technique 6: Model Divergence Is a Red Flag
Climate models disagree more as they get more sophisticated. The technique: ask "do the models agree?" If they don't (and for many projections they don't), the projection is weaker than claimed.

### Technique 7: Don't Let the Perfect Be the Enemy of the Good
Koonin is not a denier. He acknowledges warming and human influence. But he insists policy should be based on accurate science, not exaggerated fear. The technique: frame climate action as risk management, not existential crisis. "There should be no question about what the right balance is between being effective and being honest."
