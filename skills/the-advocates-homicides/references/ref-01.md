# Reference 01: Series Overview & Character Guide

## The Advocate Series — An Overview

The Advocate series by Teresa Burrell follows Sabre Orin Brown, a juvenile court attorney in San Diego who has a gift for uncovering the truth hidden beneath the surface of murder investigations. The series blends authentic legal procedure with classic whodunit mystery, creating a subgenre that Burrell has made her own: the legal suspense murder mystery.

### Series Chronology

The series began in 2009 with *The Advocate* and has grown to over 20 installments as of 2026. Book 8, *The Advocate's Homicides*, represents a turning point in the series: it is the first book where Sabre defends multiple clients across linked homicide cases. Previous installments focused on single cases with single defendants. Book 8 introduces a serial crime dimension that broadens both the investigative and emotional scope of the series.

**Where Book 8 Fits in the Series Arc:**
- Books 1-3 establish Sabre as a juvenile court attorney and introduce her investigative instincts
- Books 4-6 deepen her personal story, particularly the disappearance and re-emergence of her brother Ron
- Book 7 (Geocache) introduces a modern, technology-driven murder case with puzzle elements
- **Book 8 (Homicides)** shifts to a multi-victim, multi-defendant serial crime structure
- Book 9 (Illusion) continues the pattern of unconventional cases with a magic-themed murder
- Books 10+ increasingly incorporate JP's family background and more complex criminal enterprises

### Sabre O. Brown — The Advocate's Biography

Sabre Orin Brown is the heart of the Advocate series. A juvenile court attorney based in San Diego, she represents children and teenagers in the foster care and juvenile justice systems. By Book 8, she has built a reputation as a fierce advocate who refuses to accept surface-level explanations.

**Personal History:** Sabre's background includes the disappearance of her brother Ron, a thread that runs through multiple books. This personal trauma informs her relentless drive to find the truth — she knows what it means to live with unanswered questions. Her relationship with Ron is explored in depth in *The Advocate's Felony* (Book 6), where he resurfaces under dangerous circumstances.

**Professional Evolution:** By the time of *The Advocate's Homicides*, Sabre has graduated from defending individual clients to investigating patterns of crime that span years. The book shows her at a new level of professional maturity — more confident in her instincts, more skilled in her legal strategy, but also more burdened by the weight of the cases she carries.

**Character Traits in Book 8:**
- **Pattern Recognition**: She sees connections between cases that others miss
- **Resilience**: Defending three clients simultaneously tests her stamina
- **Moral Clarity**: She never equivocates on the principle that every defendant deserves a defense
- **Empathic Reach**: She connects with abused juveniles in ways that career prosecutors cannot
- **Investigative Thoroughness**: She follows every lead, no matter how small, across years of evidence

**Case Example 1 — Sabre's Initial Interview with the First Defendant:**
When Sabre first meets the fourteen-year-old boy accused of the initial GOOF murder, she finds a traumatized, defiant teenager who has been failed by every adult in his life. The police have already written him off as a vigilante killer. The prosecution sees an open-and-shut case. Sabre's first instinct is not to judge but to ask: what really happened? She spends the entire interview building trust, speaking to the boy not as a lawyer interrogating a client but as an adult who genuinely believes he might be innocent. This scene establishes the emotional foundation of the entire book — if Sabre cannot reach this boy, she cannot defend him.

**Case Example 2 — Sabre's Strategy for the Second and Third Defendants:**
When the second and third victims are discovered years later, Sabre faces a strategic dilemma. The new cases could help her first client — if the same killer committed all the murders, her original client might be innocent. But they could also hurt — if the pattern suggests a single killer operating over years, the timing might exclude her client but implicate the new defendants. Sabre must decide whether to consolidate the defenses or keep them separate. She chooses a middle path: coordinated investigation with separate legal strategies, allowing her to share evidence that helps all three while maintaining the individual arguments each client needs.

### JP — The Partner and Investigator

JP is Sabre's partner in investigation — a private investigator or law enforcement contact who works alongside her on cases. In *The Advocate's Homicides*, JP's role is essential for the legwork required by a multi-case investigation.

**Character Significance:** JP has been a constant presence throughout the series, providing investigative muscle, law enforcement connections, and a personal anchor for Sabre. In Book 8, he is stretched thin by the scope of the investigation — three separate cases mean three sets of witnesses, three crime scenes, three timelines. His relationship with Sabre deepens as they work under the pressure of linked homicides.

**Investigative Role in Book 8:** JP handles much of the field investigation: re-interviewing witnesses from the original murder, tracking down leads from the subsequent killings, and building the timeline that connects the victims. His ground-level perspective complements Sabre's courtroom strategy. Where Sabre analyzes evidence for its legal implications, JP analyzes it for its investigative value.

### The Accused Juveniles

Three teenagers, all abused, all accused of murders connected by the GOOF signature. Each has a different story, but their fates are intertwined.

**The First Defendant (Age 14):** The initial accused. He is the most obvious suspect — a history of abuse, proximity to the victim, motive rooted in trauma. Sabre takes his case believing in his innocence, and his defense sets the framework for everything that follows.

**The Second Defendant (Age 16):** Accused when the second body is discovered years later. The passage of time complicates his case — the evidence is older, memories have faded, and the GOOF signature on a new victim suggests either the original killer has struck again or someone is copying the pattern.

**The Third Defendant (Age 15):** Accused after the discovery of the third victim. By this point, the pattern is clear: someone is killing pedophiles and leaving the word GOOF on their bodies. The third defendant has the weakest direct evidence against him but the strongest circumstantial case — he is the third in a pattern that seems to point to a single killer.

### The Victims

All victims are pedophiles. Burrell deliberately chooses victims who are universally reviled, creating the moral ambiguity that drives the book's thematic engine. The reader is forced to confront the question: does a pedophile's murder deserve the same investigation as any other homicide? The book's answer is unequivocal — yes — but it does not let the reader arrive at that answer easily.

### Supporting Characters

**The Prosecution:** A career prosecutor who sees the GOOF cases as the culmination of years of work. The more victims that surface, the more pressure there is to secure convictions. The prosecution's narrative depends on the single-killer theory — one person, one pattern, three murders, three convictions.

**Law Enforcement:** The police investigators who handled the original crime scene and the subsequent discoveries. Their evidence forms the foundation of the prosecution's case, and their credibility is a battleground in Sabre's defense strategy.

**The Families:** The families of the accused juveniles and the victims. Their stories add emotional weight to the legal proceedings and remind the reader that every case has human consequences beyond the courtroom.

### Series Continuity

*The Advocate's Homicides* benefits from the relationships and character histories established in earlier books. Readers who have followed the series will recognize the deepening trust between Sabre and JP, understand the weight of Sabre's personal history, and appreciate how Book 8 represents a structural evolution for the series. For new readers, the book provides enough context to stand alone while offering entry points into the larger series.
