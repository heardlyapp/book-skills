# Principles of Critical Theory

**Principle 1: The Whole Is the False.** The existing social order is not rational. It appears rational — efficient, organized, productive — but it is fundamentally irrational because it reproduces domination. What is, should not be. This is the starting point of critical theory.

**Principle 2: Reason Must Be Critical.** Reason that only calculates means but cannot question ends is not truly rational. It is instrumental. True reason can ask: what is the good life? What is a just society? These questions are excluded by instrumental reason.

**Principle 3: Freedom Requires Negation.** True freedom is not found in affirming the existing order but in negating it. Critical thinking means refusing to accept what is as what must be. The first step toward freedom is to say no.

**Case: The Odyssey.** Odysseus is analyzed as the first bourgeois individual. His cunning allows him to survive by denying himself immediate gratification. He postpones pleasure, plans for the future, and calculates his moves. This is the origin of the bourgeois character.

**Case: The Sirens.** Odysseus listens to the Sirens' song while bound to the mast. His crew rows with plugged ears. This division — master and worker, enjoyment and labor — is the original form of class society.

**Case: The Sacrifice.** Odysseus sacrifices his immediate desires for long-term survival. This pattern of self-denial becomes the foundation of bourgeois civilization.
