# Key Principles

## 1. Your Advice Monster Is Automatic

"The impulse to advise is not a choice — it's a reflex. The choice is what you do after you notice it." Recognition, not elimination, is the goal.

## 2. Staying Curious Requires Practice

"Curiosity is a muscle. It atrophies when you don't use it." The more you practice asking questions, the easier it becomes.

## 3. The Best Question Is "And What Else?"

"This simple question opens up more possibilities than any other. It keeps curiosity alive and prevents premature conclusions."

> **Case: The Team That Solved Its Own Problems** (Chapter 5): A team leader started every one-on-one with "What's on your mind?" and followed each answer with "And what else?" In the first meeting, the direct report ran out of things to say after 5 minutes. By the third meeting, they filled the entire hour with their own insights and solutions. The leader barely said anything.
> **Key takeaway**: "And what else?" is deceptively simple. It creates space for the other person to think more deeply. Most people stop at the first answer. The second or third answer is often the real issue.

## 4. Silence Is Productive

"After asking a question, wait. The pause is where the thinking happens." Most people rush to fill silence. Let the other person fill it.

## 5. You Don't Need to Have All the Answers

"Leadership is not about knowing everything. It's about creating the conditions for others to find their own answers." This shift — from expert to coach — is the core transformation the book offers.

> **Case: The Certainty Paradox** (Chapter 6): A leader who projected complete confidence was admired — but his team was afraid to challenge him. When he started saying "I don't know, what do you think?" his team opened up. His admission of uncertainty paradoxically made him a more trusted leader.
> **Key takeaway**: Certainty creates distance. Vulnerability creates connection. Admitting you don't know is the most powerful coaching move.
