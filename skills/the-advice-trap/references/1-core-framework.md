# Core Framework: The Advice Monster

## The Central Insight

"Your Advice Monster is the voice in your head that wants to fix, save, and solve. It's not a bad thing — it's just that it jumps in too quickly." The book's framework is built around recognizing this habitual response and replacing it with curiosity.

> **Case: The Manager Who Always Had Answers** (Chapter 1): A highly competent manager prided herself on knowing everything. Her team brought every problem to her. She was efficient but exhausted. When she learned to pause and ask questions instead of giving answers, her team started solving problems themselves — and she had time for higher-level work.
> **Key takeaway**: Being the expert creates dependency. Being coach-like creates capability. Your team doesn't need your answers — they need your questions.

## The Three Drivers of the Advice Monster

1. **Tell It** — The impulse to share what you know, show your expertise, prove your value
2. **Save It** — The impulse to rescue others, fix their problems, take responsibility
3. **Control It** — The impulse to maintain control, ensure it's done "right," avoid mess

> **Case: The 7 Coaching Questions** (Chapter 3): The book provides seven questions that transform any conversation: 1) What's on your mind? 2) And what else? 3) What's the real challenge here? 4) What do you want? 5) What are you assuming? 6) What needs to be said? 7) What could you do to move forward? The most powerful is #2: "And what else?" — because it keeps curiosity alive.
> **Key takeaway**: You don't need to remember all seven. Just start with "What's on your mind?" and follow with "And what else?" The rest will follow naturally.
