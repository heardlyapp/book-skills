# Author's Voice + Key Scenarios

> **Case: Michael Bungay Stanier's Own Advice Monster** (Introduction): The author admits he struggles with his own Advice Monster. He is a trained coach who still catches himself giving advice too quickly. The book is not written from a place of perfection but from experience.
> **Key takeaway**: Even the author of the book struggles with the Advice Trap. It's not about perfection — it's about practice.

> **Case: The Coaching Habit's Success** (Epilogue): Stanier's previous book The Coaching Habit sold over a million copies. The Advice Trap extends the same principles, focusing on the deeper habit of staying curious.
> **Key takeaway**: The principles have been tested with millions of readers. They work across cultures, industries, and levels of experience.

## Classic Quotes

> "Stay curious a little longer."
> "Your Advice Monster wants to fix, save, and solve."
> "Questions create ownership. Answers create dependency."
> "And what else?"

## Key Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Team Members Bring Problems
Stop giving advice. Ask "What do you think?" and "And what else?"

### Scenario 2: One-on-One Meetings
Start with "What's on your mind?" Let them drive the conversation.

### Scenario 3: Feeling the Urge to Fix
That's your Advice Monster. Pause. Ask a question instead.

### Scenario 4: Silence in Conversations
Don't fill it. The silence is where thinking happens.

### Scenario 5: Wanting to Coach More
Start with just two questions: "What's on your mind?" and "And what else?"
