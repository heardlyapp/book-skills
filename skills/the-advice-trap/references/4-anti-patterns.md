# Anti-Patterns

## 1. Believing You Must Have the Answer

**The mistake**: Thinking your job as a leader is to know everything.

**Fix**: Your job is to create the conditions for others to find their own answers. Not knowing is the starting point for coaching.

## 2. Rushing to Fill Silence

**The mistake**: Answering your own question because the other person doesn't respond quickly enough.

**Fix**: Silence is where thinking happens. Wait. Count to 10 before speaking.

> **Case: The Impatient Manager** (Chapter 5): A manager asked "What do you think?" and then immediately started talking when the employee didn't answer in 3 seconds. He never found out what the employee thought. The silence was uncomfortable for him — but productive for the employee.
> **Key takeaway**: Your discomfort with silence is your problem. Don't make it the other person's problem by filling the space. They need time to think.

## 3. Forcing the Coaching Framework

**The mistake**: Using the 7 questions rigidly, like a checklist.

**Fix**: Coaching is a conversation, not an interrogation. Follow the questions that fit. Drop the ones that don't.

## 4. Coaching When Someone Just Needs Answers

**The mistake**: Using coaching questions when someone genuinely needs information or direction.

**Fix**: Coaching is for development, not for emergencies. Know when to advise and when to coach.

> **Case: The Wrong Time for Coaching** (Chapter 8): A leader tried coaching a team member who needed urgent guidance on a compliance issue. The coaching questions frustrated the employee. She just needed to know the policy. Coaching is not appropriate for every situation.
> **Key takeaway**: Context matters. Use coaching for development. Use direction for emergencies. Know the difference.

## 5. Pretending to Be Coach-Like Without Genuine Curiosity

**The mistake**: Asking questions without genuinely wanting to hear the answers.

**Fix**: Curiosity cannot be faked. If you're not genuinely interested, don't ask a question. People can tell the difference.
