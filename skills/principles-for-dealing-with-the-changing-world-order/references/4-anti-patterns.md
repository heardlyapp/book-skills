> Source: Changing World Order, Chapters 1-11 — 6 anti-patterns

## 1. "This Time Is Different"

The most dangerous belief. Every generation has believed their situation was unique. None have been right. The cycle always repeats.

> **Case: The 1920s Confidence** (Chapter 6): In the 1920s, US leaders believed the economic boom was permanent. They had "solved" the business cycle. Within 10 years, the Great Depression had arrived. "This time is different" was the most expensive mistake.

## 2. Assuming the Current Order Is Permanent

The US has been the dominant power for 80 years. That feels permanent. The British felt the same in 1900. The Dutch felt the same in 1700.

## 3. Ignoring Internal Conflict

Many analysts focus on external threats (China) while ignoring internal division. History shows internal conflict is the more dangerous marker.

## 4. Betting on a Single Outcome

The future is uncertain. The prepared investor does not bet on a single scenario — they prepare for multiple outcomes.

## 5. Underestimating Timing

The transition from old order to new order takes decades, not years. It is easy to underestimate how long decline takes.

## 6. Confusing GDP Growth with National Strength

GDP is one measure. Real strength includes education, infrastructure, health, innovation, debt sustainability, and internal cohesion. A country can grow GDP while declining in real strength.

> **Case: The Soviet Collapse** (Chapter 7): The Soviet Union appeared strong on GDP measures into the 1980s. But internal indicators — debt, corruption, internal conflict, technological stagnation — told a different story. The decline was visible years before the collapse.
