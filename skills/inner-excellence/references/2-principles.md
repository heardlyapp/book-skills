# Key Principles — Inner Excellence

## I. Heart First, Performance Second

"Inner world first, outer world follows." Most people have it backwards — trying to be successful in order to be happy. Murphy argues: "The quality of your life and the quality of your performance are deeply interwoven." Case: The Samurai trained the heart (calligraphy, poetry, tea) alongside the sword — because inner development precedes outer mastery.

## II. Your Identity Must Not Be Tied to Outcomes

"Your greatest dream is not realized in having millions of dollars or a house overlooking the ocean. Your dream is how you think these things will make you feel." Case: Bunpachiro's identity was wrapped up in being a Samurai. When the samurai era ended, he lost everything. His son Yoshitaka learned that "what he craved was not power or prestige — he wanted fullness of life."

## III. The Three Internal Opponents

The Critic (self-doubt), Monkey Mind (distraction), and Trickster (rationalization). "These three opponents are more formidable than any external competitor." Most people fail not because of their opponent — but because of the voices in their own head.

## IV. The Three Pillars: Belief, Focus, Freedom

"Extraordinary performance requires belief about who you are, focus on what matters, and freedom from fear." These pillars are developed by training the heart. "When your life is based on truth, it expands every day — revealing unknown beauties."

## V. Imagine Yourself Already Dead

The Samurai teaching: "If you are not afraid to die, you are free to truly live." This is not morbid — it's liberating. When you've accepted the worst that can happen, you are free to give everything you have. Case: Elite performers who say they "let go of the outcome" — they've accepted loss so they can fully pursue victory.

## VI. Poise Is a Choice, Not a Personality Trait

"You can choose your state regardless of circumstances." Murphy's emotional control framework: change your physiology (breathing, posture), change your focus (what you attend to), change your interpretation (the story you tell). Case: Navy SEALs train to remain calm under fire by controlling their breath and focus — not by eliminating fear.

## VII. The Journey Is the Destination

"Life is meant to be a journey of connecting with others, growing together, discovering new and amazing things — rather than a game of constant comparison and competition." Murphy's "infinite game" vs "finite game": finite games have winners and losers; infinite games are about growth and connection.
