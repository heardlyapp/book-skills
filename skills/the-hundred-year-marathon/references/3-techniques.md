# Techniques — The Hundred-Year Marathon

## How to Analyze China Strategy (Based on Michael Pillsbury)

### Technique 1: Read in the Original Language
"Many so-called China experts in the United States do not speak Chinese beyond a few words." Pillsbury's key advantage: he reads Chinese documents in the original. Key phrases like _ying pai_ (hawks), _sha shou jian_ (assassin's mace), and _fuxing zhi lu_ (road to renewal) carry connotations that are lost in translation.

### Technique 2: Watch for Dual Audiences
Chinese leaders often give speeches that mean different things to domestic and foreign audiences. Xi's "China Dream" sounds benign in translation but carries nationalist, anti-Western connotations in the original Mandarin context. The technique: read the original AND the official translation side by side.

### Technique 3: Look at What China Does, Not What It Says
Pillsbury: "The evidence of Chinese intentions lies in their actions, not their words." China says it wants "peaceful rise." Its actions — island building in the South China Sea, the Belt and Road Initiative, military modernization, cyber theft — tell a different story.

### Technique 4: Track the Hunters, Not Just the Sparrows
Pillsbury distinguishes between ying pai (hawks) and moderate voices in China. Western observers tend to focus on moderate statements because they confirm existing beliefs. The technique: track the hawkish voices that are winning the internal debate.

### Technique 5: The "Day After" Test
When asked about China's ambitions, Pillsbury has a test question: "After China surpasses the U.S., what kind of world order would China create?" Chinese hawks answer honestly: a China-led order. Moderates deflect. The technique: ask the question that reveals ultimate intentions.

### Technique 6: The Translation Trap
"None of the thirty-six stratagems would be considered a reasonable way to operate in modern American discourse." The technique: when reading Chinese strategic documents, look for the cultural references that Western translators may soften or omit. The original always tells more than the translation.

### Technique 7: Know Your Own Blindness
Pillsbury's most valuable contribution is his confession: "I was a member of the 'panda hugger' group for many decades." He acknowledges his own previous blindness. The technique: if you want to understand China, you must first understand your own biases and how they have been exploited.
