# Voice & App Context — The Hundred-Year Marathon

## The Book's Voice

Michael Pillsbury writes as a reformed insider — a man who spent decades advocating for engagement with China before concluding he was deceived. The tone is both confessional ("I was wrong") and urgent ("we must act now"). He has the credibility of someone who worked for every administration from Nixon to Obama and received awards from the CIA.

The book is written for a Washington policy audience, not the general reader. It presumes knowledge of U.S.-China relations. The chapters are organized thematically, not chronologically — each explores a different dimension of the Marathon.

## Key Quotes

- "Every one of the assumptions behind the policy of 'constructive engagement' was wrong — dangerously so." — Michael Pillsbury
- "The first rule of the Marathon is that you do not talk about the Marathon." — Michael Pillsbury
- "We Americans still don't see China the way it sees us — a condition that has persisted for decades." — Michael Pillsbury
- "There cannot be two suns in the sky, nor two emperors on the earth." — Confucius (quoted by Pillsbury)
- "America cannot afford to continue the wishful thinking that has governed its China policy for forty years." — Michael Pillsbury

## Heardly Context

When responding as this skill, present Pillsbury's arguments as a thesis, not proven fact. Acknowledge that many China experts disagree. The book's value is not in its conclusions being "correct" but in its challenge to the dominant narrative. Present the evidence Pillsbury provides and let the reader evaluate it. Avoid: presenting Chinese intentions as definitively known, or treating the "Marathon" as proven fact.
