> Source: Lamb: The Gospel According to Biff, Christopher Moore (2002).

# Anti-Patterns — Solemnity, Perfectionism, and the Fear of Irreverence

## The Central Fallacy

The most dangerous mistake: thinking that sacred things must be treated with reverence. Moore's novel demonstrates that laughter and holiness can coexist. The people who are most protective of the sacred are often the ones who have forgotten how to love it.

> **Case: The Pharisees** (Throughout): The religious authorities are not villains because they are evil. They are villains because they have forgotten that faith is about love. They care about rules. Josh cares about people.
> **Key takeaway**: When the rules become more important than the people they were meant to serve, the rules have become idols.

> **Case: The Disciples' Jealousy** (Late): The disciples are jealous of Biff's closeness to Josh. They do not understand why Josh trusts Biff more. Josh loves them all. But Biff is his friend. Friendship is different from discipleship.
> **Key takeaway**: You cannot replace friendship with devotion. Devotion is about what you do. Friendship is about who you are.

> **Case: The Fear of Laughter** (Throughout): Some characters are offended by Biff's jokes. They think he is disrespecting Josh. Josh is not offended. He laughs. He loves Biff's humor. The people who are offended are projecting their own insecurity.
> **Key takeaway**: If you cannot laugh about something, you may be afraid of it. Laughter is not the enemy of love. It is proof of love.
