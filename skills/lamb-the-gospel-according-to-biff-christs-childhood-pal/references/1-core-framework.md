> Source: Lamb: The Gospel According to Biff, Christopher Moore (2002).

# Core Framework — The Untold Gospel, the Missing Years, and the Best Friend

## The Model

The angel Raziel resurrects Biff from the dead and commissions him to write the true gospel of the missing years of Jesus. Biff, a liar and cheat, is the most unreliable — and most truthful — narrator possible.

> **Case: The Resurrection of Biff** (Opening): Raziel resurrects Biff in modern America. Biff is disoriented, confused, and uninterested in writing a gospel. He wants pizza. Raziel orders pizza. They watch TV. This is not a holy scene. It is also exactly how the story should begin. The sacred and the profane are entwined.
> **Key takeaway**: The most sacred truths often come wrapped in the most mundane packages.

> **Case: The First Meeting** (Biff's childhood): Biff sees Josh resurrecting a lizard. He is not awed. He is impressed. He wants to learn the trick. Josh cannot teach him. Biff stays anyway. He becomes Josh's friend not because Josh is divine but because Josh is interesting.
> **Key takeaway**: The greatest friendships begin not with admiration but with curiosity.

> **Case: The Wise Men** (Journey to the East): Josh and Biff travel to find the three wise men who visited at Josh's birth. They study with them. Gaspar teaches them philosophy. Balthazar teaches them martial arts. Melchior teaches them magic. The training is long, hard, and hilarious.
> **Key takeaway**: Preparation for a great purpose often looks like a strange adventure. You do not know what you are being trained for while you are being trained.
