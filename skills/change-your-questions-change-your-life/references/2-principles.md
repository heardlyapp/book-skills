# Key Principles

## 1. Questions Direct Attention

"The questions you ask determine what you see." This is the foundational insight. If you ask "What's wrong?" you'll find problems. If you ask "What's working?" you'll find solutions. Your attention follows your questions.

## 2. You Can't Change What You Don't Notice

The first skill of Question Thinking is self-awareness — noticing that you're in Judger. "Before you can choose a different path, you must recognize you're on the current one."

## 3. Judger Is Automatic; Learner Requires Intention

"Judger is your brain's default setting — it's faster, easier, and requires no effort. Learner requires conscious choice and practice." This isn't a weakness; it's how the brain works. The practice is not eliminating Judger but catching it quickly and switching.

## 4. One Good Question Is Enough

"You don't need a system of questions. You need one question you can trust at the moment you need it most." Find your switching question and practice it until it becomes automatic.

## 5. Learner Questions Are Not Soft

"Many leaders resist Learner because they think it means being nice or avoiding conflict. It doesn't. Learner questions are direct, honest, and powerful." Asking "What can we learn from this failure?" is far more challenging than asking "Who's to blame?"

> **Case: The Coaching Conversation** (Chapter 7): A manager uses Judger questions with an underperforming employee: "Why did you miss the deadline? What's wrong with you?" The employee becomes defensive. When the manager shifts to Learner questions: "What got in the way? What help do you need? What can we do differently?" the employee opens up and they solve the real problem.
> **Key takeaway**: The same situation produces completely different outcomes depending on the questions asked. The question IS the intervention.

> **Case: The Defensive Executive** (Chapter 5): A senior executive prided himself on his "high standards" — which was really Judger thinking in disguise. He thought his critical eye was what made him successful. When his team's turnover spiked, he had to face that his Judger was destroying relationships. Through Question Thinking, he learned to ask "What's going well?" alongside "What needs to improve?" — and his team's performance improved.
>
> **Key takeaway**: Judger feels productive. It's often confused with "high standards." But Judger without Learner destroys what you're trying to build. Both are needed.
