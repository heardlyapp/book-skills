# Anti-Patterns

## 1. Automatic Judger

**The mistake**: Believing your first reaction is the truth. "I'm just being honest."

**Why it fails**: Your first reaction is usually Judger — automatic and reactive. It's not "honest"; it's habitual.

**Fix**: Distinguish between first reaction and chosen response.

## 2. Believing Learner Means Being Nice

**The mistake**: "If I'm a Learner, people will walk all over me."

**Why it fails**: Learner questions are not soft. "What can we learn from this failure?" is harder than "Who's to blame?"

> **Case: The Perfectionist Judger** (Chapter 5): A high-performing executive prides herself on her critical eye, believing her Judger made her successful. She initially rejected the Choice Map as "soft." Only when she saw her Judger damaging relationships did she try switching questions — and discovered Learner made her more effective, not weaker.
> **Key takeaway**: Judger can feel productive, but it damages what you're trying to build. Learner produces better results.

> **Case: The Family Argument** (Chapter 8): A couple in repeated conflict about finances tries the Choice Map. His Judger question: "Why can't you manage money?" Her Judger question: "Why are you so controlling?" These questions ensure neither hears the other. When they switch to Learner: "What are you afraid of? What matters most to you?" they discover both want the same thing — security — and can finally problem-solve together.
> **Key takeaway**: Switching questions don't eliminate disagreement; they transform the frame from blame to understanding.

## 3. Trying to Eliminate Judger

**The mistake**: "I need to stop being judgmental forever."

**Fix**: Judger never goes away. The goal is recognition and switching, not elimination.

## 4. Rushing to Solutions

**The mistake**: Jumping to answers before understanding the problem.

**Fix**: Before offering a solution, ask three more questions.
