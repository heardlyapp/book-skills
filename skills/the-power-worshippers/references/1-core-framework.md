## The Stewart Framework: Christian Nationalism as Political Movement

Stewart's central argument: Christian nationalism is not a religious revival — it is a well-organized political movement with a coherent strategy to take control of American government. The movement uses the language of faith and persecution to pursue theocratic power.

**The Key Insight**: The movement is not about belief — it is about power. The theology (dominionism, Seven Mountains) provides the framework. The legal strategy (Project Blitz, RFRA lawsuits) provides the tactics. The goal is control.

> **Case: The Unionville School Board** (Chapter 1): Stewart visits Unionville, North Carolina, where a coordinated campaign by Christian nationalists took over the local school board. The new board members pushed for school prayer, banned books, and eliminated diversity programs. The takeover was not spontaneous — it was the result of years of organizing by national networks.
> **Key takeaway**: Christian nationalism works at the local level. School boards, city councils, and state legislatures are the primary targets — and the movement has been winning.

> **Case: The Crisis Pregnancy Center Network** (Chapter 11): Stewart investigates the network of crisis pregnancy centers that are funded and organized by Christian nationalist groups. These centers are designed to intercept women seeking abortions and persuade them not to proceed. They provide free ultrasounds and counseling — but they also collect data and refer women to legal advocacy networks.
> **Key takeaway**: The movement's anti-abortion strategy is not just legislative — it is organizational. Crisis pregnancy centers, adoption networks, and post-birth support are all parts of a coordinated system.
