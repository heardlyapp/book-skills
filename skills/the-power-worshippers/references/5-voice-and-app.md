> Source: The Power Worshippers, Introduction through Epilogue

## Author's Voice

Katherine Stewart writes as an investigative journalist — calm, meticulous, and unsparing. She does not rant. She lets the facts speak. Her tone is measured, which makes her findings even more alarming. She weaves together on-the-ground reporting (school board meetings, state legislatures, crisis pregnancy centers) with national-level analysis (ADF's legal strategy, Project Blitz's coordination, the Federalist Society's influence). The book is both a warning and a resource.

## Signature Phrases

- "The dangerous rise of religious nationalism" — the subtitle
- "Project Blitz" — the state-level legislative strategy
- "The Seven Mountains Mandate" — the theological framework
- "Religious liberty" — the redefined term
- "The legal assassination of Roe v. Wade" — the anti-abortion goal
- "The power worshippers" — those who seek power, not faith

## Application to 5 Scenarios

### Scenario 1: A journalist covering the Christian right
**Partial response**: "Follow the money and the model bills. The movement coordinates nationally. If you see the same bill in multiple states, trace it back to Project Blitz. The local story is almost always part of a national strategy."

### Scenario 2: A citizen concerned about school board changes
**Partial response**: "Attend meetings. Record them. Trace the organizations behind the candidates. Christian nationalist groups often recruit and train candidates for local office. Knowing who is behind them is the first step to responding."

### Scenario 3: A lawyer or law student
**Partial response**: "Study the ADF's legal strategy. They are playing a long game — creating sympathetic plaintiffs, winning narrow victories, building precedent. Understand their approach to anticipate and counter it."

### Scenario 4: A person of faith
**Partial response**: "Christian nationalism is not Christianity. Many Christians oppose it. The movement's theology (dominionism) is not mainstream Christianity. Defending religious freedom for all is a different position from imposing one religion on everyone."

### Scenario 5: A political strategist
**Partial response**: "The movement's strength is at the local level. Counter-strategy: invest in local races, pay attention to school boards, and expose the national coordination behind local campaigns."

> **Case: The Harvard Law Speech** (Chapter 10): Stewart describes a speech at Harvard Law School by a Christian nationalist lawyer who argued that the Constitution is not the supreme law of the land — God's law is. The audience was stunned. But the lawyer was not a fringe figure — he was a well-funded operative from a major legal organization.
> **Key takeaway**: The movement's leaders are not hiding their goals. They openly argue for the supremacy of Biblical law over the Constitution. The question is whether the public is paying attention.

> **Case: The Russian Connection** (Chapter 12): Stewart documents the international connections of American Christian nationalists. They have allied with Russian religious nationalists who share their theology and their hostility to liberalism. The alliance is ideological, not just political.
> **Key takeaway**: Christian nationalism is a global movement. American Christian nationalists see themselves as part of an international struggle against secular liberalism.
