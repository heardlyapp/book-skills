# Principles: Speech, Censorship, and Control

## Principle 1: Algorithms Are Not Neutral

Every algorithm makes choices about what to show and what to hide. These choices shape public opinion, political discourse, and cultural norms. Algorithmic choices are political choices, whether the platforms admit it or not.

## Principle 2: Section 230 Is Outdated

Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act protects platforms from liability for user content. It was designed to encourage free speech and innovation. Today, it allows platforms to censor content without accountability.

> **Case: Deplatforming** (Chapter 3): Hawley describes how platforms deplatformed political figures across the spectrum. The bans were justified as content moderation. But Hawley argues that when a handful of companies control the infrastructure of public discourse, banning someone from those platforms is effectively silencing them. Section 230 gives platforms immunity while allowing them to act as censors.
> **Key takeaway:** Platforms that control the infrastructure of speech should not have both censorship power and legal immunity. Section 230 needs reform.

## Principle 3: The Advertising Model Distorts Everything

Big Tech's revenue comes from advertising. Platforms are designed to maximize engagement because engagement drives ad revenue. This incentivizes sensationalism, polarization, and outrage. The platform's interests are not aligned with users' interests.

## Principle 4: Algorithmic Amplification

Platforms do not just host content. They amplify it. The algorithm decides what to show. It amplifies divisive content because divisive content drives engagement. The platform is not a neutral conduit. It is an active participant in shaping what people see.

## Principle 5: Surveillance Is the Business Model

Tracking users across the internet is essential for targeted advertising. This surveillance is not incidental. It is the business model. Your data is collected, analyzed, and sold without your meaningful consent.

> **Case: The Algorithm Study** (Chapter 4): Hawley cites research showing that social media algorithms amplify extreme content. Content that generates anger and outrage gets more engagement. Engagement drives ad revenue. The algorithm is not designed to inform or connect. It is designed to keep you scrolling. This structural incentive toward polarization is a feature, not a bug.
> **Key takeaway:** The algorithm is not broken. It is working exactly as designed. The problem is that the design incentives are misaligned with democratic values.
The key insight is that algorithmic amplification is not neutral. When a platform decides to amplify one type of content over another, it is making an editorial decision. The pretense that platforms are neutral conduits for user content is no longer tenable. Platforms are publishers. They should be treated as such.
The advertising model also creates a conflict between platform interests and user interests. Platforms want users to spend more time on the platform because more time means more ad revenue. To keep users engaged, platforms show content that triggers emotional responses. This means more outrage, more polarization, and more misinformation. The platform is not broken. It is optimized for what it was designed to do.
