# Core Framework: How Big Tech Got Its Power

## The Four Pillars

Hawley identifies four companies that dominate the digital economy: Google (search and advertising), Facebook (social media), Amazon (e-commerce and cloud), and Apple (mobile devices and apps). Each has a near-monopoly in its core market. Each uses its power to control adjacent markets.

## Network Effects

The foundation of Big Tech's power is network effects. A platform becomes more valuable as more people use it. Facebook has billions of users. No new social network can compete because no new network has billions of users. This creates a natural monopoly that self-reinforces.

> **Case: Google's Search Monopoly** (Chapter 1): Google controls over 90% of the global search market. It pays Apple billions annually to remain the default search engine on iPhones. Competitors like Bing and DuckDuckGo cannot compete because Google has the data to improve its algorithm and the money to buy default access. Search is a natural monopoly powered by data.
> **Key takeaway:** Google's monopoly is not accidental. It is maintained through data advantages, default deals, and anti-competitive practices.

## Data as Barrier to Entry

Every search, every click, every like generates data that improves the platform. New entrants cannot compete because they lack data. The incumbents get better because they have more users. The gap widens over time.

> **Case: Facebook's Acquisition Strategy** (Chapter 2): Facebook acquired Instagram and WhatsApp not to improve its products but to eliminate potential competitors. The acquisitions were approved by regulators who did not understand digital markets. Hawley argues that Facebook would not have built Instagram. It bought Instagram to protect its monopoly.
> **Key takeaway:** Big Tech uses acquisitions to neutralize competition. The antitrust framework has failed to stop anti-competitive mergers in digital markets.
The anti-competitive practices of Big Tech include: acquiring potential competitors, using data from third-party sellers to compete against them, making it difficult for users to switch platforms, and leveraging dominance in one market to enter adjacent markets. These practices are often subtle and difficult to prove under existing antitrust law. Hawley argues that the antitrust framework needs to be updated for the digital age.
The result of these dynamics is that the digital economy is dominated by a handful of companies in ways that the industrial economy never was. Standard Oil had about 90% of oil refining at its peak. Google has over 90% of search. Facebook has over 70% of social media. Amazon has nearly 50% of e-commerce. The concentration of power in digital markets exceeds anything seen in the industrial era.
The irony is that Big Tech uses the data collected from users to improve its products, creating a feedback loop that competitors cannot replicate. Google processes billions of searches per day. Each search improves the algorithm. A competitor starting today would have to process billions of searches to reach Google quality. But no one uses a search engine that does not produce good results. This chicken-and-egg problem means that search is a natural monopoly. The data advantage is insurmountable without regulatory intervention.
