# Anti-Patterns — The Greengrocer's Apostrophe, Over-Exclamation, and the Missing Comma

## The Central Fallacy

The most dangerous assumption of the careless writer: that punctuation is optional. Truss argues that punctuation is the difference between being understood and being misunderstood. A missing comma can cost a company millions. A misplaced apostrophe can make an educated person look ignorant.

> **Case: The Greengrocer's Apostrophe** (Chapter 1): The most common punctuation crime: using an apostrophe for a plural. "Potato's 50p." "CD's £5." The sign writer thinks the apostrophe makes the word look important. It does not. It makes the sign writer look uneducated. Truss's advice: if you are not sure whether to use an apostrophe, leave it out. A missing apostrophe is better than a wrong one.
> **Key takeaway**: Never use an apostrophe for a plural. Ever. Apples, not apple's. Tomatoes, not tomato's.

> **Case: The Exclamation Mark Explosion** (Chapter 4): Truss is alarmed by the proliferation of exclamation marks! Every email! Every text! Every social media post! The exclamation mark has lost its power because it is used everywhere! Truss suggests: one exclamation mark per 100,000 words. The more you use them, the less they mean.
> **Key takeaway**: Use exclamation marks rarely. The more you use them, the less impact they have. Save them for genuine excitement.

> **Case: The Missing Comma That Cost Millions** (Introduction): A Canadian company lost over a million dollars because of a missing comma in a contract. The contract was for five years. A missing comma made it possible to cancel after one year. Punctuation is not pedantry. It is finance.
> **Key takeaway**: In contracts, every punctuation mark matters. A missing comma can cost you millions. Punctuation is not decoration. It is law.
