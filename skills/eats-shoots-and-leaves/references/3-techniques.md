# Techniques — Dashes, Hyphens, and the Art of the Interruption

## Practical Methods

> **Case: The Em Dash** (Chapter 3): The em dash — the longest dash — creates a dramatic interruption. It is stronger than a comma, more urgent than parentheses. Truss loves the dash. She warns against overuse. A dash is an interruption. Too many dashes and your writing becomes breathless.
> **Key takeaway**: The em dash is for interruptions. Use it sparingly for maximum effect. One per paragraph is plenty.

> **Case: The Hyphen** (Chapter 3): The hyphen connects compound words: mother-in-law, well-known, twenty-two. Truss notes that hyphenation rules are changing. Many compounds that were hyphenated are now written as single words (email, not e-mail). Her advice: when in doubt, check a dictionary. When not in doubt, be consistent.
> **Key takeaway**: Hyphenate compound words that might be confusing without the hyphen. "Man eating shark" vs "man-eating shark." The hyphen saves lives.

> **Case: The Ellipsis** (Chapter 3): The ellipsis... indicates a trailing off or an omission. Truss warns against overusing it. The ellipsis is not a substitute for a period. It has a specific job: showing that something is unfinished. Overuse makes writing feel hesitant.
> **Key takeaway**: Use the ellipsis to show an unfinished thought. Three dots. No more. No less. Use it rarely.

> **Key takeaway**: The ellipsis is a powerful tool when used deliberately. Overuse makes it meaningless. Use it sparingly for genuine pauses.
