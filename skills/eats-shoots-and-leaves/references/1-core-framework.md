> Source: Eats, Shoots & Leaves, Lynne Truss (2003). A hilarious guide to punctuation.

# Core Framework — The Tractable Apostrophe and the Seventh Sense

## The Model

Truss organizes punctuation by type. Each chapter covers one mark. The book progresses from the apostrophe (most abused) to the comma (most versatile) to the dash (most dramatic) to other marks.

> **Case: The Apostrophe and the Grocer** (Chapter 1): Truss describes the "greengrocer's apostrophe" — the sign that says "Apple's 50p" or "Banana's £1." The apostrophe for plurals is the most common punctuation crime. It is everywhere. Truss is outraged. The grocer is not evil. He simply does not know. Truss's point: this is not a small thing. It is the erosion of a system that makes writing clear.
> **Key takeaway**: The apostrophe has two jobs: possession and contraction. That is all. If you learn nothing else, learn this.

> **Case: The Panda Joke** (Introduction): The title story: a panda walks into a bar, eats a sandwich, shoots the bartender, and leaves. The bartender's last words: "Why?" The panda throws a poorly punctuated wildlife guide at him: "Panda. Large black-and-white bear-like mammal, native to China. Eats, shoots and leaves." A comma after "eats" changes everything. A punctuation error becomes a matter of life and death.
> **Key takeaway**: Punctuation is not decorative. It is functional. A single comma can change the meaning of a sentence — and save a life.

> **Case: The Apostrophe of Possession** (Chapter 1): Truss explains the rule: to show possession, add 's to the owner. The dog's bone. The children's toys. If the owner is singular and ends in s (James), you can add 's (James's) or just an apostrophe (James'). Both are acceptable. Be consistent.
> **Key takeaway**: Possession is simple: the apostrophe goes on the owner. If the owner is singular, add 's. If plural and ending in s, add just an apostrophe.
