# 2 — Key Principles

> Source: *The Book Whisperer* by Donalyn Miller — all 7 chapters.

## The 8 Principles

### 1. Start with Books, Not Rules

Miller's first activity every year is a Book Frenzy, not a rules lecture. The message: "This class is about reading, not about compliance." The book on day one says more than any syllabus ever will.

### 2. Choice Is Non-Negotiable

"Readers without power to make their own choices are unmotivated." Students who choose their own books develop a reading identity. Students who are assigned books develop compliance.

### 3. Reading Volume = Reading Competence

> **Case: The 40-Book Requirement** (Chapter 4): Miller requires 40 books per year, organized by genre: 7 realistic fiction, 5 fantasy, 4 historical fiction, 4 science fiction, 4 mystery, 4 traditional literature, 4 poetry, 3 informational, 2 biography/memoir. The list is a target, not a test. Students choose which books fill each slot. Almost all students meet or exceed the goal. The purpose is volume, not rigid counting.
> **Key takeaway**: A stretch goal in reading produces volume. Volume produces competence. Competence produces confidence.

### 4. Drop What Doesn't Work

If whole-class novels, comprehension tests, book reports, reading logs, round-robin reading, and incentive programs don't produce lifelong readers — stop doing them. Replace them with reading. The courage to let go is the most important teaching skill.

### 5. The Teacher Must Be a Reader

"You cannot give away what you don't yourself possess." Miller's power as a teacher comes from her own reading life. She reads during SSR. She talks about books. She is caught reading.

### 6. Build the Library

Miller estimates her classroom library at several thousand books — all purchased with her own money. She covers paperbacks in Con-Tact paper, stamps them with her name, and loans them freely. The classroom library is not a luxury — it is the curriculum.

### 7. Every Reader Is Different

Developing readers need volume at the right level. Dormant readers need discovery. Underground readers need freedom. One-size-fits-all instruction fails all three.

> **Case: Hope, the Dormant Reader** (Chapter 2): Hope succeeded in schoolwork and excelled on state assessments — but did not see herself as a reader. Miller placed "book after book" in her hands. Some she read; others she "snuck back onto the shelf." Over time, Hope found books that spoke to her "eclectic spirit" — The Giver, Coraline. When Miller noticed Hope gravitated toward Greek mythology, a connection opened. Today, Hope is "a regular visitor to my library." The dormant reader woke up.
> **Key takeaway**: Dormant readers don't need remediation. They need discovery. One book that matches their personal taste can unlock everything.

### 8. The Goal Is Lifelong Reading

"If students walk out of our classrooms with internal motivation to read, they have gained something lasting." The test score is a byproduct, not the goal.
