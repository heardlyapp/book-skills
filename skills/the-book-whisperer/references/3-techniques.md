# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *The Book Whisperer* by Donalyn Miller — Chapters 2-6.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The Book Frenzy

Day one: clear your lesson plan. Announce: "We're choosing books now." Let students grab books from the library. Talk about favorites. Trade recommendations. Do not lecture about rules. Let the excitement carry the day.

### 2. The 40-Book Genre Requirement

Set a reading goal organized by genre, not by title. Students choose which books count for each genre. The requirement is a target, not a test. Miller's breakdown: 7 realistic fiction, 5 fantasy, 4 historical fiction, 4 science fiction, 4 mystery, 4 traditional literature, 4 poetry, 3 informational, 2 biography/memoir. Adjust for your grade level.

### 3. The Student Interest Survey

On day one, give a two-part survey: a Reading Interest-A-Lyzer (what they read, how they read, what they want to read) and a General Interest Survey (hobbies, preferences, heroes). Use the results to make personalized book recommendations.

### 4. The Preview Stack

After surveys reveal interests, Miller builds personalized stacks of 5-10 books for each student. She leaves them on desks with Post-it notes. "I whisper back with books."

### 5. The Reading Conference

Weekly or bi-weekly, sit with each student for 3-5 minutes. Ask: "What are you reading? How is it going? What are you thinking about?" The goal is not assessment. The goal is connection.

### 6. The Book Talk

Students stand up for 60 seconds and share something they're reading. Not a report — a recommendation. "This book is about X. What I love about it is Y. If you like Z, you might like this." Book talks spread reading culture.

### 7. The Reader's Notebook

A simple notebook where students track what they're reading, write responses, and set goals. No formats. No required length. "A natural way to respond."

> **Case: Kelsey's Interest Survey** (Chapter 2): Kelsey's survey revealed she loved animals, especially horses. Miller steered her toward the Animal Ark and Heartland series — books at a 3rd-grade level that Kelsey could read successfully. "As Kelsey developed experience as a reader, her confidence grew." A personalized book recommendation changed everything.
> **Key takeaway**: The survey is worthless without follow-through. The magic is not the data — it's the personalized stack of books left on the desk.

> **Case: The Book Frenzy** (Chapter 2): Jace was "not an enthusiastic reader" when he entered Miller's class. During the first-day Book Frenzy, he got caught up in the excitement — students waving books, calling out titles, trading recommendations. He walked out with three books. Years later, he told Miller: "They are still on my list of favorites."
> **Key takeaway**: The Book Frenzy works because it harnesses social energy. Students recommend to students.

### 8. The Classroom Library System

Books organized by genre in plastic shoe boxes. Stickers match books to bins. Students check out their own books. Class librarians help. Covers face out.
