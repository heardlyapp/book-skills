# 4 — Anti-Patterns

> Source: *The Book Whisperer* by Donalyn Miller — Chapter 6.

## Six Practices to Drop

### 1. Whole-Class Novels

**The trap:** "Everyone reads the same book so we can discuss it together."

**Why it fails:** One book at one pace cannot serve 30 readers at different levels and interests. It wastes the time of the strong readers and frustrates the developing ones. "When I look back on that year, I recognized that my classroom had become the same kind of classroom I reviled in my memories of school — a reading class with no place for readers."

> **Case: The View from Saturday Disaster** (Chapter 1): Miller spent a month crafting a unit on E.L. Konigsburg's Newbery winner. It was "a work of art." Students slogged through it. "They were robots." The few avid readers rushed through the activities so they could get back to "their books." The book was not the problem. The whole-class method was.

### 2. Comprehension Tests

**The trap:** "I need to know if students actually read the book."

**Why it fails:** Testing kills the motivation to read. Instead of asking "What did you learn?" it asks "Did you comply?" Replace with reading conferences.

### 3. Book Reports

**The trap:** "Students need to demonstrate understanding of what they read."

**Why it fails:** Book reports are formulaic, boring, and assess formatting ability more than reading comprehension. Replace with book talks — 60 seconds of genuine enthusiasm.

### 4. Reading Logs

**The trap:** "I need to see how much they're reading at home."

**Why it fails:** Students routinely falsify reading logs. Parents sign them without checking. The data is worthless. The resentment is real. "Reading logs are proof that reading is work, not pleasure."

### 5. Round-Robin / Popcorn Reading

**The trap:** "Students need oral reading practice."

**Why it fails:** Humiliating for struggling readers. Boring for strong ones. "The only purpose served is crowd control." Replace with paired reading or teacher read-alouds.

> **Case: Michelle's Reading Log** (Chapter 2): Michelle wrote in her reading reflection: "When you told us that you expected us to read 40 books this year, my first thought was: She. Is. Crazy. The only books I read last year were books that the school required us to read. We would do worksheet upon worksheet." She expected to be monitored on every book — vocabulary, comprehension, reports. The logs and worksheets had taught her that reading is work. Miller had to earn her trust that reading could be different.
> **Key takeaway**: When a student arrives expecting worksheets and quizzes, they have been trained by years of practice. Unlearning takes time.

### 6. Incentive Programs

**The trap:** "If I give them prizes, they'll read more."

**Why it fails:** Rewards undermine intrinsic motivation. "Children who receive rewards for reading read less than those who don't." The message: reading is something you have to be bribed to do.
