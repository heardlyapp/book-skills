## Practical Techniques

### 1. The "Consider" Pivot

Instead of "Will you do X?" ask "Will you consider doing X?" The word "consider" removes pressure and opens the door.

### 2. The Pre-Suasion Frame

Before making a request, set the context. "Is reliability important to you?" Then explain why your solution is reliable. The frame precedes the ask.

> **Case: The Donation Experiment** (Chapter 3): "Will you consider donating?" produced 30% more donations than "Will you donate?" The single word doubled the effectiveness.
> **Key takeaway**: One word can change everything. "Consider" is the most powerful word in influence.

### 3. The Generosity First

Give something of value before asking for something. Not as a bribe — as genuine generosity. The reciprocal instinct is real.

### 4. The Rejection Reframe

When someone says no, say: "I understand. Is there anything I could change that would make this work?" The question keeps the conversation open.

> **Case: The Negotiation Study** (Chapter 9): After a "no," asking "What would need to change?" led to successful renegotiation in 40% of cases. The question reframes rejection as problem-solving.
> **Key takeaway**: "No" is not the end — it is the beginning of understanding. Ask what would need to change.

### 5. The Listening Pause

When someone is speaking, do not plan your response. Listen fully. Pause for 2 seconds after they finish before responding. The pause shows you are considering what they said — and it makes your response more thoughtful.
