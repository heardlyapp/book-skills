## 7 Principles of Ethical Influence

### 1. Influence Is Service

If you are trying to influence someone for your benefit only, that is manipulation. If you are trying to help them get what they want, that is influence.

### 2. Understand Before Asking

The most powerful influence technique is listening. Understand what the other person wants before you ask for anything.

> **Case: The Listening Experiment** (Chapter 5): Chance's students were assigned to have a conversation where they focused entirely on listening — no interrupting, no advising, no one-upping. Afterward, the listeners were rated as more charismatic and persuasive than those who had done most of the talking.
> **Key takeaway**: Listening is not passive — it is the most influential thing you can do. People are influenced by those who make them feel heard.

### 3. "Consider" Opens Doors

Asking someone to "consider" is far more effective than asking them to "do." Consideration is low-pressure. Action follows consideration.

### 4. Presuade Before You Persuade

Frame the context before making your request. What people hear before your ask shapes how they respond to it.

### 5. Rejection Is Not Personal

When someone says no, they are not rejecting you — they are rejecting the request. The distinction is crucial for resilience.

### 6. Generosity Creates Influence

Giving first — without expecting anything in return — creates a desire to reciprocate. Not manipulation — human nature.

### 7. Small Changes Have Big Effects

Chance's research shows that tiny tweaks (changing one word, adding a question, shifting timing) can dramatically change outcomes.

> **Case: The Salary Negotiation** (Chapter 8): A student applied Chance's principles: asked questions first, understood what the company valued, and created a win-win solution. She did not "win" the negotiation — she solved a problem.
> **Key takeaway**: Influence is not about winning. It is about finding solutions that work for everyone.
