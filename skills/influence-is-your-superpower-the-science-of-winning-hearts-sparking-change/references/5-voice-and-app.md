> Source: Influence Is Your Superpower, Introduction through Chapter 12

## Author's Voice

Zoe Chance writes as a Yale professor who genuinely wants people to be better. Her tone is warm, research-backed, and practical. She uses stories from her own life — including her own failures at influence — to illustrate the principles. She is not a guru — she is a researcher who applies what she teaches.

## Signature Phrases

- "Influence is your superpower" — the title and the promise
- "Will you consider..." — the magic phrase
- "Pre-suasion" — framing before the ask
- "Listening is influence" — the core skill
- "Rejection is data" — reframing no
- "Generosity first" — the principle

## Application to 5 Scenarios

### Scenario 1: A manager trying to get buy-in
**Partial response**: "Start with listening. What does your team want? Frame your request in terms of their goals, not yours. Then ask: 'Will you consider this approach?'"

### Scenario 2: A job seeker negotiating an offer
**Partial response**: "Ask questions. What does the company value? Find the fit between what they need and what you offer. Negotiate on what matters to them."

### Scenario 3: A parent influencing a child
**Partial response**: "Ask 'will you consider' instead of 'do this.' Give them space to decide. Listen to their reasons."

### Scenario 4: A salesperson
**Partial response**: "Presuade before you persuade. Frame the value before you present the product. Ask 'will you consider' not 'will you buy.'"

### Scenario 5: A social activist
**Partial response**: "Start with generosity. Give value before asking for action. Understand what people care about. Frame your request in their terms."

> **Case: The 30 Rejections** (Preface): Chance was rejected by 30 publishers for this book. She used her own principles — reframed rejection as data, kept listening to feedback, and eventually found the right publisher.

> **Key takeaway**: Rejection is not failure — it is information. Keep going.

> **Case: The Charity Ask** (Chapter 3): When asked "Will you consider donating?" instead of "Will you donate?" — donations increased 30%. One word changed the outcome.

> **Key takeaway**: The smallest change in language can produce the biggest change in behavior.
