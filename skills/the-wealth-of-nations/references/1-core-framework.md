# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *The Wealth of Nations* by Adam Smith — Books I-III.

## The Three Frameworks

### Framework A: The Division of Labor and the Extent of the Market (Book I, Chapters I-III)

Smith opens the book with the most famous example in economics: the pin factory. Ten specialized workers, each performing a single operation, produce 48,000 pins per day — whereas a single worker doing all operations would be lucky to produce one or twenty. The three causes: (1) increased dexterity from repetition, (2) saving time lost in moving between tasks, and (3) the invention of machines by specialized workers. But the division of labor is limited by the extent of the market — a nail maker in the Highlands of Scotland cannot sell 300,000 nails a year because there are not enough buyers.

> **Case: The Pin Factory (Book I, Chapter I):** "The division of labour, as far as it can be introduced, occasions, in every art, a proportionable increase of the productive powers of labour." A workman not educated to the business could scarcely make one pin a day, and certainly could not make twenty. But in a small manufactory of ten men, where each performs a distinct operation (one draws the wire, another straightens it, a third cuts it, a fourth points it, a fifth grinds the top, etc.), they could together make about 48,000 pins in a day.

> **Case: The Highland Nailer (Book I, Chapter III):** Smith uses the example of a nailer in the remote Highlands who could make 1,000 nails per day. "But in such a situation it would be impossible to dispose of one thousand, that is, of one day's work in the year." The market is too small. The division of labor is bounded by the number of buyers. This is why cities — with larger markets — produce more specialized trades than villages.

### Framework B: Real Price, Nominal Price, and the Component Parts (Book I, Chapters IV-VIII)

Smith distinguishes real price (the labor the commodity can command) from nominal price (the money it sells for). The real price of everything is "the toil and trouble of acquiring it." Money is merely the veil. In every developed society, the price of any commodity resolves into three component parts: wages (for labor), profit (for the employer who advances stock), and rent (for the owner of land).

> **Case: Water vs Diamonds (Book I, Chapter IV):** Smith addresses the paradox of value: "Nothing is more useful than water: but it will purchase scarce anything; scarce anything can be had in exchange for it. A diamond, on the contrary, has scarce any value in use; but a very great quantity of other goods may frequently be had in exchange for it." He distinguishes use value from exchange value — and this distinction became the foundation of all later price theory.

### Framework C: The Progress of Opulence (Book III)

Smith traces the natural progress of opulence: (1) investment in agriculture (the most natural employment of capital), (2) manufacturing in towns, and finally (3) foreign commerce. But European history reversed this order: after the fall of Rome, the feudal system concentrated land ownership and kept agriculture unimproved. Commerce and manufacturing in free towns grew first, and only later did their accumulated capital flow back to improve the countryside.

> **Case: The Rise of Towns (Book III, Chapters III-IV):** Smith describes how serfs gradually gained freedom by living in towns. A serf who escaped his lord and lived in a town for a year and a day became free. The towns purchased charters and privileges from kings (who needed money to fight their barons). The merchants of towns accumulated capital through trade, then invested in improving land — breaking the feudal order. Smith: "Order and good government, and with them the liberty and security of individuals, were, in this manner, established in cities."
