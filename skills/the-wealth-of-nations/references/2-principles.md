# 2 — Key Principles

> Source: *The Wealth of Nations* by Adam Smith.

## The 7 Principles

### 1. Specialization Is the Engine of Productivity

The wealth of a nation depends on the productivity of its labor, and productivity depends on the division of labor. The pin factory example shows that ten specialized workers produce 2,400-48,000 times more per person than one generalist. Specialization works because of increased dexterity, saved time, and machine invention.

> **Case: The Philosopher and the Street Porter (Book I, Chapter II):** Smith argues that the difference between a philosopher and a street porter is not natural talent — it is the effect of the division of labor. "The difference between the most dissimilar characters... seems to arise not so much from nature as from habit, custom, and education." The division of labor creates the specialization that we call talent.

### 2. Self-Interest Serves the Common Good

"It is not from the benevolence of the butcher, the brewer, or the baker that we expect our dinner, but from their regard to their own interest." This is not a moral claim — it is an observation. The system of exchange channels self-interest toward mutual benefit. The invisible hand: each individual pursuing their own gain promotes the prosperity of society.

### 3. Labor Is the Real Measure of Value

Money prices change with the supply of precious metals. But the labor required to produce a commodity is a universal and invariable measure. When comparing prices across time, the quantity of labor commanded is the only reliable standard.

### 4. The Market Self-Corrects

The market price of a commodity gravitates toward its natural price. If market price rises above natural price, profits attract new producers. If it falls below, producers exit. This self-correction is central to Smith's argument against regulation.

### 5. Capital Precedes Growth

> **Case: The Savage and the Axe (Book II, Introduction):** Smith illustrates the necessity of capital: a savage who kills a deer with his bare hands will never progress. But if he first spends time making a bow and arrows (capital), his hunting becomes vastly more productive. The stock accumulated before the hunt enables the greater productivity. The same is true of machines, buildings, and tools in any economy.

### 6. Commerce Creates Liberty

The rise of towns and trade broke the feudal system of dependence. A serf in a town became free. A merchant who accumulated capital did not need a lord's protection. Commerce is not just about wealth — it is about freedom.

### 7. Money Is Not Wealth

The mercantilist system treated gold and silver as the only wealth. Smith's entire work is an argument that real wealth is the annual produce of land and labor: food, clothing, housing, tools. "No political oeconomy... has ever maintained that silver was more valuable than gold."
