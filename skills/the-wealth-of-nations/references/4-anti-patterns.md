# 4 — Anti-Patterns

> Source: *The Wealth of Nations* by Adam Smith.

## Common Errors

### 1. Confusing Money with Wealth

**The trap:** "A nation's wealth is measured by how much gold and silver it has."

**Why it fails:** This is the mercantilist error that Smith spent Book IV refuting. Gold and silver are commodities like any other. A nation can have plenty of gold but starve if it does not produce enough food. Real wealth is the annual produce of labor and land.

> **Case: The Mercantilist Fallacy (Book IV, referenced in Books I-III):** Mercantilist policy treated a trade surplus as the goal of national policy, because it brought in gold. Smith argued this was self-defeating. The goal is not to accumulate gold — it is to increase the annual produce of labor.
> **Key takeaway**: When you focus on the proxy instead of the substance, you make bad policy.

> **Case: Smith on Public Works (Book V):** Smith argued government should provide infrastructure that is "necessary for facilitating the commerce of any society" but which no individual would profitably provide. He was not anti-government — he was anti-cronyism.
> **Key takeaway**: The question is not "government or no government." It is: what should each do?

### 2. The "Free Market Means No Government" Misreading

**The trap:** "Smith believed government should never interfere in the economy."

**Why it fails:** Smith supported government provision of roads, bridges, canals, education, and defense. His argument was against specific restrictions that benefited merchants at public expense — not against all government action.

### 3. The Labor Theory of Value as a Moral Claim

**The trap:** "If labor creates all value, then workers deserve all the proceeds."

**Why it fails:** Smith observed that labor is the real measure of value — but he never claimed workers should receive the full product. Profit and rent are necessary parts of the price in a developed economy.

### 4. Ignoring the "Extent of the Market" Limit

**The trap:** "I should specialize as much as possible, regardless of market size."

**Why it fails:** The division of labor is limited by the extent of the market. The Highland nailer could not sell 1,000 nails per day because there were not enough buyers. Over-specialization without sufficient volume is waste.

### 5. The Invisible Hand as a Moral Justification

**The trap:** "Whatever I do in my own self-interest is automatically good for society."

**Why it fails:** Smith's invisible hand operates through competitive markets with well-functioning prices and property rights. When those conditions are absent — monopolies, externalities — self-interest does not serve the common good. Smith was deeply suspicious of merchants colluding to raise prices.
