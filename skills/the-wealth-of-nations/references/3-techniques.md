# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *The Wealth of Nations* by Adam Smith.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The Specialization Audit

Identify every distinct task your organization performs. Group them. For each group, ask: is there enough volume to justify a dedicated person? If one person does too many different things, you are paying for context-switching losses. Smith's three causes of productivity gain from specialization (dexterity, saved time, machine invention) apply to knowledge work as directly as to pin-making.

> **Case: The Advantages of Water Carriage (Book I, Chapter III):** Smith compares land and sea transport: a wagon carrying 4 tons between London and Edinburgh takes 6 weeks with 2 men and 8 horses. A ship carrying 200 tons with 6-8 men makes the same trip in the same time. Water transport opens larger markets, which enables more specialization. The principle: easier transportation = bigger market = more division of labor = more wealth.

### 2. The Natural Price Calculation

Ask: what is the natural price (cost of production: wages + profit + rent) for your product? Compare to market price. If market price exceeds natural price, expect competition. If below, expect consolidation. Smith's insight: prices that seem arbitrary are actually governed by discoverable principles.

### 3. The Capital Allocation Framework

Smith divides capital into fixed (machines, buildings, improvements — produce revenue without changing hands) and circulating (raw materials, finished goods, money — produce revenue by being sold). Apply this to your business: which of your investments are fixed (long-term capacity) and which are circulating (working capital)? Manage each differently.

### 4. The Four-Stage Progress Map

Smith's stages: hunting → pasture → agriculture → commerce. Each stage requires different institutions. Hunting requires no government. Pasture requires protection of property. Agriculture requires primogeniture and entails. Commerce requires contract enforcement and free exchange. Identify which stage your society, industry, or organization is in — and build the appropriate institutions.

### 5. The Productive Labor Test

> **Case: The Menial Servant vs the Manufacturer (Book II, Chapter III):** A manufacturer's labor is productive because it "fixes and realizes itself in some vendible commodity" that lasts after the work is done. A menial servant's labor is unproductive because it "perishes in the very instant of its performance." Smith uses this to argue that society grows richer by employing more people in manufacturing than in domestic service. The modern application: create things of lasting value, not just ephemeral services.
