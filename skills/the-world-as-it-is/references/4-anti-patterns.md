# 4 — Anti-Patterns

> Source: *The World as It Is* by Ben Rhodes — across all 32 chapters.

## Common Errors

### 1. The Certainty Trap

**The trap:** "I need 100% confidence before I act."

**Why it fails:** The bin Laden raid was launched on 49% confidence. Waiting for certainty means letting events make the decision for you.

### 2. The Bureaucracy Avoidance Trap

**The trap:** "I'll work around the system instead of through it."

**Why it fails:** Rhodes tried to write the Cairo speech without agency input. The agencies caught up and nearly gutted it. You can't avoid the system; you have to command it.

### 3. The "Good Intentions Are Enough" Trap

**The trap:** "I'm doing the right thing. Outcomes will follow."

**Why it fails:** Libya was the right thing (saving Benghazi). But the aftermath was a failed state. Syria was the right thing (calling for Assad to step down). But the U.S. had no follow-through. Intention is not strategy.

### 4. The Firehose Defense

**The trap:** "I'll refute every lie with a fact."

**Why it fails:** The "apology tour" narrative was a story, not a factual claim. You can't fact-check a story. You need a better story.

### 5. The Bubble Addiction

**The trap:** "This is the most important work in the world. I can't step away."

**Why it fails:** Rhodes's body gave out before his mind did. He threw up at the G8. He couldn't sleep. He dreamed about work. Proximity to power is addictive and destructive in equal measure.

> **Case: The G8 Hangover** (Chapter 12): After Buckingham Palace, after the Medvedev meeting, after Obama's speech to Parliament — Rhodes threw up in his hotel bathroom. "At that moment, the absurdity of it all seemed to close in around me."

### 6. The "We've Got This" Overconfidence

**The trap:** "We've successfully managed crises before. We can handle this one too."

**Why it fails:** The Arab Spring was not one crisis — it was five simultaneous crises. Debt ceiling, Egypt, Libya, Syria, Yemen, Bahrain — all hitting at once. No team has surge capacity for that.

> **Case: The Arab Spring Overload** (Chapters 9-14): "The balance had shifted dramatically in the direction of reaction." The White House was outrunning its own decision-making capacity. "I don't know how much longer we can keep this up," Obama said.
