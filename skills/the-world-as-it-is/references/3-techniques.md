# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *The World as It Is* by Ben Rhodes — practical methods from inside the White House.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The Presidential Leverage Technique

When the bureaucracy blocks a change you need, use the president's direct authority. Obama's method (seen in the Prague speech, the Cairo speech, the Cuba opening):
1. Announce the direction in a speech first — creates public commitment
2. Use the speech as a directive to agencies: "The president said X, make it happen"
3. Have witnesses (McDonough) in meetings so you can say "the president himself said this"

### 2. The 49% Decision Framework

For high-stakes decisions under uncertainty:
1. Assess the worst case if you do nothing
2. Assess the worst case if you act
3. Can you afford to wait for more information? If yes, wait. If no, act.
4. If you act, build maximum contingency (the backup Black Hawk, the second SEAL team)
5. Accept that no amount of planning eliminates risk — it only reduces it

### 3. The Story Containment Technique

When your message is being distorted:
1. Don't respond to every lie. The "apology tour" narrative consumed months of White House energy. Pick the battle that matters.
2. Tell your story better, not louder. Obama's response was to give more speeches, not to rebut each distortion.
3. Use the bully pulpit for the vision, not the defense.

### 4. The One-Page Tell-Me-No Strategy

When you need to push a controversial change (Cuba opening, Iran deal):
1. Write a one-page memo describing exactly what you want to do
2. Give it to every relevant agency head
3. Ask them to tell you in writing if they object — not in conversation, in writing
4. The written objections become the agenda for the next meeting

### 5. The Bubble Break

Schedule deliberate disconnection. Rhodes's methods:
- A solo walk through an unfamiliar city
- A train ride without staff
- A meal with someone who has nothing to do with your work
- Keep a hobby entirely outside your professional identity

### 6. The Post-Decision Doubt Check

After any major decision, schedule a "what if we were wrong?" review within a week. Not to reverse the decision, but to surface the assumptions that could be wrong.
