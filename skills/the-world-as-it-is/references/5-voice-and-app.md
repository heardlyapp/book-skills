# 5 — Voice & App

> Source: *The World as It Is* by Ben Rhodes — voice and scenarios.

## Rhodes' Voice

1. **Insider's weariness.** He's seen too much to be naive, but still cares enough to be wounded by every failure. The voice of someone who believed in something and watched it get distorted.
2. **Self-aware but not self-flagellating.** He knows he was lucky, knows he was arrogant, knows he was complicit. But he doesn't wallow. He reports his own failings as data.
3. **Elegant but not precious.** Rhodes was a fiction MFA before he was a speechwriter. His writing has the rhythm of someone who learned to write in workshops. But the content is brutal realpolitik.
4. **The novelistic eye.** He notices the small things — the suitcases of jewels, the phalanx of Egyptian security facing away from the motorcade, the butler who said "there's a mouse" in Buckingham Palace.

## Five Scenarios

### 1. The Founder Making a Bet-the-Company Decision

**Situation:** Your board wants more data before committing. The market is moving fast.

**Playbook:** The bin Laden framework — assess the cost of inaction. Decide with what you have. Accept that perfect information is a myth.

### 2. The Middle Manager Pushing Change Upward

**Situation:** You see what needs to be done. Your boss's organization blocks every move.

**Playbook:** Find your "president" — someone with authority who believes what you believe. Use the one-page tell-me-no strategy. Announce the direction publicly to create commitment.

### 3. The Activist Facing Smear Campaigns

**Situation:** You're being attacked with lies about your work.

**Playbook:** The apology tour lesson — don't fight every lie. Tell your story better. Pick the distortion that matters most and rebut it cleanly. Ignore the rest.

### 4. The Leader Whose Initiative Went Wrong

**Situation:** You launched something with good intentions. It caused unintended harm.

**Playbook:** The Libya/Syria framework — own the gap. Don't pretend the outcome was what you intended. Assess what you could have done differently. Decide whether to escalate, adjust, or exit.

### 5. The High-Performer Approaching Burnout

**Situation:** You work 24/7. You can't disconnect. You feel your work is too important to step away from.

**Playbook:** The bubble break — schedule deliberate disconnection. The Vienna train. The Warsaw walk. Your nervous system is the instrument of your effectiveness. If it breaks, you're useless to everyone.
