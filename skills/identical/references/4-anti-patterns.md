# 4. Anti-Patterns — Common Misreadings of Identical

> Source: "Identical" by Ellen Hopkins (2008). Common misunderstandings of the novel and its themes.

## Misreading 1: It's Just a "Problem Novel"

**What it looks like**: Dismissing the novel as a "problem novel" — a book that exists only to address social issues (abuse, addiction, eating disorders) without literary merit.

**Why it fails**: The verse form itself is a literary innovation. Hopkins uses the conventions of poetry to create an experience that prose cannot replicate. The line breaks, the white space, the cadence — these are not decorative. They are the meaning.

## Misreading 2: The Father Is a Monster

**What it looks like**: Reading the father as a pure villain — a monster who hurts his children because he is evil.

**Why it fails**: The novel shows the father's own history of abuse. He was abused by his own father. The cycle of abuse is not an excuse but an explanation. The novel refuses to reduce any character to a single dimension.

> **Case: The Father's Background** (Late novel): The novel reveals that the father was himself a victim of abuse. This does not excuse his actions — but it explains them. The cycle of abuse is passed down through generations until someone breaks it. The father never did.
> **Key takeaway**: Understanding the origins of evil does not excuse it — but it does make it more comprehensible, and therefore more preventable.

## Misreading 3: One Twin Is "Good" and the Other Is "Bad"

**What it looks like**: Reading Kaeleigh as the good twin (fragile, victimized, sympathetic) and Raeanne as the bad twin (angry, rebellious, difficult).

**Why it fails**: Both sisters are victims. Kaeleigh's passivity is as much a symptom of trauma as Raeanne's aggression. Raeanne's anger is a sign of her loyalty to her sister — she is enraged by the injustice that Kaeleigh endured. The "good twin/bad twin" framework is a reductive reading that the novel actively resists.

## Misreading 4: The Verse Format Is Just Gimmicky

**What it looks like**: Assuming the verse format is a marketing gimmick rather than a genuine literary choice.

**Why it fails**: The verse format is integral to the novel's meaning. The fragmentation of the lines reflects the fragmentation of the characters' psyches. The white space represents what cannot be said directly. The alternating voices create a rhythm that mirrors the twins' psychological entanglement.

## Misreading 5: Hoping for a Fairy Tale Ending

**What it looks like**: Expecting the novel to end with everything resolved — the father in jail, the twins fully healed, the mother sober.

**Why it fails**: The novel ends with a beginning, not a conclusion. The sisters start talking. They start confronting. They start healing. But recovery is ongoing. The novel refuses to offer a fairy tale ending because real healing doesn't work that way.

> **Case: The Ending's Ambiguity** (Final chapters): The novel ends with the twins beginning to communicate honestly. Kaeleigh is in therapy. Raeanne is trying to stay sober. The father's abuse is acknowledged but not resolved in court. This is not a tidy ending — but it is a true one. Recovery is a process, not a destination.
> **Key takeaway**: The most honest endings are not necessarily the happiest ones. An ending that acknowledges ongoing struggle is more truthful than one that resolves everything artificially.
