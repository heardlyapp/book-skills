# 3. Techniques — Verse Novel Craft

> Source: "Identical" by Ellen Hopkins (2008). Techniques drawn from Hopkins' use of verse, voice, and structure.

## The Verse Novel Method

Hopkins writes in free verse — no rhyme, no regular meter. The poetry comes from the line breaks, the white space, and the rhythm of the spoken voice.

### Creating Voice Through Lineation
Kaeleigh's lines are longer and softer — they suggest a voice that is hesitant, trying to please, holding back. Raeanne's lines are shorter and more aggressive — they suggest a voice that is impatient, angry, cutting. The same poetic form produces two distinct voices because the lineation is different.

### White Space as Pause
Hopkins uses white space (blank areas on the page) to create silence, hesitation, and emphasis. A single word on its own line carries more weight than the same word embedded in a sentence.

> **Case: Raeanne's Opening** (First Poem): The opening lines establish the verse form: "Mirror, Mirror / When I look into a mirror, / it is her face I see." The line breaks create rhythm and emphasis. "Her right is my left, double / moles, dimple and all" — the word "double" on its own line emphasizes the doubling theme.
> **Key takeaway**: In verse novels, the line break is a tool of meaning. Breaking after "double" makes the reader pause on the concept of doubleness before continuing to "moles, dimple and all."

### The Thematic Title
Each section of the novel has a thematic title that functions almost as a thesis statement for that section: "One Soul or Two," "Interesting Question," "Mirror, Mirror." These titles guide the reader's interpretation of the poems that follow.

### The Alternating Voice Technique
By alternating between the twins, Hopkins creates:
- Dramatic irony (the reader knows something one sister doesn't)
- Emotional contrast (the reader feels both sisters' pain)
- A puzzle (the reader must reconstruct the truth from both accounts)

> **Case: The Father's Abuse** (Throughout): Kaeleigh's chapters describe the abuse directly — the father coming to her room, the feelings of shame and confusion. Raeanne's chapters describe the same household without mentioning the abuse — she was not the target, so she experienced a different reality. The reader must integrate both accounts to understand the full picture.
> **Key takeaway**: Alternating unreliable narrators can reveal the truth that neither narrator alone possesses. The reader becomes the detective.
