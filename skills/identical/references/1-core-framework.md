# 1. Core Framework — The Twin Narrative Structure

> Source: "Identical" by Ellen Hopkins (2008). The framework is the dual-perspective verse narrative, with alternating chapters voiced by Kaeleigh and Raeanne.

## The Architecture of the Verse Novel

Hopkins' novel is built on a simple but powerful structural framework: two voices, one story, alternating chapters written in free verse.

### The Dual Narrative

The book is organized into sections that alternate between the two sisters:

- **Raeanne sections**: Marked with angry, confrontational language. Her poems are characterized by short, jagged lines that mirror her fragmented emotional state. She questions everything: "Mirror, Mirror" / "One Soul or Two" / "Interesting Question."

- **Kaeleigh sections**: Marked with softer, more inward-focused language. Her poems are characterized by longer, more flowing lines that reflect her desire to merge, to disappear, to be good enough. She apologizes constantly.

The reader must piece together the story from both accounts. Neither sister has the complete truth. The reader's role is active — connecting the dots that the sisters cannot see themselves.

### The Mirror Framework

The title and central metaphor is the mirror. The twins are "mirror-image identical" — a biological term meaning that their physical features are reversed (one is left-handed, the other right; one has a mole on the left cheek, the other on the right). Hopkins extends this metaphor to their psychology: Kaeleigh's internalizing is the mirror of Raeanne's externalizing. Together, they form one whole — but only if they can find each other.

> **Case: The Opening Poem** (Raeanne's first poem): "Mirror, Mirror. When I look into a mirror, it is her face I see." Raeanne establishes the central metaphor immediately: she cannot see herself without seeing her sister. "Her right is my left. My right is her left." This physical mirroring is the starting point for their psychological entanglement.
> **Key takeaway**: Establish your central metaphor in the opening lines. The mirror is not just decoration — it is the novel's way of seeing.

> **Case: The Question of the Soul** (Raeanne's "One Soul or Two"): Raeanne asks the novel's central philosophical question: "Does the Maker place two separate souls within a single cell, to spark the skirmish?" This question — whether the twins share one soul divided or two souls at war — underlies everything. The entire novel is an exploration of this question.
> **Key takeaway**: The central philosophical question of a work should be stated explicitly. The reader needs to know what the work is asking.
