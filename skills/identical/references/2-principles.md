# 2. Principles — The Hopkins Method

> Source: "Identical" by Ellen Hopkins (2008). Principles drawn from the novel's treatment of trauma, identity, and recovery.

## The 7 Principles of Identical

### Principle 1: Trauma Does Not Discriminate Between Twins
The same family, the same house, the same father — but the abuse was different for each twin. Kaeleigh was sexually abused; Raeanne was emotionally neglected. Both were traumatized, but differently. The principle: trauma is not distributed equally even within the same family.

### Principle 2: Coping Is Not Choosing
Neither twin chose her coping mechanism. Kaeleigh did not choose bulimia. Raeanne did not choose addiction. These behaviors emerged from the trauma as survival strategies. They are symptoms, not choices.

### Principle 3: The Body Remembers What the Mind Suppresses
Raeanne cannot consciously remember the abuse — it happened to Kaeleigh, not to her. But her body remembers: the anger, the nausea, the compulsion to destroy herself. The novel shows that trauma is stored in the body, not just the mind.

> **Case: Raeanne's Physical Symptoms** (Throughout): Raeanne experiences physical reactions she cannot explain — nausea when she thinks of her father, a compulsion to drink until she blacks out, a rage that feels like it belongs to someone else. The novel suggests these are her body's memory of the trauma that her conscious mind has blocked.
> **Key takeaway**: Physical symptoms can be expressions of unprocessed trauma. Unexplained physical reactions often have emotional origins.

> **Case: Kaeleigh's Bulimia** (Throughout): Kaeleigh's eating disorder is not about food. It is about control. When her father controls her body through abuse, she learns to control her body through starvation and purging. The bulimia is a direct response to the violation — a desperate attempt to reclaim ownership of her own body.
> **Key takeaway**: Self-harm behaviors are often attempts to regain control over a body that has been violated. The behavior is a symptom, not the problem.

### Principle 4: Secrets Poison the Keeper
The family's secret — the father's abuse — is known by everyone in the house but spoken by no one. This silence is more damaging than the abuse itself. The mother knows and does nothing. Raeanne suspects but cannot confirm. Kaeleigh carries the secret alone. The silence compounds the trauma.

### Principle 5: Identity Is Forged in Relationship
The twins define themselves against each other. Kaeleigh is "the egg" only because Raeanne is "the sperm." Their identities are relational. The novel suggests that we are who we are largely because of who we are next to.

### Principle 6: The Opposite of Depression Is Expression
Both sisters are depressed — Kaeleigh's depression is quiet and self-destructive; Raeanne's is loud and self-destructive. Neither can heal until they express what happened to them. Expression is the opposite of depression.

### Principle 7: Hope Is Not a Promise — It's a Practice
The novel's ending is hopeful but not resolved. The sisters begin to talk. They begin to confront their parents. They begin to heal. But recovery is shown as ongoing practice, not a completed achievement.
