# 5. Voice, Application, and Key Quotes

> Source: "Identical" by Ellen Hopkins (2008). Authorial voice drawn from the novel's verse structure and Hopkins' body of young adult work.

## Ellen Hopkins' Voice

Hopkins writes with uncompromising honesty about difficult subjects. Her signature moves:

1. **The verse form** — She writes all her novels in free verse. This is not a stylistic choice — it's a way of making the unbearable bearable. Poetry allows her to approach dark subjects with beauty and precision.
2. **The layered revelation** — She reveals the truth slowly, in fragments. The reader must piece together the story. This mirrors how survivors process trauma — not in a linear narrative but in fragments that gradually connect.
3. **The refusal to judge** — She never moralizes about her characters' behavior. The bulimia, the drinking, the sex — she presents these as survival strategies, not moral failings.
4. **The hopeful ending** — Despite the darkness of her subjects, her novels end with hope. Not false reassurance — but the possibility of healing.

> **Case: The First Poem** (Raeanne's voice): Hopkins opens with a poem that establishes voice, theme, and form simultaneously: "Mirror, Mirror / When I look into a mirror, / it is her face I see." In three lines: the title (mirror imagery), the speaker (Raeanne), the central relationship (her sister), and the verse form (free verse with line breaks).
> **Key takeaway**: The opening of a verse novel must establish everything the reader needs — voice, subject, form — in as few lines as possible.

> **Case: The Twin Voice Contrast** (Throughout): Raeanne's poems are titled with questions — "Interesting Question," "One Soul or Two." Kaeleigh's poems are titled with statements about her state of being. This difference — questioning vs. stating — captures their different psychological positions. Raeanne interrogates the world; Kaeleigh endures it.
> **Key takeaway**: In dual-narrator novels, the sections must be distinguishable not just by content but by form — different titling conventions, different line lengths, different rhetorical patterns.

## 5 Application Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Understanding a Twin
**The situation:** Someone knows identical twins and wants to understand their experience better.
**The novel's approach:** Twins are not two copies of the same person. They are two individuals shaped by a shared starting point. The novel shows that the same environment can produce opposite personalities because each twin occupies a different role in the family system.

### Scenario 2: Recognizing Hidden Abuse
**The situation:** Someone suspects a child is being abused but isn't sure.
**The novel's approach:** The signs are often contradictory — one child acts out, another withdraws. The family presents a normal face to the outside. The father is a respected judge. The abuse is hidden by the family's public image.

### Scenario 3: Supporting a Trauma Survivor
**The situation:** Someone wants to support a friend who has experienced childhood trauma.
**The novel's approach:** Don't push for details. Don't judge coping mechanisms. Be present. The sisters heal when someone witnesses their pain without trying to fix it.

### Scenario 4: Writing About Difficult Subjects
**The situation:** A writer wants to address trauma but fears being exploitative.
**The novel's approach:** Use form as a filter. The verse form creates distance that makes the material bearable. Poetry allows you to approach dark subjects with beauty and precision that prose might not achieve.

### Scenario 5: Explaining Why Someone Acts Self-Destructively
**The situation:** Someone doesn't understand why a person would hurt themselves.
**The novel's approach:** Self-destructive behavior is a survival strategy. It's a way of managing unbearable internal pain. The question is not "why would someone do that?" but "what happened to make this seem like the only option?"
