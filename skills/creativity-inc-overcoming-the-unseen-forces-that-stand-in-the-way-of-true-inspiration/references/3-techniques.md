# Techniques: Postmortems and Notes Day

> Source: Creativity, Inc., Chapters 8-10

## Postmortems Without Blame

After every film, Pixar conducts a postmortem. Not to assign blame — to learn.

> **Case: The Toy Story 2 Postmortem** (Chapter 8): After Toy Story 2, the team analyzed everything that went wrong. No finger-pointing. They identified systemic issues and fixed them. The next film was better because of it.

> **Key takeaway**: Postmortems should be blameless. The goal is to improve the system, not punish individuals.

## Notes Day

Pixar created a company-wide day dedicated to improving how it works. Employees at all levels gave feedback on Pixar's culture.

> **Case: The First Notes Day** (Chapter 9): Catmull realized that success breeds complacency. Notes Day was an intervention — a day where everyone could say what was broken. The result was a better, more transparent Pixar.

> **Key takeaway**: Even successful organizations need to stop and ask: what's broken? Notes Day institutionalizes this.

## The Steve Jobs Relationship

Catmull's honest portrayal of Steve Jobs — brilliant, demanding, and capable of growth.

> **Case: Steve's Evolution** (Chapter 10): Early Jobs was abrasive. Later Jobs learned patience. Catmull shows that even the most difficult personalities can grow when they're part of a healthy culture.

> **Key takeaway**: Blameless postmortems and Notes Day are systems for continuous improvement. Culture must be actively maintained, not assumed to persist on its own.
