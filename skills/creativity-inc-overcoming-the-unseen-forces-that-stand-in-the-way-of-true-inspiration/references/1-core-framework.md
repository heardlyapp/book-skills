# Core Framework: Pixar's Creative Culture

> Source: Creativity, Inc., Introduction and Chapters 1-3

## The Pixar Story

Ed Catmull co-founded Pixar with Steve Jobs and John Lasseter. From short films to Toy Story (1995) — the first fully computer-animated feature — Pixar revolutionized animation. Catmull's real focus: building a sustainable creative culture.

> **Case: The Toy Story Crisis** (Chapter 1): Early Toy Story didn't work. Woody was unlikeable. The story was flat. The Braintrust gave honest feedback. Filmmakers reworked everything. The result: a masterpiece that launched a studio.

> **Key takeaway**: Every great Pixar film started as a bad one. The Braintrust surfaces problems early before they become unfixable.

## Catmull's Background

A computer scientist who loved animation. He saw early that computers could transform the art form.

> **Case: The NYIT and Lucasfilm Years** (Chapter 2): Catmull learned that technical excellence alone produces nothing. You need creative excellence too. Pixar's success came from integrating both — "getting the team right."

> **Key takeaway**: Technical and creative excellence must go together. Hire brilliant people in both areas and let them collaborate.

## The Braintrust Model

The heart of Pixar's creative process. A group of trusted peers who watch works-in-progress and give honest notes. No authority to mandate changes — only insight.

> **Case: The Ratatouille Rescue** (Chapter 3): Ratatouille was struggling. A new director was brought in. The Braintrust gave him freedom to reimagine it. The film became one of Pixar's best.

> **Key takeaway**: The Braintrust offers insight without authority. Filmmakers retain ownership and decide what to use.
