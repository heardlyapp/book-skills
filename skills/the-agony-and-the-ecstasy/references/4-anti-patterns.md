# Anti-Patterns — Settling, Delegating Your Vision, and the Fear of Ruining the Marble

## The Central Fallacy

The most dangerous mistake: believing you can create great work without great suffering. Michelangelo suffered for every masterpiece. The Pietà nearly broke him. The David was carved from a flawed block. The Sistine Ceiling cost him his health. The suffering is not the price of art. It is part of the art.

> **Case: The Fear of the Blank Stone** (Throughout): Michelangelo stares at the uncarved marble. He is afraid. What if he ruins it? What if the figure he sees inside is not there? The fear does not stop him. He picks up the chisel. He makes the first cut.
> **Key takeaway**: Every creative person fears the blank page, the empty canvas, the uncarved stone. The only way to overcome the fear is to start. The first cut is the hardest.

> **Case: The Envious Rivals** (Throughout): Michelangelo's rivals spread rumors. They damage his reputation. They try to steal his commissions. He does not fight them directly. He outworks them. He creates things they cannot match.
> **Key takeaway**: Do not fight rivals. Out-create them. The best revenge is a better work.

> **Case: The Family Burden** (Throughout): Michelangelo's family drains his money. They make demands. They do not understand his work. He supports them anyway. He complains about it. He does not let it stop him.
> **Key takeaway**: You may have people who do not understand your work. Support them if you must. Do not let them stop you. The work comes first.
