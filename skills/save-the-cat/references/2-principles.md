# Key Principles — Save the Cat!

## I. The Audience Must Like Your Hero

"Liking the person we go on a journey with is the single most important element in drawing us into the story." Snyder's "Save the Cat" scene: the hero must do something likable early on. Al Pacino lets a father off in Sea of Love. In Legally Blonde, Elle Woods fights for her dog Bruiser. Without this, no amount of cool special effects or clever twists will save the movie.

## II. You Must Answer "What Is It?"

The single most important question. "If you can't tell me about it in one quick line, well, buddy I'm on to something else." A good logline has irony, creates a compelling mental picture, signals audience and cost, and includes a killer title. The logline comes before the script — not after.

## III. Structure Is Not Optional

Snyder's 15-beat beat sheet applies to every successful movie — from Die Hard to Miss Congeniality to Thelma & Louise. "These beats can be found in all genres, all cultures, and in movies from all periods." The beats are not a formula; they're a map. "You can't break the rules until you know them."

## IV. 40 Cards, No More

"40 index cards, 4 rows of 10. Act One = first row. Act Two = next two rows. Act Three = fourth row. If you can't do it in 40 cards, you don't have a movie." The index card method forces you to see the entire structure at once. Each card = one scene. "Troubleshooting at card stage costs a pencil. Troubleshooting at script stage costs weeks."

## V. A Great Villain Makes a Great Hero

"Make the Bad Guy Badder." The villain is the engine of the story. The hero is only as interesting as the obstacles they overcome. Hans Gruber makes John McClane heroic. The shark makes Brody. If your hero feels weak, your villain is too weak.

## VI. The Promise of the Premise Is Paramount

"Fun and Games" — the section from 20-55% of the script — is the promise of the premise. It's the part audiences pay to see. In Die Hard, it's McClane barefoot in the vent. In Legally Blonde, it's Elle in Harvard. Don't shortchange this section. "This is why they bought their ticket."

## VII. The Hero Must Change

"The Covenant of the Arc." The hero at the Final Image must be different from the hero at the Opening Image. If they haven't grown, there's no story. "Have I changed the hero's internal trajectory?" If the answer is no, the script isn't finished.
