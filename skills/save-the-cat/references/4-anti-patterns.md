# Anti-Patterns — Save the Cat!

## The Central Error: Thinking Your Script Is Above Structure

"I've gotten script notes from Steven Spielberg... Not one of them ever said: 'I don't know, Blake — maybe it's just too structured.'" The biggest mistake amateur screenwriters make is believing structure is a cage, not a foundation. Snyder: "I'll give you the rules. You can break them when you know them."

### Mistake 1: "I'll write the logline after the script"
The logline is not a marketing afterthought — it's the blueprint. "If you can't tell me what it's about, you haven't thought it through." Write the logline first. It saves months of writing in the wrong direction.

### Mistake 2: "My hero is cool, so the audience will like them"
"Lara Croft drives a cool car. She wears a cool latex suit. She's cool. I don't care." Cool is not likable. The Star Wars prequels failed because Anakin Skywalker never saved a cat. "You can be cool on page 50. On page 10, you need to be likable."

### Mistake 3: "The villain is just evil"
A mustache-twirling villain makes a weak hero. "Make the Bad Guy Badder." The hero's struggle is defined by the villain's power. Voldemort makes Harry heroic. Hans Gruber makes John McClane heroic. The Joker makes Batman heroic.

### Mistake 4: "The audience needs to know everything"
Laying pipe is essential — but laying it too obviously is "Too Much Marzipan." "How much is too much? When they're scraping it off the plate." If the audience doesn't need the information to understand the current scene, cut it.

### Mistake 5: "My story is too unique for genre"
Snyder argues there are only 10 genres. "Every movie ever made can be categorized by one of them." If you can't fit your movie into a genre, you have a marketing problem — not an art problem.

### Mistake 6: "I'll fix it in the rewrite"
"Troubleshooting at card stage costs a pencil. Troubleshooting at script stage costs weeks. Troubleshooting after you've sent it to your agent costs your career." Fix the structure before you write the words.

### Mistake 7: "My hero doesn't need to change"
"The Covenant of the Arc: your hero must change." If your hero is the same person at the end as at the beginning, you don't have a movie. "Have I changed the hero's internal trajectory?" If not, the script isn't finished.
