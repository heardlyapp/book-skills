# Core Framework — Save the Cat!

## Introduction: Why Another Screenwriting Book?

Blake Snyder was a working Hollywood screenwriter for 20 years who sold millions in spec scripts. "I think it would be nice if the guy writing the book on how to write a screenplay had actually sold something." His approach is practical, market-driven, and based on "the laws of physics that govern good storytelling."

**"Save the Cat":** The title comes from the most fundamental rule: the hero must do something likable early in the story so the audience roots for them. In Sea of Love, Al Pacino lets a father-offender go because the man has his young son with him. "I don't know about you, but I like Al. I'll go anywhere he takes me now." The opposite: Lara Croft 2 — "She's cold and humorless. They spent millions on her latex suit but nothing on making me care about her."

## Chapter 1: What Is It? — The Logline

The single most important question a movie must answer. "If you can't tell me about it in one quick line, well, buddy I'm on to something else."

**The four components of a great logline:**
1. **Irony:** An ironic hook grabs attention. "A cop comes to L.A. to visit his estranged wife and her office building is taken over by terrorists." — Die Hard
2. **Compelling mental picture:** "You must be able to see a whole movie in it." "She's the perfect woman — until she has a drink." — Blind Date
3. **Audience and cost:** A logline reveals who the movie is for and how much it costs. "4 Christmases" — medium-budget, four-quadrant, Christmas perennial.
4. **Killer title:** Must say what it is. Legally Blonde — "nails the concept without being so on the nose."

## Chapter 2: Give Me the Same Thing... Only Different! — The 10 Genres

Snyder argues there are only 10 movie genres. Every film fits one:
1. **Monster in the House** (Jaws, Alien, The Ring) — Sin + a confined space
2. **Golden Fleece** (Star Wars, The Wizard of Oz) — A hero goes on a journey and comes home changed
3. **Out of the Bottle** (Liar Liar, Bruce Almighty) — A wish is granted; it backfires
4. **Dude with a Problem** (Die Hard, Titanic) — An ordinary person in extraordinary circumstances
5. **Rites of Passage** (Ordinary People, 10, The Forty-Year-Old Virgin) — Life transition pain
6. **Buddy Love** (When Harry Met Sally, Lethal Weapon) — Two opposites who need each other
7. **Whydunit** (Chinatown, The Usual Suspects) — The mystery of why, not who
8. **The Fool Triumphant** (Forrest Gump, Being There) — The innocent wins
9. **Institutionalized** (One Flew Over the Cuckoo's Nest, M*A*S*H) — A group in an institution
10. **Superhero** (Superman, Spider-Man, The Matrix) — Chosen one saves the day

## Chapter 4: The Blake Snyder Beat Sheet (BS2) — 15 Beats

This is the book's most famous contribution. Every successful movie follows these 15 beats:

1. **Opening Image (1%):** A snapshot of the hero before the journey
2. **Theme Stated (5%):** Someone says what the story's about
3. **Set-Up (1-10%):** Show the hero's world and what's missing
4. **Catalyst (10%):** The inciting incident that changes everything
5. **Debate (10-20%):** The hero questions the journey
6. **Break into Two (20%):** The hero leaves the old world for the new
7. **B Story (22%):** The love story or subplot begins
8. **Fun and Games (20-55%):** The promise of the premise — the fun part
9. **Midpoint (55%):** Either a false victory or a false defeat
10. **Bad Guys Close In (55-75%):** Pressure mounts, enemies regroup
11. **All Is Lost (75%):** The lowest moment — "the whiff of death"
12. **Dark Night of the Soul (75-85%):** The hero grieves and grows
13. **Break into Three (85%):** The hero realizes what they must do
14. **Finale (85-99%):** The hero applies what they've learned
15. **Final Image (99-100%):** A snapshot of the hero after the journey — shows change

## Chapter 5: Building the Perfect Beast — Index Cards

40 index cards, 4 rows of 10. The first row = Act One. The next two rows = Act Two. The fourth = Act Three. "If you can't do it in 40 cards, you don't have a movie." Each card = a scene. The exercise forces discipline.

## Chapter 6: The Immutable Laws of Screenplay Physics

- **Save the Cat:** The hero must do something likable
- **The Pope in the Pool:** Exposition must be delivered while the audience is distracted
- **Double Mumbo Jumbo:** One supernatural element per movie, not two
- **Laying Pipe:** Every setup must have a payoff (Chekhov's gun)
- **Too Much Marzipan:** Too much of a good thing = bad
- **Watch Out for That Glacier!:** The story must move forward
- **Covenant of the Arc:** The hero must change

## Chapter 7: What's Wrong With This Picture?

Six script repair tools:
1. **The Hero Leads:** Is the hero active or reactive?
2. **Make the Bad Guy Badder:** A great villain raises the hero
3. **Turn, Turn, Turn:** Does the story twist enough?
4. **The Emotional Color Wheel:** Are you varying the audience's emotions?
5. **"Hi How Are You I'm Fine":** Cut all greeting scenes
6. **Take a Step Back:** Look at the whole forest, not just the trees
