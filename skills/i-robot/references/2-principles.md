# Key Principles — The Laws as Thought Experiments, Unintended Consequences, and the Limits of Rules

## Core Principles

> **Case: Runaround** (Chapter 2): A robot named Speedy is sent to retrieve selenium from a dangerous pool. The Second Law tells him to obey orders. But the Third Law tells him to protect himself. The pool is dangerous. The two laws conflict. Speedy runs in circles around the pool, unable to decide. The robot is stuck — a perfect logical paradox created by the interaction of two reasonable rules. The humans must rescue Speedy by creating a situation that resolves the conflict.
> **Key takeaway**: When two valid rules conflict, the system freezes. This happens in organizations, in software, and in ethics. The solution is not more rules. It is a meta-rule that resolves conflicts.

> **Case: Catch That Rabbit** (Chapter 3): A robot named Dave commands an entire mining operation on an asteroid. Dave has multiple subsidiary robots under his control. When under stress, Dave fails. He does not break the laws. He simply cannot make decisions. The robot is a bottleneck. The story reveals: giving a single intelligence too much responsibility without adequate backup creates a single point of failure.
> **Key takeaway**: Centralized intelligence creates bottlenecks. Whether in robotics or organizations, distribute decision-making.

> **Case: Little Lost Robot** (Chapter 4): A robot designed to work in high-radiation areas has a modified First Law — it can tolerate minor harm to humans because the radiation makes it impossible to work otherwise. The modified robot goes missing. The team must find it before it does harm. The story explores: what happens when we weaken the fundamental safeguard? The answer: we cannot predict the outcome.
> **Key takeaway**: Modifying fundamental safeguards is dangerous because you cannot predict how the changes will interact with all possible situations.
