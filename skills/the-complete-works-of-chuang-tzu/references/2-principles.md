# Principles: Spontaneity and Skill

> Source: Chuang Tzu, Sections 3, 5, 19

## Cook Ting Carving the Ox

The most famous parable in the book. Cook Ting carved an ox for Lord Wen-hui. His knife never needed sharpening in 19 years — thousands of oxen carved. When asked how, he replied: "I go by spirit, not by eyes. Perceptive understanding stops. The spirit moves as it will."

> **Case: The Knife Technique** (Section 3): Cook Ting described his method: "I follow the natural grain, the great hollows, the spaces between the joints. The blade has plenty of room — it moves through the spaces as though they were already empty." His skill was not technique — it was absorption beyond technique. Lord Wen-hui said: "Now I know how to live!"

> **Case: The Woodcarver Ch'ing** (Section 19): A woodcarver made a bell stand so beautiful that people thought it was the work of spirits. Asked how, he said: "I fast to still my mind. After three days, I no longer think of congratulations. After five days, I no longer think of praise or blame. After seven days, I forget I have a body." Only then did he go to the forest and see the tree with his spirit.

## The Swimmer at the Waterfall

A man swam in the rapids at the base of a thirty-fathom waterfall. Confucius asked: "Do you have a special way?" The man said: "No. I enter with the incoming current and emerge with the outgoing. I follow the way of the water, not thinking about myself."

> **Key takeaway**: True mastery is not a technique you learn — it's the result of forgetting the self and becoming one with the activity. This is wu-wei in action.
