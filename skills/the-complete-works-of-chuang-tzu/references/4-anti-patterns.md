# Anti-Patterns: What Chuang Tzu Rejects

> Source: Chuang Tzu, throughout

## Mistaking the Finger for the Moon

Chuang Tzu warns repeatedly: words are pointers, not the truth itself. The fish trap exists for the fish; once you catch the fish, you can forget the trap. The rabbit snare exists for the rabbit; once you catch the rabbit, you can forget the snare.

> **Case: The Confucian Scripture Readers** (passim): Chuang Tzu satirizes those who memorize scriptures without understanding. Knowing the words is not knowing the Way. "Those who speak don't know. Those who know don't speak."

> **Key takeaway**: If you're quoting Chuang Tzu at people to prove a point, you've missed his point entirely. Words are tools, not truths.

## Seeking Wu-wei as a Goal

The moment you "try" to be spontaneous, you're not. Wu-wei cannot be strived for. It arises naturally when you stop forcing.

> **Case: The Man Who Tried to Forget** (passim): Trying to forget is still trying. Trying to stop judging is still judging. True freedom comes when even the effort to be free is released.

## Clinging to Life and Fearing Death

Chuang Tzu rejects the immortality seekers who try to extend life through magic and alchemy. Life and death are natural transformations — like waking and sleeping.

> **Case: The Skull by the Road** (Section 18): Chuang Tzu saw a skull by the road. He asked: "Do you long for life?" The skull replied: "Why would I? In death, there are no kings, no subjects, no changing seasons. I am happier than a king."
