# Key Principles — Essentials of Classic Italian Cooking

## I. Simplicity Over Complexity

"The taste they have been devised to achieve wants not to astonish, but to reassure." Hazan's most famous recipe — Tomato Sauce with Onion and Butter — uses only three ingredients (canned tomatoes, butter, onion) and produces something far richer than the sum of its parts. Case: the Florentine T-bone steak — "grilled quickly over incandescent embers of wood fire, adding nothing but the aroma of olive oil and a grinding of pepper."

## II. Regional Identity, Not National Cuisine

"The cooking of Italy is really the cooking of regions." Venice and Naples both love seafood but speak different culinary languages. Bologna and Florence are 60 miles apart with "unbridgeable differences." There is no single "Italian cooking" — there are many. Case: Bologna stuffs veal with ham and truffles; Florence grills a T-bone with nothing but salt and oil.

## III. Flavor Builds From the Bottom

"It is not a cover, it is a base." The battuto → soffritto → insaporire sequence is the architectural foundation. "A foundation of flavor supports, lifts, points up the principal ingredients." Skip any step and the dish cannot recover.

## IV. Onion Before Garlic

"When sautéing onion and garlic together, cook the onion first." Onion takes longer to brown. If both go in at the same time, "by the time the onion became translucent the garlic would be too dark." An imperfect soffritto "will impair the flavor of a dish no matter how carefully all succeeding steps are carried out."

## V. Quality Ingredients Are Non-Negotiable

Hazan is uncompromising: "The meatiest anchovies are the ones kept under salt... Do not use anchovy paste from a tube, if you can help it." On balsamic vinegar: "The label must carry Aceto Balsamico Tradizionale di Modena. All other so-called balsamic vinegars are ordinary commercial wine vinegar flavored with sugar." On basil: "Don't ever use dried or powdered basil."

## VI. Respect the Cooked and the Raw

"Basil — the less it cooks, the better it is." Pesto should be used raw, at room temperature, never warmed up. Capers from salt taste better than vinegar-packed. Bottarga is never cooked. But some things must cook: dried beans need overnight soaking and slow simmering. Know which ingredients need heat and which don't.

## VII. Home Cooking Is the Only Real Italian Cooking

"There is no such thing as Italian haute cuisine. All roads lead to the home." Hazan wrote for home cooks, not restaurant chefs. Her recipes were tested in her kitchen, for her family. "It is cooking that spans remembered history, that has evolved during the whole course of transmitted skills and intuitions in homes throughout the Italian peninsula."
