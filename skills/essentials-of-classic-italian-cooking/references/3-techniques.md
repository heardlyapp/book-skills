# Techniques — Essentials of Classic Italian Cooking

## How to Cook Italian (Based on Marcella Hazan)

### Technique 1: The Battuto-Soffritto-Insaporire Sequence
The foundation of Italian cooking. Chop the aromatics (battuto), sauté until translucent (soffritto), then coat the main ingredients in the flavor base (insaporire). Case: Hazan explains that "one can often trace the unsatisfying taste of dishes purporting to be Italian to the reluctance of some cooks to execute the insaporire step thoroughly." Don't skip it.

### Technique 2: The Perfect Soffritto Order
Onion first, alone. When translucent, add garlic. When garlic becomes colored, add the rest. If pancetta is included, cook it with the onion from the start to use its fat. Case: "If you were to put both onion and garlic in at the same time, by the time the onion became translucent the garlic would be too dark."

### Technique 3: Butter + Oil for Sautéing
"When one might find the flavor of olive oil intrusive, Italian cooks use butter together with neutral-tasting vegetable oil." The oil raises the smoke point so you can sauté at higher temperatures without scorching the butter — no need to clarify it.

### Technique 4: The Anchovy Dissolve
Never put chopped anchovies into very hot oil — they will fry and harden instead of dissolving, and their flavor may turn bitter. Remove the pan from heat when adding anchovies. Return to heat only when they've begun to break down into a paste. Or use a double-boiler method.

### Technique 5: Tomato Sauce with Onion and Butter
Hazan's signature: one 28-oz can of peeled tomatoes (San Marzano ideally), 5 tablespoons butter, one onion peeled and cut in half. Simmer 45 minutes, discard the onion. "It is simple because the ingredients are few. It is sumptuous because the combination produces something far richer than the sum of its parts."

### Technique 6: Pasta Cooking Rules
Bolognese is never served with spaghetti — only with homemade egg pasta (tagliatelle, pappardelle, lasagne). The sauce clings to the rough, porous surface of fresh egg pasta. Factory-made dried pasta (spaghetti, penne) pairs with oil-based sauces. "The shape and type of pasta must match the sauce's ability to cling to it."

### Technique 7: The Broth Principle
"Italian broth is not stock. It is light bodied and soft spoken, helping the dishes of which it is a part to taste better without calling attention to itself." Use beef, veal, and chicken meat with marrow bones. Avoid lamb, pork, or excessive chicken giblets — their flavors are too strong.
