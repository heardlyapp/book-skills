# Core Framework — Essentials of Classic Italian Cooking

## Preface: The Philosophy

Marcella Hazan reworked her two classic books into this single volume over three years. "The dishes continued to evolve, moving always toward a simpler, clearer expression of their primary flavors, and toward a steadily diminishing dependence on cooking fat." Her philosophy: "The taste they have been devised to achieve wants not to astonish, but to reassure. It issues from the cultural memory, the enduring world of generations of Italian cooks."

She refuses to adapt recipes for the microwave: "I believe with my whole heart in the act of cooking, in its smells, in its sounds, in its observable progress on the fire. The microwave separates the cook from cooking."

## Introduction: Understanding Italian Cooking

Italian cooking is regional, not national. Bologna vs Venice vs Naples vs Florence — different languages, different cuisines. "Bologna will stuff veal with succulent Parma ham, coat it with aged Parmesan, sauté it in butter, and conceal it under shaved white truffles. Florence takes a T-bone steak, grills it over wood fire, adding nothing but olive oil and pepper."

Two dominant geographic factors: the Alps (butter zone, rice for risotto, polenta) and the Apennines (olive oil zone, pasta from hard wheat). Emilia-Romagna is "probably the only city in Italy whose name is instantly associated with food."

"There is no such thing as Italian haute cuisine because there are no high or low roads in Italian cooking. All roads lead to the home — la cucina di casa — the only one that deserves to be called Italian cooking."

## Fundamentals: Where Flavor Starts

**The Three Key Techniques:**

**Battuto:** The chopped base — typically lard (historically), onion, parsley, sometimes garlic, celery, carrot, pancetta. "At the base of virtually every pasta sauce, risotto or soup, and of numberless meat and vegetable dishes."

**Soffritto:** The sautéed battuto. Onion first until translucent, then garlic, then remaining ingredients. "An imperfectly executed soffritto will impair the flavor of a dish no matter how carefully all the succeeding steps are carried out."

**Insaporire:** "Bestowing taste." Adding vegetables or other main ingredients to the soffritto and sautéing over very lively heat until completely coated with the flavor base. "One can often trace the unsatisfying taste of dishes purporting to be Italian to the reluctance of some cooks to execute this step thoroughly."

## Signature Recipes That Define the Book

**Tomato Sauce with Onion and Butter (Salsa di Pomodoro):** Hazan's most famous recipe. One 28-oz can of peeled tomatoes, 5 tablespoons butter, 1 onion peeled and cut in half. Simmer 45 minutes, discard the onion. "It is simple because the ingredients are few. It is sumptuous because the combination of those few ingredients — tomatoes, butter, onion — produces something far richer than the sum of its parts."

**Bolognese Meat Sauce (Ragù):** Just beef, pancetta, onion, celery, carrot, butter, olive oil, wine, milk, nutmeg, a whisper of tomato. "In Bologna, meat sauce is never served with spaghetti. It is served exclusively with homemade egg pasta — tagliatelle, pappardelle, lasagne."

**Basic Homemade Meat Broth:** "The broth used by Italian cooks is not stock. It is light bodied and soft spoken, helping dishes to taste better without calling attention to itself."
