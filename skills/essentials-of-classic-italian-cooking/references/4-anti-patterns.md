# Anti-Patterns — Essentials of Classic Italian Cooking

## The Central Error: "Add Garlic and Tomatoes = Italian"

Hazan's entire book is a refutation of this. Italian cooking is about technique and regional identity, not a checklist of ingredients. "Tomato and garlic were once clichés of spaghetti-house Italian cooking." Real Italian cooking is more subtle and varied.

### Mistake 1: "More is better"
Italian cooking is about subtraction, not addition. "The dishes continued to evolve, moving always toward a simpler, clearer expression of their primary flavors." Hazan's butter-tomato sauce has three ingredients. The Florentine steak has two (meat + salt).

### Mistake 2: "Balsamic vinegar is a condiment for everything"
"Balsamic vinegar has become a cliché of what is sometimes described as 'creative' cooking." It should not be "used so often or so indiscriminately that its flavor loses the power to surprise." True Aceto Balsamico Tradizionale is precious — use it sparingly.

### Mistake 3: "Use whatever pasta shape you have"
In Bologna, meat sauce is never served with spaghetti. "Bolognese sauce is served exclusively with homemade egg pasta — tagliatelle, pappardelle, lasagne." Different sauces pair with different pasta shapes and types. The sauce and pasta are designed for each other.

### Mistake 4: "Dried herbs are fine substitutes"
"Don't ever use dried or powdered basil." Hazan takes a stand: basil only when fresh. If it's out of season, wait. "Do not use anchovy paste from a tube." It is "harsh and salty and has very little of the warm, attractive aroma" of real anchovies.

### Mistake 5: "Stock can replace broth"
"Italian broth is not stock." Stock is "a strong, dense reduction." Broth is "light bodied and soft spoken." They are different products with different purposes.

### Mistake 6: "The microwave is a valid cooking method"
Hazan tried and rejected the microwave. "The microwave does not produce satisfying textures or well-integrated flavors. It separates the cook from cooking, cutting off the emotional and physical pleasure deeply rooted in the act."
