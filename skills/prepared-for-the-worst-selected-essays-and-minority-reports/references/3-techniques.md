# Techniques: Literary Criticism

> Source: Prepared for the Worst, "CRITICAL" and "WRITERS" sections

## Comrade Orwell

Hitchens' essay on Orwell — the writer who influenced him most. Orwell set the standard: clear, honest, politically engaged.

> **Case: The Orwell Standard** (WRITERS): Hitchens defends Orwell against attacks from both right and left. Orwell was not a cold warrior — he was a democratic socialist who opposed all tyranny equally. Hitchens measures all political writing against Orwell's clarity and courage.

> **Case: The Orwell Legacy** (WRITERS): Hitchens argues that Orwell's reputation is "secure among those who have never read him." Reading Orwell reveals a more complex, more radical, more interesting writer than the myth.

> **Key takeaway**: Orwell represents everything Hitchens values: clarity, courage, independence from party. The standard for political writing.

## The Chorus and Cassandra

The writer's duty to warn — even when no one listens.

> **Case: Cassandra as Model** (CRITICAL): Hitchens argues the Cambodian genocide was foretold by writers who were ignored. The writer's duty is to speak truth, regardless of whether it changes anything. Like Cassandra, you may be doomed not to be believed. Speak anyway.

## Reading to Borges

A personal essay on visiting the blind Borges in Buenos Aires.

> **Case: The Blind Sage** (CRITICAL): Hitchens captures Borges' wit and wisdom in an intimate portrait. The piece shows Hitchens' range — not just polemic but genuine literary engagement.

> **Key takeaway**: Hitchens' literary criticism always connects text to politics, power, and human nature.
