# Voice and Application: Hitchens' Voice

> Source: Prepared for the Worst, throughout

## Hitchens' Voice

Christopher Hitchens wrote in a voice unmistakably his own: erudite, witty, unsparing, never dull. He combined high literary culture with urgent political engagement. He could quote Pericles and eviscerate a politician in the same paragraph.

> **Case: The Introduction's Signature** (throughout): "Call no man lucky until he is dead, but there have been moments of rare satisfaction in the often random and fragmented life of the radical freelance scribbler." This sentence captures everything: self-deprecating, literary, politically committed. Pure Hitchens.

> **Case: The Gordimer Frame** (Introduction): Hitchens opens with Nadine Gordimer's "posthumous writing." He admits he can't achieve her detachment. He presents his essays like an anxious parent. This is the voice of someone who takes his work seriously but not himself.

> **Key takeaway**: Hitchens' voice is the product of wide reading, deep conviction, and the refusal to be boring. Admirable but not easily imitated.

## Application: Writing

Clarity is a moral duty. Know your position and state it plainly. Defend it with evidence.

## Application: Politics

Judge by actions, not words. Apply the same standard to allies and opponents.

## Application: Intellectual Life

Read widely. Think independently. Examine every consensus before accepting it.
