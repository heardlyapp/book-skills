## 7 Principles of Osho's Teaching

### 1. You Are Not Your Mind

The mind is a machine — useful but not who you are. You are the awareness behind the thoughts. Meditation is the practice of recognizing this.

### 2. Rebellion, Not Belief

Do not believe anything — not even what Osho says. Truth cannot be found through belief. It can only be discovered through direct experience.

### 3. Active Meditation for the Modern Mind

The modern mind cannot sit still. Osho's active meditations (Dynamic, Kundalini) use movement, breathing, and catharsis to release tension before stillness can happen.

> **Case: The Man Who Could Not Sit** (Chapter 4): A businessman came to Osho and said he could not meditate — his mind was too restless. Osho gave him Dynamic Meditation. The man did it daily. After a month, he could sit in silence. Osho's point: the modern mind needs active techniques before passive ones can work.
> **Key takeaway:** If you cannot meditate by sitting, do not force it. Active meditation prepares the mind for stillness.

### 4. Sex Is Spiritual

Repressed sex becomes perversion. Conscious sex becomes meditation. Osho taught that denying sexuality is harmful — integrating it with awareness is liberating.

### 5. Love Is the Highest Flower

When two conscious beings meet, love happens naturally. Love is not something to pursue — it is the natural expression of awareness.

### 6. The Teacher Is a Provocation

A real teacher does not give you answers — he questions your questions. He disturbs your comfort. He provokes you to find your own truth.

### 7. Freedom Is the Goal

Freedom from conditioning, freedom from belief, freedom from the past — this is the ultimate goal. Not enlightenment as a state, but freedom as a continuous process.

> **Case: The Christian Woman** (Chapter 7): A devout Christian came to Osho and asked: "Is Jesus the only way?" Osho replied: "Jesus is one way. Buddha is another. The path is within you — not in any savior." She was offended. He was not trying to convert her — he was provoking her to question.
> **Key takeaway**: Osho's rebellion was not against any particular religion — it was against the very idea that truth can be owned by anyone.
