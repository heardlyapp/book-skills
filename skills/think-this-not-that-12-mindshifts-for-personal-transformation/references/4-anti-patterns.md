# Anti-Patterns: What Not to Do

## Overview

Axe's book identifies common mental traps that keep people stuck. These anti-patterns are the default mode of thinking for most people — the "not that" that must be replaced.

## Anti-Pattern 1: Letting Others Define Your Identity

The most damaging anti-pattern: giving other people the power to define who you are. A teacher, parent, boss, or even a stranger on social media says something about you, and you accept it as truth.

**Why it fails:** Other people's opinions are filtered through their own biases, experiences, and limitations. They cannot see your full potential. Yet we treat their judgments as objective facts about ourselves.

**Case — Josh Axe's teacher:** A single teacher's dismissive laugh nearly derailed his medical career. If he had accepted her judgment, he would never have become a doctor. The lesson: never let one person's limited perspective define your potential.

**Case — ADHD label:** The diagnosis of ADHD was almost accepted as "you're medically not smart." Axe had to consciously reject this definition and find his own.

**Fix:** When someone tells you what you can't do, ask: "Is this true? Does this person have the full picture? What evidence do I have to the contrary?" Develop the habit of filtering others' opinions through your own judgment.

## Anti-Pattern 2: Equating Achievement with Worth

Society teaches that your value depends on what you produce, earn, or accomplish. This creates a treadmill: you achieve one goal, feel good briefly, then need another achievement to maintain your sense of worth.

**Why it fails:** Achievement-based worth is never satisfied. There's always more to achieve. Failure — which is inevitable — becomes a crisis of identity rather than a learning opportunity. The person who defines themselves by achievement crashes hardest when they fail.

**Case — The burnout entrepreneur:** Axe saw entrepreneurs who measured their worth by revenue, headcount, or valuation. When business declined, their sense of self crumbled. Those who measured worth by who they were becoming weathered downturns without losing their identity.

**Case — The rebound patient:** Patients who equated success with "losing 20 pounds" often regained the weight. The goal was accomplished, then there was nothing left to motivate them. Identity-based change ("I am becoming a healthy person") produced lasting results.

**Fix:** After every achievement, ask: "Who did I become in the process?" Measure growth by character, learning, and relationships. Accomplishments will come and go; who you become stays.

## Anti-Pattern 3: Avoiding Self-Awareness

Many people avoid honest self-assessment because it's uncomfortable. They prefer the comfort of not knowing their blind spots.

**Why it fails:** Blind spots don't disappear because you ignore them. They accumulate. A leader who doesn't know they micromanage will lose their best employees. A partner who doesn't know they're dismissive will damage their relationships. Avoidance doesn't protect you — it harms you.

**Case — Leadership failure:** Axe notes that the most common cause of leadership derailment is lack of self-awareness. Leaders who resist feedback, avoid reflection, and assume they know themselves eventually fail — and don't understand why.

**Case — Relationship patterns:** People who repeat the same relationship failures without examining their own patterns stay stuck. Self-awareness breaks the cycle.

**Fix:** Schedule regular self-reflection. Seek honest feedback from trusted people. Ask: "What am I not seeing about myself?" The discomfort of self-awareness is temporary; the cost of avoiding it is permanent.

## Anti-Pattern 4: Living by Opinions Instead of Principles

When your decisions are driven by what others think, you become a people-pleaser with no stable core. This is exhausting and ultimately fails.

**Why it fails:** Opinions change. People change. What's approved today may be criticized tomorrow. Living by opinion is like building on shifting sand. Principles — courage, honesty, compassion — don't change.

**Case — Social media anxiety:** Axe describes how letting social media likes and comments determine your sense of approval creates constant anxiety. You can't control what others think. You can control whether you live by your principles.

**Case — Career regret:** People who make career decisions based on what their parents, peers, or society expect often end up in careers they hate. Those who make decisions based on their own principles and values find more fulfillment.

**Fix:** Write your core principles. Test decisions against them. When you feel pressure to conform, ask: "Does this align with my principles?" If yes, proceed. If no, your principles are more important than the opinion.

## Anti-Pattern 5: Viewing Fear as a Stop Sign

Most people interpret fear as a warning: "This is dangerous. Stop." But fear often points to the most important growth opportunities.

**Why it fails:** If you stop every time you're afraid, you stop growing. The comfort zone shrinks. What you're capable of shrinks with it. You become less competent and less resilient over time.

**Case — Missed opportunities:** Axe's patients' biggest regrets were not failures but missed opportunities — things they didn't try because they were afraid. The fear wasn't protecting them; it was limiting them.

**Case — Career stagnation:** Professionals who avoid stretch assignments because of fear plateau early. Those who lean into discomfort advance throughout their careers.

**Fix:** When you feel fear, pause. Say: "This is important. Fear means I'm growing." Then ask: "What would I do if I weren't afraid?" Then do that, with courage.

## Anti-Pattern 6: Trying to Quit Vices Through Willpower

Willpower is finite. Fighting a vice directly through willpower alone almost always fails, creating a cycle of failure and shame.

**Why it fails:** Willpower depletes throughout the day. In moments of depletion, the vice wins. Each failure reinforces the belief "I can't change," which becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy.

**Case — The diet cycle:** People who fight overeating through willpower ("I will simply not eat sugar") succeed for days or weeks, then crash. The crash is not a moral failure — it's a strategy failure.

**Case — The smoking quitter:** Quitting smoking through willpower alone has a low success rate. The successful approach is to gradually reduce triggers and build incompatible habits (chewing gum, exercise, breathing exercises).

**Fix:** Don't fight vices directly. Build the opposing virtue. Instead of "I won't procrastinate," practice "I will do the most important thing first." Instead of "I won't be lazy," practice "I will move my body every morning."
