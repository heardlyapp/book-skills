# Seven Principles in Depth

## Overview

Axe's 12 mindshifts organize into seven higher-level principles about mindset transformation. Each principle works independently but is more powerful in combination. Together they form a complete system for personal change.

## Principle 1: Beliefs Create Self-Fulfilling Prophecies

A belief is a mental certainty that something is true. Whether the belief is true or false, it changes your behavior, perception, and persistence — and thus changes your outcomes.

**Case — The laughing teacher:** When Josh Axe's English teacher laughed at his dream of becoming a doctor, she planted a limiting belief: "You're not smart enough." Combined with his ADHD diagnosis and failing grades, this belief had powerful "legs" supporting it. It took years to dismantle that belief and replace it with evidence that he was capable.

**Case — Placebo effect of beliefs:** Axe uses the medical parallel — patients who believe a treatment will work often get better, even with placebos. The belief itself creates physiological changes. Conversely, patients who believe they can't get better often don't, regardless of treatment quality. Beliefs are medicine — or poison.

## Principle 2: Success Is Becoming, Not Accomplishing

Society defines success by external markers: money, status, titles, possessions. But these are temporary and often empty. Real success is the person you become — the character built, the virtues cultivated, the growth achieved.

**Case — The business builder:** Axe's team members who focused on personal growth (learning, skill-building, character development) while pursuing business goals outperformed those who focused only on revenue targets. The "becoming" group built sustainable success; the "accomplishing" group burned out.

**Case — The recovery patient:** Patients who measured progress by personal transformation ("I am becoming a healthier person") maintained their results. Those who measured only by numbers ("I lost 10 pounds") often regained the weight.

## Principle 3: Self-Awareness Reveals Blind Spots

You cannot change what you do not see. Self-awareness is the foundation of all growth — without it, you repeat patterns you don't even recognize.

**Case — ADHD reframed:** Axe's initial belief about his ADHD was that it was a defect making him "not smart." Self-awareness revealed a different truth: his brain processed differently, and he needed different systems. This awareness allowed him to build around his strengths rather than fighting his nature.

**Case — Leadership blind spots:** Axe found that executives who invested in self-awareness (360° feedback, coaching, reflection) made faster progress than those who relied on their strengths alone. The most common cause of leadership failure is not lack of skill but lack of self-knowledge.

## Principle 4: Your Story Is a Choice

The events of your past are fixed. The meaning you assign to them is not. You can rewrite your narrative at any time — reframing failure as education, rejection as redirection, and limitations as challenges.

**Case — Failure narrative rewrite:** Axe's teacher's rejection, his low GPA, his ADHD — all could define his story as "not good enough." Instead, he rewrote it: "I have overcome obstacles that most people haven't faced, making me stronger." The same facts, different meaning.

**Case — Business setbacks:** Every business Axe built faced near-failure moments. The successful ones had leaders who refused to tell the "this isn't working" story and instead wrote the "we're learning what not to do" story.

## Principle 5: Identity Must Be Built From Within

If your identity depends on others' opinions, you're building on sand. A strong identity is constructed from chosen principles, values, and commitments. It doesn't shift with every criticism or compliment.

**Case — Social media identity trap:** Many people let social media likes, comments, and follower counts define their sense of worth. Axe warns this is a trap — external validation is addictive and unstable. Identity built on principles survives when the likes stop.

**Case — Principled leadership:** The best leaders have core principles that don't change. They are known for consistency. People trust them because they know what they stand for. This stability comes from internal identity, not external approval.

## Principle 6: Fear Is a Growth Signal

Fear is not the enemy. Fear points to the next area of growth. Key insight: flip fear from "stop" to "go." When fear arises, lean in.

**Case — Fear and comfort zone:** Staying in the comfort zone is actually dangerous — it guarantees stagnation. Growth happens at the edge of fear. Axe teaches that if you're not regularly experiencing discomfort, you're not growing.

**Case — Business leaps:** Every major business decision Axe made — starting a company, hiring a team, launching a product — came with fear. The fear wasn't a warning; it was confirmation that the decision mattered.

## Principle 7: Persistence Requires Positive Frame

Grit alone leads to burnout. Persistence must be paired with positive mindset — celebrating small wins, maintaining hope, trusting the process even when results are invisible.

**Case — The patient plateau:** Patients who maintained positive persistence through plateaus — trusting their process while celebrating small improvements — eventually broke through. Those who quit at the plateau never saw the breakthrough.

**Case — Business patience:** Building a business takes years. Axe emphasizes that the businesses that succeed are not necessarily the best ideas — they're the ones whose leaders could persist positively through the long valley between launch and success.
