# Practical Techniques

## Overview

Axe provides practical strategies for implementing each mindshift. This file catalogs the most actionable techniques with book cases.

## The Belief Audit

The most important technique in the book: systematically audit your beliefs to identify which are serving you and which are limiting you.

**How to do it:** Write down your beliefs in three categories: about yourself, about other people, about the world. For each belief, ask: (1) Where did this come from? (2) What evidence supports it? (3) Is it helping me or limiting me? (4) What would I believe if I chose differently?

**Case — "People can't change" belief:** Axe held this belief for years, supported by a respected colleague's opinion and personal experiences with someone who refused to change. This was limiting his leadership. He replaced it with "people do change" by finding evidence — stories of transformation from entrepreneurs, athletes, and scientists.

**Case — ADHD self-assessment:** By audited his belief that ADHD meant he was "not smart," Axe identified it as a false belief supported by a teacher's opinion and a medical label. He replaced it with a more accurate belief about his unique processing style.

## The Two-Column Reframe

For any limiting thought, write two columns: "Not That" (the limiting belief) and "Think This" (the empowering alternative).

**How to do it:** When a negative thought arises, write it down. Then write the counter-statement. Example: "Not That: I'm not qualified for this role. Think This: I have the determination and ability to learn what I don't know."

**Case — Medical school entrance:** Axe could have thought "My GPA is too low for med school." The reframe: "My GPA reflects my past effort, not my future potential. I can improve."

**Case — Business pitch:** Instead of "I'm not experienced enough to pitch this investor," think "I have a unique perspective and deep knowledge of this problem."

## The Visualization Protocol

Visualization is not passive daydreaming — it's active mental rehearsal. The brain cannot distinguish between vividly imagined success and the real thing.

**How to do it:** Find a quiet space. Close your eyes. Imagine your desired outcome in vivid detail — what you see, hear, feel, smell, and taste. Engage all senses. Run the "movie" of success from start to finish. Repeat daily.

**Case — Athlete mental rehearsal:** Olympic athletes visualize their performance in complete sensory detail before competition. The brain rehearses the neural pathways, making the physical execution more reliable.

**Case — Health visualization:** Axe's patients who visualized themselves healthy — seeing themselves with energy, at a healthy weight, enjoying activities — had better outcomes. The visualization primed their brains and bodies for success.

## The Virtue-Building Method

Instead of fighting vices directly (which creates resistance and frustration), build the opposing virtue. Virtues naturally crowd out vices.

**How to do it:** Identify a vice you want to eliminate. Identify the opposing virtue. Set small daily goals to practice the virtue. Reinforce yourself when you practice it. Don't focus on the vice — focus on the virtue.

**Case — Diligence over laziness:** Instead of fighting laziness ("I need to stop being lazy"), build diligence. Start with one small diligent action per day — making your bed, arriving five minutes early, completing one task before relaxing. Diligence becomes a habit that makes laziness unnatural.

**Case — Integrity over dishonesty:** Instead of fighting dishonesty ("I need to stop lying"), build integrity. Practice radical honesty in small things. When the small things are true, the big things naturally follow.

## The Principle Filter

Before making decisions, filter them through your chosen principles rather than through others' opinions.

**How to do it:** Write your core principles. Before decisions, ask: "Which option aligns with my principles?" Not: "What will people think?" This creates consistent, integrity-based decision-making.

**Case — Media consumption:** Axe recommends regularly evaluating your media diet. Ask: "Does this content align with my principles? Is it helping me become who I want to be?" If not, eliminate or reduce it.

**Case — Career decisions:** When choosing between opportunities, don't ask "Which pays more?" or "Which looks better on a resume?" Ask: "Which opportunity aligns with my values?" This leads to more fulfilling choices.

## The Purpose Statement

A written purpose statement provides direction and motivation. It turns vague aspirations into a compass.

**How to do it:** Complete: "My purpose is to [action] for [whom] by [how]." Keep it to one sentence. Read it daily. Use it to evaluate opportunities and decisions.

**Case — Patient motivation:** Patients with a clear purpose ("I want to be healthy enough to play with my grandchildren") maintained their health habits. Those without a purpose ("I should lose weight") lacked staying power.

**Case — Business mission:** Companies with clear missions outperform those without. The mission provides direction when tactical decisions are ambiguous.

## The Fear Flip

When fear arises, don't interpret it as a stop sign. Interpret it as a growth indicator.

**How to do it:** When you feel fear, pause and say: "Fear means this is important." Ask: "What would I do if I weren't afraid?" Then do that thing, with courage.

**Case — Fear and opportunity:** Axe found that the biggest regrets in his patients' and clients' lives were not failures but missed opportunities. The fear that stopped them wasn't protecting them — it was robbing them.

**Case — Small fears, big growth:** Start with small fears (asking for a raise, having a difficult conversation, volunteering for a stretch assignment). Each small fear faced builds the muscle for bigger ones.
