# Core Framework: The 12 Mindshifts

## Overview

Josh Axe's framework for personal transformation is built around 12 specific mindshifts — conscious shifts from limiting beliefs to empowering ones. Each mindshift follows the same structure: identify the false belief (the "not that"), understand the evidence supporting it, challenge it, and replace it with a truthful, empowering belief (the "think this").

The book is organized into 12 chapters, each covering one mindshift, with science, stories, and strategies. The framework draws from Axe's work as a functional medicine physician (seeing how mindset affected physical health), as an entrepreneur (seeing how mindset affected team performance), and as a student of leadership at Johns Hopkins.

## The 12 Mindshifts

### Mindshift 1: Create a Breakthrough by Unlimiting Your Beliefs
**Not That:** "I can't because..."  
**Think This:** "I can because..."

Beliefs are the single greatest determining factor of your future. They influence whether you become healthy or unhealthy, enjoy loving relationships or remain stuck, live with significance or wander without purpose. Limiting beliefs cap potential. Unlimited beliefs expand it.

**Case — Josh Axe's F to MD journey:** After his freshman English teacher laughed at his dream of becoming a doctor and he was diagnosed with ADHD, Axe believed he was "not smart." He replaced this belief with evidence of his own capability — studying harder, finding strategies that worked for his brain, and proving his ability step by step.

**Case — Belief legs:** A belief is like a tabletop supported by legs. Each leg is an event, experience, or opinion that reinforces the belief. To change a belief, you must identify and remove the false legs while building new ones that support the truth.

### Mindshift 2: Redefine Success by Becoming, Not Accomplishing
**Not That:** "My value comes from what I achieve."  
**Think This:** "My value comes from who I become."

External markers of success (money, status, titles) are temporary and can disappear overnight. Character, virtues, and personal growth are the only things you carry with you. The person you become in pursuit of a goal is more important than the goal itself.

**Case — Entrepreneurial mindset:** Team members who pursued goals while focused on personal development outperformed those focused only on outcomes. They built skills, character, and resilience; the outcome-focused group burned out.

**Case — Medical patients:** Patients with an "all-in, positive, hopeful mindset" reversed disease and achieved lasting health changes. Those focused only on the outcome (losing weight, lowering blood pressure) without becoming a healthier person relapsed.

### Mindshift 3: Become Self-Aware to Get Where You Need to Go
**Not That:** "I know what I'm doing."  
**Think This:** "I need to understand myself better."

Self-awareness is the foundation of all growth. You cannot change what you don't see. Honest self-assessment reveals patterns, blind spots, and areas where your behavior doesn't align with your values.

**Case — ADHD as a different operating system:** Axe could have let his ADHD diagnosis define him as broken. Instead, self-awareness helped him recognize it as a different way of processing. He built systems around his strengths rather than fighting his weaknesses.

**Case — Leadership growth:** Effective leaders invest heavily in self-awareness through feedback, reflection, and assessment tools. The absence of self-awareness is the most common cause of leadership derailment.

### Mindshift 4: Find a Why or Risk Wasting It All
**Not That:** "I'm just going through the motions."  
**Think This:** "I have a purpose that gives meaning to my efforts."

A compelling "why" provides direction, motivation, and resilience. Without it, effort is scattered, motivation fades when things get hard, and life feels meaningless. Purpose is the engine of perseverance.

**Case — Patients without purpose:** Axe's patients who lacked a compelling reason to get healthy stayed sick. Those who found a why — a child to live for, a dream to pursue — found the motivation to change permanently.

**Case — Meaningful work:** Teams with a clear sense of purpose outperform those motivated only by compensation. Purpose converts work from obligation to mission.

### Mindshift 5: Rewrite Your Role in the Story
**Not That:** "I am what happened to me."  
**Think This:** "I am the author of my own story."

The narratives you tell about your past powerfully shape your future. But these stories are choices — you can rewrite them. Your past events are fixed; the meaning you assign to them is not.

**Case — Failure as education:** Instead of defining himself by his failed grades and teacher's mockery, Axe rewrote the narrative: the teacher's doubt became fuel, not evidence. The same events, interpreted differently, led to completely different outcomes.

**Case — Identity stories:** Ask: "What story am I telling about myself?" If the story is "I'm unlucky," "I'm not the type who succeeds," or "People let me down," challenge it. Is it true? Is it helpful? What story would serve you better?

### Mindshift 6: Assemble a Team to Fulfill Your Dreams
**Not That:** "I have to do this alone."  
**Think This:** "I need a team to reach my potential."

No one achieves greatness in isolation. You need mentors, accountability partners, collaborators, and a community that shares your values. Individual effort has limits; collaborative effort has none.

**Case — Business building:** Axe built multiple multimillion-dollar businesses. Each required assembling a team of people who shared his vision and complemented his weaknesses. The businesses that grew fastest had the strongest teams.

**Case — Support systems:** Patients who had support — family, friends, coaches, community — succeeded in health transformations at much higher rates than those who tried alone. Humans are wired for connection; leverage it.

### Mindshift 7: Build an Unshakable Identity
**Not That:** "I am what others say I am."  
**Think This:** "I am who I choose to be."

If your identity depends on others' opinions, you'll be unstable. Criticism will shake you; praise will swell you. A strong identity is built from within — on values, principles, and chosen commitments that don't change based on external feedback.

**Case — External validation trap:** Axe describes how social media, workplace politics, and family expectations can pull identity in contradictory directions. The solution: define your core identity independently of these forces.

**Case — Identity and habits:** Your identity reinforces your habits and vice versa. If you identify as "a healthy person," healthy choices become natural. If you identify as "someone who can't stick to a diet," you won't. Choose your identity carefully.

### Mindshift 8: Bust Vices by Building Virtues
**Not That:** "I need to stop this bad habit."  
**Think This:** "I need to build the opposing virtue."

The most effective way to eliminate a vice is not to fight it directly but to build the virtue that crowds it out. Instead of fighting laziness, build diligence. Instead of fighting dishonesty, build integrity. Virtues naturally displace vices.

**Case — Virtue over willpower:** Willpower is finite; virtue is cultivated. Instead of relying on willpower to resist temptation, build the character trait that makes the temptation irrelevant. Diligent people don't have to fight procrastination — they're too busy working.

**Case — Leadership virtues:** Axe found that the best leaders didn't focus on eliminating their weaknesses. They focused on building virtues that made those weaknesses irrelevant. A leader who builds trust doesn't need to micromanage.

### Mindshift 9: Turn Off Opinions and Turn On Principles
**Not That:** "What will people think?"  
**Think This:** "What do my principles say?"

Living by principles (courage, honesty, compassion, excellence) rather than opinions creates stability and integrity. Principles are internal and chosen. Opinions are external and changeable.

**Case — Media diet:** Axe recommends limiting exposure to external opinions — especially news and social media — that don't align with your principles. The goal is not ignorance but intentional filtering.

**Case — Principled decision-making:** When facing a hard decision, ask: "Which option aligns with my principles?" instead of "Which option will others approve of?" The former leads to integrity; the latter leads to regret.

### Mindshift 10: Flip the Fear to Turn On the Growth
**Not That:** "Fear means I should stop."  
**Think This:** "Fear means I'm growing."

Fear often points to the next area of growth. If something scares you, it's probably important. The key is not to eliminate fear but to reinterpret it as a growth signal.

**Case — Fear as GPS:** Axe suggests treating fear like a GPS that says "growth opportunity ahead." When you feel fear, lean in instead of backing away.

**Case — Comfort zone myth:** The comfort zone is actually a danger zone — staying there ensures you stop growing. Real safety comes from staying adaptable, which requires regular discomfort.

### Mindshift 11: Visualize to Realize
**Not That:** "I'll believe it when I see it."  
**Think This:** "I'll see it when I believe it."

The brain cannot distinguish between vividly imagined success and real success. Visualization primes neural pathways, builds confidence, and increases the likelihood of success.

**Case — Athletes and mental rehearsal:** World-class athletes use visualization to prepare for competition. The brain rehearses the movement, strengthening neural connections without physical practice.

**Case — Patient healing:** Patients who visualized themselves healthy recovered faster than those who didn't. The mind's image of health influenced the body's ability to achieve it.

### Mindshift 12: Power Up Your Potential with Positive Perseverance
**Not That:** "It's too hard, I quit."  
**Think This:** "This is hard, but I'm growing."

Grit alone isn't enough — it must be paired with the right mindset. Positive perseverance means persisting out of hope and belief, not out of fear or obligation.

**Case — Business valleys:** Axe's businesses went through difficult periods. The ones that survived had leaders who persisted positively — acknowledging the difficulty while maintaining faith in the outcome.

**Case — Health plateaus:** Patients who hit plateaus in their recovery and continued with positive persistence eventually broke through. Those who quit at the first plateau never did.
