# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *The Bodies of Others* by Naomi Wolf.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The "Temporary-to-Permanent" Audit

Track any emergency measure: (1) When was it introduced? (2) What was the stated justification? (3) Is that justification still valid today? (4) Who has the authority to end it? (5) Has it been expanded beyond its original scope? If a measure persists longer than its original justification, it's no longer an emergency measure — it's a permanent policy that bypassed democratic process.

> **Case: Canada's Emergency Banking Freeze** (Introduction): The Canadian government announced it could freeze bank accounts without a court order. This was supposed to be a temporary measure targeting trucker protesters. The infrastructure for financial control — once established — would not disappear. Wolf notes: "Representative government was suspended."
> **Key takeaway**: When a temporary measure creates permanent infrastructure, the infrastructure will outlast the crisis.

### 2. The Fear Thermometer

When evaluating a policy or message: (1) Does it invoke existential threat without evidence? (2) Does it demand immediate compliance without debate? (3) Does it frame non-compliance as a moral failure or a danger to others? (4) Does it discourage or suppress questioning? The higher the fear content, the lower the rational content.

### 3. The Language Decoder

When you hear "we must," "everyone agrees," "the science says," "there is no alternative": translate these as "I am asserting authority, not making an argument." When "guidance" is enforced as law, recognize it as an end-run around democratic governance.

> **Case: CDC Guidance as Enforceable Law** (Chapter 2): Throughout the pandemic, CDC "guidance" was cited by schools, businesses, and universities as the basis for mandates. Yet the CDC has no enforcement authority. "Guidance that is enforced is not guidance — it is command without democratic accountability."
> **Key takeaway**: Distinguish between advice and authority. When advice is enforced, it's no longer advice.

### 4. The Community Audit

Identify institutions that abandoned their core mission during the crisis vs. those that maintained it. A church that stopped meeting is different from a church that found creative ways to gather safely. An institution that voluntarily surrenders its purpose has already been defeated.

### 5. The Documentation Practice

Wolf's most practical advice: document everything. Write down what you observe, when, where, and by whom. Records create accountability. When the crisis is over, the documentation will be the evidence that separates what actually happened from the official narrative.
