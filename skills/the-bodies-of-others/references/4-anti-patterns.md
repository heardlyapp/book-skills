# 4 — Anti-Patterns

> Source: *The Bodies of Others* by Naomi Wolf.

## Common Errors

### 1. The "Good Intentions" Fallacy

**The trap:** "The people implementing these measures mean well. Therefore, the measures cannot be harmful."

**Why it fails:** Wolf does not claim that pandemic officials were evil. She claims they were implementing a system that expanded beyond its original purpose. Good intentions do not prevent authoritarian drift. They often enable it — because people who mean well do not question themselves.

> **Case: The Synagogue's Decision** (Chapter 9): The rabbis who closed Wolf's synagogue were not malicious. They were following advice from "epidemiologists." They genuinely believed they were protecting their congregation. But their actions — keeping the sanctuary closed indefinitely, refusing alternatives like outdoor worship — destroyed the community they were trying to protect. Good intentions do not prevent bad outcomes.
> **Key takeaway**: The road to authoritarian control is paved with good intentions. Question outcomes, not just intentions.

> **Case: Chrystia Freeland's Transformation** (Introduction): Wolf knew Freeland as a fellow journalist. Years later, as Canadian Deputy PM, Freeland authorized bank account freezes without court orders — a power that would normally require emergency legislation. People who start as colleagues in a free society can become instruments of authoritarian control without ever considering themselves authoritarian.
> **Key takeaway**: Watch what people do, not who they used to be. Institutions and individuals change under pressure.

### 2. Normalizing the Exception

**The trap:** "This is just temporary. We'll go back to normal afterward."

**Why it fails:** When people accept restrictions long enough, they stop seeing them as restrictions. The "New Normal" becomes simply "normal." Wolf documents this at the level of language, behavior, and institutional structure.

### 3. Surrendering Judgment to "Experts"

**The trap:** "I'm not a scientist. I have to trust the experts."

**Why it fails:** Wolf argues that in a democracy, citizens are not required to surrender their judgment to any authority — including scientific authority. Experts advise. Citizens decide. The pandemic inverted this relationship, with "the science says" becoming a conversation-stopper.

### 4. The Fragmentation Trap

**The trap:** "I'm just one person. I can't make a difference."

**Why it fails:** Wolf's resistance stories are about ordinary people: the therapist who documented harms, the synagogue member who suggested alternatives, the workers who refused to enforce mandates. Fragmented individuals who resist together are the foundation of democratic resilience.

### 5. Mistaking Compliance for Safety

**The trap:** "If I follow all the rules, I will be safe."

**Why it fails:** Compliance does not guarantee safety, especially when rules are not based on evidence. Wolf documents the mental and physical health harms of isolation, the economic devastation of lockdowns, and the spiritual death of closed communities. Following the rules faithfully may have caused more harm than the virus.
