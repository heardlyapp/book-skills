# Franklin's Life

> Source: Benjamin Franklin, Chapters 1-3, 16

## Core Framework

### The Arc

| Period | Years | Key Achievement |
|--------|-------|----------------|
| Boston | 1706-1723 | Apprentice printer, runaway at 17 |
| Philadelphia | 1723-1757 | Printer, publisher, public citizen |
| London | 1757-1775 | Agent for colonies, became rebel |
| Paris | 1776-1785 | Secured French alliance, negotiated peace |
| Philadelphia | 1785-1790 | Constitutional Convention, elder statesman |

> **Case: The Arrival in Philadelphia (Chapter 1)**
> Franklin's arrival in Philadelphia at age 17 is one of the most famous scenes in American autobiography. He was bedraggled, dirty, and hungry. He bought three puffy rolls, walked up Market Street with one under each arm, eating the third. He passed the house of his future wife, Deborah Read, who saw him and thought he looked "most awkward ridiculous." Franklin wrote this scene himself, as an old man, pretending it was a letter to his son. The scene is carefully constructed to show: I started with nothing. I made myself.
> **Key takeaway:** Franklin understood the power of narrative. He knew his life was a story — and he told it to teach others. The lesson: your story is your most valuable asset. Tell it well.

> **Case: Franklin's Death (Chapter 16)**
> Franklin died on April 17, 1790, at age 84. 20,000 people attended his funeral. He was the most famous American in the world. His epitaph, written by himself: "The body of B. Franklin, Printer, lies here, like the cover of an old book, its contents torn out and stripped of its lettering and gilding, but the work shall not be lost, for it will appear once more in a new and more elegant edition, corrected and improved by the Author." He remained a printer to the end.
> **Key takeaway:** Franklin's self-written epitaph captures his character: practical, witty, self-deprecating, and optimistic. He believed in improvement — even after death. The lesson: know who you are and write your own story.
