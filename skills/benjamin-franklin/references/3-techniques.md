# Diplomacy and Revolution

> Source: Benjamin Franklin, Chapters 10-15

## The French Alliance

### Franklin in Paris

| Challenge | Franklin's Solution |
|-----------|-------------------|
| France was monarchy, America was democracy | Presented America as noble, natural, virtuous |
| French court preferred intrigue | Franklin played the simple American |
| Need for military alliance | Used his fame and charm |

> **Case: Franklin in Paris (Chapters 13-14)**
> Franklin arrived in Paris in 1776, at age 70. He was already the most famous American in Europe — the scientist who had tamed lightning. The French were fascinated by him. He played the role perfectly: he wore a simple fur cap instead of a wig, projecting the image of the natural American philosopher. He was witty, charming, and politically astute. He secured the French alliance in 1778. Without French money, troops, and navy, the American Revolution would have failed.
> **Key takeaway:** Franklin's diplomacy was a performance. He knew the French expected the simple American, so he gave them the simple American. His fur cap was a costume. His plainness was strategic. The lesson: understand what others expect of you — and use it.

> **Case: The Treaty of Paris (1783) (Chapter 15)**
> Franklin, along with John Adams and John Jay, negotiated the Treaty of Paris that ended the Revolutionary War. Franklin was 77. He was charming, patient, and strategically brilliant. The treaty was remarkably favorable to America: recognition of independence, territory to the Mississippi, fishing rights. Franklin's years of building relationships in France paid off.
> **Key takeaway:** The Treaty of Paris was the most important diplomatic achievement of the American Revolution. Franklin was its architect. The lesson: patience in diplomacy is a virtue. Build relationships before you need them.
