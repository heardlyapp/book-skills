# Science and Invention

## The Practical Genius

> **Case: The Kite and Key (Chapter 6)**
> In June 1752, Franklin conducted his most famous experiment. He flew a kite into a thunderstorm, with a key tied to the string. Electricity from the lightning traveled down the wet string to the key. Franklin touched the key and felt the shock. He proved that lightning was electricity. The experiment was incredibly dangerous — if the kite had been struck directly, Franklin would have been killed. But it worked. It made him the most famous scientist in the world.
> **Key takeaway:** The kite experiment is Franklin's defining moment. It shows his combination of curiosity, courage, and practicality. He did not just want to understand lightning — he wanted to control it. The lightning rod was born.

> **Case: The Franklin Stove (Chapter 4)**
> Franklin invented a new kind of stove that produced more heat with less smoke. He could have patented it and made a fortune. He refused. "As we enjoy great advantages from the inventions of others," he wrote, "we should be glad of an opportunity to serve others by any invention of ours." He gave his invention freely to the public.
> **Key takeaway:** Franklin's refusal to patent his stove shows his philosophy: knowledge should be shared. The lesson: not everything worth doing needs to be monetized. Generosity has its own rewards.
