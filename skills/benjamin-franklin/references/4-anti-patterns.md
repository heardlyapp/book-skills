# Anti-Patterns — Franklin's Contradictions

> Source: Benjamin Franklin, all chapters

## The Five Contradictions

### 1. The Slaveholder Who Freed Slaves
**Error**: "Franklin was a consistent advocate of freedom."
**Reality**: Franklin owned slaves early in his life, ran a newspaper that carried slave ads, and was late to the abolitionist cause. He eventually freed his slaves and became a president of the Pennsylvania Abolition Society. But his record is mixed.

### 2. The Loyalist Son
**Error**: "Franklin's family was unified in the Revolution."
**Reality**: Franklin's beloved son, William, was a Loyalist — the royal governor of New Jersey. They never reconciled. William chose the British side. Ben chose the American side. The split destroyed their relationship.

### 3. The Pragmatist Who Compromised Too Much
**Error**: "Franklin always stood on principle."
**Reality**: Franklin compromised constantly. He believed in getting things done, even if it meant accepting imperfect solutions.

### 4. The Self-Promoter
**Error**: "Franklin was genuinely humble."
**Reality**: Franklin pretended to be humble. He was actually a master self-promoter. He knew the value of his own reputation.

### 5. The Misogynist?
**Error**: "Franklin respected women."
**Reality**: Franklin's relationships with women were complex. He had affairs. His wife Deborah was left behind in Philadelphia for years while he lived in London and Paris.

> **Case: William Franklin, the Loyalist Son (Chapters 6-7, 16)**
> Franklin's relationship with his son William is the most painful story in the book. William was Ben's beloved illegitimate son. Ben raised him, educated him, and helped him become royal governor of New Jersey. When the Revolution came, William chose the British side. Ben chose the American. They never reconciled. When Ben died, he left William almost nothing in his will — just some books. The family was broken by politics.
> **Key takeaway:** The Franklin family split shows: even the most rational man can be destroyed by family conflict. The lesson: politics can break families. Choose your battles carefully.

> **Case: Franklin and Slavery (Chapters 2, 16)**
> Franklin's record on slavery is complex. He owned slaves, ran advertisements for slave sales in his newspaper, and was slow to embrace abolition. But in his later years, he became a committed abolitionist. In 1787, he became president of the Pennsylvania Abolition Society. He petitioned Congress to end slavery in 1790 — the year he died. He evolved.
> **Key takeaway:** Franklin's evolution on slavery shows: people can change. It is never too late to change your mind. The lesson: judge Franklin by his full life, not just his early years.
