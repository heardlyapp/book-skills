# Anti-Patterns

## 1. Avoiding Disagreement

**The mistake**: Building a team of people who agree with you.

**Fix**: Lincoln's team of rivals shows that productive disagreement produces better decisions.

## 2. Hiding Weaknesses

**The mistake**: Pretending to be perfect.

**Fix**: Lincoln openly discussed his depression. TR talked about his childhood asthma. FDR's polio was visible. Their vulnerability made them more relatable, not less.

> **Case: Lincoln's Melancholy** (Chapter 1): Lincoln didn't hide his depression. He spoke about it, wrote about it, and developed a deep understanding of human suffering. His willingness to acknowledge his struggles made him more human — and more trusted.
> **Key takeaway**: Vulnerability is not weakness in a leader. It's the foundation of trust.

## 3. Rigid Leadership

**The mistake**: Using the same leadership style in every situation.

**Fix**: Lincoln was patient; TR was aggressive; FDR experimented; LBJ persisted. Each adapted to the situation.

## 4. Ignoring Communication

**The mistake**: Assuming people will understand your vision without explanation.

**Fix**: FDR's fireside chats, Lincoln's letters, TR's speaking tours — great leaders communicate relentlessly.

> **Case: LBJ's Treatment of Staff** (Chapter 4): Lyndon Johnson was famously brutal to his staff — screaming, humiliating, demanding. This approach worked for passing legislation but destroyed relationships. His staff was terrified, not inspired.
> **Key takeaway**: Effectiveness in one dimension (passing laws) doesn't excuse toxic behavior in another. Leadership technique matters on all fronts.
