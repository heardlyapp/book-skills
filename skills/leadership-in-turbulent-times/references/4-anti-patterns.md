# Anti-Patterns — How Not to Lead

> Source: Leadership, Part III, Chapters 9-12, Epilogue

## The Four Styles and Their Pitfalls

### 1. Transformational Leadership (Lincoln)
**Success**: The Emancipation Proclamation reframed the Civil War as moral struggle.
**Pitfall**: Moral certainty can become self-righteousness. Lincoln avoided this through humility.

### 2. Crisis Management (TR)
**Success**: The 1902 Coal Strike settlement — first time a president intervened in a labor dispute.
**Pitfall**: Activism can become micromanagement. TR sometimes got lost in the details.

### 3. Turnaround Leadership (FDR)
**Success**: The Hundred Days (1933) — 15 major bills passed in 100 days.
**Pitfall**: Speed can sacrifice quality. Some New Deal programs were poorly designed.

### 4. Visionary Leadership (LBJ)
**Success**: Civil Rights Act of 1964, Voting Rights Act of 1965.
**Pitfall**: Vision without ethics — LBJ's achievements in domestic policy were shadowed by Vietnam.

> **Case: Lincoln and the Emancipation Proclamation** (Chapter 9)
> Lincoln waited — longer than his abolitionist allies wanted. He issued the Emancipation Proclamation only after the Union victory at Antietam in September 1862. He timed it politically, not morally. The Proclamation freed slaves only in Confederate states, not border states. It kept the border states in the Union. It made the war about slavery, which energized the North and deterred European support for the Confederacy. Lincoln's moral purpose was absolute — but his tactics were incremental.
> **Key takeaway:** Transformational leadership is not about grand gestures. It is about doing the right thing at the right time. Lincoln's moral clarity combined with tactical patience is the model.

> **Case: TR and the Coal Strike** (Chapter 10)
> In 1902, the anthracite coal strike threatened to bring the Northeast to a freeze. The mine owners refused to negotiate. TR threatened to seize the mines with federal troops. He invited both sides to the White House. He publicly shamed the owners. He created an arbitration commission. The strike was settled. The precedent: the president could intervene in labor disputes. The lesson: crisis management requires creativity — TR invented a solution that did not exist in the law.
> **Key takeaway:** When the existing rules do not work, a leader must make new rules. TR's willingness to push beyond established boundaries was the key to resolving the crisis. But this power must be used carefully — the same creativity could become dangerous without moral constraint.
