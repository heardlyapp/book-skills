# Adversity and Growth

> Source: Leadership, Part II, Chapters 5-8

## Four Setbacks, Four Responses

### The Crucible

| President | Adversity | Age | Response |
|-----------|-----------|-----|----------|
| Lincoln | Depression after career collapse | 26 | Immersed himself in work and learning |
| TR | Mother and wife died same day | 25 | Withdrew to the Badlands, then returned |
| FDR | Polio, permanently paralyzed | 39 | Refused to accept limitation, rebuilt himself |
| LBJ | Lost Senate election | 40 | Reeling for years, then heart attack repurposed him |

> **Case: FDR's Polio** (Chapter 7)
> In 1921, at age 39, Franklin D. Roosevelt was struck by polio. He never walked again. His mother urged him to retire to Hyde Park. His political career seemed over. Instead, FDR spent seven years rebuilding: learning to swim for strength, developing a new gait with braces and canes, and studying the country's problems from a wheelchair. His wife Eleanor traveled for him, becoming his eyes and ears. When he ran for governor of New York in 1928 and won, he proved that his body did not define him. His response to polio — "Above all, try something" — became his governing philosophy.
> **Key takeaway:** FDR's polio was not a limitation — it was a liberation. It forced him to rely on others, to delegate, to listen. The man who entered the White House in 1933 was not the same man who had been struck by polio in 1921. The illness made him a better leader.

> **Case: LBJ's Lost Senate Race** (Chapter 8)
> Lyndon Johnson lost the 1941 Texas Senate special election by a narrow margin — amid reports of fraud on both sides. The loss destroyed him. For years afterward, he was a different man: angry, bitter, reluctant to take risks. Then in 1955, at age 46, he suffered a massive heart attack. Lying in a hospital bed, he confronted his mortality. He decided he had been chasing the wrong things. After recovery, he was more focused, more strategic, and more willing to use his power for substantive change. The heart attack repurposed his ambition.
> **Key takeaway:** Sometimes it takes a near-death experience to clarify what matters. LBJ's heart attack transformed him from a man driven by ambition to a man driven by purpose. The difference was visible to everyone who knew him.
