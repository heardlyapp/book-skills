# Ambition and the Recognition of Leadership

> Source: Leadership, Part I, Chapters 1-4

## How Leaders Find Their Calling

Each of the four men discovered their vocation in politics early. Each had a fierce ambition that drove them forward.

### The Engine of Ambition

| President | Source of Ambition |
|-----------|------------------|
| Lincoln | "Every man is said to have his peculiar ambition" — he wanted to matter |
| TR | Born to privilege but driven to prove himself beyond his name |
| FDR | Followed his cousin Theodore, but found his own path |
| LBJ | "A steam engine in pants" — relentless, driven, consuming |

> **Case: Lincoln's First Political Speech** (Chapter 1)
> In 1832, at age 23, Lincoln announced his candidacy for the Illinois State Legislature. His campaign circular was remarkable: he admitted he had no wealth, no influential friends, and no formal education. He promised only "an unshaken confidence in the people." He lost. But he had announced himself to the world. Two years later he ran again and won. He had found his calling.
> **Key takeaway:** Lincoln's first campaign was a failure — but the failure was the beginning. He learned from it. He persevered. The difference between leaders and non-leaders is not the number of failures — it is the response to them.

> **Case: LBJ's Early Hunger** (Chapter 4)
> Lyndon Johnson's ambition was legendary. As a young congressional aide in Washington, he was described as having "a steam engine in pants." He worked 18-hour days, studied every member of Congress, memorized the rules, and learned who held power. He exhausted his staff. He exhausted himself. But within a decade, he had gone from congressional aide to the youngest Senate Majority Leader in history. His ambition was visible, almost uncomfortable — and it worked.
> **Key takeaway:** Ambition that is visible can be uncomfortable for others — but it is honest. LBJ never pretended he did not want power. He wanted it, and he used it to do things. The question Goodwin asks: did he use power for purpose or for ego?
