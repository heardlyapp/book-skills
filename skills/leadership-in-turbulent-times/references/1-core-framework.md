# Core Framework: Four Presidents, One Pattern

## The Central Insight

"Leadership is not something you're born with — it's something you develop through adversity, reflection, and practice." Doris Kearns Goodwin's book traces how four very different presidents — Lincoln, Theodore Roosevelt, FDR, and Lyndon Johnson — each developed their leadership abilities through early struggles and crises.

> **Case: Lincoln's Depression** (Chapter 1): Abraham Lincoln suffered from profound depression throughout his life. His melancholia could have paralyzed him. Instead, he developed extraordinary emotional resilience and empathy. His experience of deep suffering made him able to hold the nation together during its darkest hour.
> **Key takeaway**: Lincoln's greatest leadership strength — empathy — was forged in his deepest struggle. Adversity doesn't have to be hidden; it can be transformed into strength.

## The Four Leadership Styles

| President | Key Strength | Early Adversity | Crisis Leadership |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Lincoln | Emotional intelligence | Depression, poverty | Civil War |
| TR | Energy and action | Severe asthma | Trust-busting, Panama Canal |
| FDR | Experimentation and communication | Polio | Great Depression, WWII |
| LBJ | Political mastery | Poverty, humiliation | Civil Rights, Vietnam |

> **Case: Roosevelt's Polio** (Chapter 3): Franklin Roosevelt was paralyzed by polio at age 39. Many assumed his political career was over. Instead, he rebuilt his life, learned to appear standing (with leg braces and an aide's arm), and developed a depth of empathy that defined his presidency. Polio didn't end his career — it transformed it.
> **Key takeaway**: Setbacks can be reframed as transformations. FDR's polio gave him a patience and determination he didn't have before.
