# 4 — Anti-Patterns

> Source: *Energy and Civilization* by Vaclav Smil.

## Common Errors

### 1. Believing Energy Transitions Are Fast and Clean

**The trap:** "We transitioned from horses to cars in 20 years. Why can't we do the same with energy?"

**Why it fails:** Cars replaced horses for personal transport, but horses were not replaced as an energy source — they were replaced as a technology. Energy sources (fuels, electricity) operate on different timescales. The infrastructure for coal and oil — mines, wells, refineries, pipelines, power plants — represents trillions of dollars of sunk investment that does not disappear quickly.

> **Case: The Horse-to-Car Analogy (Chapter 7):** Smil points out that the horse-to-car transition is often cited as an example of rapid change. But horses never powered civilization's energy system. They were a niche technology for personal transport. The energy system — fuels, electricity, heat — is orders of magnitude larger and slower to change.
> **Key takeaway**: Don't confuse a technology transition (horses to cars) with an energy transition (wood to coal). They operate on completely different scales and timescales.

> **Case: The 3,000-Year Biomass Dominance (Chapter 7):** For most of human history, the primary energy source was the same: biomass (wood, crop residues, dung). It took thousands of years for coal to attain a significant share. The shift from biomass to fossil fuels is still incomplete in many parts of the world — 2.5 billion people still rely on traditional biomass for cooking. Energy transitions are not fast.
> **Key takeaway**: The time horizon of energy change is not years or decades. It is generations and centuries.

**The trap:** "Solar and wind are clean and free. Why not use them for everything?"

**Why it fails:** Every energy source has trade-offs. Solar and wind have low power density (need large land areas), low EROEI (5-10:1 vs 20-30:1 for modern oil), high intermittency (need storage or backup), and embedded energy costs in manufacturing. They are not "free" — they are different costs.

### 3. Ignoring Infrastructure Inertia

**The trap:** "New technology X is cheaper than coal. It will replace coal quickly."

**Why it fails:** Existing infrastructure has decades of useful life left. Coal plants built in 2005 will run until 2045 regardless of solar costs. Pipelines, refineries, and gas networks are built for specific fuels. The inertia of existing infrastructure is one of the strongest forces in energy transitions.

### 4. The "Silver Bullet" Fallacy

**The trap:** "Fusion / hydrogen / carbon capture / next-gen solar will solve everything."

**Why it fails:** Smil's entire book is a warning against expecting single technologies to transform energy systems. No single source has ever "solved everything" — each addition brings its own trade-offs. The future is always a mix.

### 5. Confusing Energy with Power

**The trap:** "The world consumes X terawatt-hours of electricity per year. Let me think about that in terms of power."

**Why it fails:** Energy (kWh, Joules) is stored work. Power (kW, Watts) is the rate of flow. Confusing them leads to mistakes in sizing energy systems. A wind farm may have a capacity of 100 MW (power) but generate only 250,000 MWh/year (energy) — because the wind doesn't blow all the time. The capacity factor (~25-35%) is the bridge between power and energy.
