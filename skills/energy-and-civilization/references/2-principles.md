# 2 — Key Principles

> Source: *Energy and Civilization* by Vaclav Smil.

## The 7 Principles

### 1. Energy Is the Only Universal Currency

Everything humans do requires energy conversion. Food, shelter, transportation, communication, manufacturing — all are energy transformations. Understanding energy flows is the most fundamental way to understand civilization.

### 2. Energy Density Determines Possibility

What a society can do is constrained by the energy density of its fuels. Wood limited preindustrial societies to local economies. Coal enabled industrialization. Oil enabled globalization. Each step up in density opened new possibilities.

### 3. Energy Transitions Take Generations

> **Case: Coal's 100-Year Rise (Chapter 7):** Coal took from 1700 to 1800 to reach 5% of global energy, and from 1800 to 1900 to reach 50%. The transition from wood to coal took a full century to become dominant. Expecting renewables to achieve a similar transition in 30 years is historically unprecedented.
> **Key takeaway**: A century is the relevant timescale for energy transitions. Not a decade.

> **Case: The Haber-Bosch Process (Chapter 6):** Half the world's food production depends on synthetic nitrogen fertilizers made via the energy-intensive Haber-Bosch process using natural gas. Without this fossil fuel input, 4 billion people could not be fed. The energy dependency of modern civilization is hidden in every calorie.
> **Key takeaway**: The energy that fuels civilization is not just in power plants. It is in every loaf of bread.

### 4. EROEI Determines Viability

Energy Returned On Energy Invested is the critical metric. Early oil: EROEI of 100:1. Modern oil: 20-30:1. Solar PV: 5-10:1. Corn ethanol: <1.5:1. Below 5:1, the surplus available for civilization shrinks significantly.

### 5. Qualitative Differences Matter as Much as Quantitative

Two fuels with the same MJ/kg can have vastly different utility. British smokeless coal was decisive in maritime dominance. Dry dung burns differently than wood. Location, accessibility, cleanliness, and form factor matter as much as energy content.

### 6. Power Density Explains Land Use

Modern civilization's high power density (500-2000 W/m2 for fossil plants) enables it to occupy a small land footprint. Solar and wind have much lower power density (5-10 W/m2), which means they require 100x more land for the same energy output. This is a physical constraint.

### 7. There Is No Single Solution

Smil is deeply skeptical of silver-bullet thinking. The energy future will be a mix of sources, each with trade-offs. The question is not which source is "best" — it's what mix can support civilization while managing environmental costs.
