# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *Energy and Civilization* by Vaclav Smil.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The Energy Density Comparison Matrix

When evaluating any energy source, compare: (1) Energy density in MJ/kg (or GJ/m3). (2) Power density in W/m2 of land use. (3) EROEI (energy returned on energy invested). (4) Carbon intensity (gCO2 per kWh or MJ). (5) Intermittency (hours/day, seasonal variation). (6) Geographic constraints. (7) Infrastructure requirements. No single metric tells the whole story.

> **Case: The EROEI Comparison (Chapter 1):** Early oil (1900) had an EROEI of about 100:1 — meaning 100 units of energy returned for every 1 invested in extraction. By 2000, new oil finds in difficult locations (deepwater, oil sands) had an EROEI of 5-15:1. Solar PV has an EROEI of 5-10:1. The implications: as easy oil is depleted, the net energy available to society from each barrel shrinks.

### 2. The Transition Speed Calculator

To estimate transition speed: take the time from 5% to 50% market share for the previous energy source. That is roughly how long the current transition will take — adjusted for scale. Coal: ~100 years. Oil: ~60 years. Renewables: expect similar timelines, not compressed ones.

### 3. The Net Energy Audit

When comparing energy options, always calculate net energy, not gross output. A solar panel may generate 1000 kWh/year, but what was the energy cost of manufacturing, transporting, installing, and maintaining it? The EROEI tells you whether the energy source creates surplus or consumes it.

### 4. The Prime Mover Power Ladder

Human → Draft Animal → Waterwheel → Steam Engine → Steam Turbine: each step up is a roughly 10-1000x increase in available power per unit. Understanding where a society is on this ladder explains what it can and cannot do.

### 5. The Decarbonization Reality Check

> **Case: Global Energy Mix (Chapter 7):** In 2015, fossil fuels provided approximately 85% of global primary energy. Adding nuclear and hydro brings the low-carbon share to about 15%. To reach 50% low-carbon by 2050 would require adding the equivalent of all current nuclear, hydro, and renewable capacity every 7-10 years — while also replacing retiring fossil plants.
