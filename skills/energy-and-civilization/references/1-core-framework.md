# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *Energy and Civilization* by Vaclav Smil — Chapters 1-7.

## The Three Frameworks

### Framework A: Energy Density and Civilization Potential (Chapters 1, 2, 7)

Smil's central argument: the energy density of available fuels determines what a civilization can achieve. Wood (~18 MJ/kg) powered preindustrial societies. Coal (~24-30 MJ/kg) enabled the Industrial Revolution. Oil (~42 MJ/kg) created modern transportation. Each jump in density unlocked new capabilities. The power density per unit of land area is equally critical: preindustrial farming yields 0.1-0.2 W/m2, a natural gas plant delivers 2000+ W/m2.

> **Case: British Smokeless Coal (Chapter 1):** Smil highlights that Britain's dominance in 19th century maritime transport was partly due to its abundant "smokeless" coal — a high-quality fuel with low sulfur content that was ideal for steam engines. France and Germany had coal, but not of the same quality. Identical energy density, but very different utility. The qualitative difference was decisive in world history.

> **Case: Human Bipedalism (Chapter 2):** Smil presents bipedalism as an energy-saving innovation: human walking costs about 75% less energy than quadrupedal walking in chimpanzees. This freed energy for brain development. The human brain uses 20-25% of resting metabolic energy, compared to 8-10% in other primates. Without the energy savings from bipedalism, the large brain could not have evolved.

### Framework B: The Fossil Fuel Revolution (Chapters 5, 6)

The transition from biomass to fossil fuels was the most transformative event in human history. Coal enabled steam engines that multiplied available power a thousandfold. Oil enabled internal combustion engines that made personal transportation possible. Natural gas enabled high-efficiency heating and electricity. Together, they created modern civilization — and its environmental consequences.

> **Case: The Watt Steam Engine (Chapter 5):** James Watt's separate condenser (patented 1769) improved the efficiency of steam engines from less than 1% to about 2-3%. This seems tiny by modern standards, but it was a 3x improvement. More importantly, it made the steam engine economically viable for pumping water out of coal mines — which produced more coal — which powered more steam engines. This positive feedback loop drove the Industrial Revolution.

### Framework C: Energy Transitions — Slow, Messy, Cumulative (Chapter 7)

Smil's most important finding: energy transitions take 50-100 years, no energy source has ever been completely replaced, and each new source is added on top of existing ones. The "age of coal" did not end when oil arrived. The "age of oil" did not end when natural gas grew. Coal still provides ~27% of global primary energy (2020). The pattern is accumulation, not substitution.

> **Case: The 3,000-Year Fuel Share Chart (Chapter 7):** Smil provides a remarkable chart of fuel shares over 3,000 years. Wood dominated for millennia. Coal emerged around 1700 and took until 1900 to become the leading fuel. Oil emerged around 1900 and took until 1960 to surpass coal. Natural gas has been growing since 1950 and is still lower than oil. Each new source adds to, rather than replaces, the existing base. The transition to renewables will follow the same pattern.
