### Philosophy — 5 Rules

1. **The worst thing you can do is not try.** Carter took this from Ecclesiastes. "Have I done my best?" was his daily prayer.
2. **You can lead without being a natural leader.** Carter lacked "command presence" but had vision, competence, and grit. Those are the parts you can control.
3. **Failure is not the end — it's an act break.** Carter's post-presidency was more consequential than many presidencies. The second act depends on what you do after the fall.
4. **Integrity is its own reward — and your long-term asset.** Carter's ethics cost him short-term popularity. They also gave him the Nobel Peace Prize.
5. **Plant trees you will never sit under.** The solar panels on the White House roof were ridiculed. Thirty years later, solar is the fastest-growing energy source.

### Core Framework Quick Reference

- **The Outsider's Paradox** — Carter's outsider status was his greatest asset (campaigning) and his greatest liability (governing).
- **The Moral Imperative** — Carter took on the Panama Canal Treaties knowing they'd cost him politically. "It was the right thing to do."
- **The Engineer's Toolkit** — Deconstruct every problem, know every detail, persist until solved. Camp David was saved by Carter's obsessive attention to every negotiating point.
- **Reinvention as Superpower** — After a humiliating defeat, Carter founded the Carter Center, monitored elections, eradicated guinea worm, and won the Nobel Peace Prize.
- **Plant Trees, Not Campaigns** — Carter's environmentalism, human rights policy, and energy conservation were built for generations, not reelection.

### Key Principles

1. **Do your homework.** Carter knew every detail. Admiral Rickover's question — "Did you do your best?" — drove him for a lifetime.
2. **Don't try to be liked. Try to be right.** Carter never mastered political glad-handing. He mastered policy.
3. **Failure is not permanent.** Losing the presidency was the beginning of Carter's most impactful work.
4. **Personal integrity is the only foundation.** "I did not lie to the American people" was not a slogan. It was the truth.
5. **The best results come from engagement, not orders.** At Camp David, Carter shuttled between cabins for 13 days. He didn't command peace — he negotiated it.
6. **Begin at the beginning.** The solar panels, the Alaska lands, the CDC into global health. Carter was ahead of his time because he started from first principles.
