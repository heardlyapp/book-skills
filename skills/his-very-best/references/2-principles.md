# 2 — Key Principles

> Source: *His Very Best: Jimmy Carter, a Life* by Jonathan Alter — across all chapters.

## The 7 Principles

### 1. Do Your Best — Every Time

Admiral Rickover asked the young Lieutenant Carter: "Did you do your best at the Naval Academy?" Carter confessed he had not. He spent the rest of his life trying never to have to say that again. "Have I done my best?" became his daily prayer.

> **Case: The Chalk River Meltdown** (Chapter 4): Carter was one of only two naval officers qualified to enter the core of the partially melted-down NRX reactor at Chalk River, Ontario. He spent 90 seconds inside, close to lethal radiation, dismantling fuel rods. He described it as "the most terrifying 90 seconds of my life." He went anyway, because Rickover needed data. The experience gave him a lifelong understanding of nuclear energy that no other president has ever had.

### 2. Don't Try to Be Liked. Try to Be Right.

Carter never mastered political glad-handing. He never cared to. His focus was on policy — knowing every detail, making the right decision, accepting the consequences. He lost the popularity contest. He won the historical one.

### 3. Failure is Not Permanent

Losing the presidency by the third-largest margin in history was not the end of Carter — it was the beginning of his most productive period. The Carter Center has eradicated 99.99% of guinea worm disease. That is a bigger impact than most presidents ever achieve.

### 4. Integrity is the Only Foundation

"I did not lie to the American people" was not a campaign slogan. It was a fact. Alter confirms that Carter was the only modern president who could credibly make that claim. That fact became the bedrock of his reputation.

### 5. Engagement Beats Command

Carter didn't issue orders. He negotiated every paragraph of Camp David himself, walking between cabins, carrying messages, refusing to let either party leave. The leader who engages personally earns results that no command can produce.

### 6. Begin at the Beginning

Carter thought from first principles, not from political consensus. Solar energy, human rights, CDC global health, Alaska wilderness — these were not popular positions in the late 1970s. They were correct positions derived from first principles.

### 7. Private Toughness, Public Perception

> **Case: "Jungle Jimmy"** (Chapter 10): Carter was the most publicly "weak" president since Hoover — and one of the toughest in private. As governor, he was called "Jungle Jimmy" for his combative, unyielding style. He took on the Georgia legislature, the county courthouse network, and the segregationists — and won. But in Washington, the public narrative of weakness overwhelmed the private reality of toughness.
> **Key takeaway**: The gap between how you are perceived and how you actually operate is a leadership problem, not a perception problem. If people think you're weak, you must show your toughness publicly.
