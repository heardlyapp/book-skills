# 4 — Anti-Patterns

> Source: *His Very Best: Jimmy Carter, a Life* by Jonathan Alter — Chapters 15-28.

## Common Errors

### 1. Governing Like You Campaign

**The trap:** "The same outsider approach that won me the election will win me the presidency."

**Why it fails:** Carter never learned to work the Washington system. He didn't stroke Congress. He didn't trade favors. He didn't attend the Georgetown dinner parties where deals were made. "The skills that win aren't the skills that lead."

> **Case: The Energy Bill** (Chapter 17): Carter submitted an ambitious energy plan in his first weeks. He knew every detail, could explain every tax credit, understood the technical specifications of every conservation measure. He thought mastery of the subject would carry the bill. It didn't. Congress shredded it. He assumed expertise was enough. It never is.

### 2. Micromanaging the Wrong Things

**The trap:** "If I control every detail, nothing can go wrong."

**Why it fails:** Carter personally scheduled the White House tennis court. He reviewed the schedules for White House tours. He was the same obsessive detail that saved Camp David — deployed on things that didn't matter.

### 3. The "Three Smiles" Problem

**The trap:** "My private self and my public self are the same thing."

**Why it fails:** Alter identifies three distinct Carter smiles — the warm public smile, the tight-lipped smile of suppressed anger, and the relaxed private smile. He too often showed the wrong one. The public saw the tight-lipped version and read it as coldness.

### 4. Moralizing Without Politics

**The trap:** "The truth is enough. The American people will respond to the honest diagnosis."

**Why it fails:** Carter's "malaise" speech (which he never called that) was right about America's crisis of confidence. Delivering it from the Oval Office as a sermon was politically suicidal. The truth without a political strategy is just a lecture.

### 5. The Outsider's Loneliness

Carter had no real allies in Washington. His closest advisor, Hamilton Jordan, once told him: "Mr. President, you are the best politician I have ever seen — on the campaign trail. But you don't have a single real friend in Washington." Carter didn't listen. Outsiders need more allies, not fewer.
