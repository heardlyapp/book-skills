# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *His Very Best: Jimmy Carter, a Life* by Jonathan Alter — practical methods.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The Rickover Question

Before every major decision, ask: "Have I done my best? Is there one more thing I could learn, one more call I should make, one more angle I haven't considered?" Carter asked this daily. The answer kept him honest.

### 2. The Shuttle Negotiation

At Camp David, Carter didn't convene — he shuttled. Walk between camps. Hear each side privately. Carry offers personally. Never give up. The Accords collapsed three times. Each time, Carter got the bodyguards to let him back into the cabins.

> **Case: The "Three Collapses" of Camp David** (Chapter 25): Day 5: Sadat announced he was leaving. Carter went to his cabin and said: "You can't leave. If you leave, you're betraying your people." Day 8: Begin demanded a letter from Congress. Carter personally drafted it on Camp David stationery. Day 11: Both leaders refused to sign. Carter wrote the final wording himself, walked it between cabins, and collected signatures one by one.

### 3. The "Grammarian-in-Chief" Integrity Check

Carter applied a uniquely rigorous standard: not "is this statement defensible?" but "is this statement literally true as written?" He personally edited his speeches to remove any exaggeration, any spin, any language that could not survive fact-checking. "I did not lie to the American people" — the claim is auditable.

### 4. The Post-Defeat Pivot

Carter lost the presidency $1 million in debt. He didn't try to reclaim power. He created new purpose. The formula: identify what you do best (mediation, public health, election monitoring), institutionalize it (the Carter Center), and accept that your legacy will unfold over decades, not news cycles.

### 5. The Solar Panel Signal

In 1979, Carter installed 32 solar panels on the White House roof. They were removed in 1986 when Reagan renovated. The act was symbolic — the panels generated almost no energy — but the signal was intentional: "A generation from now, this solar heater can either be a curiosity, a museum piece, or part of the greatest adventure ever undertaken by the American people." Plant the signal. Let time prove you right.

### 6. The Small Town Return

Carter returned to Plains, Georgia. He taught Sunday school. He lived in the same house. He was rooted. Rootedness is not nostalgia — it's a strategy for staying grounded when the world wants to lift you into irrelevance.
