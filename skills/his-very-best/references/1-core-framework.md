# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *His Very Best: Jimmy Carter, a Life* by Jonathan Alter.

## The Four Frameworks

### Framework A: The Outsider's Paradox

Carter's entire political career was built on being an outsider. At the Naval Academy, he was an outsider. In Plains, he was too liberal for the segregationists. In the governor's race, he ran against "big money boys." In 1976, he was the obscure peanut farmer running 0% in the polls. This worked brilliantly for winning. It failed for governing. He never learned to work the Washington system.

> **Case: The "Georgia Mafia"** (Prologue): Carter brought his inner circle from Georgia — Hamilton Jordan, Jody Powell — not Washington insiders. They were brilliant at campaigning and inept at Washington etiquette. They didn't attend Georgetown dinner parties, didn't stroke congressional egos, didn't trade favors. Carter was proud of this. It was his greatest strength and his greatest weakness.

### Framework B: The Moral Imperative

Carter framed political decisions as moral questions. The Panama Canal Treaties, the Camp David Accords, human rights policy — each was driven by "what is right" rather than "what is politically beneficial."

> **Case: The Panama Canal Treaties** (Chapter 18): Carter knew pushing through the treaties would cost him dearly. Conservatives called it "giving away the Canal." But Carter believed the US had no right to control a waterway through another country. He staked his presidency on it. The treaties passed by one vote. They prevented a war in Central America.

### Framework C: Reinvention as Superpower

After losing to Reagan in a landslide (44 states), Carter was humiliated. He returned to Plains, depressed, $1 million in debt on his peanut farm. Then he founded the Carter Center, monitored elections in 65 countries, nearly eradicated guinea worm, built houses for Habitat for Humanity, and won the Nobel Peace Prize. His post-presidency was more impactful than many presidencies.

### Framework D: Engineer's Persistence

Carter was a nuclear engineer trained by Admiral Rickover. He approached every problem — from a nuclear reactor meltdown cleanup to Middle East peace — as a deconstruction task.
