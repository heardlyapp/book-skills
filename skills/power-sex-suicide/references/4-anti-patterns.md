# Anti-Patterns

## 1. Assuming Mitochondria Are Just Energy Producers

**The mistake**: Thinking mitochondria only make ATP.

**Fix**: Mitochondria also control cell death, produce heat, generate free radicals for signaling, and regulate calcium levels. They are the control center of the cell.

## 2. Taking Antioxidant Supplements

**The mistake**: Believing that high-dose antioxidants will slow aging by eliminating free radicals.

**Why it fails**: Clinical trials show no benefit. Free radicals are essential signaling molecules. Eliminating them with supplements can actually harm the protective hormetic response.

**Fix**: Get antioxidants from whole foods, not supplements.

> **Case: The Antioxidant Supplement Failure** (Chapter 8): Multiple large clinical trials tested vitamin E, beta-carotene, and other antioxidant supplements. None consistently extended lifespan. Some (like high-dose vitamin E) actually increased mortality. The theory was wrong.
> **Key takeaway**: More antioxidants are not better. The body's own antioxidant systems, supported by whole foods and exercise, are more effective than supplements.

## 3. Ignoring the Maternal Inheritance Pattern

**The mistake**: Not understanding that mitochondrial diseases are inherited only from the mother.

**Fix**: If you have a family history of mitochondrial disease, the risk comes from the mother's side.

## 4. Expecting Quick Results from Mitochondrial Interventions

**The mistake**: Trying a supplement or diet for a week and expecting mitochondrial improvement.

**Fix**: Mitochondrial health improves over months and years of consistent lifestyle habits.

> **Case: The Exercise Timeline** (Chapter 10): Significant mitochondrial changes take 6-12 weeks of consistent exercise. A single workout does nothing. A week of training shows minimal change. But after 3 months, mitochondrial density can increase by 20-50%.
> **Key takeaway**: Mitochondrial adaptation is slow but real. Consistency matters more than intensity.

## 5. Thinking All Free Radicals Are Bad

**The mistake**: Treating all oxidative stress as harmful.

**Fix**: Low-level oxidative stress (from exercise, cold exposure, fasting) triggers beneficial adaptations. The dose makes the poison.
