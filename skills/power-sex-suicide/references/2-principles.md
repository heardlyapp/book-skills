# Key Principles

## 1. Mitochondria Were Once Bacteria

"They still have their own DNA, their own ribosomes, and they reproduce independently within your cells." This is the endosymbiotic theory — and it's one of the most important ideas in biology. Your cells are a community of cooperating organisms.

## 2. The Oxygen Trade-Off

"The very process that gives you energy also slowly damages you." Oxidative phosphorylation produces ATP — but also creates free radicals. This unavoidable byproduct is a primary driver of aging.

## 3. Sex Solves a Mitochondrial Problem

"Mitochondrial DNA is inherited only from the mother. This creates a genetic conflict that sexual reproduction may have evolved to manage." The theory: sex allows mixing of nuclear DNA while keeping mtDNA in a single lineage.

> **Case: The Uniparental Inheritance Puzzle** (Chapter 7): Why is mitochondrial DNA inherited only from one parent? In almost all species, sperm mitochondria are destroyed after fertilization. The leading theory: mixing mtDNA from two parents would create genetic conflict. Uniparental inheritance prevents this.
> **Key takeaway**: The seemingly wasteful process of sexual reproduction may have evolved to solve a mitochondrial problem. Sex is not just about mixing genes — it's about managing energy.

## 4. Apoptosis Is the Guardian of Life

"Every cell in your body carries the machinery for its own destruction. Mitochondria control this switch." Apoptosis eliminates cells that are damaged, infected, or no longer needed. When it fails, cancer can result.

## 5. Free Radicals Are Not All Bad

"Free radicals are signaling molecules, not just damage agents." The old view — that free radicals are purely harmful — is too simple. They play essential roles in cellular signaling and adaptation.

> **Case: The Free Radical Paradox** (Chapter 8): Antioxidant supplements have failed to extend lifespan in clinical trials. The reason: low levels of free radicals actually trigger protective responses in cells (hormesis). Exercise creates free radicals — and that's part of why exercise is healthy.
> **Key takeaway**: Don't try to eliminate free radicals entirely. Moderate oxidative stress (from exercise, for example) makes your mitochondria stronger.
