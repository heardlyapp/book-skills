# Core Framework — Obedience to Authority

## Preface: The Dilemma of Obedience

Milgram opens with the central question: "Obedience is as basic an element in the structure of social life as one can point to." Yet "from 1933 to 1945 millions of innocent people were systematically slaughtered on command." How could this happen? "Obedience is the psychological mechanism that links individual action to political purpose. It is the dispositional cement that binds men to systems of authority."

C.P. Snow: "When you think of the long and gloomy history of man, you will find more hideous crimes have been committed in the name of obedience than have ever been committed in the name of rebellion."

## Chapter 1: The Experiment

Two people come to a psychology lab. One is the "teacher" (the real subject), the other the "learner" (an actor). The teacher watches as the learner is strapped into a chair with an electrode on his wrist. The teacher is seated before a shock generator with 30 switches — 15 to 450 volts, marked from "SLIGHT SHOCK" to "DANGER — SEVERE SHOCK."

The teacher is instructed to test the learner on word pairs, increasing the shock for each wrong answer. The learner (who receives no shock) begins to protest: grunting at 75V, complaining at 120V, demanding release at 150V, screaming agonizingly at 285V, and falling silent at 330V.

**The result:** Before the experiment, Milgram surveyed 40 psychiatrists. They predicted that only about 1% of subjects would go to 450V — and that those would be psychopaths. The actual result: 65% of subjects went to the maximum 450 volts. "Ordinary people, simply doing their jobs, and without any particular hostility on their part, can become agents in a terrible destructive process."

## The Variations (Experiments 1-18)

Milgram ran 18 variations to test which factors increased or decreased obedience:

**Proximity:** When the learner was in the same room, obedience dropped to 40%. When the teacher had to physically force the learner's hand onto a shock plate, obedience dropped to 30%.

**Authority:** When the experiment was moved to a non-university setting (a downtown office), obedience dropped to 48%. When the experimenter left the room and gave orders by phone, obedience dropped to 20.5%.

**Peer defiance:** When other "teachers" (actors) refused to continue, the real subject was far more likely to refuse. Only 10% continued to 450V.

**Closeness of authority:** When the experimenter was physically present, obedience was highest (65%). Case: "The physical presence of authority dramatically increased compliance."

## The Agentic State

Milgram's central theoretical concept: "The essence of obedience consists in the fact that a person comes to view himself as the instrument for carrying out another person's wishes, and he therefore no longer regards himself as responsible for his actions."

This "shift" from autonomy to agency is the key psychological mechanism. "Once this critical shift of viewpoint has occurred, all of the essential features of obedience follow." Case: The teacher who said "I'm just doing my job" — this is the agentic state.

## Binding Factors

Why did subjects continue when they clearly wanted to stop? Milgram identified "binding factors" — aspects of the situation that make it difficult to break free: the sense of obligation to the experimenter, the momentum of the procedure, the awkwardness of quitting, the gradual nature of the escalation. "The subject could not quite see how to extricate himself."

## Chapter 14: The Learning of Obedience

Milgram concludes that obedience is not innate — it is learned through socialization. "The individual is shaped by his social environment." But the capacity to resist authority must also be learned. "The capacity to resist authority is not innate — it must be cultivated."

## Implications

Milgram directly connects his findings to Nazi Germany, the My Lai Massacre, and other atrocities. "The key to understanding these events lies not in the personalities of the perpetrators but in the structure of the situation in which they found themselves."
