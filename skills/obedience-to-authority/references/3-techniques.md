# Techniques — Obedience to Authority

## How to Recognize and Resist Unjust Authority (Based on Stanley Milgram)

### Technique 1: The 450V Question
Before any obedience situation, ask yourself: "Where is the 450-volt switch in this situation?" That is: what is the endpoint of this incremental process? Case: The teachers in the experiment never asked what the maximum was before starting. If they had, many might have refused earlier. "Know where you're being led before you take the first step."

### Technique 2: The Proximity Test
The closer you are to the consequences of your actions, the harder it is to obey unjust orders. If you're asked to do something that harms others, make yourself witness the harm. Case: "When the learner was in the same room, obedience dropped from 65% to 40%." The technique: if you must do something questionable, insist on seeing the results.

### Technique 3: The Peer Solidarity Check
Milgram found peer defiance was the most powerful antidote to obedience. When a confederate refused, only 10% of subjects continued. The technique: find at least one other person who shares your doubts. "Do not obey alone."

### Technique 4: The Authority Audit
Ask: "Is this person's authority legitimate? Did I consent to it? Can I withdraw consent?" Milgram found that when the experimenter left the room, obedience halved. Case: "When the experimenter gave orders by phone, compliance dropped dramatically." The technique: question the authority's right to command.

### Technique 5: The Gradual Escalation Watch
Milgram's experiment worked because of 15-volt increments. The technique: when you notice a gradual escalation, pause. Ask: "Would I have agreed to this if I had known where we'd end up?" Case: "Each step was just a little more. No single step demanded a clear moral decision."

### Technique 6: The Responsibility Reclaim
The agentic state is defined by surrendering responsibility. The technique: say aloud: "I am responsible for what I do." Case: Subjects who refused said: "I know it's the experiment, but I can't continue." They reclaimed agency. "You cannot give your conscience to another person."

### Technique 7: The "Two Ears, One Mouth" Rule
Milgram noted that people who protested while obeying (continuing to shock while complaining) were still responsible. The technique: if your actions don't match your words, you're still responsible. Speaking out is not enough — you must act. Case: "The subject who protested even as he flipped the switches was still the one flipping the switches."
