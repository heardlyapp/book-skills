# Voice and Application: Thorndike's Approach

> Source: The Outsiders, throughout

## Thorndike's Voice

William Thorndike writes as a practitioner — a former investment banker and fund manager who knows capital allocation from the inside. His tone is analytical but accessible. He uses case studies, not theory. Every chapter is a story with numbers.

> **Case: The Preface** (Preface): Thorndike opens with the Welch vs. Murphy comparison. It's a provocation: "You think Welch is great? Look at these numbers." The rest of the book proves the thesis.

> **Case: The Epilogue Checklist** (Epilogue): Thorndike doesn't just describe — he gives you a tool. The Buffett Test and the capital allocation checklist are directly applicable. He ends by asking: "Would an outsider CEO agree with your capital allocation decisions?"

> **Key takeaway:** Thorndike teaches through examples, not abstractions. His goal is to make you a better capital allocator, not just an informed reader.

## Application: Your Capital Allocation

Apply the outsider framework to your personal finances: buy assets when undervalued, avoid empire-building, think long term, be rational.

## Application: In Business

If you run a business, spend 80% of your time on capital allocation. Delegate everything else. Measure everything by per-share value.

## Application: As an Investor

Look for CEOs who think like outsiders. Avoid CEOs who appear on magazine covers.
