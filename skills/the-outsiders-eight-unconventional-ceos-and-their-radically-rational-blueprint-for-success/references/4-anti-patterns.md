# Anti-Patterns: What Outsiders Avoid

> Source: The Outsiders, throughout

## Empire-Building

The most destructive CEO behavior: building a larger company regardless of whether it creates value. Outsiders avoided this entirely.

> **Case: The Value-Destroying Acquisition** (Chapter 8): Thorndike contrasts the outsiders with typical CEOs who overpay for acquisitions to increase revenue size. These acquisitions destroy value. The outsiders only acquired when the price was right and the math worked.

> **Key takeaway:** Size is not value. Bigger is not better. The outsiders measured everything by per-share value, not revenue.

## Short-Term Thinking

Wall Street pressure is relentless. Most CEOs cave. The outsiders never did.

> **Case: The Quarterly Earnings Trap** (Chapter 5): Graham faced immense pressure to maintain quarterly earnings growth. She resisted, choosing to invest in long-term projects and buy back stock. The short-term sacrifices produced enormous long-term gains.

> **Key takeaway:** Short-term thinking is the enemy of capital allocation. Outsiders measure success in decades.

## Confusing Activity with Progress

Many CEOs feel compelled to "do something." The outsiders were comfortable doing nothing when nothing was worth doing.

> **Case: Singleton's Waiting Game** (Chapter 2): Singleton sometimes let cash pile up for years when he couldn't find attractively priced acquisitions or buybacks. He was patient. The market rewarded his patience.

> **Key takeaway:** The most important capital allocation decision is often: do nothing. Wait for the right opportunity.
