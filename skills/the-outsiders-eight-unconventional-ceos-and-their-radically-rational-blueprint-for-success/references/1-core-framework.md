# Core Framework: The Outsider CEOs

> Source: The Outsiders, Preface and Introduction

## The Outsider Thesis

Thorndike asks: who are the best CEOs of the last fifty years? Jack Welch is the conventional answer. But Thorndike finds eight lesser-known CEOs who outperformed Welch — often by a wide margin. Their secret: superior capital allocation.

> **Case: The $48 vs. $2,000** (Preface): One dollar invested in GE during Welch's tenure grew to $48 — excellent. One dollar invested in Capital Cities during Tom Murphy's tenure grew to $2,005 — 42x better. Murphy is not a household name. Welch is.

> **Key takeaway:** The best CEOs are not the ones on magazine covers. They are rational capital allocators who compound value quietly.

## Capital Allocation 101

The CEO's most important job: deciding where to deploy the company's cash. Five options: (1) reinvest in operations, (2) acquire other businesses, (3) buy back shares, (4) pay dividends, (5) reduce debt.

> **Case: Tom Murphy's Discipline** (Chapter 1): Murphy ran Capital Cities with fewer than 10 people at headquarters. He spent his time on one thing: capital allocation. He bought ABC at the right price and never overpaid. His subsidiary managers ran their businesses without interference.

> **Key takeaway:** Outsiders focus on capital allocation to the exclusion of almost everything else. They delegate operations completely.

## The Singleton Model

Henry Singleton of Teledyne is Thorndike's favorite outsider. His record: 180x return for shareholders over 30 years.

> **Case: Singleton's Buyback Machine** (Chapter 2): Singleton bought back 90% of Teledyne's outstanding shares over time. He sold stock when it was overvalued to fund acquisitions. He bought stock when it was undervalued. The stock market's irrationality was his greatest asset.

> **Key takeaway:** Singleton viewed the stock market as a tool to exploit, not a master to serve. Buybacks at the right price create enormous value.
