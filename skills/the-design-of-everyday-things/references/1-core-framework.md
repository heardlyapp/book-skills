## The Norman Framework: Understanding Interaction

Norman's framework centers on the seven stages of action — a model of how humans interact with any product. Good design bridges the Gulfs of Execution (between goal and action) and Evaluation (between result and interpretation).

**The Key Insight**: Most design failures happen in the gaps — the Gulf of Execution (user cannot figure out what to do) and the Gulf of Evaluation (user cannot figure out what happened). The designer's job is to bridge these gaps.

> **Case: The Confusing Door** (Chapter 1): Norman describes a door with a flat metal plate. The plate is a signifier that suggests you should push. But the door actually pulls. The result: everyone pushes first, then pulls. The design violated the fundamental principle: the signifier should match the action. This is so common it has a name — a "Norman door."
> **Key takeaway**: When a signifier (flat plate) conflicts with the required action (pull), users will repeatedly make the same error. The problem is not the user — it is the design.

> **Case: The Stove with No Mapping** (Chapter 3): Norman describes a stove with four identical black knobs in a row. The burners are arranged in a square. There is no correspondence between the position of the knobs and the position of the burners. Users must read tiny labels or guess. Good mapping would place the knobs in the same pattern as the burners.
> **Key takeaway**: Natural mapping — controls arranged to match their effects — eliminates the need for labels, memory, or trial-and-error.
