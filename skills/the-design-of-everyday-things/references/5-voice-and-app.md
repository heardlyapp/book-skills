> Source: The Design of Everyday Things, Chapters 1-7

## Author's Voice

Don Norman writes as a cognitive scientist explaining design in terms of human psychology. His tone is analytical, patient, and occasionally wry. He uses examples everyone has experienced (doors, stoves, watches, faucets) to illustrate universal principles. He is clear about who is at fault: the designer, never the user. The book is both a critique of bad design and a guide to making things better.

## Signature Phrases

- "Norman doors" — doors that confuse
- "Affordances and signifiers" — the core concepts
- "Gulf of execution / Gulf of evaluation" — the gaps in interaction
- "The seven stages of action" — the framework
- "Slips vs. mistakes" — types of human error
- "User-centered design" — the methodology

## Application to 5 Scenarios

### Scenario 1: A product designer
**Partial response**: "Start with Norman's principles as a checklist. Does your design have clear affordances? Do the signifiers match? Is there immediate feedback? Run the Gulf Analysis on every interaction."

### Scenario 2: A software engineer building an interface
**Partial response**: "The seven stages of action is your framework. Walk through each stage: can the user form a goal? Plan? Specify? Perform? Perceive? Interpret? Compare? If any stage is unclear, the design needs work."

### Scenario 3: Someone frustrated with a product
**Partial response**: "You are not the problem — the design is. Norman's whole point: when a product is hard to use, it is not the user's fault. The failure belongs to the designer."

### Scenario 4: A manager hiring designers
**Partial response**: "Ask candidates about affordances, signifiers, mapping, and constraints. If they can explain these clearly, they understand design fundamentals. If they cannot, they are not a designer."

### Scenario 5: A student learning design
**Partial response**: "The Design of Everyday Things is the first book every designer should read. It gives you a framework for thinking about design that you will use for your entire career."

> **Case: The London Underground Map** (Chapter 2): Norman cites Harry Beck's 1933 London Underground map as an example of excellent design. The map was not geographically accurate — but it was clear, usable, and easy to follow. Beck sacrificed accuracy for usability. This is the essence of good design: the map works because it is designed for how people use it (to navigate the system), not to be geographically correct.
> **Key takeaway**: The best design sometimes requires sacrificing literal truth for practical usability. The map is successful because it matches how the mind navigates.

> **Case: The Apple iPhone** (Chapter 7, updated edition): Norman praises the iPhone as an example of excellent design: clear signifiers (slide to unlock), immediate feedback (touch response), natural mapping (scroll with finger), and constraints (you cannot insert the SIM card wrong). The iPhone succeeded not because of technology but because of design.
> **Key takeaway**: Technical excellence is not enough. The iPhone's success was built on design principles that made the technology accessible.
