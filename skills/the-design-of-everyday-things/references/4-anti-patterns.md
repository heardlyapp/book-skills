## 6 Anti-Patterns of Bad Design

### 1. "The User Should Read the Manual"

If a product needs a manual to use, the design has failed. The product should communicate its use through its design. A sign or label is already a design failure.

### 2. "The User Error"

When users repeatedly make the same mistake, the problem is not carelessness — it is design. The "stupid user" excuse is the hallmark of bad design.

> **Case: The Three Mile Island** (Chapter 5): Norman discusses the Three Mile Island nuclear accident. The design of the control room was so confusing that operators could not understand what was happening. The operators were trained professionals. The design failed them. The accident was not human error — it was design error.
> **Key takeaway**: When trained professionals cannot use a system correctly, the design is wrong — not the professionals.

### 3. "More Features = Better Product"

Adding features without improving usability creates complexity, not value. Every additional feature should be justified by user need, not technical capability.

### 4. "Beeps and Lights Are Feedback"

Random beeps and flashing lights do not constitute good feedback. Feedback must be informative — telling the user what happened and what to do next.

### 5. "I Know What the User Wants"

Designers who assume they understand the user without testing are dangerous. User-centered design requires iteration, testing, and observation.

### 6. "Consistency Is Boring"

Consistency is not boring — it is essential. Inconsistent design forces users to learn new patterns for each interaction. Consistency makes products learnable.

> **Case: The Voice-Mail System** (Chapter 4): Norman describes a corporate voicemail system with 9 levels of menus. Users could not remember the sequence to perform even basic tasks. The designers assumed users would memorize. They did not. The system was so bad that companies hired "voicemail consultants" to teach employees how to use it. The problem was not the users — it was the design.
> **Key takeaway**: If you need a consultant to explain how to use your product, your design has failed. Complexity is not sophistication.
