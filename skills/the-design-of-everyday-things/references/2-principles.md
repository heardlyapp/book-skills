## 7 Principles of Good Design

### 1. Discoverability

The user should be able to figure out what to do without training, manuals, or trial-and-error. If they cannot, the design has failed.

### 2. Feedback

Every action must have immediate, clear feedback. No feedback = the user does not know if their action succeeded. Frustration follows.

### 3. Affordances and Signifiers Must Match

A flat plate says "push." If the door pulls, the signifier is lying. Good design ensures the signifier communicates the correct affordance.

> **Case: The Push/Pull Sign** (Chapter 1): When doors cannot be redesigned, a clear sign ("Push" or "Pull") becomes a signifier. But the sign should not be necessary — the design itself should communicate the correct action. A horizontal bar clearly invites pulling. A flat plate clearly invites pushing.
> **Key takeaway**: The best signifier is the design itself, not a label added to fix it.

### 4. Natural Mapping

Controls should be arranged to match their effects. A row of identical knobs controlling a 2x2 burner array violates natural mapping.

### 5. Constraints Prevent Errors

Physical (a plug only fits one way), logical (a puzzle only assembles one way), cultural (red means stop), and semantic (a steering wheel turns the car) constraints guide correct action.

### 6. Design for Error

Assume users will make mistakes. Design to prevent them — and when they happen, make recovery easy. The undo button is a design for error.

> **Case: The Computer Crash** (Chapter 5): Norman discusses early computer systems that lost all data when you made a typing error. The design punished mistakes. Modern systems (automatic save, undo, version history) design for error — they assume mistakes will happen and provide recovery paths.
> **Key takeaway**: Punishing users for mistakes is bad design. Good design anticipates errors and provides easy recovery.

### 7. The Power of Constraints

Sometimes the best design is limiting what the user can do. Children's toys that cannot be assembled wrong, USB plugs that only fit one way, software wizards that step through a process — constraints are not limitations, they are guidance.
