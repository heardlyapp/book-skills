# The Civilizations of the Ancient World

> Source: The History of the Ancient World, Parts I-VII

## Core Framework

Bauer organizes ancient history into major civilizations and periods, showing how they interacted and influenced each other.

### Major Civilizations

| Civilization | Period | Location | Key Achievement |
|-------------|--------|----------|-----------------|
| Sumer | 3500-2000 BCE | Mesopotamia (Iraq) | Writing, law codes, city-states |
| Egypt | 3100-30 BCE | Nile Valley | Pyramids, centralized state, 30 dynasties |
| Indus Valley | 2600-1900 BCE | Pakistan/India | Planned cities, trade networks |
| Minoan/Mycenaean | 2000-1100 BCE | Crete/Greece | The origins of Greek civilization |
| Shang/Zhou China | 1600-256 BCE | China | Bronze casting, writing, the Mandate of Heaven |
| Assyria | 900-612 BCE | Northern Mesopotamia | First empire of systematic terror |
| Persia | 550-330 BCE | Iran | Largest empire to date, multi-ethnic administration |
| Classical Greece | 500-323 BCE | Greece | Democracy, philosophy, art |
| Rome | 753 BCE-476 CE | Italy → Mediterranean | Law, engineering, empire |

> **Case: The First Written Laws** (Part I, Sumer and Babylon)
> The Code of Hammurabi (c. 1750 BCE) was not the first law code — but it is the most complete surviving one from the ancient Near East. Hammurabi's code established that law applied to everyone (though differently by class) and that punishment should fit the crime. The principle that rulers themselves are bound by law — that law is not the king's command but a standard above the king — is one of the most important ideas in human history.
> **Key takeaway**: The idea that rules are above rulers — that law is not the king's whims — was invented in Mesopotamia nearly 4000 years ago. Every constitutional government in the world traces its ancestry to this idea.

> **Case: Egypt's Extraordinary Stability** (Part I)
> Egyptian civilization lasted for 3,000 years — from the unification of Upper and Lower Egypt (c. 3100 BCE) to the death of Cleopatra (30 BCE). No other civilization has ever been as stable for as long. Bauer attributes this to geography (the Nile's predictable flooding made agriculture reliable), ideology (the pharaoh was a living god, which discouraged rebellion), and isolation (deserts on both sides limited invasion). Egypt's stability is the exception, not the rule.
> **Key takeaway**: Stability in the ancient world required geography (natural defenses), ideology (a shared belief system that legitimized rulers), and economics (enough resources to support the population). When any of these failed, civilizations collapsed.
