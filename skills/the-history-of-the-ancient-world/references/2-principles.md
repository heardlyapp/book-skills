# Empires — Rise, Peak, and Fall

> Source: The History of the Ancient World, Parts II-VII

## The Imperial Pattern

Bauer traces a consistent pattern across all ancient empires. Understanding this pattern helps us understand not just the past but also modern empires.

### The Stages of Empire

| Stage | Description | Ancient Example |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| Foundation | A leader unites a previously divided people | Cyrus uniting the Persians; Romulus founding Rome |
| Expansion | The empire conquers neighbors | Alexander's campaigns; Rome's conquest of Italy |
| Peak | Maximum territory and power | Rome under Trajan; Persia under Darius; Athens under Pericles |
| Overextension | The empire becomes too large to manage | Rome after Augustus; Persia after Xerxes |
| Internal decay | Corruption, economic decline, loss of civic virtue | Rome in the 3rd century; late Zhou China |
| Fall | The empire collapses or is conquered | Western Rome 476 CE; Mycenaean Greece 1100 BCE |

> **Case: The Persian Empire — Successful Diversity** (Part III)
> The Persian Empire (550-330 BCE) was the largest and most diverse empire before Rome. Cyrus the Great conquered Babylon not with terror but with diplomacy: he presented himself as a liberator, respected local religions, and allowed conquered peoples to maintain their own customs. The empire was organized into satrapies (provinces) with local governors, a postal system, and a network of royal roads. This model of multi-ethnic governance was unprecedented and was later adopted by Alexander and the Romans.
> **Key takeaway**: The most successful ancient empires were not the most violent — they were the most adaptable. Persia's willingness to respect local customs and delegate power made it larger and more stable than the more brutal Assyrian Empire that preceded it.

> **Case: Roman Imperial Expansion and Its Costs** (Parts V-VI)
> The Roman Republic's expansion was driven by a combination of ambition (Roman aristocrats competed for glory and wealth), necessity (preemptive wars against potential threats), and greed (conquest brought land, slaves, and tribute). But expansion destroyed the Republic: the influx of slaves and wealth enriched the elite and impoverished Italian farmers, who lost their land and moved to the city. The army became loyal to generals, not the state. The result: a century of civil war, the end of the Republic, and the establishment of the Empire.
> **Key takeaway**: The same thing that made Rome great — expansion — destroyed its republican institutions. The Republic was not killed by barbarians at the gate but by the consequences of its own success.
