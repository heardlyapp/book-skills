# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *The Infinite Game* by Simon Sinek — Chapters 1-11.

## The Three Frameworks

### Framework A: Finite vs Infinite Games (Chapter 1)

Sinek builds on James Carse's seminal distinction. Finite games (football, chess, elections) have known players, fixed rules, and an agreed-upon end. The goal is to win. Infinite games (business, politics, careers, life) have known and unknown players, flexible rules, and no finish line. The goal is not to win — it is to keep playing. Finite thinking in an infinite game leads to decline: loss of trust, cooperation, and innovation.

> **Case: The Vietnam War (Winning, Chapter 1):** The US won almost every battle in Vietnam. America lost 58,000 troops. North Vietnam lost over 3 million. America repelled every attack of the Tet Offensive and decimated the attacking forces — yet still lost the war. Why? The US was playing a finite game (battles won, territory held) while North Vietnam was playing an infinite game (national independence, generational commitment). "How do you win almost every battle, decimate your enemy and still lose the war?"

> **Case: Microsoft vs Apple Zune (Chapter 1):** Microsoft's Zune HD was technically superior to Apple's iPod. Sinek told an Apple executive the Zune was "SO MUCH BETTER" than the iPod. The exec replied, "I have no doubt." He was unfazed. Microsoft was playing a finite game — beating Apple. Apple was playing an infinite game — advancing a cause (education, creativity). At Microsoft's education summit, most presenters talked about beating Apple. At Apple's, 100% talked about helping teachers teach.

### Framework B: The Five Essential Practices (Chapters 2-11)

Sinek identifies five practices that distinguish infinite-minded leaders: (1) Advance a Just Cause — a vision of an ideal future that inspires people to contribute. (2) Build Trusting Teams — create psychological safety where people can speak up without fear. (3) Study Worthy Rivals — competitors are teachers whose strengths reveal your weaknesses. (4) Prepare for Existential Flexibility — the capacity to change strategy without abandoning the cause. (5) Demonstrate the Courage to Lead — prioritize long-term health over short-term gain.

> **Case: Kodak's George Eastman (Chapter 2):** Kodak's founder, George Eastman, built a company devoted to making photography accessible to everyone. He also recognized that advancing that vision depended on the well-being of his people. In 1912, Kodak was the first company to pay employee dividends, offer stock options, provide sick leave, and subsidize tuitions. Eastman built a hospital, founded a music school, and gave generously to education. His Just Cause — not profit — drove his decisions. Kodak thrived for generations.

### Framework C: The Courage to Lead (Chapter 11)

The ultimate test of infinite leadership is courage — the willingness to take a short-term loss for a long-term gain. This shows up in decisions to invest in people when it would be cheaper to lay them off, to prioritize quality over quarterly earnings, and to stay true to the cause when the market pressures you to abandon it.

> **Case: The Responsibility of Business (Chapter 5):** Sinek argues that the modern corporation's obsession with shareholder primacy is a finite mindset. The real responsibility of business is to use its will and resources to advance the Just Cause. Profits are not the goal — they are the fuel. "People come before profit" is not a sentiment. It is a strategic orientation that produces better long-term results.
