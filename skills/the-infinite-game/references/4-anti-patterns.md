# 4 — Anti-Patterns

> Source: *The Infinite Game* by Simon Sinek.

## Common Errors

### 1. Treating Business as a Finite Game

**The trap:** "We need to beat our competitors. We need to be number one. We need to win."

**Why it fails:** There is no such thing as winning an infinite game. The fixation on "winning" leads to short-term thinking, ethical compromises, and burnout. The companies that dominate their industries for generations are not obsessed with beating competitors — they are obsessed with advancing a cause.

> **Case: The Vietnam War (Introduction):** America won almost every battle and lost the war. The US measured success by body counts and territory — finite metrics. North Vietnam measured success by national independence and generational commitment — an infinite goal. The infinite-minded player does not quit, so the finite-minded player cannot win.
> **Key takeaway**: When you treat an infinite game as finite, you can win every battle and still lose. The metrics that matter in the short term are not the ones that matter in the long term.

> **Case: The Zune vs iPod (Chapter 1):** Microsoft spent millions developing a superior product — and failed. They were trying to "win" against Apple, while Apple was trying to "advance a cause." Microsoft's finite mindset made them the better product maker. Apple's infinite mindset made them the industry-defining company.
> **Key takeaway**: Product superiority does not defeat a stronger cause. The infinite game wins.

**The trap:** "We have a mission statement. That's our purpose."

**Why it fails:** Most mission statements are generic corporate exercises. A Just Cause is different: it is a vision of an ideal future so compelling that people would dedicate their careers to it even without personal benefit. Mission statements describe what you do. A Just Cause describes why it matters.

### 3. Ethical Fading

**The trap:** "Just this once, I'll cut a corner to meet the numbers. We'll make up for it later."

**Why it fails:** Ethical fading — the slow erosion of ethical standards in pursuit of finite goals — is one of Sinek's key warnings. Each small compromise makes the next one easier. By the time you realize what has happened, your culture is broken.

### 4. Mistaking Arrogance for Confidence

**The trap:** "We don't need to learn from our competitors. We're the best."

**Why it fails:** The Apple executive's "I have no doubt" response was not arrogance. It was confidence in his own game. He acknowledged the Zune's quality and moved on. Arrogance says "we are the best and have nothing to learn." Confidence says "we have a cause worth pursuing, and we can learn from anyone."

### 5. Rigidity in the Face of Change

**The trap:** "This strategy worked for 20 years. We should not abandon it now."

**Why it fails:** A strategy that worked for 20 years may not work for the next 5. Existential flexibility requires the courage to abandon successful strategies when they no longer serve the cause. The leader who cannot change course will eventually take the organization off a cliff.
