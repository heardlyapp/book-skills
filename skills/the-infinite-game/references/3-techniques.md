# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *The Infinite Game* by Simon Sinek.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The "What Game Are You Playing?" Test

When making a decision, ask: are we playing a finite game (we have an opponent, a deadline, a score) or an infinite game (we are advancing a cause, there is no finish line)? Most strategic mistakes come from treating an infinite game as finite. Sinek's opening example: the US treated Vietnam as a finite game. North Vietnam treated it as an infinite game. The finite player cannot beat the infinite player.

> **Case: Microsoft vs Apple (Chapter 1):** Microsoft treated the MP3 player market as a finite game — they wanted to "beat" Apple's iPod with the Zune. Apple treated it as part of an infinite game — advancing creativity and education. Microsoft focused on the product. Apple focused on the cause. The Zune was better. It lost.
> **Key takeaway**: A better product does not guarantee success if your competitor is playing a different game. The infinite game wins over the finite game.

> **Case: Kodak's Just Cause (Chapter 2):** George Eastman's vision was making photography accessible to everyone. This Just Cause drove decisions that made Kodak one of the most innovative and generous employers of its era. The cause — not the quarter — guided strategy. Kodak survived its founder by decades because the cause was bigger than any one leader.
> **Key takeaway**: A Just Cause that is bigger than any product or leader creates an organization that can outlast its founders.

Define your Just Cause by answering: (1) What is the ideal future we want to create? (2) Who benefits from this future? (3) Why would someone dedicate their career to this cause, even if they could earn more elsewhere? (4) Will this cause still be relevant 50 years from now? (5) Does this cause serve others, not just ourselves?

### 3. The Psychological Safety Audit

Assess your team's trust level: (1) Do team members admit mistakes without fear? (2) Do they challenge each other's ideas directly? (3) Do they ask for help when stuck? (4) Do they feel safe being vulnerable with each other? If the answer to any is "no," you have a trust problem. The leader sets the tone by modeling vulnerability first.

### 4. The Worthy Rival Analysis

Identify a Worthy Rival — an organization or person whose strengths expose a weakness in your own approach. The goal is not to beat them. The goal is to learn from them. Ask: "What does this rival do better than us? What can we learn from their approach that would make us stronger?"

### 5. The Existential Flexibility Check

Periodically ask: "If we were to start fresh today, would we still choose our current strategy? If not, what would we do differently?" The answer reveals whether you are staying with a strategy because it serves the cause, or because it is familiar. The courage to change course is the mark of an infinite leader.
