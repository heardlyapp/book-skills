# 2 — Key Principles

> Source: *The Infinite Game* by Simon Sinek.

## The 7 Principles

### 1. There Is No Such Thing as Winning in Business

Business is an infinite game. The goal is not to be declared "number one" — it is to keep playing, keep improving, keep contributing. Claims of being "the best" or "number one" are based on cherry-picked metrics over arbitrary time frames.

> **Case: British Airways' "World's Favourite" Claim (Chapter 1):** BA claimed to be "the world's favourite airline" based on carrying more international passengers — which meant their operation was expansive, not necessarily preferred. The finite-minded leader fixates on such metrics. The infinite-minded leader ignores them and focuses on advancing the cause.

### 2. A Just Cause Is the Foundation

Without a Just Cause, organizations default to finite thinking. The Just Cause must be: (1) for something (affirmative, not against something), (2) inclusive (open to all who share it), (3) service-oriented (benefits others), (4) resilient (able to survive political, technological, and cultural change), and (5) idealistic (bold, perhaps unreachable, but worth pursuing).

### 3. Trust Is a Strategic Advantage

Trusting Teams are built on psychological safety. When team members cannot speak up, admit mistakes, or challenge ideas without fear, the organization loses its ability to innovate and adapt. Trust is not a "nice to have" — it is the condition for long-term survival.

### 4. Worthy Rivals Are Teachers

> **Case: The Apple Executive's Response (Chapter 1):** When told that Microsoft's Zune was better than the iPod, the Apple executive replied, "I have no doubt" — and moved on. He did not argue, defend, or dismiss. He acknowledged the other player's strength and stayed focused on his own game. A Worthy Rival reveals your weaknesses. That is their value to you.

### 5. Existential Flexibility Is the Capacity to Change Course

The cause is fixed. The strategy is flexible. Existential flexibility means having the courage to abandon a strategy that once worked because it no longer serves the cause. This is harder than it sounds — it requires admitting that past success does not guarantee future survival.

### 6. The Organization Must Outlast Its Leaders

The ultimate test of leadership: does the organization thrive after you leave? If your success depended on you being there, you were not building an organization — you were building a dependency. Build systems, culture, and causes that survive their founders.

### 7. Courage Is Taking a Short-Term Loss for a Long-Term Gain

Every decision that prioritizes long-term health over short-term performance requires courage. The market will pressure you to cut costs, lay off people, and cut corners. The infinite-minded leader resists that pressure — not out of stubbornness, but out of commitment to the cause.
