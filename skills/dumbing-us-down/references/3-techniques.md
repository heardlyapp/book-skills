# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *Dumbing Us Down* by John Taylor Gatto — Chapters 2-5.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The Nine-Hour Audit

Calculate your child's week: sleep + school + homework + commuting + TV/screens + activities = ?. If private time is less than 15 hours, the system is consuming their childhood.

### 2. The Monongahela Test

Does your child spend time with people of different ages? Are adults in your community accessible? Is there a "river" — a laboratory for unsupervised exploration? If no, you need to create one.

### 3. The Guerrilla Teacher Strategy

> **Case: Gatto's Lab School** (Chapter 2): Gatto ran a secret Lab School program within the public school system. Each child gave 320 hours/year of community service. A 12-year-old girl traveled with her mother to apologize to a police chief for littering. Another pair apprenticed with a newspaper editor. Children learned through real work with real adults. When the district noticed how successful — and cheap — the program was, they killed it.
> **Key takeaway**: You can do real education inside a broken system — but you will probably have to keep it quiet.

> **Case: The Disappeared Student** (Chapter 2): A 16-year-old boy dropped out of New York City schools after realizing he spent his entire school day "waiting." Waiting for the teacher to finish with another student. Waiting for the bell. Waiting for morning to pass. He started apprenticing with a carpenter and read 40 books on his own time that year.
> **Key takeaway**: When a teenager chooses to learn, they learn faster and deeper than any classroom can match.

### 4. The Family Curriculum

Replace homework with family time. Replace scheduled activities with unstructured exploration. Replace textbooks with libraries, museums, workshops, and conversations with real people.

### 5. The Deschooling Transition

If you're leaving the system: expect 1-2 months of "withdrawal" — boredom, anxiety, questioning. Then real learning begins. Gatto's suggestion: start with independent study, then community service, then apprenticeships.

### 6. The Sunday Afternoon Rule

Protect one day per week as completely unscheduled. No homework, no activities, no screens. Let children lead. Let them be bored. "Boredom is the engine of creativity."
