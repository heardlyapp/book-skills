# Anti-Patterns — Ignition!

## The Central Error: "Theoretical Performance Is All That Matters"

"Performance" (specific impulse) obsessed the propellant community — but it's only one factor. "A fuel with wonderful theoretical performance that can't be handled safely, or that corrodes your engine, or that polymerizes into sludge, is useless." The history of propellant development is a history of beautiful theories destroyed by ugly reality.

### Mistake 1: "Boron will solve all our problems"
The Air Force spent hundreds of millions on the HEF (High Energy Fuels) program. Boron fuels had exceptional theoretical energy. "Diborane was a gas that exploded on contact with air. Pentaborane was a liquid that was almost as toxic as nerve gas." The result was catastrophic. "The siren song of boron" destroyed careers and wasted a fortune.

### Mistake 2: "The Germans know everything"
After Operation Paperclip, the US assumed German rocket engineering was superior. "The Germans had the experience" — but some of their propellant choices (like hydrogen peroxide for the V-2 guidance system) were poor. "We had to unlearn some of what the Germans taught us." Case: The Redstone missile originally used German-specified alcohol/LOX; later the US switched to kerosene/LOX (RP-1), which was simpler.

### Mistake 3: "More energetic = better"
"Exotics" were fuels with higher energy content. "The more energy a fuel contains, the more likely it is to release it when you don't want it to." Case: Hydrazine nitroform (a "monopropellant" that needed no oxidizer) was too sensitive. "It was a wonderful propellant. The only problem was that the slightest shock would set it off."

### Mistake 4: "If it works in the lab, it's ready for production"
"The gap between a good idea and a practical propellant is enormous." Case: When Clark's team found that a mixture of nitrogen tetroxide and nitric acid (IRFNA — Inhibited Red Fuming Nitric Acid) solved the materials corrosion problem, they thought they were done. "Then we discovered that it attacked the tank materials. Then we discovered it attacked the gaskets. Then..." Each fix revealed a new problem.

### Mistake 5: "Classified information is sacred"
"The intelligence reports we got about Soviet propellant work were often wrong." The secrecy that was supposed to protect US work also deprived US researchers of valuable information. Case: Clark notes that the Soviets independently developed many of the same propellants the US did, suggesting the secrecy was counterproductive.

### Mistake 6: "Test cells are optional"
"No one in the propellant business ever forgot the value of a good test cell — until management started cutting budgets." Case: Clark describes budget-driven decisions to "skip the small-scale test and go straight to the engine test" — which invariably caused explosions and cost more in the long run.
