# Reference: The Characters

## Overview
The novel is narrated by Mannie, a lunar native, which gives the story a distinctive voice. The characters are vivid, complex, and motivated by different visions of freedom.

## Case: Mannie — The Everyman Revolutionary
Manuel Garcia O'Kelly is a reluctant revolutionary. He does not start as an activist — he is a computer technician with a good job and a comfortable life. He gets drawn into the revolution because he believes in the cause, but he never becomes a zealot. His narration is pragmatic, dryly humorous, and deeply human. Mannie represents the ordinary person who is forced by circumstances to take extraordinary action. He is the reader's entry point into the story.

## Case: The Professor — The Ideological Leader
Bernardo de la Paz is the intellectual engine of the revolution. A former political exile from Earth, he has thought deeply about freedom, governance, and human nature. His lectures to the other characters (and to the reader) are the philosophical core of the novel. The Professor believes that free people will naturally create just institutions. He is the book's conscience — and its most articulate defender of liberty.

## Case: Wyoming Knott — The Activist
Wyoh is a professional revolutionary. Where the Professor provides the philosophy and Mannie the pragmatism, Wyoh provides the passion and the organizational skills. She is the one who recruits Mannie to the cause. She serves as the revolution's field commander, organizing cells, distributing propaganda, and maintaining morale. She is willing to sacrifice everything — her freedom, her life — for the cause she believes in. She is the heart of the revolution.
