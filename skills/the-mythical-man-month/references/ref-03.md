# Reference: Team Structure

## Overview
Brooks proposes the surgical team model for software development — a small, elite team built around a chief programmer.

## Case: The Surgical Team
A surgical team consists of a chief programmer (the "surgeon"), supported by a co-pilot (junior programmer who knows the code), an administrator, an editor, a secretary, a toolsmith, a tester, and a language lawyer. The team has 7-10 people but the design is driven by the chief programmer. This model aims to have the productivity of a small team with the capacity of a larger one.

## Case: The Chief Programmer
The chief programmer is the most productive programmer on the team. They design the system, write the critical code, and make all architectural decisions. Everyone else supports their work. The chief programmer team model contrasts with the conventional approach where many programmers work in parallel without clear leadership.

## Case: Small Team Advantages
Small teams (4-7 people) produce better work than large teams because communication is manageable. Brooks argues that the best software is produced by small teams of good people, not by large teams of average people.

## Case: The Two-Week Rule
Brooks suggests that if a project requires more than about 20 people, it may be too large for a surgical team. In that case, the project should be organized as a "chief programmer hierarchy": multiple surgical teams working under a top-level architect. The communication must be carefully managed.

## Case: The Clarifying Effect of Small Teams
Small teams have another advantage: anyone who is not performing is immediately visible. In a team of 7, a weak member is obvious. In a team of 100, weakness can hide. The surgical team model creates accountability.
