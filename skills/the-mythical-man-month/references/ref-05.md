# Reference: Applying Brooks Today

## Overview
Brooks's insights remain relevant decades later. Understanding them helps avoid the same mistakes.

## Case: Estimation and the Man-Month
Modern software projects still struggle with estimation. Brooks's analysis of why men and months are not interchangeable is as relevant as ever. Agile methods (sprints, velocity) attempt to address this by measuring actual output and projecting forward. But the fundamental issue remains: adding people to a late project does not help.

## Case: The Second-System in Modern Products
The second-system effect is visible in many modern software products. A successful first product is followed by a version that is over-engineered, feature-bloated, and loses the original's focus. The history of Windows, Photoshop, and many web applications shows the second-system effect in action.

## Case: Small Teams Today
The surgical team model has been validated by the success of small-team startups. WhatsApp had 55 employees when serving 900 million users. Instagram had 13 employees when bought for $1 billion. Small teams of outstanding people continue to outperform large teams of average people.

## Case: Conceptual Integrity in Modern Systems
The principle of conceptual integrity is more relevant than ever. Successful platforms (iOS, Android, Kubernetes) maintain a consistent design philosophy across releases. The architect's role has evolved (platform teams, design systems) but the principle endures.

## Case: Remote Work and Brooks's Law
Remote work changes the communication dynamics but does not invalidate Brooks's law. Communication overhead for distributed teams can be higher, making the addition of new people even more costly. Brooks's insights about partitioning and communication are amplified in remote contexts.
