# Anti-Patterns

## 1. Using Influence Tactics Without Genuine Care

**The mistake**: Manipulating others by pretending to care.

**Fix**: People can sense insincerity. The Go-Giver approach only works when it's genuine.

## 2. Focusing on Winning

**The mistake**: Approaching every interaction as a contest to be won.

**Fix**: Seek the third way — a solution that serves both parties. Win-lose is actually lose-lose over time.

> **Case: The Win-Lose Negotiator** (Chapter 2): A negotiator prided himself on winning every deal. He got great terms — but people stopped wanting to deal with him. His reputation for hard bargaining cost him more business than he gained.
> **Key takeaway**: Win-lose is a short-term strategy. The person you defeat today will remember tomorrow. True influence requires that both parties succeed.

> **Case: The False Generosity** (Chapter 6): A salesperson gave prospects gifts and favors — but with the clear expectation that they would buy. Prospects felt manipulated. The "generosity" was actually pressure. When she gave without strings — genuinely helpful information, no strings attached — relationships improved and sales eventually followed.
>
> **Key takeaway**: Giving with strings is not giving — it's manipulation. True generosity gives freely without expecting return. The return comes naturally, but it cannot be demanded.

## 3. Giving with Strings Attached

**The mistake**: Giving only when you expect something in return.

**Fix**: True giving has no conditions. Give freely, without expectation.

## 4. Trying to Influence Without Listening

**The mistake**: Speaking first and trying to persuade without understanding the other person.

**Fix**: Your influence is proportional to your understanding. Listen first.

## 5. Confusing Charisma with Influence

**The mistake**: Believing that influence requires a charismatic personality.

**Fix**: Influence comes from genuine care and consistent value-giving, not personality. Introverts can be highly influential.
