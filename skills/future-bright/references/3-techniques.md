> Source: "Future Bright: A Transforming Vision of Human Intelligence" by Michael E. Martinez (Oxford University Press, 2013), Chapter 8.

## Ten Strategies to Enhance Intelligence

Chapter 8 is the practical culmination of the entire book. Martinez translates a century of research into ten actionable strategies. He emphasizes that these are investments, not pills — they require sustained effort and the returns compound over years, not days. The portfolio should be diversified: don't invest exclusively in one strategy, but allocate effort across multiple dimensions.

### Strategy 1: Set a Goal to Enhance Intelligence

"Nothing matters more to the pursuit of a new life direction than having a clear and definite goal." The first and most important step is believing that enhancement is possible. The preceding chapters provide the evidence; this strategy supplies the resolve.

Martinez distinguishes between passive nurture (what happens *to* you) and self-nurturance (what you deliberately choose for yourself). Adults must shift to self-nurturance — becoming "managers of their own life span intellectual development." The goal must be matched with the will to achieve it, which means choosing challenge over complacency consistently.

**Practical steps:** Write down a specific intelligence-enhancement goal (e.g., "Read 24 nonfiction books this year" or "Learn conversational Mandarin"). Track progress. Review quarterly.

### Strategy 2: Build Fluid Intelligence Through Problem Solving

Fluid intelligence is the most neglected form of intelligence in modern life. Because it requires novelty, it is naturally eroded by routine. The solution is to deliberately embed problem solving into daily experience.

Martinez distinguishes well-structured problems (like chess, with clear rules) from ill-structured problems (like real-life challenges, with fuzzy goals and unclear means). Both are valuable, but **ill-structured problems are more powerful** because they force flexibility and tolerance for ambiguity.

**Practical steps:** Change careers or take on a role with different demands. Travel to a new culture for an extended period. Learn a new language. Take up a genuinely challenging hobby. Run for local office. Do anything that "forces you to think in new ways."

### Strategy 3: Build Crystallized Intelligence Through Book Reading

"Books structure ideas almost uniquely." The vocabulary, concepts, and extended reasoning found in books are rare in everyday conversation. Nonfiction is especially valuable for building structured knowledge; novels build verbal ability and social understanding.

Martinez coins the term "aliteracy" — the ability to read combined with the failure to do so. Many capable readers let television and distractions squeeze out reading time. The solution is simple: build the habit of daily book reading and hold to it for the long term.

**Practical steps:** Set aside 30 minutes daily for book reading. Prioritize nonfiction with substantive arguments. Keep a reading journal. Discuss what you read with others to deepen understanding.

### Strategy 4: Become an Expert

Expertise — a coherent knowledge base of approximately 50,000 distinct patterns — is the most powerful form of crystallized intelligence. It is achieved through extended, focused experience in a domain.

The route to expertise is "deliberate practice" — structured effort aimed at improving specific aspects of performance. This requires discipline, feedback, and sustained commitment over many years. Martinez notes that "expert novices" — beginners who adopt the thinking patterns of experts (self-monitoring, prioritizing key ideas, managing their own learning) — are the most likely to reach mastery.

**Practical steps:** Deepen knowledge in your chosen field. Pursue advanced degrees or certifications that require sustained cognitive effort. Engage in self-study with structured goals. Seek feedback from recognized experts.

### Strategy 5: Develop Effective Character

Conscientiousness — responsibility, follow-through, attention to detail — is the personality dimension that most strongly predicts success. It is independent of IQ and equally powerful. The good news: like intelligence, it can be developed.

Martinez argues that effective character is "the mix of personality characteristics that support success" — including conscientiousness, intellectual openness, emotional stability, and (in some contexts) extraversion. These traits can be cultivated through practice, feedback, and a commitment to standards of excellence.

**Practical steps:** Set specific behavioral goals around follow-through. Keep a commitments journal. Build systems that enforce accountability. Surround yourself with people who model conscientious behavior.

### Strategy 6: Find Your Intellectual Niche

Intelligence is not just a quantity but also a kind. People have different ability profiles, and success depends on finding environments that match those profiles. Martinez calls this the **ecological fit** approach to intelligence.

He recommends honestly appraising three questions: What do I do well? What do I do poorly? What do I enjoy? The goal is not to eliminate weaknesses but to find environments where strengths are valued and the weaknesses are less relevant. "We can exercise intelligence not just in developing our ability profile, but also in choosing and modifying our environments."

**Practical steps:** Audit your current activities against your strengths and enjoyment. Shift time away from activities that match neither toward those that match both. When choosing a job or field of study, prioritize fit over prestige.

### Strategy 7: Cultivate Social Intelligence

Social intelligence — the ability to "read" others' behavior and intentions, and to act skillfully in social contexts — is a legitimate and important form of intelligence. It includes emotional intelligence (understanding one's own and others' emotions) but is broader, encompassing the productive side of social behavior.

There is no formula, only practice. "To avoid interactions with others is to compound a problem rather than to solve it."

**Practical steps:** Put yourself in social situations that require negotiation and collaboration. Practice listening more than speaking. Seek feedback on your interpersonal effectiveness.

### Strategy 8: Seek Like-Minded Friendships

Drawing on Vygotsky's theory, Martinez argues that "the human mind is, at root, a social entity." Intelligence operates in the space *between* people, not just inside individual heads. Your IQ is partly shaped by "the IQs of others with whom you come into contact."

Good friends are "sources of intellectual nourishment." Debate, dialogue, and collaboration with capable peers make everyone smarter. "We become smarter by the company we keep."

**Practical steps:** Cultivate friendships with intellectually curious, accomplished people. Join discussion groups or professional communities. Engage in regular, meaningful dialogue about substantive topics.

### Strategy 9: Seek Optimal Nutrition

The mind-brain connection means that cognitive performance requires proper fuel. About 10% of US households lack nutritionally adequate diets. Supplementation — especially for children in poverty — can boost cognitive test scores. Omega-3 fatty acids, vitamins, minerals, and adequate protein are essential for brain function.

**Practical steps:** Prioritize whole foods over processed ones. Ensure adequate intake of omega-3s (fatty fish, flaxseed), B vitamins, and iron. Maintain regular meals to provide steady glucose to the brain.

### Strategy 10: Exercise

"Of all the strategies for raising cognitive performance, exercise may be the most counterintuitive." Yet it works: exercise stimulates brain vascularization (new blood vessels) and synaptogenesis (new connections between neurons). These effects have been demonstrated in rodents, monkeys, and humans.

Exercise is particularly effective at preserving cognitive function in older adults and can "reduce the typical decline of cognitive function associated with advanced years."

**Practical steps:** Establish a regular exercise routine that spans the decades. Include both cardiovascular exercise (for vascularization) and strength training. Aim for 30-60 minutes most days.

### Cases from the Book

**Case 7: The Brain Boost Project.** Martinez's own intervention program in local public schools put his ideas into practice. The program demonstrated that structured experience could raise children's intelligence — not just their test scores. This is the real-world validation of the book's thesis.

**Case 8: Vygotsky's Social Mind Theory.** The Russian psychologist showed that thinking is not purely private — it can occur in the social space between people. When you debate a friend, ideas are "rendered wonderfully explicit in the form of language." Both participants walk away smarter.


> **Case: The Kumon Method** (Chapter 6): Martinez examines the Kumon math program as a case study in deliberate practice. Students do daily worksheets that are slightly above their current level, building skills incrementally. The method doesn't just teach math — it builds concentration, persistence, and the habit of daily practice. Research shows Kumon students not only improve in math but show gains in general cognitive abilities.
> **Key takeaway:** The principles of deliberate practice — incremental challenge, consistent effort, immediate feedback — can enhance intelligence beyond the specific skill being practiced.

> **Case: The Head Start Program** (Chapter 6): Martinez reviews the evidence from Head Start and similar early intervention programs. Children who participated in high-quality early education showed IQ gains of 5-10 points that persisted into adulthood. The gains were largest for children from disadvantaged backgrounds. The key ingredients: cognitive stimulation, language-rich environments, and supportive teaching.
> **Key takeaway:** Early intervention works. The best time to enhance intelligence is early childhood, but structured cognitive enrichment benefits learners of any age.
