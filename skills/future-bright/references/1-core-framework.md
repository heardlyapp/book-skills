# Core Framework — Future Bright

## Introduction: You've Been Thinking About Intelligence Wrong

Martinez's core thesis: "You've been thinking about intelligence in the wrong way. Almost everyone assumes that intelligence is a genetically programmed trait like eye color — set from birth and unalterable. Yet the extensive body of research on human intelligence demonstrates that this assumption is false." IQ scores fluctuate within a person's lifetime and across generations (the Flynn Effect). "This central fact raises the all-important question of what causes IQ scores to shift up or down."

The book advances a powerful idea: human intelligence is modifiable. "With the right kinds of experience, intelligence can be learned and IQ scores will rise in parallel."

## Chapter 1: The Sine Qua Non of Success

Intelligence is now "the unrivaled capital resource of the 21st century workforce." The economy has shifted from industrial to information-based. Robert Reich's "symbolic analysts" — workers who gather, interpret, and act on information — are the most sought-after. The wage premium for higher education has grown significantly. "A college degree translates into a 10- to 15-point IQ advantage." Computers have forced the human mind to concentrate on what it does best, and that is intelligence.

## Chapter 2: What Is Intelligence?

Martinez defines intelligence as "the ability to solve problems and to create products that are valued in one or more cultural settings." He references Sternberg, Gardner, Piaget. Key distinction between fluid intelligence (Gf — the ability to solve novel problems) and crystallized intelligence (Gc — accumulated knowledge). "Fluid intelligence peaks in young adulthood and then declines. Crystallized intelligence increases throughout life."

## Chapter 3: Varieties of Intelligence

Multiple intelligences theory: Gardner's eight intelligences (linguistic, logical-mathematical, spatial, musical, bodily-kinesthetic, interpersonal, intrapersonal, naturalistic). Sternberg's triarchic theory (analytical, creative, practical). Martinez argues these all share a common core — the ability to process information and solve problems.

## Chapter 4: Brain and Mind

The brain's architecture: neurons, synapses, plasticity. "The brain is not a static organ. It changes in response to experience." Intelligence correlates with: brain size (modestly), gray matter volume, white matter integrity, speed of neural transmission, and the efficiency of the prefrontal cortex. Case: London taxi drivers have enlarged hippocampi — their brains physically changed to accommodate their knowledge of city streets.

## Chapter 5: The Nature-Nurture Dilemma

IQ heritability is about 50%, meaning half the variance is environmental. The Flynn Effect (rising IQs by 3 points per decade) proves that environmental factors substantially shape intelligence. "If genes alone determined IQ, IQs would not rise." Case: Adoption studies show that children adopted into higher-SES families have IQs 12-16 points higher than their biological parents.

## Chapter 6: How Experience Cultivates Intelligence

The three levers: cognitive complexity of environments (enriched vs deprived), quality of schooling, and the home environment. Case: The "Brain Boost" project — Martinez's own intervention program that raised IQ scores in children through targeted cognitive training and enrichment.

## Chapter 7: The Superstructure of Effectiveness

Martinez's three-component model: **Fluid Intelligence** (problem-solving ability), **Crystallized Intelligence** (knowledge), and **Effective Character** (the "smart heart"). Effective character includes: curiosity, openness to experience, persistence (grit), self-regulation, and intellectual humility. "Intelligence is not just about IQ. It includes the dispositions of the heart that make intelligence effective."

## Chapter 8: Ten Strategies to Enhance Intelligence

1. **Broaden your experiences** — exposure to diverse ideas and environments
2. **Push beyond the easy** — deliberate practice with challenging material
3. **Learn to think critically** — question assumptions, seek evidence
4. **Master the art of questioning** — good questions drive learning
5. **Seek feedback** — accurate self-assessment requires external input
6. **Cultivate effective character** — curiosity, persistence, humility
7. **Read widely and deeply** — reading increases crystallized intelligence
8. **Exercise your brain** — cognitive training, puzzles, new skills
9. **Pursue education actively** — formal education reliably raises IQ
10. **Build relationships that stimulate thinking** — intellectual conversation
