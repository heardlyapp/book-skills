# The Club and Its Founders

> Source: The Presidents Club, Introduction, Chapters 1-2

## Core Framework

### The Birth of the Club

| President | Party | When | Key Role |
|-----------|-------|------|----------|
| Herbert Hoover | R | 1929-1933 | Disgraced, blamed for Depression |
| Harry Truman | D | 1945-1953 | Invited Hoover back from exile |
| Dwight Eisenhower | R | 1953-1961 | The club's official founding moment |

> **Case: The Inauguration That Started It All** (Introduction, Chapters 1-2)
> At Eisenhower's inauguration in January 1953, the outgoing president Truman invited the disgraced former president Hoover to the ceremony. This was unprecedented: Hoover had been treated as a pariah since leaving office in 1933. He and Truman talked on the platform, and Truman suggested they work together on postwar relief. They became friends. The "Presidents Club" was born from this moment — two men from opposite parties who discovered they had more in common with each other than with anyone else.
> **Key takeaway:** The club was founded on a simple idea: only someone who has been president can understand what it is like. Partisanship fades. The shared experience of the office is more powerful than party loyalty.

> **Case: Hoover's Redemption** (Chapters 1-2)
> When Truman became president in 1945, he could have treated Hoover with the contempt that FDR had shown him. Instead, Truman asked Hoover to help with postwar food relief. Hoover, grateful to be useful, threw himself into the work. He traveled the world, organized food shipments, and saved millions from starvation. His reputation was partially restored. Truman later said: "I don't think there is any man in public life who has worked as hard or as effectively for world peace as Herbert Hoover." This was a stunning reversal for a man who had been the most hated president in modern history.
> **Key takeaway:** Truman's decision to rehabilitate Hoover was politically risky but historically wise. Hoover was a deeply competent man who had been in the wrong job at the wrong time. Truman saw past the failure to the talent. The lesson: look for the potential in your predecessors — they may surprise you.
