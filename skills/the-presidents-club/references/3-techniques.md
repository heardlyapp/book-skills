> **Case: Carter's Secret North Korea Mission** (Chapter 21)
> In 1994, North Korea was on the verge of developing nuclear weapons. The Clinton administration was preparing for a military confrontation. Clinton asked Jimmy Carter to go to Pyongyang — not as an official envoy, but as a private citizen and former president. Carter met with Kim Il Sung. He negotiated a freeze on North Korea's nuclear program. The deal was controversial — Carter was seen as going rogue — but it averted a war. Clinton later said: "He had the stature to do what no sitting president could do."
> **Key takeaway:** Former presidents can go where sitting presidents cannot. Their status allows them to take risks, make offers, and speak truths that would be impossible for the current administration. This is the club's most valuable service.

> **Case: Eisenhower Advises Kennedy After the Bay of Pigs** (Chapters 5-7)
> After the failed Bay of Pigs invasion in 1961, Kennedy called Eisenhower at Gettysburg. He asked for advice. Kennedy, still in his 40s, was humiliated. Eisenhower, in his 70s, could have said "I told you so." Instead, he listened. He asked questions. He told Kennedy: "There is no formula for being president. You have to use your own judgment." He did not criticize. He supported. Kennedy later said: "He helped me more than anyone."
> **Key takeaway:** Eisenhower's response to Kennedy's call for help is the club at its best. He did not gloat. He did not lecture. He offered what Kennedy needed: reassurance and perspective. The lesson: when someone who has failed asks for your help, do not point out their mistakes. Point them forward.
