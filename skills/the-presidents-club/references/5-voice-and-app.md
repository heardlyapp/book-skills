# Gibbs and Duffy's Voice & Application Scenarios

> Source: The Presidents Club, all chapters, Introduction, Conclusion

## Authorial Voice

Nancy Gibbs and Michael Duffy (both Time editors) write as insider journalists with access to the club. Their voice is:

- **Authoritative and detailed**: They have access, documents, and interviews.
- **Dramatic but balanced**: Each relationship is a story with heroes and villains — but they let the reader judge.
- **Humanizing the presidency**: They show presidents as people: jealous, generous, petty, wise.
- **Structurally brilliant**: Each chapter is a mini-biography of a relationship.

### Classic Lines from the Book

- "The club has never numbered more than six."
- "The club's rule: the sitting president is always the club president."
- "No matter the politics, you know and understand the weight of the decisions the other guy had to make."
- "He had the stature to do what no sitting president could do."
- "The presidents club: a hidden instrument of power that changed the course of history."

## Five Application Scenarios

### Scenario 1: New Leader
You are taking over a role from someone else.
**Lesson**: Reach out to your predecessor. They can help you — not because they want to control you, but because they have been where you are.

### Scenario 2: Former Leader Leaving a Role
You are stepping down from a position of authority.
**Lesson**: Stay available. Offer help without being asked. Do not criticize your successor publicly. The transition will be smoother, and your legacy will be protected.

### Scenario 3: Person Managing a Transition
You are helping two people exchange roles.
**Lesson**: Create space for the outgoing and incoming leaders to build a relationship. The clubs model: the shared experience is the bond.

### Scenario 4: Professional in a Competitive Field
Your competitors could be your allies.
**Lesson**: The presidents club shows that the people who compete with you most intensely can also be your greatest allies — after the competition is over.

### Scenario 5: Citizen Trying to Understand Presidents
You find presidents remote and strange.
**Lesson**: They are as human as anyone. The club reveals their insecurities, their rivalries, their friendships. The presidency does not make you superhuman — it makes you more of who you already are.

> **Case: The Letter From Nixon to Clinton** (Introduction)
> Bill Clinton told the authors: "A month to the day before he died, Nixon wrote me a letter about Russia. And it was so lucid, so well written. I reread it every year." This letter — from a disgraced former president to a sitting president of the opposite party — is the club in its purest form. Nixon had nothing to gain. Clinton had nothing to fear. They were just two people who understood the weight of the office.
> **Key takeaway:** The Nixon-Clinton letter is the most beautiful image in the book. Two presidents, from different parties, one disgraced and the other at the height of his power, connected by a shared experience that no one else in the world shared. This is what the club is for.

> **Case: The Dinner for Five Presidents** (Chapter 20)
> After the 2008 election, five living presidents — Bush 41, Carter, Clinton, Bush 43, and Obama — met at the White House. Carter told the authors: "We wanted to educate president-elect Obama in a nice way without preaching to him." The moment was extraordinary: five men who had held the most powerful office in the world, sitting around a table, sharing what they had learned. This is the club at its best: a living institution of accumulated wisdom.
> **Key takeaway:** The dinner of five presidents is the living proof of the club's value. Five people who had experienced the impossible job, passing their knowledge to the next person. Every organization should have such a moment — where the past and future leaders sit together and share what they know.
