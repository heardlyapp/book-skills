# Anti-Patterns — What Kills Dreams

> Source: Dream Big, Chapters 5, 6, 9, 11, Conclusion

## The Five Dream Killers

### 1. Going Solo
**Pattern**: You believe you should figure everything out yourself, achieve by yourself, succeed by yourself.
**Why it fails**: No great achievement is solo. The solo hero myth is a cultural fiction that creates isolation, burnout, and abandoned dreams. The moment you share your dream with someone, it becomes more real — and more likely to happen.

### 2. Waiting for the Perfect Moment
**Pattern**: "I'll start when I have more money / experience / time / confidence."
**Why it fails**: The perfect moment never comes. Life is always messy. The people who achieve their dreams are not the ones who waited for perfect conditions — they're the ones who started despite imperfect ones.

### 3. Perfectionism
**Pattern**: Everything must be polished, professional, and ready before you show anyone.
**Why it fails**: Perfectionism is procrastination wearing a fancy suit. The first version of anything that matters will be imperfect. The key is to launch imperfectly and improve through feedback, not to hide until you're perfect.

### 4. Comparing Your Path to Others'
**Pattern**: You measure your progress against people who are further along, have different gifts, or started earlier.
**Why it fails**: Comparison is the thief of joy and the killer of dreams. Your path is unique. Someone else's success does not diminish your potential. Run your race, not theirs.

### 5. Giving Up After the First Failure
**Pattern**: Your first attempt doesn't work, and you conclude the dream was wrong.
**Why it fails**: First attempts almost always fail. That's normal. The question is not whether you fail but whether you learn from it and try again. The only true failure is quitting.

### The Dream Detox Protocol

1. **Identify** — Which dream killer is active in your life right now?
2. **Name it** — Say it out loud to someone: "I'm afraid to start because ________."
3. **Counter it** — What would be the opposite action? (Going solo → ask for help. Perfection → launch imperfectly.)
4. **Take one step** — The antidote to every dream killer is action.

> **Case: The Woman Who Quit Too Early** (Chapter 6)
> Goff tells the story of a woman who started a business with passion and initial success. When her first major client pulled out, she concluded she wasn't cut out for entrepreneurship. She closed the business and went back to a job she hated. A year later, she learned that her former competitor faced similar setbacks but persisted and eventually built a successful company. The difference was not talent or resources — it was the willingness to try again.
> **Key takeaway**: The difference between success and failure is often just one more attempt. The people who make it are not the ones who never fail — they're the ones who fail and keep going.

> **Case: The Perfectionist Who Never Published** (Chapter 9)
> Goff describes a writer friend who spent years perfecting a single manuscript. He revised it dozens of times, sent it to beta readers, and revised it again. But he never published it. He was waiting for it to be "ready" — a standard that shifted every time he got feedback. Goff's advice: publish an imperfect version. Get it out into the world. Learn from real readers. The book that eventually came out was not the "perfect" one he had been writing for years — it was a different, better book that emerged from actually engaging with readers.
> **Key takeaway**: Art made in isolation is never as good as art refined through engagement with the world. Publishing imperfectly allows evolution through interaction. A first draft published is worth more than a masterpiece never shared.
