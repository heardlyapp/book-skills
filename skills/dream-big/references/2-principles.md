# Action Principles — Starting and Sustaining Momentum

> Source: Dream Big, Chapters 3, 4, 5, 6, 11

## The Action Framework

Goff's approach to action is counterintuitive: start before you're ready, accept imperfection, and let action create clarity rather than the other way around.

### The Readiness Myth

"I'll start when I have enough money / experience / time / confidence / support." Goff calls this the Readiness Myth. It's the endless loop that keeps dreams on the shelf. The truth: you will never feel ready. The feeling of readiness comes from acting, not from preparing.

### Start Small, Start Now

| Scale | Action | Timeframe |
|-------|--------|-----------|
| Micro | One phone call, one email, one conversation | Next 24 hours |
| Medium | A prototype, a trial run, a first draft | Next week |
| Macro | The full commitment, launching publicly | Next 3 months |

### The Thermometer vs. Thermostat Exercise

1. Identify one area of your life where you've been reacting (thermometer).
2. Decide what you want your internal temperature to be in that area (thermostat).
3. Act according to your chosen temperature, regardless of external conditions.
4. Repeat daily until it becomes a habit.

### The One-Sentence Dream

Goff's challenge: describe your dream in one sentence. If you can't, you haven't done the work of clarification. If you can, you have something concrete to share with others, to ask for help with, and to measure progress against.

> **Case: The Friend Who Waited Too Long** (Chapter 3)
> Goff tells the story of a friend who spent years planning to start a business. He researched, wrote business plans, attended seminars, and networked. But he never launched. He was waiting to feel "ready." Goff challenged him: what's the smallest possible version of your business you could start tomorrow? The friend started a minimal version — a website and a single service offering — and within three months had paying customers. The "ready" version he had been planning would have taken another year.
> **Key takeaway**: The smallest version of your dream that you can start today is infinitely better than the perfect version that you'll start "someday." Perfectionism is the most socially acceptable form of procrastination.

> **Case: The Community That Saved the Dream** (Chapter 1)
> Goff describes a project that was failing — a nonprofit he was starting was on the verge of collapse. The problem wasn't the idea or the execution. The problem was that he was trying to do it alone. When he finally reached out to friends, mentors, and colleagues, they didn't just offer encouragement — they offered expertise, connections, and financial support that turned the project around. The act of asking for help was the turning point.
> **Key takeaway**: Going alone is not a badge of honor — it's a strategy for failure. The moment you share your dream is the moment it becomes real. Asking for help is not weakness; it's the smartest thing you can do.
