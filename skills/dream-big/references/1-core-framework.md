# The Dream Clarity Framework

> Source: Dream Big, Introduction, Chapters 1, 2, 3, 4

## Core Framework

Bob Goff's approach to dreams is practical, not mystical. He doesn't tell you to "find your passion" and wait for clarity. He tells you to start moving, and clarity will find you.

### The Three Dream Zones

| Zone | Description | Answer to "What do I want?" |
|------|-------------|-----------------------------|
| The Comfort Zone | Safe, known, predictable | Someone else's dream for you |
| The Fear Zone | Scary, uncertain, challenging | The edge of your real dream — you can feel it because it's uncomfortable |
| The Growth Zone | Active pursuit with community | You're living it — not perfectly, but actively |

### The Five Clarifying Questions

1. **What makes you lose track of time?** — Flow states point to your natural gifts and passions.
2. **What breaks your heart about the world?** — Your calling is often found at the intersection of your compassion and your competence.
3. **What would you do if you knew you couldn't fail?** — This strips away fear and reveals desire.
4. **What are you already doing that feels like play?** — Often your dream is already present in your life, just not taken seriously yet.
5. **What would you regret not having tried?** — The deathbed test. Look backward from the end to see what matters now.

### The Dream Test

| Question | If Yes | If No |
|----------|--------|-------|
| Does it scare you? | Good — it's big enough | Maybe it's too small |
| Does it serve others? | Sustainable | Will feel empty |
| Does it require you to grow? | It will change you | Not challenging enough |
| Would you do it for free? | It's genuinely your dream | It might be someone else's |

> **Case: Bob's Early Attempts** (Introduction)
> Goff describes his own journey of pursuing dreams that didn't work out — failed business ventures, misguided career choices, relationships that ended badly. But he learned from each failure what he truly wanted. The process of elimination was as valuable as direct discovery. He learned that he loved helping people discover their potential, and that became the foundation of everything he's done since.
> **Key takeaway**: You don't have to know your dream on the first try. Experimentation and failure are part of the clarification process. Every wrong turn tells you something about where the right road is.

> **Case: The Thermometer vs. Thermostat Distinction** (Chapter 2)
> Goff tells the story of a friend who was constantly reacting to circumstances. When things were good, he felt good. When things were bad, he felt terrible. He was a thermometer — reflecting his environment. Goff challenged him to become a thermostat — to set the temperature, to decide internally what he would be regardless of external conditions. This is not about denying reality but about choosing your response to it.
> **Key takeaway**: The difference between people who pursue their dreams and people who don't is not talent or luck — it's whether they let circumstances determine their actions or they determine their actions regardless of circumstances. Be a thermostat.
