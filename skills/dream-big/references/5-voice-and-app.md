# Bob Goff's Voice & Application Scenarios

> Source: Dream Big, all chapters; Introduction

## Authorial Voice

Bob Goff writes with infectious, slightly chaotic joy. His voice is:

- **Conversational and personal**: He addresses you directly, tells stories from his own life, and writes like he's talking to a friend over coffee.
- **Joyful and urgent**: He believes you don't have time to waste on small dreams. The urgency is love, not pressure.
- **Counterintuitive and challenging**: He pushes back against conventional wisdom (waiting to be ready, going solo, playing it safe).
- **Faith-infused but broadly accessible**: Goff is a Christian and references his faith, but the principles are framed universally — about purpose, community, and action.
- **Messy and authentic**: He shares his failures as openly as his successes. His credibility comes from honesty, not from being impressive.

### Classic Goff Quotes

- "There are books of which the backs and covers are by far the best parts. I've read a couple of those. Don't settle for the cover story. It's in the middle of our lives where all the good stuff happens."
- "If you want to go fast, go alone. If you want to go far, go together."
- "Thermostats set the temperature; thermometers just read it."
- "The biggest dreams are the ones that aren't about you."
- "Start before you're ready. You'll never feel ready."
- "Don't let what's wrong with you keep you from focusing on what's right with you."

## Five Application Scenarios

### Scenario 1: College Graduate Unsure of Direction
You've just finished school and feel pressure to have a clear career path. Everyone asks what you're "going to do."
**Solution**: You don't need to know your dream yet. You need to start experimenting. Try three different things in the next year — volunteer, work an entry-level job in a field that interests you, take a class in something completely unrelated. Clarity comes through action, not contemplation. And don't go alone — find a mentor or peer group exploring similar questions. (Read references/1-core-framework.md.)

### Scenario 2: Mid-Career Professional with a Sideline Dream
You have a stable job and a dream on the side. You're afraid to pursue it because it might fail, and you can't afford to lose your income.
**Solution**: Start the smallest possible version of your dream while keeping your day job. A prototype, a pilot, a side project. Prove it works on a small scale before you commit fully. And find your community — other side-project dreamers, mentors who've made the leap. (Read references/3-techniques.md, the start small framework.)

### Scenario 3: Person Whose Dream Didn't Work Out
You pursued something with passion and it failed. Now you're wondering if you should try again or give up.
**Solution**: Failure is not a verdict — it's data. What did you learn? What would you do differently? Is this dream still alive or has it transformed into a different one? Goff's key insight: the only true failure is quitting. Take a pause to grieve and recover, then decide: do you try again, or is there a new dream that has emerged from the ashes of the old one? (Read references/4-anti-patterns.md.)

### Scenario 4: Someone Supporting a Dreamer
Your spouse, friend, or child has a big dream that scares you. You want to support them but also worry about practicalities.
**Solution**: Your role is encourager, not gatekeeper. Ask "What do you need from me?" not "Is this practical?" Your fear is not a reason for them to shrink their dream. At the same time, you can help them think through the practical steps and connect them with people who can help. The best support is specific and action-oriented. (Read references/3-techniques.md.)

### Scenario 5: Person Who Feels Their Dream Is Too Late
You're older. You feel like you missed your window. The dream feels ridiculous at your age.
**Solution**: There is no age limit on dreams. Some of the most significant achievements in history came from people who started late. What you have now that you didn't have at 25 is perspective, resilience, and a clearer sense of what matters. The question is not "Am I too old?" but "What would I regret never having tried?" (Read references/5-voice-and-app.md.)

> **Case: Bob Goff's Own Story** (Introduction)
> Goff didn't have an easy path. He describes multiple failed businesses, a period where he felt completely lost, and the slow process of discovering what he truly wanted. His dream — to help people discover and pursue their own dreams — emerged not from a single moment of clarity but from years of trial, error, and persistent action. This is the book's most powerful asset: Goff is not a theorist. He's someone who lived what he writes.
> **Key takeaway**: The person who writes about pursuing dreams is not always someone who has always known their dream. More often, they're someone who stumbled, failed, and gradually found their way. Your path doesn't need to be linear to be valid.

> **Case: The Love That Sustains the Dream** (Chapter 12)
> Goff describes how his wife Maria and his family have been central to his ability to pursue big dreams. They are not passive supporters — they are active co-creators, offering ideas, honest feedback, and the kind of tough love that only family can provide. Goff warns: don't sacrifice your closest relationships for your dream. The dream that costs you the people you love is a dream pursued wrong.
> **Key takeaway**: The people closest to you are not obstacles to your dream — they are partners in it. If your dream requires you to damage your primary relationships, something has gone wrong. Realign before proceeding.
