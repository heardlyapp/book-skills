# Anti-Patterns — His Truth Is Marching On

## The Central Error: Sanctifying Lewis Without Emulating Him

"The witness of a Lewis and of a King and a Malcolm and a host of others was — and is — necessary to reform a nation in which racist ideas still prevail." The danger is treating Lewis as an untouchable icon rather than a model to follow.

### Mistake 1: "Saints are perfect people"
Lewis was no plaster saint. "He could be prideful and stubborn," Meacham acknowledges. The 1986 congressional campaign against his old friend Julian Bond was "tumultuous." Lowery's jab: "I never heard anybody in the black community say that [Lewis is a saint]." Lewis was human. That's what made him a useful model — his flaws made his achievements real.

### Mistake 2: "Nonviolence is weak or passive"
Malcolm X argued for self-defense, including violence if necessary. Lewis rejected this — but respected Malcolm. "Nonviolence is for the courageous," Lawson taught. Lewis proved it: beaten, clubbed, gassed, arrested 45 times, never striking back.

### Mistake 3: "Hope means things will get better on their own"
Lewis' hope was active, not passive. "Without the Utopians the experiment fails." He did not wait for change — he marched toward it. "The arc of the moral universe is long, but it bends toward justice" — but the arc won't bend "without devoted Americans pressing for the swerve."

### Mistake 4: "You should follow the established leadership"
Thurgood Marshall told Lewis to stop the Rides. NAACP leaders opposed the Freedom Rides. Kennedy asked for a "cooling-off period." Lewis and his generation knew something the older generation didn't: sometimes direct action is necessary. "This was not about being allowed to sit separately at a counter. It was about being treated exactly the same."

### Mistake 5: "The movement is over when you're de-elected"
Lewis was pushed out of SNCC in 1966 as it grew militant. He went to Congress for 33 years. The movement didn't end — it evolved. "In the final analysis, we are one people, one family, one house."

### Mistake 6: "Charisma is essential for leadership"
"Quietly charismatic, forever courtly, implacably serene" — but Lewis was not a great orator like King. He froze at his first speaking opportunity. He was short, stocky, dark-skinned — "not what you would call elegant." Yet he bent history. Leadership does not require eloquence.

### Mistake 7: "You can be a saint alone"
Lewis didn't do it alone. Diane Nash, Jim Lawson, Bernard Lafayette, Fannie Lou Hamer, Martin Luther King Jr. — the Beloved Community was a community. "The people of conscience never left the house. They stayed together."
