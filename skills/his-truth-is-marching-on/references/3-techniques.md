# Techniques — His Truth Is Marching On

## How to Live Like John Lewis (Based on Meacham's Portrait)

### Technique 1: Train for Suffering Before You Suffer
Lawson's workshops were not gentle. Students role-played being beaten, being dragged, being screamed at. "The discipline of nonviolence was as rigorous as the training for any athlete or any soldier." Lewis did this for months before the first sit-in. When real violence came — Wooden crate on his head on the Freedom Ride, state troopers on the bridge — he didn't flinch because he had already rehearsed it.

### Technique 2: Use Memory as Fuel, Not Weight
Lewis remembered Frank Carter. He remembered the rusty spigot. He remembered Recy Taylor and Jesse Thornton. But those memories did not make him bitter — they made him committed. The contrast between the "silver fountain" and "rusty spigot" burned into his consciousness and drove his life's work.

### Technique 3: Take Failure as a Call to Continue
Lewis' first speech for King — he froze. Stood at the podium for 30 seconds, couldn't speak, fled the room. King told him: "You can do this. The Lord will see you through." He learned. By 1963, he was one of the speakers at the March on Washington.

### Technique 4: Let the Work Outlast the Season
"John Lewis had the remarkable degree of toughness, of inner courage, of commitment," Diane Nash said. "I always had the impression that he was just never going to let anybody turn him around." When SNCC turned militant and de-elected him, Lewis didn't stop. When he was diagnosed with cancer, he didn't stop. The tools were adaptable; the mission was not.

### Technique 5: Replace Fear with Preparation
Lewis was afraid. Everyone was. But "the discipline of nonviolence" replaced raw fear with prepared response. He knew how he would react when beaten. He had practiced. The technique: don't pretend fear doesn't exist — train past it.

### Technique 6: Build for the Long Arc
Meacham: "Without the Utopians the experiment fails, for without a peak to scale we would remain in the valley." Lewis believed in the arc — but knew it only bends through human action. His daily discipline: one march, one arrest, one speech, one vote at a time.

### Technique 7: Speak Your Farewell
Lewis' final public appearance was his last march across the bridge. He knew he was dying. He spoke plainly: "We have a lot of work to do. So don't get weary! Keep the faith!" He did not say goodbye — he gave marching orders.
