# Core Framework — His Truth Is Marching On

## Overture: The Last March (Selma, March 2020)

John Lewis returned to the Edmund Pettus Bridge for the 55th annual commemoration — weeks after his 80th birthday, dying of pancreatic cancer. Frail but unbowed, he led the pilgrimage and spoke: "We were beaten. Tear-gassed. Bullwhipped. On this bridge, some of us gave a little blood to help redeem the soul of America."

A voice called from the crowd: "We love you, John." He replied: "I — I love you, too." Then: "We have a lot of work to do. So don't get weary! Keep the faith! Keep picking 'em up and puttin' 'em down."

He died four months later, on July 17, 2020. "This is the last march," he said before leaving for Selma. It was.

## Chapter 1: A Hard Life, A Serious Life (1940-1957)

**The biography of a saint.** Meacham's thesis: Lewis was "a saint in the classical Christian sense." Time magazine named him a "Living Saint" in 1975, alongside Mother Teresa and Dorothy Day. James Lawson agreed without reservation: "He clearly tried to be one who followed Jesus."

**Great-grandfather Frank Carter:** Born into slavery in 1862 in Pike County, Alabama. Lewis knew him until age 7. Carter was "light, very fair" — a white man was likely his father, a subject shrouded in silence. Carter couldn't read or write but "could do financial transactions in his head faster than the man on the other side of the desk."

**The lynching of Jesse Thornton (June 1940):** When Lewis was 4 months old, a 26-year-old black man in nearby Luverne was lynched for failing to call a white policeman "Mister." His body was dumped in a swamp. "The cause of his death is a mystery that will probably never be solved."

**The rape of Recy Taylor (1944):** When Lewis was 4, a 24-year-old black woman was kidnapped, gang-raped by seven white men after church. Two all-white grand juries refused to indict. Rosa Parks investigated the case as an NAACP fieldworker. "I can't help but tell the truth of what they done to me."

**Lewis' response:** He saw the "silver fountain for white people and the rusty spigot for colored drinking." The contrast burned itself into his consciousness. "From my earliest memories, I was fundamentally disturbed by the unbridled meanness of the world around me."

**Using the handsaw:** At age 9, Lewis and his cousin Della Mae grabbed a handsaw and went to cut down the tallest pine tree they could find — "because we were going to build ourselves a bus and roll right out of Alabama."

## Chapter 2: The Spirit of History (1957-1960)

**Buffalo (1951):** At 11, Lewis visited relatives in New York. Rode an escalator for the first time. Couldn't believe blacks and whites mixed together freely. White families lived next door to his Buffalo relatives "on both sides."

**The radio preacher (1955):** At 15, heard a young minister named Martin Luther King Jr. on the radio. "I felt something. I felt a connection. I knew I wanted to do what he was doing." He wrote to King, who responded and sent him a bus ticket.

**Meeting King (1958):** Lewis traveled to Montgomery. King greeted him: "Are you the boy from Troy? Is John Lewis the boy from Troy?" Then King asked Lewis to speak at a church. Lewis froze — stood at the podium for 30 seconds unable to speak, then fled. King told him: "You can do this. The Lord will see you through."

**American Baptist Theological Seminary (1957-1961):** Lewis enrolled at ABT in Nashville. There he met Rev. James Lawson, who was leading Tuesday night workshops in Gandhian nonviolence.

## Chapter 3: Soul Force (1960-1961)

**Lawson's workshops:** "The discipline of nonviolence was as rigorous as the training for any athlete or any soldier." Students role-played being attacked, being spat on, being dragged from their seats. "We learned to see the attacker as a human being."

**The first Nashville sit-in (February 1960):** Lewis was among the first to sit at Woolworth's. They wore suits and dresses — a strategic choice to highlight the contrast with jeering white mobs. The Krystal fumigation: locked inside while manager turned on insecticide fogger. Bevel chanted "Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego." Fire department broke them out.

**The Freedom Rides (May 1961):** Lewis was not on the Anniston bus — a last-minute trip to Philadelphia saved his life. "When I heard the news, I felt shock and guilt." He led the continuation from Nashville. In Montgomery, a mob of several hundred attacked. Lewis was beaten with a wooden crate. "I remember lying on the concrete floor, blood streaming into my eyes."

## Chapter 4: In the Image of God and Democracy (1963-1964)

**Chairman of SNCC at 23:** Elected June 1963. $10/week salary. Six shirts total. Moved to Atlanta.

**March on Washington (August 28, 1963):** Lewis was the youngest speaker. His original speech was too radical — criticized the Kennedy bill as "too little, too late." The Archbishop of Washington demanded changes. Lewis toned it down. "Which side is the federal government on?" became "Wake up America!"

**The bomb that killed four girls (September 15, 1963):** 16th Street Baptist Church, Birmingham. Lewis: "I was there that night, standing in the rubble, trying to make sense of something that made no sense."

## Chapter 5: We Are Going to Make You Wish You Was Dead (1965)

**Bloody Sunday (March 7, 1965):** Lewis and Hosea Williams led 600 marchers across the Edmund Pettus Bridge. State troopers ordered them to disperse. Lewis recalled turning to look back at the crowd, then hearing "Troopers, advance!"

He was clubbed on the head. Skull fractured. Concussion. Lying on the pavement, he was ready to die. "I thought, this is it. This is what they do. And I was not afraid."

The nation watched on television. Eight days later, President Johnson introduced the Voting Rights Act. "The images of that Sunday helped push LBJ to call for legislation guaranteeing voting rights."

## Chapter 6-7: The Congressman (1987-2020)

**De-election from SNCC:** Lewis was removed as chairman in 1966 as SNCC turned militant under Stokely Carmichael. "I was sad, but not bitter. I had not changed; the movement had."

**The 1986 congressional campaign against Julian Bond:** A bitter contest. Lewis won an open seat in Georgia's 5th district after a hard-fought primary against his old friend. Lowery: "John Lewis gives the appearance of humility. I don't know whether he's humble or not, but white folk think he's humble."

**17 terms in the House:** Known as "the conscience of the Congress." Led sit-ins on the House floor in 2016 for gun control. John McCain: "I've walked across the Edmund Pettus Bridge with John Lewis. I know what it means. He earned the right to be angry when things go wrong."

**The diagnosis (December 2019):** Stage 4 pancreatic cancer. "I have been in some kind of fight — for freedom, equality, basic human rights — for nearly my entire life. I have never faced a fight quite the one I have now."

**The afterword:** Written by Lewis shortly before his death. "When you see something that is not right, you must say something. You must do something. Democracy is not a state. It is an act. Get in good trouble, necessary trouble."
