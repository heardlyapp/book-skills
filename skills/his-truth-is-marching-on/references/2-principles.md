# Key Principles — His Truth Is Marching On

## I. Hope Is Not Wishful Thinking — It Is an Act of Will

Meacham's central argument: "Lewis believed hope shaped history." He rejected the view that human appetites and ambition are the controlling forces of history. Instead, he insisted that "the better angels of our nature" could prevail — if men and women devoted themselves to making it so. "We have to be hopeful. Never give up, never give in, keep moving on."

## II. The Beloved Community Is the Kingdom of God on Earth

Lewis defined it: "Nothing less than the Christian concept of the Kingdom of God on earth. All human existence throughout history has strived toward community, toward coming together. That movement is as inexorable, as irresistible, as the flow of a river toward the sea." This was not a vague ideal — it was a concrete goal that guided every decision, every march, every arrest.

## III. Sainthood Is Measured by What You Suffer for Others

"One test of a saint, closely tied to the test of a martyr, is the willingness to suffer and die for others. Which Lewis was willing to do — again and again and again." He was beaten, tear-gassed, bullwhipped, arrested 45 times. His skull was fractured. "He was always on the front line, wherever the front line was." — Bernard LaFayette.

## IV. Costly Grace Requires Everything

Meacham quotes Bonhoeffer: "Costly grace is costly because it costs a man his life, and it is grace because it gives a man the only true life." Lewis embodied this exact idea — he did not see sacrifice as a burden but as the natural price of following Jesus. His mother's hands, split from cotton fields, were the first evidence that suffering was part of love.

## V. The Opposition Has a Face — Look at It With Love

Lawson taught: "The enemy is not the person who hates you. The enemy is the hatred itself." Lewis looked his attackers in the eye. When Bull Connor drove Lewis to the Tennessee line at midnight and left him in Klan country, Lewis refused to respond to Connor's attempts at small talk. Not out of anger — out of discipline. "We fought with love."

## VI. You Can Outlast the Movement — and You Must

Lewis was de-elected as SNCC chairman in 1966. He went on to serve 33 years in Congress. He was still leading sit-ins at age 76 (on the House floor, for gun control). "In the final analysis, we are one people, one family, one house," he said in 2020. "There may be some setbacks. But we are going to get there."

## VII. Democracy Is an Act, Not a State

Lewis' afterword: "Democracy is not a state. It is an act." The vote was the most powerful weapon. "When you see something that is not right, you must say something. You must do something. Get in good trouble, necessary trouble." He was still marching toward Selma as he was dying.
