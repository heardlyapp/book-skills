# 5 — Voice & App

> Source: *The Big Book of Serial Killers* by Jack Rosewood & Rebecca Lo.

## The Book's Voice

The book is clinical and encyclopedic — each profile follows the same structure: Background → Murders → Arrest and Trial → Outcome → Trivia. The tone is factual, never sensationalized. The horror is in the facts themselves, not in the telling.

> **Example: David Berkowitz Profile Structure** — Background (adoption, birth circumstances, army service) → Murders (6 documented shootings, each with date and location) → Arrest (witness saw car being ticketed) → Trial (pleaded guilty despite insanity defense) → Outcome (365 years, multiple parole denials) → Trivia (quotes: "I am a monster. I am the Son of Sam.")

> **Example: Jeffrey Dahmer's Final Quote** — Asked at sentencing if he had anything to say, Dahmer responded: "I have no excuse for what I did. I don't know how to explain it." The book's refusal to editorialize makes the horror hit harder.

## Five Scenarios

### 1. The True Crime Writer

Use geographic profiling to identify unknown killer patterns. Map victim locations. Look for the center of gravity of early victims.

### 2. The Psychology Student

Study the typologies: organized/disorganized, the four motivations, the escalation pattern. Each profile is a case study in criminal psychology.

### 3. The Crime Fiction Author

Use real arrest methods from the book — the parking ticket, the escaped victim, the traffic stop — to make your fictional investigations realistic.

### 4. The Forensic Science Learner

Follow the forensic timeline: pre-fingerprint → fingerprint → blood typing → DNA. Each case shows the forensic state-of-the-art of its time.

### 5. The Safety-Conscious Reader

Victimology is not about fear — it's about awareness. Killers target availability and vulnerability. Understanding the selection criteria allows better risk assessment.
