# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *The Big Book of Serial Killers* by Jack Rosewood & Rebecca Lo — David Berkowitz, Ted Bundy, Richard Chase, Jeffrey Dahmer, John Wayne Gacy, Ed Kemper profiles.

## The Two Frameworks

### Framework A: Organized vs. Disorganized

The FBI's foundational division of serial killers.

**Organized:** Plans ahead, selects specific victim types, brings weapons, exerts control during the kill, conceals bodies. Often has above-average intelligence, is socially competent, and follows the case in the media.

**Disorganized:** Kills impulsively, uses available weapons, leaves the body in view, often kills near home or work. Typically lower intelligence, socially isolated, and does not follow the media coverage.

> **Example: Ted Bundy (Organized)** — Bundy was charming, articulate, and law student. He lured victims by feigning injury (asking women to help him load something into his car). He drove them to remote locations, raped and murdered them, and often returned to the bodies. He followed the investigation closely and even attended a victim identification meeting. His planning and control made him virtually impossible to catch through witness descriptions.

> **Example: Richard Chase (Disorganized)** — "The Vampire of Sacramento" was deeply delusional (believed Nazis were poisoning his blood). He killed randomly near his home, drank victims' blood, and mutilated bodies in ways that served no logical purpose. He left evidence everywhere. He was caught quickly because his crimes had no plan or concealment. The organized/disorganized divide is not about intelligence — it's about method.

### Framework B: The Capture Patterns

Despite the myth of the brilliant profiler, serial killers are almost always caught through routine police work.

> **Example: David Berkowitz (Son of Sam)** — Berkowitz was caught because a witness saw his car being ticketed near a fire hydrant the night of a shooting. The parking ticket records led police to his address. He had been parking illegally and getting tickets — and that is what led to his capture. Not profiling. Not a dramatic DNA match. A parking ticket.

> **Example: Jeffrey Dahmer** — Dahmer was caught because a potential victim, Tracy Edwards, escaped from Dahmer's apartment while handcuffed and flagged down police officers. The officers returned to Dahmer's apartment, found Polaroid photos of mutilated bodies, and arrested him. Routine response to a man in handcuffs led to one of the most notorious captures in history.
