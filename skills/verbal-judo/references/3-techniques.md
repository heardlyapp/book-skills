## Practical Techniques

### 1. The "You May Be Right" Response

When criticized, respond: "You may be right. Can you tell me more?" This disarms the attacker and opens a conversation.

### 2. The Paraphrase Check

After someone speaks, paraphrase: "So what I hear you saying is..." This confirms understanding and makes the other person feel heard.

### 3. The Empathy Statement

"I can see why you would feel that way." This is not agreeing — it is acknowledging. The distinction is crucial.

> **Case: The Disgruntled Customer** (Chapter 6): A customer is angry about a product. Instead of defending the product, the representative says: "I can see why you would be frustrated. Let me see what I can do." The customer calms down.
> **Key takeaway**: Empathy is not agreement. It is validation. When people feel validated, they stop fighting.

### 4. The Option Give

Instead of "Do this," say "You can either do X or Y. Which works for you?" Options create the feeling of control.

### 5. The Pause

When attacked, pause for two seconds before responding. The pause breaks the reaction cycle.

### 6. The Strip Phrase

When someone uses loaded language (name-calling, accusations), "strip" the phrase of its emotional charge by repeating it neutrally. "You said I am incompetent. Tell me specifically what I did that was incompetent."

> **Case: The Strip Phrase** (Chapter 8): A man calls an officer "racist." Instead of defending, the officer says: "You said I am racist. Help me understand what I did that seemed that way." The man explains his perspective. The conversation moves forward.
> **Key takeaway**: When someone attacks with a label, strip the label by asking for specifics. The specifics are usually less inflammatory than the label.
