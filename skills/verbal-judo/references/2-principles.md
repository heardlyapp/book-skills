## 7 Principles of Verbal Deflection

### 1. Redirect, Don't Resist

When someone attacks, do not defend. Redirect their energy. Defense escalates. Redirection resolves.

### 2. Dignity Is Everything

People cooperate when they feel respected. Treat everyone with dignity, even — especially — when they are not treating you with dignity.

> **Case: The Arrest Without Force** (Chapter 7): Thompson trained officers to say to a suspect: "I need you to put your hands behind your back. I know this is not what you want, but we can sort it out at the station. Will you work with me?" The suspect complied without resistance.
> **Key takeaway**: Even in the most coercive situation, treating a person with dignity reduces the likelihood of violence.

### 3. Acknowledge Before Redirect

Before you can redirect someone, you must acknowledge their position. "I hear you. I understand." Then redirect.

### 4. "You May Be Right"

These four words are the most powerful in verbal judo. They do not admit fault. They open the door.

### 5. Questions Over Statements

A question engages the brain. A statement triggers resistance. Ask, do not tell.

### 6. The 5 Universal Truths

Everyone wants dignity, to be asked (not told), to understand why, to have options, and to get a second chance.

### 7. Professionalism Is Control

Staying calm when the other person is not is the definition of professionalism. You cannot control them — but you can control yourself.

> **Case: The Roommate Conflict** (Chapter 9): A roommate leaves dirty dishes. Instead of saying "Wash your dishes," (telling, no dignity) try: "Hey, when the dishes pile up, it creates a smell. Could we maybe keep them clean together?" (explain why, offer choice).
> **Key takeaway**: The same request framed differently gets dramatically different results. Tell less, ask more.
