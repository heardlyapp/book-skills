## 6 Anti-Patterns of Verbal Conflict

### 1. "I Must Defend Myself"

Defense escalates. When you defend, you signal that the attack hit its target. The attacker continues.

### 2. "Telling Them to Calm Down Works"

Telling an angry person to calm down is the fastest way to make them angrier. Acknowledge first. Then redirect.

### 3. "Winning the Argument Is the Goal"

The goal is not to win — it is to resolve. Winning an argument can lose a relationship.

> **Case: The Married Couple** (Chapter 9): Thompson describes a couple arguing over whose turn it is to do dishes. The argument is not about dishes — it is about respect. The verbal judo approach: acknowledge the feeling, find the real issue.
> **Key takeaway:** The surface issue is rarely the real issue. Address the underlying need (respect, control, fairness) and the surface issue resolves.

### 4. "Apologizing Is Weakness"

Apologizing for the situation (not for fault) shows strength. "I am sorry this happened to you" is not an admission of guilt — it is empathy.

### 5. "If I Give Options, I Lose Control"

Giving options gives the other person a sense of control — which makes them more likely to cooperate. You are not losing control — you are gaining their voluntary compliance.

### 6. "Some People Are Just Unreachable"

There are genuinely difficult people. But most people who seem unreachable are simply not being approached the right way.

> **Case: The Man Who Would Not Exit** (Chapter 1): A man refused to leave a building. Security called police. The officer used verbal judo: "Sir, I understand you have your reasons. But we need you to step outside. Will you do that for me?" The man complied. No force needed.
> **Key takeaway**: Most people want to cooperate. They just need to feel heard first.
