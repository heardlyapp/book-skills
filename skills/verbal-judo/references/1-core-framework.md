## The Thompson Framework: Redirect, Don't Resist

Thompson's framework is based on a simple insight: when someone attacks you verbally, your instinct to defend makes things worse. The alternative is to redirect — use their energy toward resolution.

**The Key Insight**: People are not resisting you — they are resisting the feeling of being controlled. Give them dignity, and they will cooperate.

> **Case: The Traffic Stop** (Chapter 2): Thompson describes a traffic stop where the driver immediately became hostile. Instead of escalating ("Sir, you need to calm down"), Thompson said: "I understand this is frustrating. Let me explain why I stopped you." The driver calmed down. The redirect worked.
> **Key takeaway**: When someone is angry, do not tell them to calm down. Acknowledge their feeling, then redirect.

> **Case: The Angry Spouse** (Chapter 5): A man calls his wife a name. She could defend herself or attack back. Instead, she says: "You may be right. What is really bothering you?" The man stops. He realizes he is not angry at her — he is stressed about work.
> **Key takeaway**: "You may be right" is not surrender. It is a tactical pause that opens the door to real communication.
Thompson's training emphasizes that the redirect is not manipulation — it is respect. You are not tricking people. You are treating them with dignity, which makes cooperation natural.
 This is the essence of verbal judo.
 Respect is the foundation.
