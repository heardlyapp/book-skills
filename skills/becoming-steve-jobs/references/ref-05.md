# Reference: The Creative Partnership

## Overview
Jobs could not have built Apple alone. His partnerships with Wozniak, Ive, Cook, Catmull, and Lasseter were essential to his success.

## Case: Jobs and Wozniak
Steve Wozniak was the engineering genius behind the Apple I and II. Jobs was the business mind. Their partnership was complementary: Wozniak wanted to build elegant machines; Jobs wanted to sell them. The partnership ended when Jobs left Apple, and they never fully reconciled. Wozniak's assessment: "Jobs was the business guy, and I was the technical guy. That worked for a while, but it wasn't sustainable."

## Case: Jobs and Jony Ive
Ive was the design genius behind the iMac, iPod, iPhone, and iPad. Jobs recognized Ive's talent and protected him from the corporate bureaucracy. They ate lunch together every day. Ive said Jobs was "the most remarkable human being I've ever met." After Jobs's death, Ive struggled to maintain Apple's design culture without Jobs's editing. The partnership was the most productive in industrial design history.

## Case: Jobs and Tim Cook
Cook was Jobs's operational opposite — calm, methodical, and ruthlessly efficient. Jobs hired Cook in 1998 to fix Apple's supply chain. Cook did that and then took over day-to-day operations. Cook's mastery of logistics — turning inventory into cash — was essential to Apple's profitability. Jobs did not groom Cook to be his creative successor; he groomed Cook to run the company so that Jobs could focus on products.
