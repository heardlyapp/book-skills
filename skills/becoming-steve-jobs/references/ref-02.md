# Reference: The Wilderness Years

## Overview
Jobs's ouster from Apple in 1985 was the most painful event of his professional life — and the most necessary. The 12 years away from Apple transformed him from a brilliant but destructive force into a leader capable of building a lasting institution.

## Case: NeXT's Commercial Failure
Jobs poured $100 million of his own money into NeXT, a company that built beautiful, expensive computers that almost no one bought. The NeXT cube was a design masterpiece — a perfect black magnesium cube — but it cost $6,500, had no software ecosystem, and sold fewer than 50,000 units. The company failed as a computer maker. But the NeXT operating system became the foundation of macOS, and the web was invented on a NeXT machine. Jobs learned that great products need great business models.

## Case: Pixar's Unexpected Success
Jobs bought Pixar from George Lucas for $10 million in 1986. He poured another $50 million into it before Toy Story (1995) made it profitable. Jobs initially saw Pixar as a hardware company (the Pixar Image Computer). Ed Catmull and John Lasseter taught him that the real opportunity was storytelling, not technology. Jobs learned to let creative people lead creative work — a lesson that transformed Apple.

## Case: Jobs's Personal Transformation
During the wilderness years, Jobs married Laurene Powell, had children, and reconnected with his biological sister, author Mona Simpson. He mellowed. He learned to trust people. When he returned to Apple in 1997, he was not the same person who had left in 1985. "I would have never been so successful at Apple if I hadn't been fired," he later reflected.
