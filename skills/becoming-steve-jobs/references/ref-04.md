# Reference: Jobs's Leadership Evolution

## Overview
Jobs's leadership style evolved dramatically over his career. The young Jobs was brilliant, tyrannical, and incapable of building a lasting organization. The mature Jobs retained his intensity but learned to channel it productively.

## Case: The Early Jobs — Tyrannical Genius
In the early Macintosh days, Jobs told an employee who had spent months on a design: "You just don't get it. You're going to be a janitor when you leave here." He called people idiots, refused to acknowledge paternity of his daughter Lisa, and parked in handicapped spaces. His intensity attracted brilliant people who wanted to change the world, but his cruelty alienated the same people.

## Case: The Mature Jobs — Demanding but Inspiring
After his return to Apple, Jobs was still demanding — perhaps more demanding — but he had learned to inspire rather than terrorize. He told his team: "This is the most important thing I'll ever do." He pushed them to create the iPhone in 30 months, refusing to compromise on the screen, the materials, or the software. The difference was that he now inspired people to exceed their own expectations rather than driving them to exhaustion.

## Case: Building a Successor
In his final years, Jobs focused on building an Apple that could survive without him. He groomed Tim Cook, protected the design culture, and embedded Apple's values in its processes. When Jobs died in 2011, Apple's stock briefly dipped but recovered. The company continued to innovate — not because Jobs was still there, but because he had built an institution that could sustain his vision.
