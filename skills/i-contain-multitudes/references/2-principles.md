# Symbiosis and Co-Evolution

> Source: I Contain Multitudes, Chapters 2, 4, 6, 7

## The Symbiosis Spectrum

Symbiosis means "living together." It is not a single type of relationship but a spectrum. Most importantly, the same pair of organisms can shift along this spectrum depending on context — a helpful gut microbe can become harmful if the immune system is compromised.

| Relationship | Host benefits? | Microbe benefits? | Example |
|-------------|---------------|-------------------|---------|
| Mutualism | Yes | Yes | Bobtail squid and Vibrio fischeri |
| Commensalism | Neutral | Yes | Most human skin bacteria |
| Parasitism | No | Yes | Mycobacterium tuberculosis |
| Amensalism | No | No | Certain competitive gut microbes |
| Context-dependent | Sometimes | Sometimes | Helicobacter pylori: harmful in some, protective in others |

## The Greatest Symbiosis of All

The eukaryotic cell — the building block of all animals, plants, fungi, and algae — originated from a symbiosis. Around 2 billion years ago, a bacterium merged with an archaeon. The bacterium lost its free-living existence and became the mitochondrion — the energy powerhouse of every eukaryotic cell. This single union has never been duplicated since. It's why all complex life on Earth exists.

## Partner Choice and Control

Hosts are not passive vessels. Animals actively select their microbial partners through:
- **Immune tolerance** — The immune system must learn to tolerate beneficial microbes while attacking pathogens
- **Antimicrobial secretions** — Many animals produce chemicals that selectively kill some microbes while nurturing others
- **Physical structures** — Specialized organs (like the squid's light organ) create exclusive habitats for specific symbionts
- **Vertical transmission** — Many animals pass their symbionts directly to offspring, ensuring continuity

## The Long Waltz of Co-Evolution

Hosts and their microbes evolve together. Changes in one drive changes in the other. This co-evolution means that the microbiomes of related animal species are more similar than those of distant relatives. The human microbiome is distinct from the chimpanzee microbiome, which differs from the gorilla microbiome — just as the forests of Borneo differ from those of Madagascar.

> **Case: The Bobtail Squid and Vibrio fischeri** (Chapter 4)
> The Hawaiian bobtail squid is one of the most studied models in symbiosis. It hunts at night and uses light-producing bacteria (Vibrio fischeri) in a specialized light organ to camouflage itself against the moonlit water. The squid hatches without any bacteria. Within hours, it actively recruits Vibrio fischeri from the surrounding seawater while excluding all other microbial species. The squid provides nutrients; the bacteria provide light. The light organ has a sophisticated lens, reflector, and shutter — all evolved to manage the partnership. Every morning, the squid vents 90% of its bacteria into the ocean and allows the remaining 10% to repopulate over the day, maintaining a healthy population.
> **Key takeaway**: Symbiosis is actively managed, not passively received. The squid's light organ is as complex as an eye, but its function is to house and control microbes. This model shows that animals evolved sophisticated organs specifically for managing microbial partnerships.

> **Case: Termites and Their Gut Microbes** (Chapter 6)
> Termites are famous for eating wood — one of the toughest substances in nature. They can do this because their guts contain a complex community of microbes (bacteria and protozoa) that break down cellulose into digestible compounds. The partnership goes deeper: the microbes also fix nitrogen (converting atmospheric N2 into usable forms), supplementing the termite's wood-based diet. The termite gut is structured like a bioreactor with different chambers for different microbial communities. Without these microbes, termites would starve.
> **Key takeaway**: Symbiosis enables capabilities that neither partner could achieve alone. The termite's ability to eat wood is not a property of the termite — it's a property of the termite-plus-microbes system. This is true of most animals for most of their capabilities.
