# The Microbiome as an Ecosystem

> Source: I Contain Multitudes, Prologue, Chapter 1

## Core Framework

Every animal is a living ecosystem. The concepts ecologists use to describe rainforests, coral reefs, and grasslands apply equally to the microbial communities living in and on our bodies. Understanding this framework is the foundation of the entire book.

### Key Ecological Concepts Applied to the Microbiome

| Ecological Concept | Microbiome Equivalent | Example |
|--------------------|----------------------|---------|
| Habitat | Body site (gut, skin, mouth, genitals) | The skin microbiome is dominated by Propionibacterium; the gut by Bacteroides |
| Succession | Colonization from birth to adulthood | Bifidobacterium dominate in milk-fed infants; Bacteroides take over after weaning |
| Keystone species | Microbes with disproportionate influence | Certain Clostridium species that regulate immune T-cell development |
| Invasive species | Pathogens disrupting a healthy community | Clostridium difficile after antibiotic treatment |
| Ecosystem collapse | Dysbiosis — loss of microbial diversity | Inflammatory bowel disease, severe antibiotic disruption |
| Island biogeography | Each host body is an island | Your right hand shares only 1/6 of its species with your left hand |
| Disturbance and recovery | Antibiotics, diet change, illness | Recovery can take weeks to months; sometimes never returns to original state |

### The Host as Archipelago

Each animal body is not a single island but a chain of islands — different body sites host different microbial communities. The variations between body sites are larger than the variations between people: the bacteria on your forearm are more similar to those on my forearm than to those in your mouth.

### Core Functions, Not Core Species

Early researchers hoped to find a "core microbiome" — a set of species present in every human. They failed. No single bacterial species is universal. Instead, the core exists at the functional level: certain jobs (digesting fiber, producing vitamin K, training the immune system) are always performed by some microbe — just not the same one in every person. This functional redundancy makes the ecosystem resilient: if one species is lost, another can take over its role.

> **Case: The Pangolin at San Diego Zoo** (Prologue)
> Rob Knight swabs Baba the pangolin's face, body, paws, and scales. Each site yields a different microbial community. The pangolin is not one animal — it's thousands of microbial ecosystems living together. Knight has swabbed his own body parts too, as has Ed Yong, as has every creature in the zoo. Every animal is a zoo in its own right. This single image — a scientist gently swabbing a pangolin's nose — encapsulates the book's central insight.
> **Key takeaway**: We can see the macro-scale pattern (every animal carries a unique microbiome) by doing for microbes what Darwin and Wallace did for visible species: catalog who lives where and ask why.

> **Case: The 10:1 Ratio Myth** (Chapter 1)
> For years, every article, TED talk, and textbook repeated the claim that we contain 10 microbial cells for every human cell — making us rounding errors in our own bodies. Yong traces this number to a single "back-of-the-envelope calculation" by microbiologist Thomas Luckey in the 1970s, which became enshrined as fact through endless repetition. The latest estimates: roughly 30 trillion human cells and 39 trillion microbial cells — a near-even split.
> **Key takeaway**: Scientific "facts" about the microbiome deserve skepticism. The field is young, numbers are inexact, and a compelling story (we're mostly bacteria!) can overwhelm the evidence. The truth — that we contain multitudes, whether 10:1 or 1:1 — is remarkable enough without exaggeration.
