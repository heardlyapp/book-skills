# Anti-Patterns — Going Nuclear Too Early, Forgetting Dependencies, Buying into All-or-Nothing

## The Central Fallacy

The most dangerous mistake: believing that you must either fully embrace Google or fully leave it. Thomas calls this "all-or-nothing thinking." You can leave Gmail and keep YouTube. You can switch to ProtonMail and keep Google Maps. Every step toward privacy counts, even if you never complete the full migration.

> **Case: The Nuclear Regret** (Step 6): Users who delete their account too early discover they cannot log into services they forgot existed. They lose access to purchased apps. They lose years of photos. The book's entire structure is designed to prevent this. The final chapter — "Going Nuclear" — is only reached after five chapters of preparation.
> **Key takeaway**: Delete last. Migrate first. This is the single most important rule of leaving any platform.

> **Case: The Hidden Login** (Step 3): Many websites and apps use "Sign in with Google" instead of letting you create a separate account. Stack Overflow, Quora, Medium, and countless others. When you delete your Google account, you lose access to these accounts unless you changed the login method first. This is the "hidden login" problem.
> **Key takeaway**: Check every service you use for "Sign in with Google" buttons. Change those accounts to username/password before you delete.

> **Case: The Perfectionism Trap** (Introduction): Thomas addresses this directly: "You don't have to do everything at once. You don't have to do it perfectly. Any step toward privacy is a step worth taking." The person who decides "if I can't leave Google completely, I won't bother at all" is making the perfect the enemy of the good.
> **Key takeaway**: Partial migration is still migration. If you only switch to a privacy-respecting browser, you have made progress. If you switch your email and keep Maps, that is progress. Do not let perfectionism stop you.
