# Techniques — Scientific Methodology and Modeling

> Source: Thermoinfocomplexity, Chapter 2–5

## Stochastic Systems and Probability

Thermoinfocomplexity models systems as fundamentally stochastic. The laws governing reactions and evolution describe which events are more likely than others — not deterministic certainties. This is a crucial methodological departure from physics' traditional "hard science" approach.

> "The laws which govern a stochastic system will themselves not be true in a deterministic sense; they will, rather, describe which events are more likely than others to occur."

Mohit distinguishes between **proximate explanations** (what happened) and **ultimate, physicochemical explanations** (how it happened). The book's central methodological contribution is providing the latter for evolutionary biology.

## The Information Manifold

A key methodological tool is the **information manifold** — a "mesh" on which physical and thermodynamic concepts (matter, energy, temperature) exist, interact, and may be observed. Derived from the Natural Quantum Theory of Information:

- **Theorem 1 (Information Topology):** Given any probability measure, the natural metric induces a smooth manifold structure. For classical discrete measures, the manifold is 0-dimensional. For continuous or quantum measures, it is higher-dimensional, with dimensions given by independent parameters of the associated Hamiltonian.

This tool allows scientists to study the geometric and topological properties of information spaces, connecting abstract probability to physical observation.

## Strange Attractors and Feedback Loops

Complex adaptive systems follow **attractor pathways** — sets of states the system is attracted to over time. Types include:

- **Point attractors:** A single stable state (often a state of lowest Gibbs free energy)
- **Limit cycles:** A one-dimensional closed loop the system follows repeatedly (e.g., an ideal pendulum)
- **Strange attractors:** Fractal-dimensional attractors with non-integer dimensions (e.g., Lorenz Attractor, estimated dimension ~2.05)

Systems develop through **positive feedback loops** (events that make themselves more likely) and **negative feedback loops** (events that make themselves less likely). The combination of both shapes the attractor landscape.

> "These dynamical systems can develop from self-interactions in various ways. One of the most important is through feedback loops. Positive feedback loops occur when an event makes itself even more likely to occur in the future... Negative feedback loops occur when an event makes itself less likely to occur in the future."

## Autocatalytic Sets

An **autocatalytic structure** is any distribution or pattern of energy flows that makes itself more likely to persist in the future. Mohit uses the metaphor of rain falling on a mountainside: water erodes channels that become deeper and smoother, making future water flow through these paths more likely.

In biology, **autocatalytic sets** — such as RNA enzymes (ribozymes) that catalyze the creation of protein enzymes, which in turn catalyze the creation of the original RNA — form the basis of metabolism and natural selection.

> "From the scale-free viewpoint of Thermoinfocomplexity, we can view an autocatalytic set of enzymes as a single object, and, should we choose to do so, discuss large populations of autocatalytic sets."

> **Case:** Use the water-on-mountainside metaphor to explain the emergence of metabolic pathways. Early Earth's primordial soup is like a mountain slope — energy (sunlight, geothermal heat) flows through it like water. Initial random chemical interactions create "channels" (proto-pathways) that become more efficient over time. These autocatalytic structures persist and deepen, eventually forming the stable metabolic networks we recognize in living cells.

> **Case:** Model a social network as a strange attractor. A city's economic activity follows patterns that arise from countless individual decisions. Like the Lorenz Attractor, the city's dynamics exist in a bounded region of state space but never exactly repeat. Positive feedback (success attracts more resources) and negative feedback (resource depletion limits growth) shape the attractor landscape, keeping the system within its basin of attraction.

## Allometric Scaling and Complexity Density

The book applies allometric scaling relationships (mass vs. metabolic rate) to demonstrate Thermoinfocomplexity principles. Geoffrey West's work shows that log-log relationships between animal mass and metabolic rate follow power laws derivable from three-dimensional space. Mohit argues these relationships can be improved by correlating **systemic complexity density** to energy efficiency — the more tightly networked a system, the more efficiently it distributes energy.

> "Intuitively, the more tightly-networked the system is—and thus the greater its systemic complexity—then the more it can distribute energy without waste."
