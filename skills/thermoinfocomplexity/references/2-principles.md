## Complexity Defined

For decades, complexity and emergence eluded rigorous mathematical definition (Kolmogorov, Gell-Mann, and the Santa Fe Institute produced useful but incomplete formulations). Thermoinfocomplexity fills this gap.

**Complexity = Expected Natural Information Content.** It is a real-valued invariant of an information-theoretic system that captures how much more information the whole possesses than the sum of its parts.

> "We define complexity as a real valued invariant of an information-theoretic system that intuitively captures the above ideas. This invariant can be motivationally derived from the principle that the whole is more than the sum of its parts."

## Emergence Defined

An **emergent system** is one at a **local maximum of complexity** — a system that is meaningfully its own entity, following rules different from those of its components. The emergent complexity (Γe) is an integer-valued invariant that can be given as the Euler characteristic of the terminal homology theory applied to the information manifold.

> "We define emergent systems to be those systems which are maximally complex, and an integer-based invariant of this system becomes its emergent complexity."

## Complex Adaptive Systems (CAS)

A CAS is an emergent system that **persists in its structure over time**. Molecules, organisms, ant colonies, and human societies are all examples. A flock of birds is a CAS; half a flock is not. A key property: emergent complexity is constant for a CAS — it does not undergo phase transitions. (Weak CASs, which can experience phase transitions, have at most countably many discontinuities corresponding to death or fundamental reorganization.)

## Self-Organization and Entrainment

Complex adaptive systems emerge through **entrainment** — the resonant coupling of periodic oscillations in metabolic pathways. When two or more systems couple, creating access to additional energy sources, entrained cooperation arises. This manifests in strange attractor pathways reflecting the newly coordinated metabolic activity of the emergent system.

> "The increased energy efficiency, formed in response to the depletion of an existing energy source, is the product of the resonant coupling of periodic oscillations in the metabolic pathways already established by the previously independent systems."

> **Case:** Analyze the evolution from single cells to multicellular organisms. Individual cells each have their own metabolic cycles. As energy becomes scarce, cells that couple their metabolic rhythms create a more energy-efficient system. The entrainment of these oscillations produces the coordinated behavior we recognize as a multicellular organism — a higher-order CAS operating at a local maximum of energy efficiency.

> **Case:** Apply the concept of strange attractors to understand an ecosystem's dynamics. Like Edward Lorenz's three-dimensional mathematical model of atmospheric convection, a predator-prey system follows attractor pathways shaped by feedback loops. The system never exactly repeats itself but stays within the basin of attraction — a strange attractor with non-integer dimension — until a perturbation of sufficient magnitude pushes it into a new attractor basin.

## Energy Efficiency as Selective Principle

The **Energy Efficiency Theorem** (Theorem 5) states that an Emergent CAS operates at a local maximum of energy efficiency. This does not refer to a maximum with respect to time, but with respect to the space of possible configurations. This establishes one of the core claims: energy-efficient configurations are selected by the principle of least action.

**Theorem 6:** Every ECAS has a finite operational temperature range, and the weighted-average ECAS runs "close to the top" of this range. This elegantly explains why mammals maintain body temperatures between 35°C and 40°C — despite their proteins functioning across a 0–41°C window.

> "Mammals, being made essentially of water and all of the same proteins, have a potential operating temperature window of between 0° C... and 41° C... Yet mammals run hot, with standard body temperatures between 35° C and 40° C with only a very few exceptions. Theorem 6 proves why."
