# Key Principles of Essentialism

> Source: Essentialism, Chapters 1–20

## Principle 1: The Invincible Power of Choice

> Source: Essentialism, Chapter 2

The first and most crucial Essentialist skill is recognizing your ability to choose. McKeown's own story of quitting law school illustrates this powerfully: he had always known logically he could choose not to study law, but emotionally it had never been an option. He realized that by refusing to choose "not law school," he had made a choice by default — a bad one.

The "learned helplessness" experiments from Martin Seligman and Steve Maier demonstrate this principle at work. When people believe their efforts don't matter, they respond in one of two ways: they check out completely, or they become hyperactive, accepting every opportunity because they don't believe they have a real choice. Both are symptoms of forgetting the power of choice.

**> Case: Greg McKeown's Law School Decision**
McKeown was halfway through law school in England when a friend's passing comment — "If you decide to stay in America" — triggered a realization. The friend assumed he had a real choice. This launched a personal exploration where McKeown asked himself: "If you could do only one thing with your life right now, what would you do?" Law school wasn't on the list. He quit and moved to America to become an author and teacher.

**Key insight:** Options can be taken away; our ability to choose cannot. Every time you say "I have to," you're surrendering choice. Replace it with "I choose to."

## Principle 2: The Unimportance of Practically Everything

> Source: Essentialism, Chapter 3

McKeown draws heavily on the Pareto Principle (80/20 Rule) and the power law to argue that certain efforts produce exponentially better results than others. The overwhelming reality: most things are worthless, and very few things are exceptionally valuable.

He tells the fable of Boxer the horse from *Animal Farm*, whose answer to every setback was "I will work harder" — until he was broken and sent to the knackers' yard. Boxer's tragedy mirrors what happens when we respond to every challenge with more effort instead of discernment.

The chapter makes the case that distinguishing the "trivial many" from the "vital few" applies to every kind of human endeavor. Warren Buffett makes big bets on the essential few and says no to merely good ones. Ferran Adrià closes El Bulli for six months a year to focus on the essence of his craft.

**> Case: The Paper Route Lesson**
As a twelve-year-old, McKeown delivered newspapers for one pound per hour. He realized he could wash cars for two pounds per car and clean three in an hour — changing his effort-to-reward ratio from 1:1 to 1:6. Later, in a customer service job, he asked "What is the most valuable result I could achieve?" and focused on retaining customers rather than just taking calls. He achieved a zero cancellation rate and significantly increased his contribution and earnings.

**Key insight:** Discerning the vital few from the trivial many isn't a one-time exercise — it's a continuous practice. Always ask: "What is the highest point of contribution I can make right now?"

## Principle 3: Embrace Trade-offs Deliberately

> Source: Essentialism, Chapter 4

Trade-offs are real, unavoidable, and — when embraced deliberately — strategic. McKeown tells the story of Southwest Airlines, which made conscious trade-offs (point-to-point flights, no meals, no seat assignments) to maintain a consistent low-cost strategy, while Continental Airlines failed trying to "straddle" two incompatible strategies.

Erin Callan, former CFO of Lehman Brothers, shared how trade-offs by default crept into her life: "I didn't start out with the goal of devoting all of myself to my job. It crept in over time." She advises making trade-offs deliberately rather than letting them happen to you.

**> Case: The Harvard Parents**
McKeown met parents on a flight whose son was accepted to Harvard. Their strategy: have him try many activities, but as soon as it became clear an activity wouldn't be his "big thing," they pulled him out. They consciously chose the trade-off of focusing on one deep pursuit over many shallow ones.

**Key insight:** Instead of "How can I do both?" ask "Which problem do I want to solve?" Trade-offs represent an opportunity to be strategic about where you invest.

## Principle 4: Protect the Asset (Yourself)

> Source: Essentialism, Chapter 8

The best asset for making a contribution to the world is yourself. McKeown tells the story of Geoff, a hyper-successful microcredit CEO who worked so relentlessly that his organs started shutting down at age 36. His doctor gave him two options: medication for life, or disengage for 1–2 years to recover. Geoff learned the hard way: "Protect the asset."

The chapter cites research showing the best violinists (from Anders Ericsson's famous study) slept an average of 8.6 hours per night — an hour more than the average American. Sleep allows the brain to encode and restructure information, literally making us smarter and more creative overnight.

**> Case: The Google Nap Pod**
McKeown visited Google to test their nap pods. Despite fifty people working on the floor, only one person had used it that week. This illustrates how even companies that provide sleep-friendly environments have employees who don't take advantage — the stigma of sleep persists.

**Key insight:** Sleep is not a luxury or a weakness. It is the foundation of high performance. One hour more of sleep equals several more hours of much higher productivity.

## Principle 5: Extreme Criteria — The 90% Rule

> Source: Essentialism, Chapter 9

Derek Sivers's rule: "If it's not a HELL YEAH, it's a no." McKeown formalizes this as the 90% Rule: when evaluating an option, score it 0–100 on the single most important criterion. If it's any lower than 90, change the rating to 0 and reject it.

This principle forces you to confront trade-offs directly. It's hard to turn down a "good" 70 or 80 — but accepting merely good opportunities eats up the time and energy you need for truly great ones.

**> Case: Vitsoe's Hiring Process**
The furniture company Vitsoe has an ultra-selective hiring process: phone interviews to strip away visual cues, multiple interviews, a full-day work trial, and a team-wide questionnaire. One candidate who performed flawlessly was rejected because he threw tools carelessly into the box. "If it isn't a clear yes, then it's a clear no."

**Key insight:** Your selection criteria determine the quality of your life. Broad criteria = cluttered life. Extreme criteria = essential life.

## Principle 6: Saying No is a Leadership Capability

> Source: Essentialism, Chapter 11

McKeown devotes an entire chapter to the art of the graceful no. He uses the powerful example of Rosa Parks, whose quiet but resolute "No, I'm not" on a Montgomery bus sparked the civil rights movement. Her courage came from deep conviction, not from being naturally assertive.

Peter Drucker, whom McKeown calls the father of modern management, was a master of the graceful no. When asked to participate in a study on creativity, Drucker famously declined: "Productivity in my experience consists of NOT doing anything that helps the work of other people but to spend all one's time on the work the Good Lord has fitted one to do, and to do well."

**> Case: Stephen Covey's "No" to an Old Friend**
When Stephen Covey ran into an old friend who invited him and his daughter Cynthia to dinner at the Wharf, Covey had a choice. He said no gracefully: "Not tonight. Cynthia and I have a special date planned." That night became one of Cynthia's most cherished memories — a decision that "bonded him to me forever because I knew what mattered most to him was me."

**Key insight:** A clear no is more graceful than a vague yes. Build a repertoire of "no" responses: the awkward pause, the soft no, "Let me check my calendar," "Yes, what should I deprioritize?" Popularity is temporary; respect is lasting.

## Principle 7: Execution Through Subtraction, Not Force

> Source: Essentialism, Part IV (Chapters 15–20)

The final section of the book argues that execution should not be forced — it should be made effortless by removing obstacles. Instead of adding more effort, the Essentialist subtracts obstacles, celebrates small wins, builds routines, and focuses on what's important now (WIN).

McKeown introduces the concept of "buffer" — preparing for the unexpected by building slack into your schedule. He advocates for "subtraction" — not adding more to achieve more, but removing barriers that stand between you and your goal. Progress comes from small wins that build momentum. Flow comes from routine that makes execution automatic.

**> Case: The Essentialist Executive**
After implementing the Explore-Eliminate-Execute cycle, the book's opening character Sam Elliot transformed his work life: he went home at a decent time, shut down his phone, went to the gym, ate out with his wife. His performance ratings went up. His work became fulfilling again. Not because he did more — because he did less but better.

**Key insight:** Don't ask "How can I force this to happen?" Ask "What obstacles can I remove to make this happen naturally?"


> **Case: The Journalist's Pile** (Chapter 6): McKeown tells of a journalist who kept a pile of "interesting articles" that grew to over 2,000 items. He spent hours each week filing, organizing, and categorizing them. He never read any of them. The pile was a monument to his inability to select what mattered. When he finally threw the entire pile away, he felt liberated. He learned that selection is not about what to keep — it's about what to throw away.
> **Key takeaway:** If you're not selecting, you're just accumulating. Selection requires the courage to discard. Every "keep" should be a conscious choice, not a default.

> **Case: The Investment Banker's Sabbath** (Chapter 8): An investment banker, known for working 24/7, decided to protect one day per week as completely work-free. He didn't check email, take calls, or think about deals. Initially, it felt impossible. Within a month, his productivity on other days increased, his decision-making improved, and he felt more energetic. Protecting space for rest and thinking made him more effective, not less.
> **Key takeaway:** Space is not a luxury — it's a productivity tool. The busiest people need it most.
