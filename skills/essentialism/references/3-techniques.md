# Essentialist Techniques: Practical Tools for the Disciplined Pursuit of Less

> Source: Essentialism, Parts II–IV (Chapters 5–20)

## Explore Techniques: How to Discern the Vital Few

### Technique 1: Create Space (Escape)

> Source: Essentialism, Chapter 5

The most fundamental Essentialist practice is creating space to think. Without space, you can't discern what matters. McKeown profiles several leaders who build space into their schedules:

- **Jeff Weiner (LinkedIn CEO):** Schedules up to two hours of blank space every day, divided into thirty-minute increments. Uses this time to think about the big picture — where the company will be in 3–5 years, how to improve products, how to widen competitive advantages.
- **Frank O'Brien (Conversations):** Holds a "Do-Not-Call-Monday" once a month — a full day with no phones, no email, no agenda — just thinking and talking. Even clients know not to expect a response.
- **Bill Gates:** Takes "Think Weeks" twice a year, even during Microsoft's most frenetic expansion. He secludes himself for a week to read articles and books and think about the bigger picture.

**Practical application:** Schedule "thinking time" into your calendar — even 20 minutes a day reading classic literature rather than checking email. Create a "monk mode" email bounceback during focused work periods.

**> Case: The Executive in a Motel**
An executive who couldn't stop checking social media gave his phone away and spent eight weeks in a motel with no internet to complete a major project. While extreme, it illustrates the principle: create the space you need to do your best work.

### Technique 2: Find the Lead (Look)

> Source: Essentialism, Chapter 6

In every set of facts, something essential is hidden. A good journalist finds it by looking beyond surface information for what really matters. McKeown shares Nora Ephron's journalism class story where her teacher told the class their leads were all wrong — the real lead was "There will be no school Thursday." This taught her that journalism wasn't about regurgitating facts but figuring out the point.

**Practical applications:**
- **Keep a journal** — write less than you feel like writing (to build the habit), then review every 90 days for patterns
- **Get out into the field** — the Embrace team went to Nepal and discovered 80% of babies were born at home with no electricity, changing their entire incubator design
- **Filter for the fascinating** — Thomas Friedman listens for what is not being said
- **Clarify the question** — when a team is stuck, ask "What question are we trying to answer?"

**> Case: The Embrace Baby Incubator**
Jane Chen's team was challenged to design a baby incubator for 1% of the traditional $20,000 cost. Instead of jumping into a cost-reduction problem, they went to Nepal and discovered the real constraint: no electricity in rural villages. The solution was the Embrace Nest, a wax-based warming device that requires zero electricity — a breakthrough that would have been missed if they hadn't explored the real problem first.

### Technique 3: Rediscover Play

> Source: Essentialism, Chapter 7

Play is not trivial — it's essential for brain plasticity, creativity, and exploration. Stuart Brown, founder of the National Institute for Play, studied 6,000 individuals and concluded that "nothing fires up the brain like play."

McKeown identifies three ways play helps explore: (1) broadens the range of options, (2) reduces stress and opens the exploratory brain, (3) stimulates executive function (planning, prioritizing, analyzing).

**Practical application:** Reconnect with what excited you as a child and reintroduce it into your life. Desk toys, improv classes, and playful office environments aren't distractions — they're essential tools for exploration.

### Technique 4: Protect Your Sleep

> Source: Essentialism, Chapter 8

McKeown challenges the machismo of sleep deprivation. He cites research showing that the best violinists in Anders Ericsson's study slept 8.6 hours per night — more than the average. Harvard's Charles Czeisler compares sleep deficit to having a 0.1% blood alcohol level.

**Practical application:** Treat sleep as a competitive advantage. Jeff Bezos gets eight hours and says "I'm more alert and I think more clearly." Build sleep into your schedule systematically. Even a brief nap can enhance creativity by helping the brain make new neural connections.

### Technique 5: The 90% Rule and Extreme Criteria (Select)

> Source: Essentialism, Chapter 9

When evaluating any option, score it 0–100 on the single most important criterion. If it's below 90, reject it. Derek Sivers's formulation: "If it's not a HELL YEAH, it's a no."

McKeown provides a three-step filtering process:
1. Write down the opportunity
2. List three minimum criteria to even be considered
3. List three extreme/ideal criteria for approval
If the opportunity doesn't pass the minimum criteria: it's a no. If it doesn't pass two of the three extreme criteria: it's still a no.

## Eliminate Techniques: How to Cut Out the Trivial Many

### Technique 6: Craft an Essential Intent (Clarify)

> Source: Essentialism, Chapter 10

An essential intent is both concrete and inspirational — one decision that settles one thousand later decisions. Brad Pitt's organization "Make It Right" had the essential intent "to build 150 affordable, green, storm-resistant homes for families living in the Lower 9th Ward." It was specific enough to measure, yet inspiring enough to mobilize action.

**Practical application:** Ask "If we could be truly excellent at only one thing, what would it be?" and "How will we know when we're done?"

### Technique 7: The Graceful No (Dare)

> Source: Essentialism, Chapter 11

McKeown provides eight specific scripts for saying no gracefully:

1. **The awkward pause** — count to three, let silence work for you
2. **The soft "no but"** — "I'm consumed with X right now, but I'd love to connect after"
3. **"Let me check my calendar"** — buy time to evaluate deliberately
4. **Email bouncebacks** — use autoresponders for focused periods
5. **"Yes, what should I deprioritize?"** — force the requester to confront the trade-off
6. **Say it with humor** — a lighthearted "Nope!" can be disarming
7. **"You are welcome to X, I am willing to Y"** — offer a limited alternative
8. **"I can't do it, but X might be interested"** — redirect without abandoning

### Technique 8: Uncommit (Cut Losses)

> Source: Essentialism, Chapter 12

Sunk-cost bias traps us in failing projects. McKeown offers several tactics:
- **Pretend you don't own it yet** — "If I weren't already invested, how much would I invest now?"
- **Get over the fear of waste** — the "don't waste" rule accumulates over a lifetime, making it hard to walk away
- **Admit failure to begin success** — there's no shame in admitting you're now wiser
- **Get a neutral second opinion** — someone outside the situation can see it clearly

## Execute Techniques: How to Make Execution Effortless

### Technique 9: Buffer (Prepare for the Unexpected)

> Source: Essentialism, Chapter 15

Build slack into your schedule to handle the unexpected. The optimal buffer is 50% — if you think a task will take one hour, schedule 90 minutes. This prevents last-minute firefighting.

### Technique 10: Subtract (Remove Obstacles)

> Source: Essentialism, Chapter 16

Instead of asking "What can I add?" ask "What obstacles stand in my way?" Identify the single biggest bottleneck and remove it. Don't add more resources; eliminate what's blocking progress.

### Technique 11: Small Wins (Progress)

> Source: Essentialism, Chapter 17

Celebrate small wins to build momentum. The key is not to measure progress by giant leaps but by consistent, small advances. Create a "done list" alongside your to-do list. Reward completion, not just effort.

### Technique 12: Flow (Build Routines)

> Source: Essentialism, Chapter 18

Design routines that make the essential things automatic. The goal is to reduce decision fatigue by turning repeated essential actions into habits. McKeown recommends starting small — automate one essential activity at a time.

### Technique 13: Focus on WIN — What's Important Now

> Source: Essentialism, Chapter 19

Don't worry about yesterday or tomorrow. Ask constantly: "What's important *right now*?" This present-centered question cuts through anxiety about the past and future, enabling clear execution in the moment.
