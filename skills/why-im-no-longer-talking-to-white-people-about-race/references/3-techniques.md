# Techniques — Why I'm No Longer Talking to White People About Race

## How to Think About Race (Based on Reni Eddo-Lodge)

### Technique 1: Learn Your Country's True History
Eddo-Lodge's university course on the slave trade changed her life. She visited Liverpool docks and realized the history was a train ride away. The technique: educate yourself about your own country's racial history — not just America's. In Britain: Dr. Harold Moody, the 1919 race riots, Taranto mutiny, Windrush.

### Technique 2: Listen Before You Speak
"Amid every conversation about Nice White People feeling silenced by conversations about race, there is a sort of ironic and glaring lack of understanding or empathy for those of us who have been visibly marked out as different for our entire lives." The technique: if you're white, your first instinct should be to listen, not to explain.

### Technique 3: Distinguish Prejudice from Racism
Prejudice is individual. Racism is systemic — discrimination plus power. "A white person can experience prejudice. They cannot experience racism in a system designed to benefit them at the expense of others."

### Technique 4: The University Challenge
Eddo-Lodge's white friend dropped the slave trade class because "it's just not for me." The technique: if you have the privilege to opt out of learning about racism, push yourself to stay in the room. "This didn't seem like information you could opt out from learning."

### Technique 5: Apply Intersectionality
Kimberlé Crenshaw's framework: don't treat race, gender, and class as separate issues. "A black woman is not just a woman plus blackness." The technique: when analyzing any social issue, ask which overlapping identities are in play.

### Technique 6: The Boundary Test
Eddo-Lodge set a boundary: she stopped explaining race to people who refused to hear. The technique: your emotional energy is finite. "I can't have a conversation with them about the details of a problem if they don't even recognize that the problem exists."

### Technique 7: The "Where Were You?" Question
Eddo-Lodge suggests asking white people who claim colorblindness: "When did you first realize you were white?" Most white people have never asked themselves this question. The technique: the first step to understanding white privilege is recognizing that you have a race — and that it matters.
