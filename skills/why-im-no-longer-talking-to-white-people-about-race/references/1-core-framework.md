# Core Framework — Why I'm No Longer Talking to White People About Race

## Preface: The Blog Post

On February 22, 2014, Eddo-Lodge published a blog post titled "Why I'm No Longer Talking to White People About Race." It went viral. "I can no longer engage with the gulf of an emotional disconnect that white people display when a person of colour articulates their experience." The response was split along racial lines: people of color said "thank you for articulating my experience." White readers responded with guilt and pleas: "Please don't give up on white people."

She turned the blog post into a book — paradoxically — to continue the conversation. "I've turned it into a book to continue the conversation. Since I set my boundary, I've done almost nothing but speak about race."

## Chapter 1: Histories — The Erased History of Black Britain

**The slave trade:** British slavery lasted over 270 years (1562-1833). An estimated 11 million Africans were transported. British slave owners received compensation after abolition — the enslaved received nothing. "Less than two hundred years later, that damage is still to be undone."

**World War I:** Over a million Indian soldiers fought for Britain. British West Indies Regiment (BWIR) suffered discrimination — lower pay, trench-digging, segregation. The 1918 Taranto mutiny. After the war: the 1919 race riots. Charles Wootton was publicly lynched in Liverpool — thrown into the docks by a white mob, pelted with bricks until he drowned.

**Dr. Harold Moody:** Arrived from Jamaica in 1904, became a doctor, faced racist hiring. Founded the League of Coloured Peoples in 1931 — Britain's first anti-racism campaign. "His respectable, middle-class job positioned him as a beacon for black people in 1920s and 1930s Britain."

**Windrush generation:** The 492 Caribbeans who arrived in 1948. "Most of my knowledge of black history was American history." The erasure of Black British history is systematic.

## Chapter 2: The System — Structural Racism

Eddo-Lodge defines racism as "discrimination plus power." "It was about being in the position to negatively affect other people's life chances."

**Housing:** Black families in Britain were systematically denied council housing. "Nottingham council's housing department was found to be flagging the names of minority ethnic tenants with red stars." The 1981 Brixton uprising was a response to racist policing.

**Education:** Black children were disproportionately excluded from school. "In Birmingham, a child of Black Caribbean heritage is almost four times more likely to be permanently excluded from school than a white British child."

**Policing:** The Macpherson Report (1999) found the Metropolitan Police institutionally racist after the Stephen Lawrence murder. Stop and search powers were used disproportionately against black people. "The Stephen Lawrence case showed us that even doing everything right — being a middle-class, well-spoken, ambitious young black man — will not protect you."

## Chapter 3: What Is White Privilege?

White privilege is not about individual wealth or happiness — it's about not being disadvantaged by your race. "To be white is to be human; to be white is universal. I only know this because I am not."

**The emotional disconnect:** "You can see their eyes shut down and harden. It's like treacle is poured into their ears." White people have the privilege of not thinking about race. People of color don't have that choice.

## Chapter 4: Fear of a Black Planet

Media representation of black people is overwhelmingly negative. "When white people pick up a magazine, scroll through the Internet, read a newspaper or switch on the TV, it is never rare or odd to see people who look like them in positions of power."

## Chapter 5: The Feminism Question

Eddo-Lodge examines the failure of mainstream feminism to address race. "White feminism" ignores the specific experiences of women of color.

**The suffragette movement's racism:** Emmeline Pankhurst and the WSPU were willing to exclude black women. "The suffragettes were not afraid to use racist arguments to make their case."

**Intersectionality:** Coined by Kimberlé Crenshaw. The idea that multiple forms of oppression (race, gender, class) intersect. "A black woman is not just a woman plus blackness — she experiences the world differently from both white women and black men."

## Chapter 6: Race and Class

The argument that class matters more than race is a way to dismiss racism. "The left has often been guilty of subsuming race under class." The two cannot be separated. "When the left says 'class not race,' they're asking non-white people to abandon their identities in favor of a common class identity that has already excluded them."

## Chapter 7: There's No Justice, There's Just Us

A call to action. "Every voice raised against racism chips away at its power." Organizing, educating, and speaking out are essential. "We can't afford to stay silent."
