# The Corporate AI Race — Strategy, Talent, and Power

> Source: Genius Makers, Chapters 6, 7, 8, 12, 14, 15, 16

## How Big Tech Competed for AI Dominance

The AI race was not won by the best technology alone — it was won by the most aggressive, strategic, and well-funded organizations.

### The Corporate Strategies

| Company | Strategy | Key Moves | Outcome |
|---------|----------|-----------|---------|
| Google | Acquire talent, build infrastructure, scale everything | Bought Hinton's company, DeepMind, DNNresearch; built TensorFlow; TPUs | Dominant in search, cloud, and research |
| Facebook | Open-source talent magnet | Hired LeCun, created PyTorch, open-released models | Leading in open-source AI ecosystem |
| Microsoft | Partnership with OpenAI | Invested $1B, then $13B into OpenAI; integrated GPT | Came from behind to compete with Google |
| DeepMind | Pure research → product | Acquired by Google, kept independence, built AlphaFold | Best pure research lab, limited product impact |
| OpenAI | Non-profit → For-profit pivot | Started as non-profit safety org, restructured for compute funding | Leading in large language models (GPT) |

### The Talent War

The AI talent market was unlike anything tech had seen: top PhDs commanded $1M+ compensation packages before they had published anything. Google famously hired Hinton's entire team after ImageNet — paying $44 million for a company with no product. The strategy was simple: buy the people, buy the future.

### The Trophy Breakthroughs

- **AlphaGo (2016)** — DeepMind's AI beat world champion Lee Sedol at Go. The most visible AI victory. "Move 37" — a non-human, counterintuitive move — demonstrated AI's alien intelligence.
- **ImageNet (2012)** — The starting gun. AlexNet's victory proved deep learning worked.
- **GPT-3 (2020)** — OpenAI showed that scaling language models produced emergent abilities no one had predicted.
- **AlphaFold (2020)** — DeepMind solved protein folding, a 50-year grand challenge in biology.

> **Case: The OpenAI Schism** (Chapters 14-16)
> OpenAI was founded in 2015 as a non-profit dedicated to ensuring AGI benefits all of humanity. Elon Musk was a founding donor. By 2018, Musk wanted to take over control. Altman and the board refused. Musk left, taking his funding. OpenAI faced a crisis: non-profit funding couldn't buy the compute it needed. In 2019, OpenAI created a "capped-profit" structure, raising $1B from Microsoft. Critics called it a betrayal of the original mission. Supporters said it was the only way to compete. The drama culminated in 2023 with Altman's firing and rehiring — a boardroom battle that revealed the deep tensions at AI's heart.
> **Key takeaway**: The tension between "AI for all" and "AI needs massive capital" is the central contradiction of the AI industry. OpenAI's journey from idealistic non-profit to Microsoft-backed giant illustrates the impossible choice: stay pure and irrelevant, or compromise and compete.

> **Case: Google's Missed Opportunity** (Chapters 6-7)
> Google had the world's best AI research team (Google Brain), the world's most massive dataset (search, YouTube, Gmail), and the world's most powerful computing infrastructure. They had the pieces to build GPT before OpenAI. But they hesitated — afraid of the reputational risk of releasing a chatbot that could say harmful things. This caution created the opening for OpenAI to become the AI leader. Google's "don't be evil" ethos became a competitive disadvantage.
> **Key takeaway**: In a technology race, caution is a strategic risk. Google's fear of bad PR allowed OpenAI to capture the public imagination and the market lead. The question "what if it goes wrong?" must be balanced with "what if we wait too long?"
