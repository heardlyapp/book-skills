# Core Framework: Early Radiation and the First Accidents

> Source: Atomic Accidents, Introduction and Chapters 1-2

## The Crash at Crush

Mahaffey opens with a spectacular 1896 train collision: two locomotives crashed head-on at 100 mph for entertainment. The boilers exploded, killing three people and injuring many. The engineer was fired — then rehired the next day.

> **Case: Bill Crush's Wreck** (Introduction): The Katy Railroad staged the event for publicity. Spectators were killed by flying boiler fragments. Mahaffey uses this to set the theme: our love of technological spectacle often exceeds our understanding of the risks involved.

> **Key takeaway**: Every new technology has a learning period where the dangers are discovered the hard way.

## Early Radiation Pioneers

Marie Curie, the Radium Girls, and early researchers suffered from radiation exposure before the dangers were understood. Radiation was invisible, effects were delayed — a deadly combination.

> **Case: The Radium Girls** (Chapter 1): Women painting watch dials with radium-226 ingested the radioactive paint by licking their brushes. They developed jaw necrosis and died painful deaths. The companies denied responsibility for years, claiming the radium was harmless.

> **Case: The Uranium Miners** (Chapter 1): Navajo miners in the 1950s were not warned about radiation hazards. They developed lung cancer at devastating rates. The government knew the risks but didn't tell the workers.

> **Key takeaway**: Invisible dangers are the most dangerous. When you can't see, smell, or feel the hazard, safety depends entirely on those who know telling those who don't.
