# Anti-Patterns: Design Flaws and Human Error

> Source: Atomic Accidents, Chapters 4, 7-10

## The Soviet Culture of Secrecy

The USSR hid the Chernobyl accident for days. Even plant operators were not fully informed about the RBMK's dangerous characteristics. Secrecy made the disaster worse.

> **Case: The Denied Disaster** (Chapter 9): After Chernobyl exploded, Soviet authorities denied anything happened. Swedish monitors detected the radiation. By the time evacuation was ordered, many had already received lethal doses. The secrecy contributed directly to the death toll.

> **Key takeaway**: Transparency is a safety feature. Secrecy kills.

## Design Basis Assumption Failure (Fukushima)

Fukushima was designed for a 5.7m tsunami. The actual wave was over 14m. Backup generators were in the basement, which flooded immediately.

> **Case: The Flooded Generators** (Chapter 10): Post-disaster analysis showed the vulnerability was known. Previous engineers had warned about the placement. Management discounted the risk. Three meltdowns followed.

> **Key takeaway**: "It can't happen here" is a dangerous mindset. Design basis assumptions must be regularly challenged.

## Regulatory Capture

At Three Mile Island, the NRC was too accommodating to the nuclear industry. At Fukushima, TEPCO's cozy relationship with regulators meant safety upgrades were delayed.

> **Case: The Ignored Warning** (Chapter 9): A design flaw similar to TMI's stuck valve had caused incidents at other reactors. The information existed but was not effectively shared across the industry.

> **Key takeaway**: Independent regulation is essential. When regulators become partners, safety suffers.
