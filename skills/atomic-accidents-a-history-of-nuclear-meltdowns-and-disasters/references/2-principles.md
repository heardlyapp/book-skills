# Principles: Reactor Accidents — Causes and Consequences

> Source: Atomic Accidents, Chapters 3-5, 9

## The RBMK Design Flaw (Chernobyl)

The Soviet RBMK reactor had a fundamental design flaw: a positive void coefficient. As water turned to steam, the reaction accelerated instead of slowing down. This made an explosion under certain conditions inevitable.

> **Case: The Chernobyl Safety Test** (Chapter 9): On April 26, 1986, operators attempted a safety test at low power. The RBMK's design made the reactor unstable at low power. When the control rods were inserted, a design flaw caused a power surge that blew the reactor apart. The graphite moderator caught fire, sending radioactive material across Europe.

> **Key takeaway**: Chernobyl was not operator error alone. The reactor design was fundamentally unsafe. Under the right conditions, disaster was guaranteed.

## Three Mile Island

A stuck valve, misleading instrument readings, and confused operators led to a partial meltdown.

> **Case: The Stuck Valve** (Chapter 9): A pressure relief valve stuck open. Operators misinterpreted the high water level reading (it was false — caused by steam bubbles) and turned off emergency cooling. The core partially melted.

## Windscale (UK, 1957)

A graphite-moderated reactor caught fire, releasing radioactive iodine across the UK.

> **Case: The Burning Pile** (Chapter 5): The Windscale reactor was air-cooled. A Wigner energy buildup in the graphite ignited. The fire burned for days, releasing significant radioactivity.
