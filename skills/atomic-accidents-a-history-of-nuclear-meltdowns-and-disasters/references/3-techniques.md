# Techniques: Nuclear Weapons Accidents and Criticality

> Source: Atomic Accidents, Chapters 2, 6-8

## The Demon Core

A plutonium core used at Los Alamos killed two scientists in separate criticality accidents. Both involved "tickling the dragon's tail" — using screwdrivers to keep the beryllium reflector slightly away from the core.

> **Case: Harry Daghlian** (Chapter 6): On August 21, 1945, Daghlian accidentally dropped a tungsten carbide brick onto the demon core. The assembly went critical, giving him a lethal dose. He died 25 days later.

> **Case: Louis Slotin** (Chapter 6): On May 21, 1946, Slotin was demonstrating criticality to a group. His screwdriver slipped. The core went critical, giving everyone in the room a radiation dose. Slotin died 9 days later.

> **Key takeaway**: The demon core shows how close to the edge nuclear workers operated in the early years — and how a single slip can be fatal.

## Broken Arrows (Lost Nuclear Weapons)

The US has lost or damaged dozens of nuclear weapons in accidents. None have detonated, but some have scattered plutonium.

> **Case: Palomares, Spain (1966)** (Chapter 8): A B-52 carrying four hydrogen bombs collided with a tanker. Two bombs ruptured, scattering plutonium near a Spanish village. The US spent months removing contaminated soil.

## SL-1 Reactor Accident (Idaho, 1961)

A technician pulled a control rod too far, causing a steam explosion that killed three men.

> **Key takeaway**: Nuclear weapons are designed with multiple safeties — but "Broken Arrows" show the risk of transporting them.
