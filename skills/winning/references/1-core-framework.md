# Core Framework: Candor + Differentiation

Welch's core insight: most organizations are crippled by a lack of candor. People withhold truth to avoid conflict, protect themselves, or "be nice." This creates a culture where problems fester, decisions are made on incomplete information, and the best ideas lose to the most politically safe.

## The Candor Framework

Candor isn't about being brutally honest — it's about being productively honest. Welch distinguishes between candor (truth in service of improvement) and cruelty (truth in service of ego). Candor requires:

1. **Specificity** — Not "you need to improve" but "you missed three deadlines this month, and here's the impact"
2. **Timeliness** — Feedback given weeks later has lost its power. Address issues in real time.
3. **Respect** — The goal is to help the person and the organization, not to vent
4. **Reciprocity** — Leaders must invite candor about their own performance, not just dispense it

> **Case: The Missing Truth** (Chapter 2): Welch describes visiting a GE business where everyone told him things were "going well." But the numbers told a different story. No one wanted to be the bearer of bad news. Welch gathered the leadership team and said: "Either someone tells me what's really happening, or I'll assume you're all incompetent." The truth came out. The business had serious quality problems that had been hidden for months. Once the truth was on the table, they could fix it.
> **Key takeaway:** When no one is bringing you bad news, it's not because there is no bad news. It's because you haven't made it safe to tell you.

## Differentiation (The 20-70-10 System)

Welch's most famous — and controversial — framework. Every organization should differentiate its people into three groups:

- **Top 20%** — The A players. They get the best raises, bonuses, promotions, coaching, and opportunities. They are the engine of the organization. Invest disproportionately in them.
- **Vital 70%** — The B players. They do solid work, but they're not transforming the organization. They need coaching, development, and motivation. Many can become A's with the right support.
- **Bottom 10%** — The C players. They're not performing, and they're not improving. They need to be managed out — respectfully, generously, but firmly. Keeping them hurts everyone.

> **Case: The Reluctant Manager** (Chapter 3): A GE manager asked Welch to let him keep a low-performing employee "just one more year." The employee was a nice person, well-liked, trying hard. But he consistently missed his targets. Welch's response: "You're not doing him any favors. He knows he's not performing. He's unhappy. And keeping him sends a message to everyone that mediocrity is acceptable." The manager let him go. Six months later, the former employee called to thank him — he'd found a role that was a better fit and was thriving.
> **Key takeaway:** Keeping a low performer is not kindness. It's avoidance. The person knows they're not succeeding. Help them find a better fit — inside or outside the organization.
