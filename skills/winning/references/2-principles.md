# Principles: The Welch Leadership Code

## Principle 1: Candor Is the Foundation
Without candor, nothing else works. You can't differentiate fairly if you won't tell people where they stand. You can't build a mission if no one will admit the current strategy is failing. You can't execute if bad news travels slowly. Candor is not a "soft" skill — it's the hardest and most important leadership discipline.

## Principle 2: Fair Doesn't Mean Equal
Differentiation is often attacked as "cruel" or "Darwinian." Welch's response: what's cruel is treating everyone the same when their contributions are vastly different. Your top performers deserve more. Your underperformers deserve honesty. Equal treatment of unequal performers is unfair to everyone.

> **Case: The Star Player's Exit** (Chapter 3): A GE business lost its best salesperson because the manager couldn't bring himself to give her a differentiated raise — he gave everyone 4% across the board. The star left for a competitor where she'd be paid what she was worth. The manager's "fairness" cost the business its top performer. Welch's response: "You got fairness. You also got mediocrity."
> **Key takeaway:** Treating everyone the same is the most unfair thing you can do. Differentiate rewards based on contribution. Anything else drives away your best people.


> **Case: The Integrity Test** (Chapter 1, "Mission and Values"): A GE business was considering a large deal that would boost quarterly earnings — but it required cutting corners on a customer contract. Welch asked the team: "Would you be proud to read about this on the front page of the Wall Street Journal?" The answer was no. They walked away from the deal. Values weren't words on a wall — they were a decision framework.
> **Key takeaway:** Your values are only real when they cost you something. If you can't pass the "front page test," don't do it.

## Principle 3: Mission Must Be Lived
A mission statement that doesn't drive daily decisions is worse than no mission at all — it creates cynicism. Welch's test: can any employee tell you, in two sentences, how their work today connects to the mission? If not, the mission is wallpaper, not leadership.

## Principle 4: Values Are Behaviors, Not Beliefs
Most companies have values that sound nice (integrity, teamwork, customer focus). Welch insists values must be specific behaviors that are actually rewarded and punished. If teamwork is a value, do you promote people who collaborate or people who hoard information? Your promotion decisions reveal your real values.

## Principle 5: Speed Is a Competitive Weapon
Welch believed that speed — the ability to move fast, decide fast, and learn fast — was the most underrated competitive advantage. Most organizations move too slowly because they're afraid of mistakes. Welch's rule: better a fast decision that's 70% right than a perfect decision that arrives too late.

## Principle 6: Manage Out With Dignity
The bottom 10% are not bad people — they're in the wrong role. Welch's approach: be honest, be generous (severance, outplacement, references), and be clear. Letting someone go with dignity is better than letting them languish in a role where they'll never succeed.


## Principle 7: Celebrating Wins Matters More Than You Think
Welch believed that organizations dramatically under-celebrate. Most leaders move from one crisis to the next, never pausing to recognize success. He insisted on celebrating wins publicly and frequently. "When you win, have a party. When you lose, learn the lesson and move on. But celebrate the wins." This creates momentum and energy that carry through the hard times.
