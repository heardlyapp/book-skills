# Techniques: Welch's Practical Tools

## Technique 1: The 5-Slide Strategy Review
Welch's famous framework for any strategy discussion. Before any strategy meeting, prepare exactly 5 slides:

1. **Where are we now?** — Market position, competitive landscape, financial health
2. **Where are we going?** — The big bet: what will be different in 3-5 years
3. **How will we get there?** — The specific initiatives and resources required
4. **Who is our competition?** — Their moves, their strengths, their weaknesses
5. **What keeps me up at night?** — The real risks, not the sanitized version

**Modern adaptation:** Before any major initiative, write 5 slides. If you can't, you haven't thought it through. If you need more than 5, you're overcomplicating it.

> **Case: The 50-Page Strategic Plan** (Chapter 5): A GE business presented a 50-page strategic plan to Welch. He stopped them on page 3. "If you can't tell me where you're going in 5 slides, you don't know where you're going." The team went back, condensed their thinking to 5 slides, and discovered they didn't actually agree on the direction. The 5-slide format forced the clarity that 50 pages had hidden.
> **Key takeaway:** Brevity is not a communication choice — it's a thinking tool. The constraint of 5 slides forces the real strategic decisions.

## Technique 2: The Candor Audit
Run a periodic candor audit: anonymously survey your team on a simple question — "What's the one thing we're not talking about that we should be?" The answers will tell you where your culture is failing. Then address the patterns publicly.

## Technique 3: The Values in Action Review
At every performance review, evaluate people on both results AND values. Someone who delivers results but violates values is a problem (the "jerk who gets results"). Someone who lives values but doesn't deliver may need coaching. Someone who does neither is a C player. Someone who does both is an A player — promote them.

## Technique 4: The "What Keeps Me Up at Night" List
Welch insisted that every leader have a personal list of the 3-5 things that genuinely worry them. Not the sanitized corporate version — the real fears. Share it with your leadership team. This practice builds trust and surfaces problems before they become crises.

## Technique 5: The Management Calendar
Welch's habit: block time on your calendar for the most important leadership activities — performance reviews, strategy discussions, visits to the field. If it's not on the calendar, it won't happen. "You can't manage what you don't measure — and you can't lead what you don't schedule."

> **Case: The Plant Visit** (Chapter 6): Welch spent 50% of his time at GE visiting plants and offices, talking directly to employees at all levels. He didn't rely on reports or briefings. He went to where the work was done and asked questions. This practice surfaced problems that had been filtered out by layers of management.
> **Key takeaway:** Information degrades as it travels up the hierarchy. The most valuable data comes from the front line. Go there. Ask questions. Listen.
