# Anti-Patterns

## 1. The "Everyone Does It" Rationalization

**The mistake**: Believing that because aggressive information-gathering is common, crossing the line isn't serious. "It's just how the industry works."

**Why it fails**: Common does not equal legal. The government prosecutes patterns of behavior, not isolated incidents. If "everyone does it," the government can prosecute everyone.

## 2. Compartmentalization as a Shield

**The mistake**: Assuming that not knowing the details of illegal activity protects you from liability if you created the culture that encouraged it.

**Why it fails**: The government went after SAC Capital for the culture it created, not just the specific trades. The company paid $1.8 billion. Personal deniability doesn't protect you from corporate liability.

## 3. Trusting the "Expert"

**The mistake**: Accepting information from expert networks or industry consultants at face value, without verifying its source.

**Why it fails**: The expert may not realize they're sharing MNPI. Or they may know exactly what they're doing. Either way, you're responsible for what you trade on.

> **Case: The Consequences** (Epilogue): The SAC Capital case ended with $1.8 billion in fines, the firm's guilty plea, and the shutdown of its external investment business. Steven Cohen was barred from managing other people's money for two years (a relatively light outcome). Several portfolio managers went to prison. "Cohen survived, but the price was enormous."
> **Key takeaway**: The consequences of crossing the black edge are not abstract — they involve billions in fines, prison sentences, and destroyed careers.

## 4. Waiting to Be Caught

**The mistake**: Seeing suspicious activity and looking the other way. "It's not my problem."

**Why it fails**: When the investigation arrives, the first people to come forward get significantly better treatment. Those who knew and said nothing become targets themselves.

## 5. The Icarus Problem

**The mistake**: Believing you're too successful, too clever, or too well-protected to be caught.

**Why it fails**: SAC Capital was one of the most successful hedge funds in history. Its founder was a billionaire. The government still brought them down. No one is untouchable.
