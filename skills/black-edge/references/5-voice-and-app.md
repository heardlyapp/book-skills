## Key Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Suspicious Information at Work
**Situation**: A financial professional receives information that seems too specific.

**Solution**: Document it. Don't trade on it. Report it. If compliance doesn't act, go to the SEC. The earlier you report, the better your position.

### Scenario 2: Firm Culture Concerns
**Situation**: User works at a firm where aggressive information-gathering is celebrated.

**Solution**: Evaluate the culture against the SAC Capital model. Is compliance respected or ridiculed? Are people who raise concerns punished? If the culture is toxic, leave — the risk is not worth the compensation.

### Scenario 3: Considering Cooperation
**Situation**: User has been contacted by investigators or is considering coming forward.

**Solution**: Hire a lawyer immediately. The decision to cooperate is the most consequential you'll make. Cooperators who come forward first get dramatically better treatment than those who wait.

### Scenario 4: Compliance System Design
**Situation**: User is designing compliance systems for a financial firm.

**Solution**: Build systems that assume good-faith errors will happen. The key is not preventing every mistake — it's catching and correcting them before they become fatal. A whistleblower hotline, mandatory reporting, and a culture that rewards compliance over performance.

### Scenario 5: Understanding the SAC Case
**Situation**: User wants to understand what happened and why it matters.

**Solution**: The SAC Capital case is the definitive example of how culture drives compliance failure. Read the book for the full narrative. The key insight: you don't need explicit orders to break the law — you just need to make clear that results are all that matter.
