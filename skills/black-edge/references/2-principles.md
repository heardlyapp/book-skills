# Key Principles

## 1. The Culture Creates the Crime

The most important lesson of the SAC Capital case: explicit orders to break the law were not necessary. The culture — intense pressure to generate edge, compensation tied to results, and contempt for compliance — was sufficient. "You don't need to tell someone to break the law. You just need to make it clear that results are all that matter."

## 2. Cooperators Are the Weakest Link

"The flip is the most powerful tool in the government's arsenal." When a suspect decides to cooperate, they bring down everyone above them. The Galleon case unraveled through cooperators, which then led to SAC. One person's decision to cooperate changes everything.

## 3. The Edge Blinds You

"The pursuit of edge — information advantage — is addictive." Traders who cross the line often start with small, borderline actions that escalate. The edge itself becomes the goal, and the legal line becomes invisible. By the time someone realizes they've crossed, they're too deep to turn back.

> **Case: The Expert Network Trap** (Chapter 6): Expert networks like Primary Global Research connected investors with industry experts. The service was legitimate — experts provided research and context. But some experts began sharing material non-public information. Investors who thought they were "just being thorough" were actually trading on inside information.
> **Key takeaway**: Legitimate tools and structures can become conduits for illegal activity. The intent matters less than the information actually being shared.

## 4. Compartmentalization Is Not a Defense

Steven Cohen's defense was always: "I didn't know." He was careful not to receive direct inside information. But the government's theory was: he created and encouraged the culture. The company was forced to pay $1.8 billion and plead guilty. Personal insulation is not a get-out-of-jail card.

## 5. The Government Has More Tools Than You Think

"Wall Street used to think the SEC and FBI were slow and outmatched. They were wrong." Modern investigative techniques — wiretaps, data analysis, cooperating witnesses, trading pattern analysis — have become sophisticated. The perception of regulatory weakness is itself a danger.
