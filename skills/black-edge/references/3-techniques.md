# Techniques: Investigation Patterns and Compliance

## The Flip Protocol

When federal investigators build an insider trading case:

1. **Start small** — Begin with a clear violation (someone caught trading on a tip)
2. **Flip the suspect** — Offer a deal: cooperate in exchange for reduced charges
3. **Work up the chain** — Each cooperator provides evidence against someone higher
4. **Corroborate** — Wiretaps, trading records, and testimony must align
5. **Build the pattern** — Multiple cooperators telling the same story is devastating

## The Compliance Checklist

Questions every financial professional should ask:

- [ ] Do I have a clear written policy on accepting information from external sources?
- [ ] Am I required to report any conversations with industry experts?
- [ ] Is there a system to flag unusual trading activity?
- [ ] Do I document the basis for every significant trade?
- [ ] Is there a culture where compliance questions are welcomed or discouraged?
- [ ] What happens to people who raise compliance concerns?

## Recognizing Red Flags

| Red Flag | What It Suggests |
|----------|-----------------|
| A tip that is "too specific" or "too timely" | Possibly inside information |
| Source is vague about where they got the information | Protecting the chain |
| "Just trust me on this one" | Intentionally avoiding documentation |
| Unusual confidence about a near-term event | Advance knowledge |
| Asking you not to share the source | Awareness that it's illegal |

> **Case: The Informant** (Chapter 8): A SAC Capital employee who witnessed questionable practices became an informant for the FBI. He recorded conversations, gathered documents, and provided the government with a window into SAC's inner workings. His cooperation was a turning point in the investigation.
> **Key takeaway**: The person sitting next to you on the trading desk could be wearing a wire. Cooperators are everywhere — and they usually start cooperating because they believe in the case, not because they're forced.
