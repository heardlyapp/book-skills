# Core Framework: The Three Pillars of Franklin's Wealth System

Benjamin Franklin's approach to wealth isn't a system — it's a philosophy rooted in three interconnected pillars. Each pillar reinforces the others, and together they form a complete approach to life and money.

## Pillar 1: Industry (勤勉)

> "Diligence is the mother of good luck."
> "Lost time is never found again."
> "Early to bed and early to rise, makes a man healthy, wealthy, and wise."

Industry means more than working hard — it means working smart, consistently, without idleness. Franklin believed that employment itself is happiness. An idle person rusts. A busy person grows. But industry isn't about being busy for its own sake — it's about producing useful value. The person who spends 10 hours on busywork is less industrious than the person who spends 2 hours on high-impact work.

**Key insight:** Industry compounds like money. Each productive hour builds skill, reputation, and opportunity that makes future hours more valuable. The industrious person doesn't just earn more — they become capable of earning even more.

> **Case: The Sleeping Fox** (Poor Richard's Almanack): Franklin wrote "The sleeping fox catches no poultry" to illustrate that opportunity favors the active. He observed that two neighbors with equal land and resources would end up in vastly different financial positions simply because one was industrious and the other was lazy. Not talent, not inheritance — just the habit of getting up and working.
> **Key takeaway:** Talent without industry is a lamp without oil. You can have all the ability in the world, but if you don't show up and work, it produces nothing.

## Pillar 2: Frugality (节俭)

> "A penny saved is a penny earned."
> "Beware of little expenses; a small leak will sink a great ship."
> "Rather go to bed supperless than rise in debt."

Frugality isn't about deprivation — it's about **choosing what matters**. Franklin famously told the story of a boy who paid too much for a whistle — he wanted it so badly that he overpaid, and the whistle wasn't worth what he gave up. Everything you overspend on is your "whistle."

Franklin's frugality is active, not passive. It's not about being cheap — it's about recognizing the true cost of things and choosing accordingly. A frugal person isn't someone who never spends money. It's someone who never wastes money.

**Key insight:** It's not what you earn but what you keep that makes you wealthy. The gap between income and spending is your only wealth-building lever. You can double your income and still be broke if your spending keeps pace.

> **Case: The Small Leak** (Poor Richard's Almanack): A merchant's ship had a tiny leak that went unnoticed. Over time, the slow drip filled the hold and the ship sank. Franklin used this as a metaphor for small, regular expenses: "A small leak will sink a great ship." The single daily expense that seems too small to matter — over 30 years, it's a fortune lost.
> **Key takeaway:** Small, regular expenses you ignore are the most dangerous. A daily $5 coffee over 30 years at 7% interest = ~$190,000. The leak is invisible. The damage is catastrophic.

## Pillar 3: Prudence (审慎)

> "Look before, or you'll find yourself behind."
> "He that goes a borrowing goes a sorrowing."
> "The used key is always bright."

Prudence means thinking before acting, living within your means, and maintaining your reputation. Franklin's view of credit was simple: your word is your bond. The most trifling action that affects your credit matters.

Prudence is the bridge between industry (earning) and frugality (keeping). Without it, you earn but can't keep, or keep but can't grow. It's the wisdom to know the difference between what you want and what you need, between a good opportunity and a trap.

**Key insight:** Prudence isn't about avoiding risk — it's about taking the right risks. Franklin was a serial entrepreneur and inventor. He took plenty of risks. But he never risked his reputation, his credit, or his ability to sleep at night.
