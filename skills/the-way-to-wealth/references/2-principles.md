> Source: The Way to Wealth, Poor Richard's Almanack

# Principles: Franklin's Seven Maxims

## Principle 1: Industry Beats Idleness
"The sleeping fox catches no poultry." There is no substitute for consistent, focused work. Talent without industry is a lamp without oil. Luck is what happens when preparation meets opportunity — and preparation comes from daily work.

Franklin observed that the difference between a prosperous man and a poor one was rarely talent, education, or inheritance. It was almost always the habit of getting up early and working while others slept. Industry is the great equalizer.

## Principle 2: Frugality Over Income
You can always earn more, but if you spend everything, you'll never be wealthy. "It is easier to suppress the first desire than to satisfy all that follow it." The wealthy person isn't the one who earns the most — it's the one who needs the least.

> **Case: The Rich Man Who Lived Poor** (Poor Richard's Almanack): Franklin tells of a wealthy merchant who dressed plainly, ate simply, and lived modestly. His neighbors assumed he was struggling. When he died, they discovered he had been one of the richest men in the colony. He had earned steadily but spent little, and the gap had compounded over decades.
> **Key takeaway:** The person who looks rich is often not. The person who looks poor may be the wealthiest in the room.

## Principle 3: Time Is the Only Non-Renewable Resource
"Dost thou love life? Then do not squander time, for that's the stuff life is made of." Money can be re-earned. Time cannot. Every hour spent in idleness or on things that don't matter is an hour you'll never get back.

Franklin was obsessed with time management. His daily schedule was planned to the hour. He viewed wasted time not as a missed opportunity but as a moral failure — because time is literally the raw material of life.

## Principle 4: Debt Is a Trap
"The borrower is slave to the lender." Debt gives you something today but takes your freedom tomorrow. If you must borrow, do it sparingly and pay it off as fast as possible. The person who owes nothing is richer than the person who owns everything on credit.

> **Case: The Debtor's Regret** (Poor Richard's Almanack): Franklin describes a young tradesman who borrowed money to open his shop. He bought fine furniture on credit, entertained lavishly, and appeared successful. When his creditors called, he had nothing to pay them. His shop was seized, his reputation destroyed, and he spent years working to repay debts that the initial spending would have taken months to save for.
> **Key takeaway:** Borrowing against future income assumes you'll have that income. Life doesn't guarantee it. Debt is a bet against your future self.

## Principle 5: Reputation Is Your Currency
"It is hard for an empty sack to stand upright." A good name is more valuable than money. It opens doors, attracts partners, and creates opportunities that no amount of cleverness can substitute for.

Franklin believed that every small action affects your reputation disproportionately. Showing up late, breaking a small promise, or being seen in bad company — these "trifles" compound into a reputation that either opens doors or closes them.

## Principle 6: Watch the Small Things
"A small neglect may breed great mischief." The little leaks — the forgotten subscription, the unnecessary upgrade, the impulse buy — are invisible individually but catastrophic collectively. Wealth isn't built in big decisions; it's built in thousands of small ones.

## Principle 7: Pride Is Expensive
"Pride is as loud a beggar as want, and a great deal more saucy." Much of our spending is about signaling status. The expensive car, the branded clothes, the luxury vacation — they cost far more than their price tag. They cost your financial freedom. True wealth is invisible.
