> Source: The Way to Wealth, Poor Richard's Almanack & The Autobiography of Benjamin Franklin

# Techniques: Franklin's Practical Methods

## Technique 1: Franklin's Daily Schedule
Franklin planned every day to the hour. His schedule:
- **5-7am:** Rise, wash, address "What good shall I do this day?" — plan the day
- **8-11am:** Work (productive labor — his printing business)
- **12-1pm:** Read, eat, review
- **2-5pm:** Work (second session)
- **6-9pm:** Put things in order, supper, music or conversation, review "What good have I done today?"
- **10pm-4am:** Sleep

**Modern adaptation:** Set 3 priorities daily. Morning for deep work. Afternoon for meetings and admin. Evening for review and planning tomorrow. The key is the morning question and evening review — they bookend the day with intention.

> **Case: Franklin's Autobiography** (The Autobiography of Benjamin Franklin): Franklin wrote that this schedule was "the most important decision I ever made." He followed it for decades with minor adjustments. He credited this daily structure with enabling him to run a printing business, conduct scientific experiments, serve as a diplomat, and write prolifically — all in the same life.
> **Key takeaway:** Structure isn't restriction — it's liberation. A planned day multiplies your effective time.

## Technique 2: The 13 Virtues Tracking
Franklin tracked 13 virtues weekly, focusing on one per week in rotation:
1. Temperance — Eat not to dullness; drink not to elevation.
2. Silence — Speak not but what may benefit others or yourself.
3. Order — Let all your things have their places.
4. Resolution — Perform without fail what you resolve.
5. Frugality — Make no expense but to do good to others.
6. Industry — Lose no time; be always employed in something useful.
7. Sincerity — Use no hurtful deceit; think innocently and justly.
8. Justice — Wrong none by doing injuries or omitting benefits.
9. Moderation — Avoid extremes; forbear resenting injuries.
10. Cleanliness — Tolerate no uncleanliness in body, clothes, or habitation.
11. Tranquility — Be not disturbed at trifles.
12. Chastity — Rarely use venery but for health or offspring.
13. Humility — Imitate Jesus and Socrates.

**Method:** Create a simple chart. Each day, mark every violation of that week's virtue. The goal isn't perfection — it's awareness. Over 13 weeks, you cycle through all virtues four times per year.

## Technique 3: The "Whistle" Audit
List your three most expensive recurring expenses that don't align with your values. For each, ask:
1. "What am I really paying for? (utility, status, convenience, habit)"
2. "Is this worth what I'm giving up?" (The whistle test)
3. "What would I do with this money if I weren't spending it here?"

Then eliminate at least one. Franklin's point: most of our expensive habits are whistles we've been paying too much for, for too long.

## Technique 4: Pay Yourself First (Franklin's Version)
Before any expense, set aside a portion for savings. Franklin called this "paying the first debt." He observed that people who waited until the end of the month to save what was left never saved anything. Modern translation: automate savings on payday before you see the money.

## Technique 5: The Credit Journal
Keep a simple log of every promise you make — to yourself and others. Track: promises made, promises kept, promises broken. Franklin believed that trifling actions (showing up late, forgetting a small favor) damage your reputation disproportionately. The journal builds accountability.

> **Case: The Printer's Promise** (The Autobiography): As a young printer, Franklin made it his rule to deliver every job exactly when promised. Other printers in Philadelphia were notoriously late. Franklin's reliability became his competitive advantage. Customers chose him not because he was cheaper or better — but because he showed up when he said he would.
> **Key takeaway:** Reliability is rare. Being the person who does what they say they'll do is a superpower in any field.

## Technique 6: The Industry Test
At the end of each day, ask: "What useful thing did I accomplish today?" If the answer is "nothing" or "I was busy but not productive," adjust tomorrow. Industry is not about being busy — it's about producing value.
