# 5 — Voice & App

> Source: *Financial Intelligence* by Karen Berman, Joe Knight & John Case.

## The Authors' Voice

Berman, Knight, and Case write as teachers — not academics lecturing from a podium, but patient instructors who have spent years in training rooms with non-financial managers. The tone is encouraging, not intimidating. Every concept is explained with plain language, concrete examples, and a clear "why this matters to you." The book is designed to be read sequentially — each chapter builds on the previous one — but also works as a reference.

> **Case: The "What Is Financial Intelligence?" Framework (Preface):** The authors define financial intelligence as four skill sets: understanding the foundation (the three statements), understanding the art (estimates and assumptions), understanding analysis (ratios and ROI), and understanding the big picture (context). This framework is the backbone of the book — and the reason it works for non-finance managers. It's not about turning them into accountants. It's about giving them the tools to participate in financial conversations.

> **Case: The Setpoint Example (Throughout):** Joe Knight was CFO of Setpoint, a roller coaster and factory automation manufacturer. Throughout the book, Setpoint's financial statements are used as the running example. The reader learns about each concept through a real company making real products. "When Joe needed to buy $200,000 worth of steel for a new project, he had to decide: should he expense it or capitalize it?" The choice affected profit, assets, and cash flow — and the reader learns why.

## Five Scenarios

### 1. The New Department Manager

You've just been put in charge of a department or business unit. You need to understand the P&L, the key drivers of profitability, and how your decisions affect the numbers. Start with the income statement. Find the three biggest line items. Ask: "What drives these numbers?"

### 2. The Small Business Owner

You run your own company. You know your product and customers. You're less confident about financial statements. The key: separate profit from cash. A small business can grow too fast — the profit looks great but the bank account is empty. Manage the cash conversion cycle relentlessly.

### 3. The Project Manager Evaluating ROI

You need to decide whether to invest in a new machine, software, or marketing campaign. Use the ROI framework from Part 6: estimate the cash inflows and outflows over the project's life, calculate the net present value (NPV), and compare to your cost of capital.

### 4. The Non-Profit Leader

Nonprofits have P&L statements too — they just call them something different. The principles are the same: understand revenue sources, track expenses against budget, and manage cash flow. The big difference: profit is called "net assets" and the goal is sustainability, not shareholder return.

### 5. The CEO or Senior Leader Building a Financially Literate Organization

You want everyone in the company to understand the numbers. Start with training, share financial reports regularly, explain the key metrics, and reward people who use financial information in their decision-making. The payoff: better decisions at every level.
