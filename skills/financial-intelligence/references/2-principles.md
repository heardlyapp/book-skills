# 2 — Key Principles

> Source: *Financial Intelligence* by Karen Berman, Joe Knight & John Case.

## The 7 Principles

### 1. Finance Is an Art, Not a Science

Every number contains estimates. Depreciation requires choosing a useful life. Revenue recognition requires judging when revenue is earned. Reserves are inherently uncertain. The books are always a best approximation, not an exact picture.

> **Case: The Software Company That Shipped Empty Boxes (Chapter 1):** The company technically followed GAAP: revenue recognized upon shipment. But the transaction had no economic substance — the customers returned the cartons. The numbers were "correct" but misleading. Financially intelligent managers look beyond the reported number to understand the assumptions behind it.
> **Key takeaway**: The number is not the truth. It's a representation of the truth. Understand what's behind it.

> **Case: The Growing Company That Ran Out of Cash (Chapter 16):** A fast-growing company won a huge contract. The income statement showed record profit. But the company had to buy inventory and hire labor upfront, while the customer had 60-day payment terms. The company was profitable on paper — but couldn't make payroll. This is why cash flow is the reality check.
> **Key takeaway**: Growth eats cash. The faster you grow, the more cash you need for inventory and receivables. Track cash flow, not just profit.

Accrual accounting means revenue is recorded when earned, not when cash arrives. Expenses are matched to revenue, not when paid. So profit is always a calculation based on assumptions. Cash, on the other hand, is what's actually in the bank account. Both matter, but they tell different stories.

### 3. The Balance Sheet Always Balances

Assets = Liabilities + Equity. This is not an accident. Every transaction affects both sides. When a company borrows money, cash goes up (asset) and debt goes up (liability). When it earns profit, assets go up and retained earnings (equity) go up. Understanding the equation means understanding the business.

### 4. Context Is Everything

A 5% net margin is terrible for a software company but excellent for a grocery chain. A debt-to-equity ratio of 1.0 is fine for a utility but dangerous for a tech startup. Never evaluate numbers without context.

### 5. Ratios Reveal What Numbers Hide

Raw numbers tell you size. Ratios tell you efficiency, health, and performance. The five most important: revenue growth, net profit margin, ROE, free cash flow, and debt-to-equity.

### 6. Working Capital Is the Lifeblood

A company can be profitable and still go bankrupt if it runs out of cash to pay its bills. Managing the cash conversion cycle — how fast you collect cash from customers vs how fast you have to pay suppliers — is one of the most important jobs in any business.

### 7. Financial Transparency Improves Performance

Companies that teach financial literacy to all employees — not just managers — outperform those that don't. When people understand how they affect the numbers, they make better decisions and feel more committed.
