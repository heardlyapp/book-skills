# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *Financial Intelligence* by Karen Berman, Joe Knight & John Case — Parts 1-8.

## The Three Frameworks

### Framework A: The Three Financial Statements (Parts 2, 3, 4)

Every company tells its financial story through three documents: the income statement (profit over a period), the balance sheet (assets, liabilities, and equity at a point in time), and the cash flow statement (how cash actually moved). Together they provide a complete picture — but only if you understand how they connect.

> **Case: The Software Company's Empty Boxes (Chapter 1):** A software company boosted quarterly revenues by shipping customers empty cartons just before quarter-end. Under accounting rules, revenue was recognized when the product shipped — even though the customers returned the cartons the next quarter. The revenue recognition rule was technically followed, but the economic reality was false. This is why Berman and Knight emphasize: "You can't always trust the numbers."

> **Case: Profit vs Cash at a Growing Company (Chapter 16):** A fast-growing company lands a huge contract. The income statement shows record profit. But the company has no cash — it spent everything on inventory and labor upfront, and the customer won't pay for 60 days. Profit is an opinion (based on revenue recognition and accrual accounting). Cash is what's in the bank. Both matter. But they're not the same.

### Framework B: The Artful Aspects of Finance (Parts 1, 2)

Every number on a financial statement involves estimates, assumptions, and judgments. Depreciation methods, revenue recognition timing, reserve levels, and expense classification all affect reported profit. Two companies with identical operations could report very different profits depending on their accounting choices.

> **Case: The One-Time Charge (Chapter 3):** Companies can take a "one-time charge" to cram a bunch of bad news into a single quarter — restructuring costs, write-offs, legal settlements. Future quarters then look better because the bad stuff is already accounted for. The numbers are legitimate under GAAP. But they paint a misleading picture of ongoing profitability if you don't understand what's happening.

### Framework C: Ratios as Context (Parts 5, 6)

Raw numbers are meaningless without context. A company with $10 million in profit could be a standout or a disaster depending on its size, industry, debt level, and asset base. Ratios provide the context that raw numbers cannot.

> **Case: The Big Five Numbers (Chapter 25):** Berman and Knight identify the five most important numbers for investors: revenue growth, earnings per share (EPS), net profit margin, return on equity (ROE), and free cash flow. Each reveals something different. Revenue growth shows momentum. ROE shows how efficiently the company uses shareholder capital. Free cash flow shows real financial flexibility. No single number tells the whole story.
