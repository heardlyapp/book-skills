# 4 — Anti-Patterns

> Source: *Financial Intelligence* by Karen Berman, Joe Knight & John Case.

## Common Errors

### 1. Believing the Numbers Are Absolute Truth

**The trap:** "If it's on the financial statement, it must be exactly right."

**Why it fails:** Every number involves estimates, assumptions, and judgments. Depreciation requires guessing the useful life of an asset. Revenue recognition requires deciding when revenue is earned. Reserves require estimating future liabilities. The numbers are the best approximation, not the truth.

> **Case: The One-Time Charge (Chapter 3):** Companies can dump all their bad news into a single quarter as a "one-time charge." The charge is legitimate. But it means that quarter looks terrible and future quarters look artificially good. An executive who takes the numbers at face value would think the company had a bad quarter and then recovered — when actually the underlying performance didn't change.
> **Key takeaway**: Always ask: "Is this a one-time event or does it reflect ongoing performance?" Non-recurring items can dramatically distort the picture.

> **Case: Enron's Off-Balance-Sheet Shenanigans (Chapter 1):** Enron used complex off-balance-sheet entities to hide debt and inflate profits. The transactions were technically legal under then-current accounting rules. But they completely misrepresented the company's financial health. The lesson: when financial statements seem too good to be true, they probably are.
> **Key takeaway**: If you can't understand where a number comes from, you can't trust it. Complexity is often a cover for manipulation.

### 2. Confusing Profit with Cash

**The trap:** "The company made a profit, so we must have more money than last year."

**Why it fails:** Accrual accounting means revenue is recognized when earned, not when cash arrives. A company can show a big profit while burning through cash — especially if it's growing fast (more inventory, more receivables). The cash flow statement exists specifically to bridge this gap.

### 3. Ignoring Working Capital

**The trap:** "I focus on profit. Working capital is just details."

**Why it fails:** Many profitable companies have gone bankrupt because they couldn't pay their bills. Managing the cash conversion cycle — how fast you collect, how fast you turn inventory, how slowly you pay — is as important as managing profit.

### 4. Using Ratios Without Context

**The trap:** "Our net margin is 10%. That must be good."

**Why it fails:** 10% net margin is excellent for a grocery chain (average 2-3%) and terrible for a software company (average 20-30%). Ratios must be evaluated against industry benchmarks, historical trends, and company strategy.

### 5. Keeping Finance a Mystery

**The trap:** "Finance is for the finance people. The rest of us just need to worry about our jobs."

**Why it fails:** Berman and Knight's research shows that companies with financially literate employees outperform those without. When people understand how their decisions affect the numbers, they make better decisions. Financial transparency is not a risk — it's a competitive advantage.
