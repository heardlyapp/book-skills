# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *Financial Intelligence* by Karen Berman, Joe Knight & John Case.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The Income Statement Reading Protocol

Find Revenue at the top. Subtract Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) to get Gross Profit. Subtract Operating Expenses to get Operating Profit (EBIT). Subtract Interest and Taxes to get Net Profit. Calculate each as a percentage of revenue: Gross Margin, Operating Margin, Net Margin. Compare to industry benchmarks and historical trends.

> **Case: The Drop in Gross Margin (Invocation Test):** When a department manager is asked why gross margin dropped, the answer is always in the components: (1) Did revenue drop? (2) Did COGS rise? (3) Both? In manufacturing, the most common cause is rising material costs, labor inefficiency, or price discounting. Trace the change to its source.
> **Key takeaway**: Any change in a financial metric can be traced to its components. Don't guess — trace.

> **Case: The Empty Box Revenue Boost (Chapter 1):** A software company shipped empty boxes to customers before quarter-end, then took them back the next quarter. Revenue was recognized on shipment. The revenue recognition rule was followed. But the economic substance was a fiction. The technique to expose this: compare revenue growth to cash collections from customers.
> **Key takeaway**: When revenue grows faster than cash collections, question the quality of revenue.

Cash Conversion Cycle = Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) + Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO) — Days Payables Outstanding (DPO). A negative CCC means you get paid before you have to pay your suppliers — the ideal. A long positive CCC means you're financing your customers, which ties up cash.

### 3. The Ratio Analysis Frame

Step 1: Calculate the ratio. Step 2: Compare to (a) the same period last year, (b) the budget/plan, and (c) industry peers. Step 3: Ask why. A ratio that changes significantly always has a story behind it.

### 4. The EBITDA Transparency Tool

EBITDA = Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. It shows operating profitability before non-cash charges and financing costs. Useful for comparing companies with different capital structures. Dangerous because it ignores the cost of replacing assets (depreciation).

### 5. The Working Capital Audit

Review the three levers of working capital: (1) Inventory — is there too much? Is it turning fast enough? (2) Receivables — what are the payment terms? How long do customers actually take? (3) Payables — are you paying early when you don't need to?
