# 4 — Anti-Patterns: Common Mistakes in Spatial Design

> Source: "A Pattern Language: Towns, Buildings, Construction" by Christopher Alexander

## Anti-Pattern 1: The Architect-Expert Fallacy

**The mistake:** Believing that good design must be done by trained architects imposing their vision from above, rather than by the people who use the space using a shared language of patterns.

> **Case: Le Corbusier's Marseilles Block** (Pattern 159, Buildings): Alexander cites Le Corbusier's Unité d'Habitation as an example of "the complete neglect of this pattern" — each apartment is very long and narrow, getting all light from one narrow end. Rooms at the far end are dark and uncomfortable. A famous architect's design, beautiful in photographs, but deeply uncomfortable to live in. The residents avoided the interior spaces.
> **Key takeaway**: Expert design without pattern thinking creates beautiful failures. The user's lived experience is the only valid test.

**The fix:** Use the pattern language as a shared vocabulary between users and designers. The user identifies the problems; the designer helps translate them into patterns and solutions.

## Anti-Pattern 2: The Detail Trap (Part-to-Whole)

**The mistake:** Starting with a beautiful detail (a custom door, a unique tile, a designer lamp) and building the room around it, instead of starting with the largest patterns and working down.

> **Case: The Wrong Sequence** (General): Someone picks expensive Italian tiles for their kitchen floor before considering whether the kitchen gets Light on Two Sides (159), whether it has a proper Kitchen Layout (139), or whether it connects to the Outdoor Room (163). The result: a beautiful floor in a dysfunctional kitchen that nobody wants to spend time in.
> **Key takeaway**: A detail only succeeds if the larger patterns above it are sound. Fix the room first, then the finishes.

**The fix:** Always read the sequence of patterns from largest to smallest before buying anything. Start with: site → building → room → detail. Not the reverse.

## Anti-Pattern 3: The Box (Rooms Without Natural Subspaces)

**The mistake:** Creating rooms as rectangular boxes with furniture pushed against the walls, ignoring the human need for alcoves, nooks, and subspaces that provide a sense of enclosure and privacy within larger spaces.

> **Case: The Modern Rectangular Room** (Pattern 179, Alcoves): "No one can be comfortable in a room which does not have natural subspaces." Alexander describes a rectangular living-dining room with the same ceiling height throughout and furniture evenly distributed. People feel exposed and restless; they don't stay long. The room feels like a corridor with furniture in it.
> **Key takeaway**: A room with no subspaces forces people into the center of attention. They can't find their "place" in the room. Every room needs alcove-like zones.

**The fix:** Divide each room into alcoves using partial walls, columns, changes of level, changes of ceiling height, or furniture arrangement. Each alcove should be at least 6 feet by 6 feet. Create a gradient from the main space to intimate subspaces.

## Anti-Pattern 4: The Dead Edge

**The mistake:** Designing the building's edge as a simple wall with windows, ignoring the crucial transition zone between inside and outside where people naturally want to be.

> **Case: Buildings Without Edges** (Pattern 160, Building Edge): "If the building edge fails, the spaces never become popular." Alexander describes buildings where the transition from inside to outside is abrupt — a door opens directly onto the sidewalk, or a window looks out onto a blank wall. These edge zones are dead spaces. Nobody gathers there.
> **Key takeaway**: The edge is the most important part of any building. It's where people naturally want to be. If it's dead, the building feels dead.

**The fix:** Create gradual transitions: paths, porches, patios, terraces, arcades, and semi-enclosed outdoor rooms between inside and outside. Make the edge deep enough to linger in (Patterns 160-168).

## Anti-Pattern 5: Single-Source Lighting

**The mistake:** Lighting a room from only one side, creating steep light gradients, glare around heads and objects, and a dark far end that feels unwelcoming.

> **Case: Hotels and Offices** (Pattern 159, Light on Two Sides): Many hotel rooms and offices have a single window wall with the bed/desk at the far end. The result: the area near the window is too bright, the far end is too dark, and glare on screens or reading material causes discomfort. People turn on artificial lights even during the day, trying to compensate.
> **Key takeaway**: "If you build a room with light on one side only, you can be almost certain that you are wasting your money. People will stay out of that room if they can possibly avoid it."

**The fix:** Give every room outdoor space on at least two sides. If impossible, use high ceilings, white walls, large windows with deep reveals, or a shallow room depth (under 8 feet with two windows side by side).

## Anti-Pattern 6: Uniform Ceiling Height

**The mistake:** Using the same ceiling height for every room, ignoring the psychological effect of varying ceiling heights on the sense of enclosure, intimacy, and grandeur.

> **Case: Developer Apartments** (Pattern 190, Ceiling Height Variety): Alexander notes that modern construction tends to standardize ceiling heights. The result: all rooms feel the same — no sense of compression and release, no hierarchy of spaces. The bedroom feels as large as the living room; the entrance feels no different from the hallway.
> **Key takeaway**: Ceiling height is not a structural decision — it's a psychological one. Vary it to create spatial hierarchy.

**The fix:** Use lowest ceilings (7-7.5 feet) for intimate spaces like alcoves and bedrooms. Use standard heights (8-9 feet) for general rooms. Use higher ceilings (10+ feet) for public rooms and entrance spaces. The transition between heights marks a change in the quality of the space.
