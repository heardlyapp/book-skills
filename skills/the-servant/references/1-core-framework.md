# Core Framework · Authority vs Power · The 6 Principles

> Source: *The Servant*, Chapters 1-3

---

## Chapter 1: The Definitions

The book's central distinction:

| | Power | Authority |
|---|---|---|
| Definition | The ability to force or coerce someone to do your will | The skill of getting people to willingly do your will |
| Source | Position, title | Character, service, relationship |
| Result | Compliance (reluctant) | Commitment (willing) |
| Durability | Lasts only while you hold the position | Lasts beyond your position |

> **Case:** The story of the monastery — the leader of the week-long retreat had no title over the participants, yet they followed his guidance. He had authority, not power.

## Chapter 2: The Old Paradigm vs The New Model

**Old Paradigm:** "I'm the boss. Do what I say." Command-and-control. The leader holds power and uses it to get results.

**New Model:** The leader serves the team. Authority is earned through service, character, and meeting others' needs. Results come through trust, not fear.

## Chapter 3: The 6 Principles of Servant Leadership

1. **Patience** — Showing self-control and giving others time to grow
2. **Kindness** — Giving attention, appreciation, and encouragement
3. **Humility** — Being authentic, without pretense or arrogance
4. **Respect** — Treating others as important people
5. **Selflessness** — Meeting the needs of others before your own
6. **Forgiveness** — Giving up resentment when wronged

> **Case:** The story of the executive who blamed his team for poor results (Chapter 1). On the retreat, he discovered that his impatience, criticism, and selfishness had destroyed his authority. He had power as a VP — but no authority with his team.
