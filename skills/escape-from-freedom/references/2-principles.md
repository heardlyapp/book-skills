# The Mechanisms of Escape — Authoritarianism, Destructiveness, Conformity

> Source: Escape from Freedom, Chapters 5, 6

## The Three Main Mechanisms

When the burden of negative freedom becomes unbearable, individuals and societies adopt psychological mechanisms to escape. These are not conscious choices but unconscious responses to anxiety.

### 1. Authoritarianism (Sadomasochism)

The most important mechanism. It has two faces that are often combined:

| Face | Expression | Psychological Drive |
|------|-----------|-------------------|
| Masochistic | Desire to submit, surrender, merge with a powerful other | Relief from the burden of selfhood and decision |
| Sadistic | Desire to dominate, control, absorb others | Proof of power that compensates for felt powerlessness |

The authoritarian character admires power and despises weakness — both in themselves and others. They love those who have power over them and despise those they have power over. This is not contradiction but a system: the desire for submission and domination are two sides of the same coin.

### 2. Destructiveness

Not identical to sadism. The destructive character does not want to dominate — they want to eliminate the threatening other entirely. Fromm argues that destructiveness arises when the impulse to live and create is blocked. The energy that cannot be expressed constructively turns to destruction. This is why periods of social rigidity and blocked creativity produce waves of destructiveness.

### 3. Automaton Conformity

The most widespread escape mechanism in modern democracy. The individual adopts entirely the kind of personality offered to them by cultural patterns. They become exactly like everyone else. The illusion: they think they are making their own choices. The reality: their preferences, values, and even emotions are socially determined. This is the most subtle and pervasive form of the escape from freedom because it is not experienced as escape at all.

> **Case: The Psychology of Nazism** (Chapter 6)
> Fromm argues that Nazism appealed specifically to the German lower-middle class, which was the most affected by economic instability and social isolation after WWI. This group had the strongest authoritarian character structure: admiration for power, hatred of the weak, and a desperate need to submit to a leader. Hitler offered: a "magic helper" (the Führer) to submit to, an outgroup (Jews, Communists, foreigners) to dominate, and a movement to belong to. The Nazi ideology was not irrational — it was psychologically rational for people who could not bear the burden of freedom.
> **Key takeaway**: Authoritarianism grows in conditions of economic insecurity and social atomization. People do not choose authoritarianism because they are evil — they choose it because the burden of freedom without community or security is unbearable. The solution is not just political but psychological: a society that enables positive freedom.

> **Case: The Psychology of the Protestant Reformation** (Chapter 3)
> Fromm interprets Luther and Calvin's theology as a reflection of the emerging bourgeois' psychological needs. Luther's emphasis on the total depravity of man and salvation through grace alone created intense anxiety and submission to God. Calvin's doctrine of predestination created even more isolation: no one could know if they were saved. The psychological function of these doctrines: they made the individual feel powerless and dependent — thus prepared to accept the authority of God (and later, secular authorities) without question.
> **Key takeaway**: Religious doctrines are not just beliefs about the cosmos — they are expressions of psychological needs. A theology that emphasizes human powerlessness and dependence will be attractive to people who feel powerless and dependent. The demand for submission to God prepares the psychological ground for submission to earthly authority.
