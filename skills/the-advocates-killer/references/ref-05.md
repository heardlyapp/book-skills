# Legal Ethics & Professional Boundaries

## Reference: Ethical Dilemmas in The Advocate's Killer

This reference file examines the legal ethics and professional boundary challenges that Sabre O. Brown faces in *The Advocate's Killer*. As a juvenile court attorney, Sabre operates under a unique ethical framework. When her own life is threatened and she becomes personally entangled in a murder investigation, the boundaries between professional duty, personal safety, and ethical obligations become dangerously blurred.

---

## Case Study 1: Attorney-Client Privilege in Crisis

The attorney-client privilege is the cornerstone of Sabre's professional identity. But what happens when the attorney is also a potential victim?

**The Privilege Problem:** Attorney-client privilege protects communications between an attorney and their client from disclosure. In typical circumstances, this is straightforward. But in *The Advocate's Killer*, Sabre faces a unique dilemma:

- She represents children whose parents may include the killer
- Information shared by her clients could identify the murderer
- But she cannot disclose client communications without violating privilege
- If she knows a client's parent is dangerous, can she act on that knowledge without breaching confidentiality?

**The Child Client Exception:** Dependency court creates a special ethical framework because the attorney's client is a child:
- Children may not fully understand privilege
- The attorney's duty is to the child's best interests, not necessarily the child's stated wishes
- Information from a child about their parent's behavior may be critical to safety — but is it privileged?
- Courts have held that the parent-child privilege does not extend to dependency proceedings in the same way it applies in other contexts

**Duty of Confidentiality vs. Duty of Protection:** Sabre also has a duty to protect her clients from harm. If she believes a client's parent is the killer, does her duty of protection override her duty of confidentiality? The legal answer is complex:
- The threat of future harm may justify disclosure under the "crime-fraud exception"
- The imminent threat exception allows attorneys to disclose information to prevent death or substantial bodily harm
- But the threshold for "imminent" is high — general suspicion does not qualify

### Real-World Case: ABA Formal Opinion 10-456 (2010)

The American Bar Association addressed the tension between confidentiality and self-protection in Formal Opinion 10-456. The opinion held that an attorney who receives threats from a client may disclose otherwise confidential information to the extent reasonably necessary to protect themselves. However, the opinion narrowly defines "self-protection" — the threat must be specific and credible, not general or speculative. This opinion is directly relevant to Sabre's situation: as the threats escalate from anonymous texts to packages, the threshold for self-protective disclosure may be crossed. But the ambiguity creates the ethical tension that drives her dilemma: does she protect herself or her clients?

## Case Study 2: Conflicts of Interest When the Attorney Is a Target

When Sabre becomes a target of the same person who may have connections to her clients, the potential for conflicts of interest multiplies:

**Types of Conflicts Sabre Faces:**
- **Current Client Conflict:** If the killer is the parent of a child Sabre represents, can she continue representing that child while investigating the parent? Her personal interest in identifying the killer conflicts with her professional duty to provide zealous representation.
- **Past Client Conflict:** If the killer is a former client's family member, Sabre's knowledge from the prior representation may be relevant — but may also be privileged. She cannot use former client information to the detriment of that client.
- **Witness Conflict:** If Sabre becomes a witness in the murder trial (through receiving the threatening messages), she may be disqualified from serving as counsel of record in any related proceeding. An attorney cannot be both witness and advocate in the same case.
- **Personal Interest Conflict:** ABA Model Rule 1.7 prohibits representation that would be materially limited by the lawyer's own interests. Sabre's interest in her own safety and the safety of her family may materially limit her ability to represent her clients objectively.

**The Solution in Practice:** When conflicts arise:
- The attorney must disclose the conflict to all affected clients
- Informed consent must be obtained in writing
- If the conflict cannot be resolved, the attorney must withdraw
- Withdrawal itself creates complications — new counsel must be brought up to speed, potentially delaying proceedings and harming the client

### Real-World Case: In re Marriage of Johnson (California, 2018)

In a California family law case, a mother's attorney was threatened by the father during a custody proceeding. The attorney filed for a restraining order against the father while simultaneously representing the mother in the custody case. The court disqualified the attorney, finding that his personal interest in the restraining order created a conflict with his duty to his client. The court reasoned that the attorney's judgment might be clouded by his personal animosity toward the father, and that his role as a party to the restraining order proceeding would inevitably affect his representation. This case illustrates the real-world consequence Sabre faces: by becoming personally involved in the investigation, she may lose the right to represent her clients.

## Case Study 3: The Prosecutor's Charging Decision and Sabre's Professional Duties

Once the killer is identified, the charging decision intersects with Sabre's professional obligations:

**The Prosecutor's Role:** The district attorney decides whether to file charges based on:
- Sufficiency of evidence
- Likelihood of conviction
- Interests of justice
- Wishes of the victim's family

**Sabre's Dilemma:** If the killer is a client's parent, Sabre faces conflicting obligations:
- She wants justice for the murdered social worker
- She wants to protect her child client's remaining parent
- She may have concerns about the child's placement if both parents are removed from the home
- She may possess information that could help or hurt the prosecution

**Ethical Withdrawal:** If Sabre's testimony or involvement is essential to the case, she cannot continue as the child's attorney. The withdrawal must be done in a way that minimizes harm to the child — transitioning the case to another attorney smoothly and ensuring the child's needs remain paramount.

**The Ultimate Ethical Question:** How far should an attorney go to protect a client who is connected to someone who wants to kill them? This is the ethical heart of *The Advocate's Killer*. Sabre's professional oath demands that she represent her clients zealously, without regard to her personal safety. But she is also a mother, a daughter, and a human being with a right to self-preservation.

### Real-World Framework: California Rules of Professional Conduct, Rule 1.6

California's ethical rules provide a framework that mirrors Sabre's dilemma:
- **Rule 1.6(a):** An attorney shall not reveal confidential information without informed consent
- **Rule 1.6(b)(1):** An attorney may reveal confidential information to the extent reasonably necessary to prevent a criminal act that the attorney reasonably believes is likely to result in death or substantial bodily harm
- **Rule 1.6(b)(3):** An attorney may reveal confidential information to establish a claim or defense in a controversy between the attorney and the client

The "reasonably believes" standard gives Sabre discretion — but also creates risk. If she discloses information based on a reasonable belief that the killer will harm her or her clients, she is protected. But if her belief is deemed unreasonable, she could face disciplinary action. This ambiguity is exactly where Burrell places her protagonist: in the gray zone between professional duty and personal survival.

## Case Study 4: The Investigator-Attorney Relationship

JP's role as Sabre's private investigator raises its own ethical considerations:

**The Ethical Framework for PI-Attorney Collaboration:**
- Communications between Sabre and JP are generally protected by the work product doctrine and attorney-client privilege if JP is acting as Sabre's agent
- JP's investigative findings must be shared with law enforcement only through proper channels
- JP cannot engage in illegal activity (trespass, hacking, impersonation) even to protect Sabre
- Evidence obtained illegally by JP is inadmissible in court — and could taint the entire case

**JP's Limitations:** As a private investigator, JP has less authority than law enforcement:
- He cannot make arrests
- He cannot compel testimony
- He cannot access sealed court records
- He cannot enter private property without permission
- He cannot conduct searches without a warrant

These limitations create tension in the plot. Sabre and JP must work within these boundaries while the killer operates without any. The asymmetry between the investigator's constraints and the killer's freedom is a source of dramatic tension.

## Practical Applications

1. **Map Sabre's Ethical Obligations:** For each client Sabre has in the book, identify the specific ethical duties she owes. When those duties conflict with her personal safety, the ethical dilemma is revealed.

2. **Analyze the Withdrawal Threshold:** At what point should Sabre withdraw from her cases due to conflict of interest? The ABA Model Rules provide guidance, but the decision is Sabre's — and the wrong decision could harm her clients and her career.

3. **Balance Privilege and Protection:** For each piece of potentially incriminating information Sabre possesses, ask: is it privileged? If so, is there an exception that would permit disclosure? The answer determines both Sabre's legal liability and the plot progression.

4. **Evaluate JP's Investigation for Admissibility:** Assume every action JP takes will be scrutinized in court. Does his investigation hold up to evidentiary standards? If not, his findings may be useless in the prosecution of the killer.
