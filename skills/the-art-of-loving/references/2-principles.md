# Key Principles

## 1. Love Is Giving, Not Receiving

"Mature love is an active power that gives, not a passive emotion that receives." The capacity to give — of your time, attention, care, and knowledge — is the essence of loving. Giving is not about sacrifice; it's about experiencing your own abundance.

## 2. Self-Love Is the Foundation

"The love of myself is inseparably connected with the love of any other being." Fromm directly challenges the belief that self-love and love of others are opposites. You cannot love others if you cannot love yourself. Self-love is not selfishness — it's the prerequisite for loving others.

> **Case: The Self-Sacrificing Parent** (Part II): Fromm describes a mother who sacrifices everything for her children — her time, her interests, her relationships. This appears loving, but Fromm argues it's often a form of hidden selfishness. A mother who cannot love herself cannot genuinely love her children. Her "sacrifice" creates guilt and dependency.
> **Key takeaway**: Self-sacrifice is not the same as love. True love requires that the giver also love themselves. You cannot give what you don't have.

## 3. Mature Love Preserves Integrity

"Paradoxically, the experience of separateness gives rise to love — and love overcomes separateness while preserving individuality." In mature love, two people become one while remaining two. They don't merge or lose themselves in each other.

## 4. Love Requires Knowledge

"To know the other person deeply is an essential element of love." Fromm distinguishes between superficial knowledge (knowing about someone) and deep knowledge (knowing their essence, their real needs). This knowledge requires active effort and attention.

## 5. The Ability to Be Alone

"The ability to be alone is the condition for the ability to love." Fromm argues that people who cannot tolerate being alone are incapable of genuine love because they come to relationships from neediness, not from wholeness.

> **Case: The Clinging Partner** (Part II): A partner who cannot be alone clings to their relationship out of fear of abandonment. This creates dependency, not love. Fromm argues that the capacity to be alone — to sit with yourself without needing distraction — is the foundation of the capacity for genuine connection.
> **Key takeaway**: If you cannot be alone, you cannot truly love. Your neediness will prevent you from seeing the other person as they are.
