# Core Framework: The Strategy Logic Flow

> Source: Playing to Win, Introduction and Chapter 1

## The Five Questions

Strategy is an integrated cascade of five choices: Winning Aspiration → Where to Play → How to Win → Capabilities → Management Systems. Each constrains the next. They cannot be answered in isolation.

> **Case: The Olay Turnaround** (Chapter 1): Olay was a struggling brand — seen as old-fashioned, declining below $800M. P&G chose to reposition it in "mass prestige" skincare. This WTP decision drove everything: new products (anti-aging), new packaging (gold, premium), new channels (department stores), new pricing.

> **Case: The P&G Transformation** (Introduction): When Lafley became CEO, P&G was underperforming. The winning aspiration: "undisputed leader in consumer products." This was neither generic nor vague — it was a specific commitment to make the hard choices.

> **Key takeaway**: The cascade forces clarity. Choosing Where to Play determines How to Win determines Capabilities determines Systems. Each choice narrows and focuses the next.

## Reverse Engineering

To understand a competitor's strategy, look at their actual choices — not their stated strategy.

> **Case: The Competitor's Move** (Chapter 1): When a competitor launches a new product in a premium segment, their WTP is that segment. Their HTW is whatever they've done to win there. Reading these choices reveals their real strategy — regardless of what their press releases say.

> **Key takeaway**: Strategy is revealed through actions, not words. Reverse engineering is the skill of reading those actions.
