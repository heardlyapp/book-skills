# Anti-Patterns: Strategic Mistakes

> Source: Playing to Win, Chapters 7-8

## Confusing Goals with Strategy

"We will grow 20%" is a goal. The strategy is the integrated set of choices that makes growth achievable. Without the choices, the goal is a fantasy.

> **Case: The Growth Trap** (Chapter 7): Companies set ambitious growth targets without making the hard strategic choices. "We'll grow in new markets" — which markets? What's the WTP? The HTW? Without answers, it's not strategy — it's hope.

> **Key takeaway**: A goal without a strategy is just a wish. The cascade of choices is what distinguishes strategy from aspiration.

## Avoiding Trade-offs

Trying to be everything to everyone results in being nothing to anyone.

> **Case: The Everything Brand** (Chapter 7): P&G made explicit trade-offs. Olay chose mass prestige, giving up mass market. Pantene chose science-backed premium, giving up the value segment. These trade-offs were painful — and essential.

> **Case: The Line Extension Trap** (Chapter 8): Companies extend brands into every possible category. The result: brand dilution. P&G learned that a clear WTP (where we play) and HTW (how we win) must limit what the brand does.

> **Key takeaway**: The most important strategic decisions are what you choose NOT to do. Strategy without sacrifice is not strategy.

## Failing to Reverse Engineer Competitors

Ignoring what competitors' actions reveal about their real strategy.

> **Case: The Missed Signal** (Chapter 8): A competitor entering a premium segment signals their WTP shift. Ignoring this signal means being caught off guard.

> **Key takeaway**: Strategy is revealed through actions, not words. Watch what competitors DO.
