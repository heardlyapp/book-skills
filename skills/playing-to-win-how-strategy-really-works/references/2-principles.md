# Principles: Where to Play and How to Win

> Source: Playing to Win, Chapters 3-4

## Where to Play (WTP)

The competitive arena: which customers, channels, geographies, and product categories. This is the most consequential strategic decision a company makes.

> **Case: Olay's WTP Shift** (Chapter 3): Olay moved from "basic skincare for older women" to "mass prestige anti-aging." New customers (younger, wealthier), new channels (department stores), new competitors (Lancôme, Estée Lauder). A complete redefinition of the competitive arena.

> **Key takeaway**: WTP defines everything — who you compete with, what capabilities you need, how you win. Changing it changes the entire game.

## How to Win (HTW)

The value proposition and competitive advantage. Must be specific enough to guide every decision.

> **Case: Pantene's HTW** (Chapter 4): Pantene's HTW was "hair care backed by science." The tagline "hair so healthy it shines" captured a unique position — not just cleaning hair but making it genuinely healthier. This HTW drove product development (pro-vitamin formula), packaging (science-forward), and advertising (dermatologist endorsements).

> **Case: Folgers' HTW** (Chapter 4): Folgers positioned as "the best part of waking up" — capturing the everyday ritual, not just coffee drinking. This was a different HTW than Starbucks (premium experience) or Maxwell House (value).

## Strategic Fit

WTP and HTW must be coherent. Premium HTW requires premium WTP. Mass market HTW requires mass market WTP.

> **Key takeaway**: WTP and HTW are two sides of the same strategic choice. Define both with equal rigor and ensure they fit together.
