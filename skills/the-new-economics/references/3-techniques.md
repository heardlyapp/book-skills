# Techniques — Leadership, Psychology, and the New Management

## Practical Methods

> **Case: Leadership vs Management** (Chapter 5): Deming distinguishes: "A leader is someone who understands the work and removes obstacles so people can do their best work. A manager is someone who sets targets and monitors performance." The leader studies the process. The manager studies the numbers. The leader asks "What can I remove?" The manager asks "Who is to blame?" Deming gives the example of a production line where the best worker was always assigned the most difficult jobs — so her output numbers looked average while others looked better. A manager would rank her lower. A leader would recognize she is carrying the hardest load.
> **Key takeaway**: Are you leading or managing? Leaders remove obstacles. Managers assign blame. The difference is visible in how your team talks about you.

> **Case: Intrinsic Motivation** (Chapter 6): Deming argues that all people have intrinsic motivation — a natural desire to do good work, to learn, to contribute. Traditional management extinguishes this desire through: ranking, quotas, bonuses, fear, and arbitrary targets. Deming writes: "The greatest waste in America is the failure to use the abilities of people." He draws on the psychology of Douglas McGregor (Theory X and Theory Y): Theory X assumes people are lazy and need to be controlled. Theory Y assumes people want to do good work and need to be supported.
> **Key takeaway**: Management is not about motivating people. People are already motivated. Management is about removing the obstacles that demotivate them.

> **Case: Drive Out Fear** (Chapter 5): Deming names fear as the greatest enemy of quality. "People are afraid to speak up. They are afraid to report problems. They are afraid to suggest improvements." When a mistake happens, the first instinct is to hide it — because blame follows. Deming's prescription: create a culture where mistakes are studied, not punished. "Every mistake is data about the system. If you punish the person who reports it, you lose the data."
> **Key takeaway**: If your people are afraid to tell you about problems, you will never fix them. The first job of leadership is to make it safe to speak.
