# Key Principles — Variation, Control Charts, and the Funnel Experiment

## Core Principles

> **Case: The Funnel Experiment** (Chapter 9): Deming demonstrates: a funnel drops marbles onto a target. You can adjust the funnel's position based on each marble's landing point — correcting every error (Rule 2). Or you can leave the funnel in place and accept the natural variation (Rule 1). Rule 2 produces wildly more variation. Rule 1 produces a stable, predictable pattern. The lesson: adjusting a process based on every data point increases variation. The best adjustment is often no adjustment at all.
> **Key takeaway**: Stop tampering with your processes. Every time you "fix" a process based on one data point, you may be making it worse. Study the data over time.

> **Case: Shewhart Control Charts** (Chapter 8): Deming's mentor Walter Shewhart developed a simple tool: plot data over time with a center line (average) and upper/lower control limits (typically 3 standard deviations above and below). Points within the limits are common cause variation — from the system. Points outside the limits are special cause — something specific happened. Management must respond differently to each. Common cause requires system change. Special cause requires investigation.
> **Key takeaway**: Learn to draw a control chart. It is the single most powerful tool for deciding when to act and when to leave things alone.

> **Case: The Heavy Losses** (Chapter 2): Deming catalogs the "heavy losses" caused by current management practices: performance ratings that destroy teamwork, management by numbers that encourage gaming, competition that divides rather than unites, fear that silences the people who know what is wrong. Deming writes: "The losses are not measurable. They are unknowable. But they are enormous." The cost of bad management is invisible because no one measures what could have been.
> **Key takeaway**: The cost of bad management is invisible. The work not done. The ideas not shared. The improvements not made. You cannot measure what did not happen — but that does not mean it is not costing you.
