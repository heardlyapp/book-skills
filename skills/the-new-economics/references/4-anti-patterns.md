# Anti-Patterns — Merit Pay, Management by Numbers, Blaming the Worker

## The Central Fallacy

The most dangerous assumption in management: that you can manage a system by managing the numbers. Deming's lifetime of work shows that management by numbers — targets, quotas, rankings, performance ratings — produces the opposite of what it intends. People learn to game the numbers, hide problems, and optimize for what is measured at the expense of what matters.

> **Case: The Futility of Merit Ratings** (Chapter 6): Deming calls performance ratings "the greatest single destroyer of joy in work." He argues that individual ratings are statistically invalid (they ignore system effects), psychologically destructive (they create fear and competition), and morally wrong (they rank people who cannot be ranked because their performance depends on the system). He notes that Japanese industry — which adopted Deming's ideas — largely does not use individual performance ratings. American industry, which ignored Deming, is addicted to them.
> **Key takeaway**: If you rank your people, you are destroying teamwork, encouraging gaming, and making accurate evaluation impossible. Stop.

> **Case: Management by Numbers** (Chapter 2): Deming describes a company that set a target: reduce defects by 50%. The workers found a way to meet the target: they stopped reporting defects. The defect rate "improved" — but quality did not. Management celebrated. Deming comments: "The most important numbers are unknown and unknowable." If you manage by visible numbers alone, people will make the numbers look good — at the expense of what the numbers are supposed to represent.
> **Key takeaway**: The most important things in your organization are not measurable. Customer loyalty. Employee engagement. Innovation. Trust. If you manage only by what you can count, you will destroy what you cannot count.

> **Case: Blaming the Worker** (Chapter 7): The Red Beads experiment is a devastating indictment of blaming individuals. The workers try as hard as they can. They improve their technique. They concentrate. Nothing changes. The defect rate is determined by the ratio of red to white beads in the bowl — a system parameter that no worker can influence. Deming's conclusion: "When a worker cannot control the outcome, do not blame the worker. Fix the system."
> **Key takeaway**: Next time something goes wrong, before asking "Who did this?" ask "What about our system allowed this to happen?" The answer will tell you where to fix.
