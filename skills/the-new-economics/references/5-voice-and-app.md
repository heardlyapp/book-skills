# Author Voice and Application Scenarios — Deming's Perspective and Everyday Application

## W. Edwards Deming's Voice

Deming writes with the authority of someone who has seen the data and knows he is right. His tone is direct, sometimes blunt, occasionally impatient. He does not argue — he demonstrates. His most powerful teachings are not stated but shown through experiments (Red Beads, Funnel) that the reader can visualize. He is not interested in being liked. He is interested in being understood.

## Five Common Application Scenarios

**Scenario 1: The Manager Overseeing a Performance Review Cycle** — "It's review season and I hate it. My team is anxious, I don't have good data, and the ranking feels unfair."

> **Case: The Case Against Performance Ratings** (Chapter 6): Deming's solution: abolish them. Replace them with feedback focused on the system, not the individual. Ask: "What can I do to make your work easier?" "What obstacles are in your way?" "How can we improve the process together?" This is harder than filling out a form. But it produces better results.

**Scenario 2: The Executive Reacting to Every Monthly Report** — "Sales were up last month. This month they're down. I need to find out what's wrong."

> **Case: The Funnel Experiment** (Chapter 9): Deming would say: plot 20 months of data. Are the ups and downs within the control limits? If yes, do not react. The system is stable. If you react to every fluctuation, you will increase the variation.

**Scenario 3: The Quality Manager Adding More Inspections** — "We're finding too many defects at the end of the line. I'm adding more inspectors."

> **Case: Quality Built In** (Deming throughout): "Quality cannot be inspected in." The defects you find at the end are symptoms of earlier process failures. Adding inspectors does not fix the process. It only tells you how many defects the process produced. Fix the process upstream.

**Scenario 4: The Department Head Whose Team Is Disengaged** — "My team seems bored and unmotivated. They do the minimum. I've tried bonuses and it did not help."

> **Case: Intrinsic Motivation** (Chapter 6): Deming would say: your team did not lose their motivation. You extinguished it through targets, rankings, and control. Remove the obstacles. Give them autonomy. Show them how their work serves the whole. Trust them.

**Scenario 5: The New CEO Trying to Transform a Traditional Company** — "I inherited a top-down culture with lots of blame and fear. How do I change it?"

> **Case: Transformation Requires Commitment** (Chapter 11): Deming's answer: transformation cannot happen halfway. The CEO must understand and commit to the System of Profound Knowledge. It starts with the leader. The leader must learn, then teach, then demonstrate through action.
