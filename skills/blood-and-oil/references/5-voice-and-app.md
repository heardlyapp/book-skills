# Author's Voice + Key Scenarios

## The Authors' Voices

Bradley Hope and Justin Scheck are investigative journalists. Their tone is:
- **Fact-packed narrative** — every claim is sourced, every detail documented
- **No editorializing** — they let the facts build the case against MBS
- **Access-driven** — they interviewed dozens of insiders, former officials, and diplomats

## Key Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Business in Saudi Arabia
**Situation**: User is evaluating a business partnership with a Saudi entity.

**Solution**: Use the Political Risk Checklist. Understand that this is a political relationship, not a commercial one. Build exit clauses. Consider reputational risk. Don't assume the relationship will survive a succession change.

### Scenario 2: Geopolitical Analysis
**Situation**: User needs to analyze Saudi foreign policy for investment or strategic planning.

**Solution**: Apply the MBS Playbook framework. What stage is Saudi in? What's driving MBS's current behavior? What are the regional dynamics? Saudi decisions that look irrational from the outside make sense when you understand the internal consolidation logic.

### Scenario 3: Understanding Vision 2030
**Situation**: User wants to understand whether Saudi modernization is real.

**Solution**: Yes, the modernization is real — MBS genuinely wants to transform the economy and society. But it's modernization without liberalization. The reforms serve consolidated power. Invest accordingly.

### Scenario 4: Khashoggi and Ethics
**Situation**: User is struggling with whether to engage with Saudi entities after Khashoggi's murder.

**Solution**: The Khashoggi calculus is personal. The book provides the facts — the murder, the cover-up, the impunity. Your decision about engagement is an ethical choice, not just a business one. Some investors walked away. Others stayed. Both decisions have consequences.

### Scenario 5: Succession Risk
**Situation**: User wants to understand what happens after MBS.

**Solution**: MBS has eliminated all potential rivals. But that doesn't mean the system is stable. The succession fix was personal, not institutional. When MBS leaves power — however that happens — the succession problem will return. Long-term planning should account for this.
