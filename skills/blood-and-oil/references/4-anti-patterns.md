# Anti-Patterns

## 1. Assuming Reform Means Liberalization

**The mistake**: Seeing social reforms (women driving, entertainment, tourism) and assuming Saudi Arabia is becoming a liberal democracy.

**Why it fails**: Reform and repression are not opposites under authoritarianism. They're complementary strategies. The reforms make MBS popular; the repression makes him feared. Both consolidate his power.

## 2. Assuming Economic Ties Create Political Influence

**The mistake**: "If we invest enough in Saudi Arabia, we'll have leverage over their behavior."

**Why it fails**: Autocrats use foreign investment as a tool, not a constraint. Saudi Arabia has taken billions from the West while pursuing policies — the Yemen war, Khashoggi's murder, oil price manipulation — directly contrary to Western interests. The investment didn't buy influence; it bought access.

## 3. Separating the Ruler from the Regime

**The mistake**: "MBS is the reformer; the regime is the problem."

**Why it fails**: MBS IS the regime. He consolidated power by eliminating every check on his authority. The Ritz-Carlton purge didn't target corrupt officials; it targeted anyone with independent power. There is no distinction between the reformer and the autocrat.

> **Case: The Saudi Investment Mirage** (Chapter 13): MBS courted Western tech giants and investors — SoftBank's Masayoshi Son, Silicon Valley executives, Hollywood producers. The investments were real. But they were also a strategy: by tying powerful Western interests to Saudi Arabia's success, MBS created a constituency that would defend him regardless of his domestic or foreign policies.
> **Key takeaway**: The investments were not a sign of integration into global norms. They were a shield against international criticism.

## 4. Overestimating US Leverage

**The mistake**: "The US can pressure Saudi Arabia because of the security relationship and oil dependence."

**Why it fails**: Saudi Arabia has diversified its relationships — China is now Saudi's biggest oil customer, Russia works with Saudi on OPEC+. The US-Saudi relationship is important but no longer decisive. Saudi Arabia's options have expanded while the US's leverage has shrunk.

## 5. Ignoring the Regional Dynamics

**The mistake**: Analyzing Saudi Arabia in isolation from its regional competition with Iran, Turkey, Qatar, and the UAE.

**Why it fails**: Almost every Saudi decision is shaped by regional competition. The Yemen war is about Iranian influence. The blockade of Qatar is about regional dominance. The normalization with Israel is about the anti-Iran coalition. Ignoring the regional frame misses the strategic logic.
