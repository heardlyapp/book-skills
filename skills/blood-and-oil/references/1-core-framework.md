# Core Framework: The MBS Playbook

## The Central Thesis

"Mohammed bin Salman is not just a Saudi prince — he's a new kind of autocrat for the 21st century: tech-savvy, media-savvy, ruthless, and operating on a global stage." The book traces how MBS consolidated power in record time by combining traditional authoritarian tactics with modern media and economic tools.

> **Case: The Succession Fix** (Chapters 1-2): MBS was not the natural heir. The Saudi succession was designed to pass between brothers, not down to sons. MBS engineered a series of moves — building relationships with his father King Salman, marginalizing his cousin Crown Prince Mohammed bin Nayef, and positioning himself as the reformer — to rewrite the succession rules.
> **Key takeaway**: In authoritarian systems, succession is always the most dangerous moment. MBS understood that the path to power required eliminating not just rivals, but the system that produced them.

## The Five-Step Playbook

1. **Fix the succession** — Eliminate rivals, rewrite the rules, secure the father's backing
2. **Transform the economy** — Vision 2030: diversify away from oil, attract foreign investment, create a "business-friendly" environment
3. **Open society** — Women driving, tourism, entertainment, cinemas — genuine reforms that build popular support and Western approval
4. **Crush dissent** — The Ritz-Carlton purge, Khashoggi's murder, suppression of activists — the iron fist behind the velvet glove
5. **Project power** — Yemen war, oil price wars, sports investments, media platforms — Saudi Arabia as a global player

> **Case: The Ritz-Carlton** (Chapter 7): In November 2017, MBS ordered the arrest of hundreds of Saudi Arabia's wealthiest and most powerful people — princes, ministers, billionaires — and held them in the Ritz-Carlton hotel. They were released only after signing over cash, assets, and properties. The message: no one's wealth or position made them safe. Power flowed from MBS alone.
> **Key takeaway**: The Ritz-Carlton was not about corruption — it was about consolidation. MBS wasn't fighting corruption; he was demonstrating that he held absolute power.
