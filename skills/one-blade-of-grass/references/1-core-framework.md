# Core Framework — One Blade of Grass

## Prologue: The Awakening

Shukman opens with a meditation retreat in Gallup, New Mexico. Rain lashing down. A "glowing calm was seeping up through me, like an incoming tide." Then: "With what felt like a seismic jolt, the room seemed to blow wide open. The whole scene became an infinitely broad expanse, and it was as if I was sucked into that expanse myself and became part of it." He was one with the world. "All had been well, secretly well, all along."

## Part One: A Troubled Beginning

**Childhood:** Shukman grew up in England with a difficult home life. His parents' marriage was troubled. He suffered from chronic, severe eczema from childhood — "skin deep" pain that never stopped. "My skin was a battlefield."

**The Gap:** As a teenager, he had a spontaneous experience of "the gap" — a moment when the usual sense of self dropped away. He didn't know what it was, but it planted a seed.

**Travels:** He became a travel writer, living in Brazil, Mexico, and elsewhere. He wrote novels, poetry. But something was missing. "Tired of London, tired of life."

## Part Two: The Path

**The Ox and the Mosquito:** Shukman encountered Zen through a book by Dogen. "One bright pearl" — Dogen's teaching that the entire universe is a single bright pearl. This resonated with his childhood "gap" experience.

**Teachers:** He found his way to Joan Rieck Roshi, his first Zen teacher. Then John Gaynor Roshi, a koan master in the Sanbo Zen lineage. Then Ruben Habito Roshi. "Teacher, teacher" — the memoir traces his search for the right teacher.

**Koan Practice:** The Sanbo Zen school uses koans — paradoxical questions that cannot be solved by the thinking mind. "Sweet obedience" — the surrender to the koan, to the teacher, to the practice. Case: Shukman worked through hundreds of koans over years.

**Monastery:** He spent time at monasteries in the US and Japan. The "black hole" — a period of intense doubt and difficulty in practice.

## Part Three: Integration

**Calf Born of a Bull:** After a decade of practice, Shukman began to teach. "No other" — the realization that there is no separation between self and other.

**Mountain Cloud:** He co-founded Mountain Cloud Zen Center in Santa Fe, New Mexico. "The koan of the suburbs" — bringing Zen practice into everyday life.

**Epilogue:** Shukman reflects on what it means to be "cooked" — the slow transformation that practice brings. "You just know. You're done."

## The Backstory: Gensha's Awakening

Shukman tells the story of the ninth-century Chinese master Gensha. Gensha was a fisherman, became a monk, then left the monastery feeling he wasn't good enough. On his way out, he stubbed his toe on a rock — it "exploded." In agony, he had an epiphany: "There is no body. So where does this pain come from?" He returned to his master: "Not a single step was taken." His master welcomed him back. "This whole universe is one bright pearl."
