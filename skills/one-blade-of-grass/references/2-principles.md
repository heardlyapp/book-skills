# Key Principles — One Blade of Grass

## I. Awakening Is Possible — But It's Not the End

Shukman's awakening in the New Mexico meditation hall was real and profound. But it was not the end of his journey. "Either you're cooked or you're not." The awakening was a beginning, not a finish. Case: After his awakening, he still had to set up the interview room. Life continued.

## II. The Koan Path Works

The Sanbo Zen school uses koans — questions that cannot be solved by thinking. "What is the sound of one hand?" Shukman worked through hundreds over years. Case: "Sweet obedience" — the koan was not something to figure out but something to become.

## III. Suffering Can Be the Door

Shukman's chronic eczema was a lifelong trial. "My skin was a battlefield." But the same sensitivity that made his skin a source of pain also made him open to subtle inner experiences. Case: The "gap" experience as a teenager came during a moment of extreme skin pain.

## IV. A Teacher Is Essential

"You cannot do this alone." Shukman emphasizes the need for a teacher. Case: His search for the right teacher — Joan Rieck Roshi, then John Gaynor Roshi, then Ruben Habito Roshi. "Teacher, teacher" — the title of the chapter where he finally finds his root teacher.

## V. The Unseen Life Is Always There

"All had been well, secretly well, all along." Zen practice does not create something new — it reveals what was always there. Case: Gensha's "one bright pearl" — the whole universe is already perfect.

## VI. Practice Takes Time

"You can get this impression in the world of meditation: people go in ordinary; then something happens in the middle; then they reemerge new, different." But the "cooking" takes years. Case: Shukman's journey spanned decades from his first "gap" experience to becoming a teacher.

## VII. Mountains Are Mountains Again

The Zen saying: Before Zen, mountains are mountains. During Zen, mountains are not mountains. After Zen, mountains are mountains again. Case: "So whatever the cooking did, it wasn't supposed to lead to some otherworldly result." The ordinary becomes extraordinary simply by being ordinary.
