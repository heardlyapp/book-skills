# Techniques — One Blade of Grass

## How to Practice Zen (Based on Henry Shukman)

### Technique 1: Koan Surrender
A koan cannot be solved by thinking. Shukman's technique: stop trying to figure it out. "Sweet obedience" — let the koan work on you, not the other way around. Case: Shukman would sit with a koan for weeks or months. The answer was not found — it found him.

### Technique 2: The Wall Gaze
During his awakening, Shukman's "gaze fixed on the wall in front of me — a wall of adobe, glowing in the light of a lamp. An intense love for the wall welled up." The technique: don't look at the wall. Become the wall. Case: "All of a sudden, the room seemed to blow wide open."

### Technique 3: The Toe Stub Epiphany
Gensha stubbed his toe and awakened. The technique: when something painful or unexpected happens, don't just react. Ask: "Who is feeling this? Where does this pain come from?" Case: "There is no body. So where does this pain come from?" The pain is real. The self that suffers may not be.

### Technique 4: The Gap Recognition
Shukman had a "gap" experience as a teenager — a moment when the usual sense of self dropped away. He didn't know what it was. The technique: when you notice a moment of selflessness — a gap between thoughts, a moment of pure perception — recognize it. That's it. Case: "I didn't know it at the time, but that gap was my first taste of what Zen calls kensho."

### Technique 5: The Retreat Reset
Shukman attended many meditation retreats. The technique: commit to periods of intensive practice. "A retreat can shake loose what daily practice cannot." Case: His breakthrough happened on the third or fourth night of a retreat.

### Technique 6: The Teacher Interview
Sanbo Zen uses dokusan — private interviews with the teacher. The technique: bring your koan, your doubt, your confusion. Don't pretend to understand. Case: "The teacher would see right through me. There was no hiding."

### Technique 7: The Book Burial
Shukman had a period of "book burial" — giving up reading spiritual books to sit with his own experience. The technique: stop seeking answers in books. Sit with the question itself. Case: "I had to stop reading about Zen and start doing it."
