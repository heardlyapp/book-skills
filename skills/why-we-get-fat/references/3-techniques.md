> Source: Why We Get Fat, Chapters 7-14 — practical techniques from the carbohydrate-insulin framework

## 1. The Carbohydrate Elimination Protocol

Taubes's core dietary strategy: eliminate the carbohydrates that spike insulin, and let the body regulate appetite naturally.

**What to eliminate**: All sugar (sucrose, HFCS), all refined grains (bread, pasta, rice, tortillas, crackers, cereal), all starchy vegetables (potatoes, corn, beets), all fruit juices, all beer and sugary drinks, and all "low-fat" processed foods (which are typically high in sugar).

**What to eat**: Meat, poultry, fish, eggs, non-starchy vegetables (leafy greens, broccoli, cauliflower, peppers, etc.), natural fats (butter, olive oil, avocado, coconut oil), full-fat dairy (cheese, cream, yogurt), nuts and seeds.

> **Case: The Two-Week Test** (Chapter 13): Taubes recommends a two-week trial: eliminate all carbohydrates except vegetables. No counting calories. No portion control. Eat as much meat, fat, and vegetables as you want. Most people lose 5-10 pounds in two weeks without hunger. The goal is not the weight loss — it's the experience of satiety without carbs.
> **Key takeaway**: The two-week test is not a diet — it's an experiment. See what happens to your hunger, your energy, and your weight when you remove the hormonal stimulus.

## 2. The "Eat When You're Hungry" Rule

One of the most radical implications of the carbohydrate-insulin hypothesis: you should not count calories. You should count carbohydrates. If you eat low-carb, your appetite will naturally regulate itself. Eat when you're hungry. Stop when you're full.

**How to do it**: Eat three meals a day. No snacking (snacking spikes insulin). Each meal should include protein, fat, and vegetables. If you're hungry between meals, you ate too many carbs. Increase fat and protein at the next meal.

> **Case: The Satiety Response** (Chapter 12): Taubes describes the experience of patients who switch to low-carb: within a week, they report being "full" after meals in a way they never experienced on low-fat diets. They stop thinking about food between meals. This is not psychological — it's physiological. The body is accessing its own fat stores, so the brain is not signaling hunger.
> **Key takeaway**: Hunger is not a moral failing. It is a hormonal signal. Fix the hormones, and the hunger goes away.

## 3. The Fructose Elimination Rule

Fructose is metabolized differently from other sugars and is uniquely problematic. Taubes recommends eliminating all sources of concentrated fructose: table sugar (half fructose), HFCS, fruit juice, honey, agave, dried fruit, and (initially) whole fruit.

**How to do it**: Read ingredient labels. Avoid anything with sugar, HFCS, cane syrup, fruit juice concentrate, or "evaporated cane juice." These are all sugar by different names. Whole fruit (especially berries) can be reintroduced in small quantities after weight stabilizes.

> **Case: The Liver Effect** (Chapter 10): Taubes explains that fructose is metabolized entirely in the liver, where it promotes de novo lipogenesis — the creation of fat from sugar. This process contributes to fatty liver disease, insulin resistance, and increased fat storage. A 2011 study found that regular consumption of sugar-sweetened beverages increased liver fat by 27% in ten weeks — even when total calories were controlled.
> **Key takeaway**: Sugar is not just "empty calories." It is actively harmful because of its unique metabolic effects. The liver converts excess fructose directly to fat.

## 4. The Hunger-Satiety Journal

A simple tool for understanding your body's response to different foods. For one week, record what you eat and rate your hunger 1-10 before and after each meal.

**What to watch for**: If you feel hungry 2-3 hours after a meal, that meal had too many carbohydrates. If you feel satisfied for 4-6 hours, the meal had the right balance of protein, fat, and fiber.

> **Case: The Learning Process** (Chapter 14): Taubes emphasizes that learning to eat low-carb is a skill. Most people need 2-4 weeks to "learn" what satiety feels like without carbohydrates. The Hunger-Satiety Journal accelerates this learning by making the connection between food choices and hunger explicit.
> **Key takeaway**: You don't need to count carbohydrates forever. After 4-6 weeks, you will intuitively know what your body can handle.

## 5. The Metabolic Adaptation Strategy

Weight loss plateaus are normal and expected. The body is designed to resist weight loss (it's a survival mechanism). When you hit a plateau, don't cut calories further — reassess carbohydrate intake.

**How to handle a plateau**: First, confirm you're truly low-carb (under 50g net carbs/day, ideally under 20g). Second, increase fat intake (fat is the fuel on low-carb). Third, consider intermittent fasting (skip breakfast, eat lunch and dinner). Fourth, check for hidden carbohydrates in sauces, condiments, medications, and "low-carb" packaged foods.

> **Case: The Hidden Carb Trap** (Chapter 14): Many processed "low-carb" foods add sugar alcohols, fiber, and other ingredients that can still spike insulin in sensitive individuals. Taubes recommends avoiding all processed foods during the initial phase. Eat real food: meat, fish, eggs, vegetables, and natural fats. If it has a label, it might stall your progress.
> **Key takeaway**: The most common cause of a plateau is hidden carbohydrates. The most reliable solution is to eat only foods that don't have ingredient labels.
