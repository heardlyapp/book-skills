# Techniques: The Creative Practice

## Tuning In Practice

1. Set aside time each day for non-doing — sit quietly, walk, shower, daydream
2. Notice what captures your attention — that's a signal
3. Follow curiosity without judgment — even if it seems irrelevant
4. Record ideas immediately — they disappear if not captured
5. Trust the ones that keep coming back

## Letting Go Practice

When you're stuck:
1. Ask: "What am I attached to in this outcome?"
2. Imagine releasing that attachment — what would you create if you didn't care about the result?
3. Create the worst possible version of your idea — on purpose
4. Notice that the world doesn't end
5. Now create freely

> **Case: The Blank Page** (Chapter 30): Rubin describes the terror of the blank page. The solution is not to wait for inspiration but to start moving. Write anything. Play any note. Make a mark. The act of starting creates momentum. The blank page is only terrifying until you put something on it.
> **Key takeaway**: The hardest part of any creative project is starting. Once you start, the process takes over. Lower the barrier to starting until it feels easy.

> **Case: The 10,000 Hours Myth Reframe** (Chapter 35): Rubin challenges the idea that creativity requires 10,000 hours of practice. Mastery matters, but the best creative work often comes from beginners or outsiders who haven't learned what's "impossible." The untrained eye sees possibilities the expert has learned to ignore.
>
> **Key takeaway**: Beginner's mind is not just a nice idea — it's a creative advantage. Sometimes knowing less allows you to see more.

## Collaboration Practice

- Listen more than you speak
- Create safety for others to be vulnerable
- Don't impose your vision — help them find theirs
- Trust that the combined vision will be better than either individual vision
- Give feedback on what IS working, not just what isn't

## The Editing Mindset

- First: create freely, no judgment
- Second: step back and see what you have
- Third: edit ruthlessly
- Fourth: release it
