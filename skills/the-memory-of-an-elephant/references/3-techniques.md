# 3. Techniques — Animal Narration

> Source: "The Memory of an Elephant" by Alex Lasker (2021). Techniques for writing from an animal's perspective.

## Sensory Primacy

The elephant narrator's world is dominated by smell, sound, and touch — not sight. Human narration is visual; elephant narration is olfactory and auditory.

> **Case: The Sensory World** (Chapter 1): "Sounds and smells overwhelmed me: the grasses and the soil under foot, the boom of thunder and the loud patter of the afternoon rain." This is not anthropomorphism — it is an attempt to represent a sensory world dominated by smell and hearing.
> **Key takeaway**: Writing from an animal's perspective requires changing which senses dominate the narrative.

## The Absence of Abstraction

The elephant narrator does not think in abstractions. He thinks in specific memories. He does not think "freedom" — he thinks "the open sky and the stars."

> **Case: The Human vs. Elephant Voice** (Throughout): The human chapters are full of abstract thought, plans, and regrets. The elephant chapters are immediate and physical. The contrast creates the novel's texture.
> **Key takeaway**: A non-human narrator requires a non-human cognitive style. No abstract nouns. No planning. Only memory and sensory input.

## The Circular Structure

The novel ends where it began: the elephant returning to the place of his birth. The final chapters mirror the opening, creating a sense of completion.

> **Case: The Return** (Final chapters): The elephant reaches the farm where he was born. He recognizes the smells, the sounds, the shape of the land. The narration echoes Chapter 1 — the same sensations, but now colored by a lifetime of experience.
> **Key takeaway**: A circular structure is emotionally satisfying because it honors the journey. The return to the beginning is not a repetition — it is a transformation.
