# 2. Principles — Elephant Consciousness

> Source: "The Memory of an Elephant" by Alex Lasker (2021). Principles drawn from the novel's portrayal of elephant intelligence.

## The 7 Principles

### Principle 1: Elephants Remember Everything
The novel's central claim: elephants have near-perfect recall. Every sight, sound, and smell from the moment of birth is preserved. This is not poetic license — it is based on scientific understanding of elephant cognition.

### Principle 2: Memory Is the Self
For the elephant narrator, his memories are his identity. He is what he remembers. Without memory, he would not know who he is.

> **Case: The First Memories** (Chapter 1): The elephant describes his first hours: "Sounds and smells overwhelmed me: the grasses and the soil under foot, the boom of thunder, the fat brown river." These memories, fifty years old, are as vivid as yesterday.
> **Key takeaway**: The most powerful memories are sensory, not conceptual. We remember through our bodies.

### Principle 3: Animals Grieve
The novel portrays elephant grief as real and devastating. Elephants mourn their dead, visit their bones, and remember loss.

### Principle 4: Conservation Requires Connection
The humans who fight to save elephants are those who have known individual elephants. Abstract concern is not enough.

### Principle 5: Home Is a Memory
The elephant's journey is not geographical — it is a journey toward the memory of belonging.

### Principle 6: The Bond Between Species Is Real
The elephant and the boy share a connection that transcends language. It is felt, not spoken.

### Principle 7: Time Circles Back
The elephant's return to his birthplace is the novel's central image. Life does not move in a straight line — it circles back.

> **Case: The Elephant's Grief** (Mid-novel): When the elephant recalls a member of his herd who was killed by poachers, his narration becomes fragmented. He cannot describe the event directly — the grief is too great. The reader understands his loss through the break in his voice.
> **Key takeaway**: The most profound emotions are shown, not told. A broken sentence can convey more than a detailed description.
