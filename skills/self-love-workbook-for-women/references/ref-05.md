# Reference 05: Self-Care Practices — Sustainable Self-Love Habits for Daily Life

## Overview

Self-care has become a buzzword reduced to bubble baths and face masks, but true self-care is far deeper. It is the intentional practice of meeting your own needs — physical, emotional, mental, and spiritual — so that you can show up as your full self in the world. Megan Logan's workbook emphasizes sustainable self-care: not a luxury you earn, but a non-negotiable foundation for everything else in your life. Self-care is not selfish; it is the prerequisite for being present, compassionate, and capable.

## The Four Pillars of Sustainable Self-Care

1. **Physical Self-Care**: Sleep, nutrition, movement, rest, and medical care
2. **Emotional Self-Care**: Honoring your feelings, processing emotions, seeking support
3. **Mental Self-Care**: Stimulating your mind, setting boundaries around information intake, protecting mental energy
4. **Spiritual Self-Care**: Practices that connect you to meaning, purpose, or something larger than yourself (nature, meditation, creativity, community)

## The Difference Between True Self-Care and "Toxic Self-Care"

| True Self-Care | Toxic Self-Care |
|----------------|-----------------|
| Restores your capacity | Escapes from responsibility |
| Requires honesty with yourself | Is performative (for social media) |
| Addresses root causes | Temporarily numbs discomfort |
| Sometimes uncomfortable (therapy, hard conversations) | Always comfortable (retail therapy, numbing) |
| Is sustainable long-term | Is often expensive or unsustainable |
| Helps you grow | Keeps you stuck in patterns |

## Practical Exercises

### Exercise 1: The Self-Care Audit
Track your actual daily habits for one week across the four pillars. For each day, note:
- Did I get 7-8 hours of sleep?
- Did I eat at least one nourishing meal?
- Did I move my body in a way that felt good?
- Did I check in with my emotions honestly?
- Did I do something that brought me joy or curiosity?
- Did I connect with someone who makes me feel seen?

Rate each day on a scale of 1-10. Notice patterns: When is your self-care lowest? What precedes those dips? What helps you bounce back?

**Example 1 — The Burned-Out Consultant:**
Amara was a management consultant working 60-hour weeks. She considered "self-care" ordering takeout and watching Netflix until she fell asleep. Her average self-care score was 3/10.
*The shift*: Rather than overhauling everything, Amara chose ONE non-negotiable: 7 hours of sleep. She set a phone curfew at 10 PM, even if work wasn't done.
*Result*: After two weeks, she was less reactive to work stress. She had more patience with herself and others. The work was still there, but she faced it with more clarity. Over time, she added a 15-minute morning walk (movement) and weekly dinner with a friend (connection).
*Key lesson*: Start with ONE non-negotiable. Don't try to fix everything at once.

**Example 2 — The Stay-at-Home Mom:**
Carmen had two young children and felt she had "no time" for self-care. She felt guilty any time she spent on herself. Her self-care score was 2/10.
*The shift*: Carmen redefined self-care for her season of life. Instead of needing a full yoga class, she started with 5 minutes of deep breathing in the car before picking up the kids from school. Instead of a long bath, she took a 3-minute shower with her favorite essential oil while her partner watched the kids.
*Result*: These micro-practices accumulated. She stopped feeling depleted by 10 AM. She eventually joined a weekly evening walk group — her partner took over bedtime once a week.
*Key lesson*: Self-care scales to your season of life. Micro-moments count. And asking for help with childcare IS self-care.

### Exercise 2: The Joy List
Create a list of 20 things that bring you genuine joy, separated into:
- **Five-minute joys** (brewing the perfect cup of tea, stretching, listening to one song)
- **Thirty-minute joys** (reading, calling a friend, taking a walk, taking a bath)
- **Two-hour joys** (a hobby, a movie, hiking, cooking a new recipe, visiting a museum)
- **Full-day joys** (a road trip, a spa day, spending a day creating)

Keep this list somewhere visible. When you feel depleted, scan the list and pick something accessible. The act of choosing — even something small — restores a sense of agency.

### Exercise 3: The "What Do I Need Right Now?" Check-In
Several times a day, pause and ask yourself: "What do I need right now?" The answer might be:
- A glass of water
- To stand up and stretch
- To take three deep breaths
- To say no to the next request
- To ask for help
- To take a 10-minute break
- To validate my own feelings

This simple check-in builds the muscle of attunement to your own needs — something many women have never practiced.

## Journaling Prompts

1. "If self-care were a relationship I was committed to, what would I do differently this week?"
2. "What message did I receive growing up about people who 'put themselves first'? How does that message still influence me?"
3. "When I feel guilty about taking care of myself, whose voice am I hearing? What would I tell that voice?"
4. "What is one form of self-care that sounds nice but feels like a chore to me? Can I release the pressure to do it and find something that genuinely nourishes me?"
5. "If I had unlimited energy and time, how would I care for myself? What small version of that can I do today?"

## The 80% Rule

Perfection is the enemy of sustainable self-care. Aim for 80% consistency, not 100%. If you miss a day or a week, forgiveness is the most important self-care practice of all. Guilt after a "missed" self-care day often leads to abandoning the practice entirely. The compassionate response to a missed day is: "That's okay. I'll start again right now."

## Key Takeaways

- Sustainable self-care is foundational, not indulgent
- Start with one non-negotiable habit — the single most impactful change you can make
- Micro-moments of self-care are valid and accumulate meaningfully
- Create a Joy List as a go-to resource when you feel depleted
- The daily check-in builds the muscle of self-attunement
- Perfection is the enemy — aim for 80% and practice self-forgiveness for the rest
- Asking for help is a form of self-care, not a failure of independence
