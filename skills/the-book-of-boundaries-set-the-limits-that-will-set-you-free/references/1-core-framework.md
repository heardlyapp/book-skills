# Core Framework: What Boundaries Are (and Aren't)

> Source: The Book of Boundaries, Introduction and Chapters 1-2

## The Core Definition

A boundary is a limit you set for yourself, not a demand on others. This distinction changes everything about how boundaries work.

> **Case: The Phone Call Boundary** (Chapter 1): "You can't call me after 9 PM" is a demand — controlling someone else. "I won't answer calls after 9 PM" is a boundary — controlling yourself. This reframing dramatically reduces resistance, resentment, and guilt.

> **Key takeaway**: You can only control yourself. A healthy boundary describes YOUR action, not what someone else must do.

## The Guilt Problem

Guilt is the #1 reason people avoid boundaries. Urban normalizes it as part of the process.

> **Case: Urban's Own People-Pleasing Recovery** (Introduction): Urban describes herself as a former people-pleaser. When she started setting boundaries, she felt selfish, guilty, and terrified of losing relationships. She learned guilt fades, freedom lasts, and real relationships survive boundaries.

> **Case: The Friend Who Tested the Boundary** (Chapter 2): A friend pushed back hard when Urban set a boundary. Urban felt terrible — until she realized the friend was testing whether Urban would enforce it. She did. Their friendship became stronger.

> **Key takeaway**: Guilt is part of learning. Set the boundary anyway. The guilt passes. The freedom and better relationships remain.
