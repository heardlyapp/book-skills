# Anti-Patterns · What Makes Other Countries Less Happy · Common Mistakes

> Source: *The Year of Living Danishly*, Chapters 1, 2, 6, 7, 10

---

## Anti-Pattern 1: The Busyness Trap

**The UK/US pattern:** Work long hours, earn more money, outsource everything (cooking, cleaning, gardening), buy more stuff, work more to pay for it. Happiness is always in the future: "I'll be happy when I get promoted / buy a house / retire."

**The Danish alternative:** Work fewer hours, do more yourself, spend less, own less. Accept that beyond a basic threshold, more money doesn't make you happier.

> *"Over a certain basic threshold, it's simple life maths: Fewer new shiny things = fewer hours overtime = happier life."*

**Why this is hard for non-Danes:** Your identity is probably wrapped up in your work. When Helen goes freelance, being known as "Lego Man's wife" terrifies her. "My work has always been my identity. So the idea of doing less of it is terrifying."

> **Case:** Helen's London life: 12-hour work days, second stabbing in her neighbourhood in 2 months, on/off tonsillitis that antibiotics couldn't shift, insomnia, fertility treatments fitted around work, Sunday night chest-tightening anxiety about the week ahead. In Denmark: 34-hour work weeks, home by 4 PM, time for walks on the beach. The productivity of Danish workers is still 3rd in the OECD.

## Anti-Pattern 2: The Trust Deficit

**The problem:** In many countries, we assume strangers are dangerous until proven otherwise. This creates an enormous cognitive burden. We lock everything, suspect everyone, and waste energy on constant vigilance.

**The cost of low trust:**
- Security systems, insurance, locks, alarms
- Legal contracts for everything
- Constant suspicion in social interactions
- Reduced willingness to help strangers
- More stress, less happiness

> **Case:** Helen's neighbours (the Mr Beards) go through her recycling bin. In the UK/US this would be a violation. In Denmark, it's neighbourly — they want to make sure she's sorting waste correctly. They show up at 8 AM (when she's not dressed) to teach her about the right bin. And they mean well.

> **Case:** Helen buys a used car. The dealer offers to translate the entire 9-page Danish contract on the spot. She learns that dealers don't get commission in Denmark, so there's no incentive to mislead. "I'm unlikely to get ripped off."

## Anti-Pattern 3: Performance Identity

**The mistake:** Measuring your worth by your salary, title, or possessions. The "résumé virtues" (what you do) overshadow the "eulogy virtues" (who you are).

**The Danish antidote:** Jante's Law makes showing off bad form. No one cares about your job title. A CEO and a cleaner play football together. Binmen are respected. The question isn't "what do you do?" but "how are you?"

> **Case:** At Lego, no one wears ties, no one uses titles. The motto is "Lego over ego." If you stay late and work too much, you're more likely to get an efficiency leaflet dropped on your desk than any sympathy. "Don't put up with presenteeism" is the unspoken 11th rule of Jante's Law.

## Anti-Pattern 4: The Choice Overload Trap

**The mistake:** Believing more choices = more freedom = more happiness. In reality, too many options create anxiety, regret, and paralysis.

**Danish simplicity in action:**
- Limited car models due to 180% sales tax — everyone drives practical cars
- Limited food choices in Jutland (pork, herring, potatoes, pastries)
- Limited clothing styles (skinny jeans, leather jacket, scarf, topknot for women; skinny jeans, hoodies for men)
- Pre-planned social calendar weeks in advance — no "what do you want to do tonight?" anxiety
- Standard furniture, standard home layouts, standard recycling rules

**Why it works:** "Cutting down on choice can take some of the hassle out of modern life. Freedom within boundaries."

> **Case:** After months of limited restaurant options in Jutland, Helen visits Copenhagen and is overwhelmed by choice: Japanese, Mexican, Lebanese, Michelin-starred restaurants. She realises that having many good options is overwhelming in its own way.

## Anti-Pattern 5: The Gender Equality Complacency Trap

**The mistake:** Assuming that because Denmark is progressive on gender equality, the work is done. This leads to a blind spot where sexism persists unchecked.

**The Danish contradiction:** Denmark has legalised abortion since 1973, equal pay since 1976, a female PM and queen, 40% women in parliament, 52 weeks of parental leave, and 78% of mothers return to work. Yet:
- 52% of Danish women report having been victims of physical or sexual violence (highest in EU)
- Workplace jokes about women drivers are still common
- Interviewers ask women about baby plans
- Plastic surgery ads feature 2-metre-toll naked breasts on buses
- Pole dancing competitions for teenage girls

> **Case:** Comedian Sanne Søndergaard tells Helen: "It's as though people here assume that we're equal so they can be as sexist as they want. Because Denmark was first with a lot of equality issues, like gay rights and abortion laws, I think we're also getting the backlash first."

> **Case:** A 9-year-old neighbour tells Sanne she wishes she was a boy so she wouldn't "have to get new breasts all the time." She's picked up this message from the plastic surgery ads on buses everywhere.

## Anti-Pattern 6: The Holiday Divorce Trap

**The Danish paradox:** Danes get 5 weeks paid holiday and the whole country shuts down in July. But spending too much continuous time together can destroy relationships.

**The stats:**
- 42.7% of Danish marriages end in divorce (4th highest in Europe)
- July is the most popular month for divorce filings
- Two-thirds of couples argue on summer holidays
- 32% of Danes have been unfaithful (tied for most in Europe)

**The lesson:** Even good things in moderation. Danes take their holidays (good) but the length can push couples apart (bad). Friendly Neighbour split from her husband after 3 weeks in Tuscany: "It was too long with nowhere to hide."

> **Case:** Helen and Lego Man go to Sicily for 2 weeks. Week 1 is bliss. Week 2 they're bickering about laundry bins, wet towels, cereal chocolate chips, timing of dinner reservations, and jumper sharing. "We are both right. We are both ridiculous."

## Anti-Pattern 7: The Outsourcing Fallacy

**The mistake:** Working more so you can pay someone else to live your life — cooking, cleaning, gardening, childcare, even waxing. You earn money to outsource everything, but lose the time to actually enjoy your life.

**The Danish way:** DIY approach. Do your own cooking, clean your own home, raise your own kids (with state support). Danes have fewer outsourced services because they have more time. The tax system discourages extreme wealth accumulation — so there's less incentive to chase the next rung on the corporate ladder.

> **Case:** Helen's London life required takeout meals, cleaners, and a social calendar that exhausted her. In Jutland, she cooks, cycles (badly), joins clubs, bakes, and has time to read books. She's not richer — but she's happier.

## Anti-Pattern 8: The Material Happiness Mirage

**The belief:** "When I have X (car, house, promotion, relationship), I'll be happy."

**The Danish reality:** "Danes don't believe that buying more stuff brings you happiness." The happiness economist Christian Bjørnskov confirms this. Beyond a basic threshold of security, material accumulation doesn't move the happiness needle.

> **Case:** Helen asks Christian Bjørnskov if Danes are happy just because they have low expectations. "Categorically not. Danes have high expectations AND they're realistic." They don't expect less — they expect the right things.
