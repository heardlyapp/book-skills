# Anti-Patterns: Creativity Killers

> Source: Out of Our Minds, Chapters 9-10

## Fear of Being Wrong

The single biggest barrier. Schools punish mistakes; adults learn to avoid them. But without mistakes, there's no originality.

> **Case: The Stigmatized Error** (Chapter 9): In most classrooms, being wrong is humiliating. Students give only the "safe" answer. This conditioning lasts a lifetime — meetings full of adults who won't suggest ideas that might fail.

> **Case: The Creative Risk** (Chapter 9): Robinson contrasts timid organizations with genuinely creative ones. Pixar, Google, and other innovative companies embrace failure as part of the process. The difference is cultural, not individual.

> **Key takeaway**: "If you're not prepared to be wrong, you'll never come up with anything original." Fear of error is the enemy of creativity.

## The Starving Artist Myth

The idea that creativity requires suffering or poverty.

> **Case: The "Real Job" Pressure** (Chapter 10): Creative people are told to pursue art as a hobby and get a "real job." This kills creative ambition. Robinson argues creativity is not a luxury — it's essential to human flourishing. The myth of the suffering artist causes real damage.

## Separating Arts and Sciences

The false dichotomy that artists do arts and scientists do science. Both require creativity.

> **Case: The Renaissance Model** (Chapter 10): Leonardo da Vinci, Einstein, Steve Jobs — all combined arts and sciences. The separation is modern and artificial.

> **Key takeaway**: The separation of arts and sciences is artificial and destructive. The most innovative people and organizations combine both.
