# Core Framework: The Housing Shortage

> Source: Golden Gates, Preface & Chapters 1-2

## The Fundamental Problem

The housing crisis is not about greed, speculation, or tech workers. It's about a dire shortage of available housing in places where people and companies want to live. For decades, America solved its housing needs by building progressively cheaper neighborhoods progressively farther out. That model has failed, and cities are now paying the price.

> **Case: The Postwar Dream** (Preface): In 1945, Life magazine profiled three LA families making $50K, $10K, and $3K annually. The fireman's family — the lowest income — had a six-room bungalow with modern appliances, a backyard patio, and a lemon tree. Today, a fireman in LA can't afford a home anywhere near the station. The difference isn't income — it's housing supply. The postwar model of cheap suburban expansion is over.

> **Case: The BARF Beginning** (Chapter 1): Sonja Trauss showed up at a San Francisco Planning Commission meeting as "a member of the public" to speak for 83 subsidized apartments in the Tenderloin. That seven-hour meeting gave birth to BARF — the Bay Area Renters Federation. A movement that started with one person at a microphone, arguing that housing abundance is a progressive cause.

## The Zoning Barrier

Single-family zoning covers 75% of residential land in most U.S. cities. This is the primary barrier to building more housing. Local planning commissions controlled by existing homeowners have veto power over new development.

> **Key takeaway**: The housing crisis is a political problem, not a technical one. We know how to build. The question is whether we're allowed to.
