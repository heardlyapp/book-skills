# Principles: YIMBY vs NIMBY

> Source: Golden Gates, Chapters 3-6

## The YIMBY Movement

Sonja Trauss founded BARF (Bay Area Renters Federation) as a counterweight to the well-organized homeowners who dominate local planning. The idea: renters need a voice in housing decisions because landlords and developers won't represent their interests. The movement grew from a single person at a microphone to a national force.

> **Case: The Suburban Strategy** (Chapter 5): BARF shifted from San Francisco to the suburbs — places like Lafayette and Piedmont that had used zoning to exclude multi-family housing. The strategy: sue suburbs that weren't complying with state housing law. This was a new front in the housing war — using the courts to force suburbs to accept density.

> **Case: The State Legislation Battle** (Chapter 6): The "second housing package" — a set of state bills to preempt local zoning. The most ambitious was SB 827, proposed by Senator Scott Wiener, which would have allowed fourplexes near transit stops. It failed after fierce opposition from both conservative suburbs and progressive cities worried about gentrification. The defeat showed how hard statewide housing reform is.

## NIMBY Logic

NIMBYs aren't simply selfish. They fear change, traffic, parking problems, and neighborhood character. Homeowners also have a financial interest in limiting supply — their house is their largest asset. The book shows NIMBYs as complex people, not villains.

> **Key takeaway**: The housing debate isn't good vs evil. It's competing legitimate interests: renters need affordable homes; homeowners want to protect property values and neighborhood character. Progress requires acknowledging both.
