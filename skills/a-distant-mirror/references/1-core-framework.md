# The Black Death — The Cataclysm That Changed Europe

> Source: A Distant Mirror, Chapters 3, 5, 7

## Core Framework

The Black Death (1347-1351) was the most devastating event in European history. Nothing before or since has killed such a large proportion of the population in such a short time.

### The Plague

| Fact | Detail |
|------|--------|
| Cause | Yersinia pestis bacterium, carried by fleas on rats |
| Arrival in Europe | 1347, via Genoese trading ships from the Crimea |
| Mortality | 25-50% of Europe's population died within 3 years |
| Death toll | Estimated 75-200 million |
| Recurrence | Every few years for centuries |
| Medical knowledge | None. Doctors had no idea what caused it. |

### Social Consequences

- Labor shortages → peasants demanded higher wages → the feudal system began to crack
- Decline of church authority → prayers did not stop the plague
- Scapegoating → Jews were massacred in many cities
- Psychological trauma → the Flagellants, the Dance of Death art motif
- Economic transformation → land values fell, wages rose

> **Case: The Siege of Caffa** (Chapter 3)
> In 1346, Mongol forces besieged the Genoese trading city of Caffa on the Crimean Peninsula. The Mongols were struck by the plague. Before withdrawing, they used catapults to hurl their dead over the city walls — one of the earliest examples of biological warfare. The Genoese fled on ships, carrying the plague to Europe. The first outbreak was in Sicily in October 1347.
> **Key takeaway**: The Black Death arrived in Europe through trade. The plague followed the same trade routes that brought silk and spices. Globalization has always been a vector for disease.

> **Case: The Flagellants** (Chapter 5)
> Across Europe, groups of people called Flagellants marched through towns, whipping themselves as a public penance. They believed the plague was God's punishment for sin. They attracted huge crowds, denounced the clergy, and called for radical reform. The church eventually suppressed them. The Flagellants were a symptom of mass psychological trauma: people who could not understand the plague and needed someone to blame — and a way to atone.
> **Key takeaway**: When a society faces a catastrophe it cannot explain, it invents explanations and rituals. The Flagellants were not irrational — they were responding to an unprecedented crisis with the cultural tools available to them.
