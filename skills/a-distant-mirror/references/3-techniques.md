# Enguerrand de Coucy — A Mirror of His Age

> Source: A Distant Mirror, Chapters 1, 8, 10, 11, 12, 15

## The Sire de Coucy

Barbara Tuchman uses the life of Enguerrand VII, Sire de Coucy (1340-1397), as the narrative thread for her study of the 14th century. His life touched every major event of the era.

### His Life in Context

| Event | Year | Age | What Happened |
|-------|------|-----|---------------|
| Born | 1340 | 0 | Heir to the most powerful lordship in France |
| Black Death | 1348 | 8 | Survived the plague — his father died |
| Captured at battle | 1360 | 20 | Taken prisoner by the English, ransomed |
| Marriage | 1365 | 25 | Married Isabella, daughter of the English king |
| Daughter married | 1385 | 45 | His daughter married into the English royal family |
| Failed crusade | 1396 | 56 | Led a crusade against the Ottoman Turks |
| Died | 1397 | 57 | Died in a Turkish prison after the Battle of Nicopolis |

> **Case: The Marriage That Bridged a War** (Chapter 8)
> In 1365, Enguerrand de Coucy married Isabella of England, daughter of King Edward III. This was not a love match — it was a political arrangement to secure a truce in the Hundred Years War. De Coucy received lands in England and a royal bride. Isabella received a powerful French noble. The marriage showed that even in the midst of war, the nobility could make alliances across enemy lines. But it also created divided loyalties: de Coucy was both a French noble and the son-in-law of the English king.
> **Key takeaway**: Marriages of the nobility were political transactions. De Coucy's marriage to an English princess illustrates the divide between the ruling class (who could make peace) and the peasants (who only suffered the war).

> **Case: The Crusade That Ended in Disaster** (Chapter 15)
> In 1396, de Coucy joined a crusade against the Ottoman Turks. The crusade was a disaster. The French knights were arrogant and undisciplined. At the Battle of Nicopolis (1396), the Turks destroyed the crusader army. De Coucy was captured. He died in a Turkish prison the following year. The crusaders had refused to listen to experienced advisors who warned them about Turkish tactics. Their arrogance cost them their lives.
> **Key takeaway**: De Coucy's death in a Turkish prison is the final illustration of the chivalric tragedy: noble ideals (crusading for Christ) led to foolish decisions (charging into a trap) and a pointless death. The ideals were noble; the execution was catastrophic.
