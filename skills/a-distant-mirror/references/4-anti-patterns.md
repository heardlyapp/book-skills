# Anti-Patterns — The Church Crisis and Medieval Scapegoating

> Source: A Distant Mirror, Chapters 5, 10, 11

## The Four Catastrophic Errors

### 1. The Avignon Papacy
**Problem**: The Pope lived in Avignon (France) instead of Rome for 70 years. The Papacy appeared to be a puppet of the French king.
**Impact**: Undermined the church's spiritual authority. The Pope was supposed to be above politics — instead, he was a political pawn.

### 2. The Great Schism
**Problem**: In 1378, two popes were elected — one in Rome, one in Avignon. Each excommunicated the other. Christendom was divided.
**Impact**: The Schism destroyed the church's claim to unity. If there were two popes, which one was legitimate? The question had no good answer.

### 3. The Persecution of the Jews
**Problem**: As the plague spread, Jews were accused of poisoning wells, causing the plague.
**Impact**: Massacres of Jewish communities occurred across Europe. Over 200 Jewish communities were destroyed. The survivors fled to Eastern Europe.

### 4. The Failure of the Nobility
**Problem**: The nobility continued their tournaments, feasts, and rivalries while the peasantry starved and the countryside burned.
**Impact**: The feudal system lost legitimacy. The nobility was supposed to protect the people. When they failed, the social order began to crack.

> **Case: The Flagellants and the Jews** (Chapter 5)
> The Flagellants saw the plague as God's punishment and demanded repentance. They also blamed the Jews. In cities across Germany and France, the Flagellant movement incited attacks on Jewish communities. Sometimes local authorities tried to protect the Jews; sometimes they participated. When the Flagellants reached a city, the Jewish community had to be saved by the authorities or be destroyed. The pattern of blaming a minority for a catastrophe would be repeated many times in history.
> **Key takeaway**: Every crisis creates scapegoats. The 14th century blamed Jews for the plague. The 20th century blamed Jews for Germany's problems. The tendency to blame a vulnerable minority is not limited to any one time or place — it is a recurring human failure.

> **Case: The Dance of Death** (Chapter 10)
> The "Danse Macabre" (Dance of Death) became a popular artistic motif in the 14th century. It showed Death leading people from all walks of life — pope, emperor, knight, peasant — in a dance toward the grave. The message: everyone dies. The Dance of Death expressed the psychological trauma of the plague. If death could strike anyone, anywhere, at any time, then the traditional social hierarchy was meaningless. Death was the great equalizer.
> **Key takeaway**: The Dance of Death was not just an artistic motif — it was a cultural response to trauma. When society cannot make sense of mass death, it creates art that makes death visible and universal. The Dance of Death was the 14th-century equivalent of a memorial service.
