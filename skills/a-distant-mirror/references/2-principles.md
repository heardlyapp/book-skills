# The Hundred Years War — The Foolishness of Princes

> Source: A Distant Mirror, Chapters 2, 4, 6, 9

## The War

The Hundred Years War (1337-1453) was not really 100 years of continuous fighting — it was a series of conflicts punctuated by truces, treaties, and periods of peace. But the overall effect was the same: continuous devastation of the French countryside.

### Key Battles

| Battle | Year | Result | Significance |
|--------|------|--------|-------------|
| Crecy | 1346 | English victory | Longbow defeats French knights. End of the age of cavalry |
| Poitiers | 1356 | English victory | French king captured. Ransom bankrupted France |
| Agincourt | 1415 | English victory | Same tactics, same result. Longbow again proves decisive |
| Orleans | 1429 | French victory | Joan of Arc turns the tide. Beginning of French recovery |

### The Free Companies

After battles, mercenaries (free companies) roamed the countryside, living off the land, looting and burning villages. Tuchman shows that the war was not fought by noble knights in shining armor — it was fought by armed bands who terrorized the peasantry. The true victims of the war were not the soldiers but the civilians.

> **Case: The Chevauchée** (Chapters 4, 6)
> The English strategy in France was not to fight battles but to conduct chevauchées — massive raids through French territory, burning villages, destroying crops, and killing civilians. The goal was to demonstrate that the French king could not protect his people, and to weaken the French economy. Tuchman describes these raids in appalling detail: villages burned, women raped, crops destroyed, people driven from their homes.
> **Key takeaway**: War in the 14th century was not about glorious battles. It was about terrorizing civilians. The same is true of most wars in most centuries.

> **Case: The Jacquerie — Peasant Revolt** (Chapter 6)
> In 1358, French peasants (called "Jacques" by the nobles) rose up in a desperate revolt. They attacked castles, killed nobles, and burned records of their debts. The nobles responded with savage repression: thousands of peasants were killed, their villages burned, their families murdered. The revolt was crushed — but it showed how desperate the peasantry had become under the combined weight of war, plague, and oppressive taxation.
> **Key takeaway**: The Jacquerie was not a rebellion against authority — it was a cry of desperation from people who had nothing left to lose. When the nobility taxed and warred without mercy, the peasantry eventually broke. And when they broke, they were crushed.
