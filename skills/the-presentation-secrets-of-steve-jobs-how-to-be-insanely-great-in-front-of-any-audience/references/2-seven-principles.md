# Seven Principles in Depth

## Overview

Gallo's research across decades of Jobs keynotes reveals seven principles that separate extraordinary presentations from mediocre ones. Each principle is grounded in cognitive science and demonstrated repeatedly in Jobs's presentations. This file expands each principle with specific examples from the book.

## Principle 1: Plan in Analog

Jobs never opened Keynote or PowerPoint first. He sketched, storyboarded, and scripted on paper. This approach forces clarity before design. When Gallo worked with a start-up entrepreneur preparing for a Wal-Mart pitch, they spent the entire first day sketching the story on a whiteboard — no computer. After writing the narrative, they created only five slides for a 15-minute presentation.

**Why it works:** Writing a script first unlocks the visual storytelling potential of presentation tools. Cliff Atkinson's research shows that most people fail at presentations because they start designing slides before they have a story. The correct sequence is: Write → Sketch → Produce.

**Case — Cranium:** Founder Richard Tait sketched the idea for a board game where everyone could excel in at least one category on a cocktail napkin during a cross-country flight. Cranium became a worldwide sensation, later purchased by Hasbro. A vision simple enough to fit on a napkin.

**Case — Southwest Airlines:** Attorney Herb Kelleher met client Rollin King at St. Anthony's Club in San Antonio. King sketched three circles (Dallas, Houston, San Antonio) connected by lines on a napkin. That single image — one of the most famous "napkin sketches" in corporate history — launched Southwest Airlines, which reinvented air travel in America.

## Principle 2: Answer "Why Should I Care?"

Jobs's presentations always answered this question first. He never led with features, specs, or technology. He led with the benefit to the user.

**Case — iPhone launch:** Jobs didn't start with processor speed, screen resolution, or storage capacity. He said: "Today Apple reinvents the phone." Then he showed how the iPhone made calls better, played music, browsed the Internet, and was "ten times easier to use." The benefit came first; the specs followed.

**Case — Intel Core 2 Duo:** Two sales scenarios from the book illustrate the difference. Scenario one: "An Intel dual-core processor has two performance engines that simultaneously process data at a faster rate." Scenario two: "Think of the microprocessor as the brain of your computer. With Core 2 Duo, you get two brains in one. That means you can download music while running a virus scan and it won't slow down." Same product. One confuses. The other sells. The difference is answering "Why should I care?"

## Principle 3: Create a Twitter-Like Headline

Every Jobs presentation had a single, memorable headline under 140 characters. This headline became the core of all marketing communications.

**Case — iPod:** "1,000 songs in your pocket." Seven words. It didn't describe the storage capacity, the battery life, or the design. It described the experience. The headline was so effective that it became Apple's marketing message for years.

**Case — iPhone:** "Today Apple reinvents the phone." Seven words. Subject-verb-object. The headline was so powerful that every news article about the iPhone launch led with it or paraphrased it. Jobs repeated it three times during the presentation.

## Principle 4: Draw a Verbal Road Map

The rule of three: break your presentation into three messages. The human brain can hold only three or four items in short-term memory. Three is optimal.

**Case — iPhone reveal structure:** "Today we're introducing three revolutionary products. The first: a widescreen iPod with touch controls. The second: a revolutionary mobile phone. The third: a breakthrough internet communications device." Only after listing all three did Jobs reveal: "These are not three separate devices. This is one device. And we are calling it iPhone."

**Case — Macworld 2008:** Jobs structured the MacBook Air launch into three acts, matching the narrative arc. Act 1: here are the existing notebooks (MacBook family). Act 2: here's the problem — notebooks have to compromise between portability and performance. Act 3: here's the solution — MacBook Air, the world's thinnest notebook.

## Principle 5: Introduce the Antagonist

Jobs created drama by identifying a clear villain — the status quo, a competitor, or a systemic problem. The villain creates tension that the hero (Apple's product) resolves.

**Case — Intel transition (2005):** Jobs spent the first half of his presentation detailing the failure of PowerPC's roadmap — Motorola and Freescale couldn't deliver the chips Apple needed. He showed charts, gave examples, built the case that the current path was unsustainable. Only then did he reveal the hero: Intel processors. The audience understood and trusted the decision because they experienced the problem first.

**Case — iPhone "problem" setup:** Jobs opened by showing four smartphones side-by-side and criticizing each one: they had half-keyboards, styluses, clunky interfaces. "Nobody wants a stylus," he famously said. "Who wants a stylus? You have to get 'em and put 'em away. Yuck." The existing phones were the antagonist. The iPhone was the hero.

## Principle 6: Deliver a Multisensory Experience

Jobs appealed to all senses, not just auditory. His presentations combined visual slides, physical props, live demos, video, and guest speakers.

**Case — MacBook Air envelope:** Jobs didn't just say "the MacBook Air is thin." He walked to a table, opened a standard manila envelope, and pulled the laptop out of it. The audience erupted. No slide could have conveyed the thinness as powerfully as that single prop.

**Case — iPhone live demo:** Jobs spent 30 minutes of the 90-minute keynote doing a live demo of the iPhone — scrolling through albums, making calls, sending emails, browsing the web on Google Maps, pinching to zoom. He didn't describe features. He performed them. The audience saw the product in action, creating emotional conviction that no features list could achieve.

## Principle 7: Rehearse Until Effortless

Jobs rehearsed every keynote for weeks. The result looked spontaneous because the preparation was exhaustive.

**Case — iPhone 2007 vocal analysis:** Gallo provides a line-by-line breakdown of Jobs's delivery during the iPhone launch, noting where he paused, where he raised his voice, where he slowed down. Each pause was calculated. Each inflection was rehearsed. The four pillars of his stage presence — eye contact, open posture, hand gestures, vocal variety — were practiced until they became second nature.

**Case — Cisco CEO John Chambers:** Gallo describes Chambers roaming the audience like a preacher, making eye contact, calling people by name, placing a hand on someone's shoulder. This looks effortless — but Gallo confirms it comes from "hours of relentless practice." Chambers knows every word on each slide.
