# Voice and Application

## Overview

The ultimate test of Gallo's framework: can these techniques be applied by people who aren't Steve Jobs? The answer is yes — but only if you adapt the principles to your own personality, context, and audience. This file covers how to apply Jobs's techniques across different scenarios without falling into the trap of imitation.

## Finding Your Own Voice

Jobs's black mock turtleneck, jeans, and New Balance sneakers became iconic — but only because they were authentic to him. Gallo makes clear: do not copy Jobs's outfit, mannerisms, or style. Copy his structure, his discipline, and his audience-first mentality. Find your own authentic delivery.

**Case — Schiller Learns from the Best:** Apple marketing chief Phil Schiller adopted Jobs's presentation techniques for his own keynotes. He used the same three-act structure, the same minimalist slides, the same dramatic reveals. But Schiller didn't copy Jobs's onstage persona — he developed his own style, more technical and detailed, playing to his strengths as a product expert. The intermission in Gallo's book is dedicated to Schiller's adaptation, proving the framework transfers even within the same company.

**Case — Apple Store employees:** Gallo reports that Apple Store employees use *The Presentation Secrets of Steve Jobs* to learn how to pitch products to customers on the showroom floor. They don't dress like Jobs or speak like Jobs — they use his principle of answering "Why should I care?" by demonstrating how products improve customers' lives. The framework scales from a keynote stage to a one-on-one sales conversation.

## Application: Product Launch Keynotes

The most obvious application. If you're launching a product, use the three-act structure:
1. Establish the problem (antagonist)
2. Reveal your solution (hero)
3. Demonstrate it working (experience)
4. Provide evidence (customer data, press)

**Techniques to prioritize:** Twitter-like headline, holy shit moment, live demo, props, share the stage with partners.

**Case — iPhone launch (2007):** This is the textbook example. Problem: smartphones are complicated. Hero: iPhone. Demo: 30 minutes of live product demonstration. Evidence: market data and analyst reactions. Each element was executed perfectly, creating the most successful product launch in history.

**Case — iPad launch (2010):** Jobs's iPad launch used a different approach. Instead of a single holy shit moment, he built the reveal gradually — first establishing the category problem (something between a phone and a laptop), then slowly building to the reveal: a device that was "far more personal than a laptop." The gradual buildup created a different kind of drama from the iPhone launch, proving the framework is flexible.

## Application: Sales Presentations

For sales professionals, the key principles to prioritize are: Answer "Why should I care?" (Principle 2), Introduce the Antagonist (Principle 5), and Create a Headline (Principle 3).

**Technique:** The "Why Should I Care" frame. Before every sales call, write down: "My customer should buy this because ________." If you can't complete this sentence in plain English, you're not ready.

**Case — Wal-Mart pitch:** Gallo's client spent 60 days getting a meeting with Wal-Mart. The pitch used only five slides. The story came first. Each slide was a single visual. The presentation answered one question: "Why should Wal-Mart care about your technology?" The result: a beta test that led to a major account.

**Case — Intel Core 2 Duo retail scenario:** The difference between "An Intel dual-core processor has two performance engines" and "You get two brains in one computer, so you can download music while running a virus scan and it won't slow down" is the difference between losing and winning a customer. Both describe the same product. One answers "Why should I care?" The other doesn't.

## Application: Investor Pitches

For entrepreneurs pitching investors, the priority is: Establish the problem as urgent (Antagonist), present your solution as inevitable (Hero), demonstrate traction with numbers (Dress Up Data), and end with a clear ask.

**Technique — The drama formula:** "Here's the problem. Here's why it matters. Here's how we solve it." This is the most persuasive structure in human communication. Combine it with the rule of three: three slides, three key metrics, three reasons why you're the team to execute.

**Case — Goldman Sachs IPO prep:** Gallo prepared the CEO of a Silicon Valley semiconductor start-up for an investor presentation. The CEO pulled out a chip the size of a fingernail and said "You wouldn't believe the sound this generates." They turned the demo into the centerpiece of the pitch. The IPO became a huge success. An investor later called Gallo and said, "I don't know what you did, but the CEO was a hit." The technique: show, don't tell.

## Application: Internal Presentations

For internal presentations (all-hands, team updates, board reports), the most important principles are: Draw a Road Map (Principle 4) and Dress Up Numbers (Principle 6).

**Technique — The rule of three with numbers:** "Here are three things you need to know this quarter. One: revenue is up 20%. Two: we've hired 15 new engineers. Three: Product X launches next month." Give the roadmap, elaborate on each, summarize at the end. Apply the SRC framework to every number (Specific, Relevant, Contextual).

**Case — Jobs's internal product meetings:** While Gallo's book focuses on public keynotes, the principles originated from Jobs's internal product meetings. The same approach — start with the problem, propose a solution, demonstrate, rehearse — was used when pitching ideas to the executive team. The structure is universal.

## Application: Conference Keynotes

For conference keynotes, the full three-act structure applies. Priority: Create a Holy Shit Moment (Principle 6), Share the Stage (Principle 7), and use the 10-Minute Rule to keep the audience engaged.

**Technique — Modality switching every 10 minutes:** Plan your keynote in 10-minute blocks. Block 1: slides and talking (problem setup). Block 2: customer video (third-party validation). Block 3: live demo (seeing is believing). Block 4: guest speaker (new voice). Block 5: big reveal (holy shit moment). Block 6: Q&A. This structure ensures the audience never has a chance to check out.

**Case — Macworld Expo keynotes:** Jobs's Macworld keynotes were always structured around modality switching. He'd start with slides, move to a demo, show a video, bring someone on stage, do another demo, and end with a "one more thing" surprise. The audience stayed engaged for 90+ minutes because the format kept changing.

## The Big Picture: Why These Techniques Transfer

Gallo's book proves that Jobs's techniques are not personality-dependent. They are structure-dependent. The same principles that made Jobs the most captivating communicator on the world stage — plan in analog, answer "why should I care," create a headline, use the rule of three, introduce an antagonist, deliver multisensory experiences, rehearse relentlessly — have been adopted by:
- Phil Schiller at Apple
- Cisco CEO John Chambers
- Entrepreneurs pitching to Wal-Mart and Goldman Sachs
- The CEO of SanDisk launching products at CES
- Start-up founders preparing IPOs
- Apple Store employees on the sales floor
- MBA students at Stanford, Berkeley, and UCLA

The framework works at every scale because it's rooted in how the human brain processes information, not in who delivers it. The techniques are learnable. The discipline is replicable. The only thing that can't be copied is Jobs himself — and you don't need to. You need to be the best version of your own communicating self.
