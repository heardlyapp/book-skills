# Anti-Patterns: What Not to Do

## Overview

Gallo's book is as much a catalog of presentation sins as it is a guide to excellence. The most common mistakes — all of which Jobs avoided — are deeply ingrained in corporate culture. This file identifies the key anti-patterns and shows, through book cases, why they fail.

## Anti-Pattern 1: Bullet Points Kill Presentations

The first thing you see in PowerPoint's Format menu is "Bullets & Numbering." This is a trap. Bullet points are the least effective way to deliver information intended to be recalled and acted upon.

**Why it fails:** The brain processes images 60,000 times faster than text. Bullet points force the audience to read while the speaker talks, creating cognitive overload. The audience can't fully do either. Research shows that text-heavy slides reduce retention by up to 40% compared to visual slides.

**Case — Jobs's slides:** In a typical Jobs keynote, slides contained one image and sometimes one word. When introducing the iPhone, the slide simply showed a giant iPhone with the date "January 9, 2007." No feature list. No bullet points. No spec table. The image alone conveyed the message. The audience's attention was on Jobs, not on reading.

**Case — The Wal-Mart pitch:** Gallo's client needed only five slides for a 15-minute presentation to Wal-Mart executives. Each slide was a single image or simple concept. No bullets. The result: a winning pitch that earned the client a beta test. "Creating the slides did not take as much time as developing the story," Gallo writes.

**Fix:** One image per slide. One word per slide if you must use text. Never put more than three words on any slide. Let the audience focus on you and the image, not on reading.

## Anti-Pattern 2: Selling Features Instead of Benefits

The most common presentation mistake: listing what your product does without explaining why anyone should care. Presenters assume their audience shares their excitement about specs.

**Why it fails:** People don't buy products — they buy better versions of themselves. Features describe the product. Benefits describe the customer's improved life. The brain is a "lazy piece of meat that tries to preserve energy" — making the audience work to connect specs to their lives guarantees disengagement.

**Case — Two sales scenarios:** Gallo presents the same customer asking about a notebook computer. Salesperson A: "An Intel dual-core processor has two performance engines that simultaneously process data at a faster rate." Results: customer confused, leaves. Salesperson B: "Think of the microprocessor as the brain of your computer. With these chips, you get two brains in one. That means you can download music while running a virus scan and it won't slow down." Results: customer engaged, ready to buy.

**Case — Press release epidemic:** Gallo deconstructs real press releases as exhibits of anti-pattern behavior. One example: an electric utility announced a new CEO with 32 years of experience and 20 years living in Hawaii. The release never said what the CEO planned to do for customers. Another: a pizza chain announced it won "Pizza Chain of the Year" but offered no benefit to customers. Neither answer "Why should I care?"

**Fix:** Before every piece of communication, write one sentence answering "Why should the audience care?" If that sentence mentions a feature, rewrite it. Every feature can be translated into a benefit. Do that translation before presenting.

## Anti-Pattern 3: Jargon and Buzzword Pollution

Jargon creates a roadblock to the free exchange of ideas. When speakers use industry-specific language, they assume their audience understands — but they rarely do. Words like "synergy," "best of breed," "paradigm shift," and "solution" are meaningless filler.

**Why it fails:** Jargon makes the speaker seem like they're hiding behind language. It signals insecurity rather than expertise. Research shows audiences trust speakers who use plain language more than those who use jargon. The fog index metric — years of education needed to understand speech — should be as low as possible.

**Case — Jobs vs. Gates language analysis:** Jobs's fog index was significantly lower than Gates's in both 2007 and 2008 keynotes. Jobs's words were simpler, his sentences shorter, his phrases less abstract. Jobs's favorite words were everyday language — "amazing," "gorgeous," "cool," "unbelievable." These words resonate because they're real.

**Case — Mission statement epidemic:** Gallo attacks corporate mission statements as the worst culprit of jargon creep. "Mission statements typically are long, convoluted, jargon-laden paragraphs created in multiple committee meetings and destined to be forgotten." Apple's mission statement is the opposite: simple, clear, impactful, and full of emotive words.

**Fix:** Record yourself explaining your product. If you hear one buzzword, rewrite it in plain English. If you wouldn't say it to a friend at a bar, don't say it in a presentation.

## Anti-Pattern 4: Reading from Slides or Notes

Slides are visual aids, not speaker scripts. When presenters read from slides, they destroy eye contact, lose connection with the audience, and signal that they don't know their material.

**Why it fails:** Eye contact is associated with honesty, trustworthiness, sincerity, and confidence. Breaking eye contact loses the connection. The audience doesn't need you to read to them — they can read themselves. If the slide contains your entire speech, the audience questions why they need you at all.

**Case — Jobs's textless slides:** Because Jobs's slides were almost entirely visual (one image, few words), he was forced to speak to the audience rather than to the screen. He glanced at a slide and immediately turned back to the audience. During demonstrations, his eye contact was nearly constant. The result: the audience felt personally addressed, not lectured to.

**Fix:** Design slides that are useless without you — images that need your explanation, demonstrations that need your context. If your slides can be understood without you, you've made yourself redundant.

## Anti-Pattern 5: Under-rehearsing

Most presenters spend more time on their slides than on their delivery. They prepare content but not performance. The result: stiff, awkward, and unconvincing delivery.

**Why it fails:** A great story falls flat without great delivery. The same slides can be inspiring or boring depending on the speaker's voice, gestures, and presence. Audiences judge the speaker more than the slides. Rehearsal transforms anxiety into confidence and scripted words into natural conversation.

**Case — Jobs's rehearsal discipline:** Jobs rehearsed for weeks before every keynote. He practiced every gesture, every pause, every slide transition. Gallo reports that even after 30 years of experience, Jobs never winged it. The result: his delivery appeared spontaneous because the preparation was exhaustive.

**Case — Cisco's John Chambers:** Chambers roams the audience during presentations, making eye contact, calling people by name, placing a hand on a shoulder. "I know as a fact that Chambers's confidence is the result of hours of relentless practice," Gallo writes. "He knows every word on each of his slides, and he knows exactly what he's going to say next."

**Fix:** Rehearse out loud, standing up, with your slides, at least five times before a high-stakes presentation. Record yourself. Watch it. Correct your posture, gestures, and pacing. Do it again.
