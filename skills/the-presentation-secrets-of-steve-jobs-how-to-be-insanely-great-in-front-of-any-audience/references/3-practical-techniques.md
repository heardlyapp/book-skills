# Practical Techniques

## Overview

Beyond the seven principles, Gallo's book provides a toolkit of specific techniques for every aspect of presentation creation and delivery. This file catalogs the most actionable techniques with book cases.

## The Nine Elements of Great Presentations

Gallo identifies nine components that every persuasive presentation script should contain:

### 1. Headline
One sentence, ≤140 characters, subject-verb-object format. Write it before anything else.
- **Case — iPod:** "1,000 songs in your pocket." Jobs used this in the 2001 launch and it became Apple's tagline.
- **Case — iPhone:** "Today Apple reinvents the phone." Seven words that defined the launch.

### 2. Passion Statement
Fill in: "I'm excited about this product/company/idea because it _____________."
- **Case — Chris Gardner:** Gallo interviewed the real-life subject of *The Pursuit of Happyness*. When asked how he found strength while homeless with his two-year-old son, Gardner said: "Find something you love to do so much, you can't wait for the sun to rise to do it all over again." That's a passion statement.

### 3. Three Key Messages
Identify exactly three messages before creating slides. The rule of three is backed by cognitive science — short-term memory holds only 3-4 items.
- **Case — iPhone launch:** Three products (iPod + phone + internet device) = one iPhone. The structure was the message.

### 4. Metaphors and Analogies
Aristotle called metaphor "the most important thing by far." Jobs used metaphors constantly.
- **Case — "Bicycle for the mind":** Jobs said, "What a computer is to me is the most remarkable tool we have ever come up with. It's the equivalent of a bicycle for our minds." This metaphor made a complex tool understandable and emotionally resonant.
- **Case — iTunes for Windows:** When asked about iTunes being the most popular app on Windows, Jobs said: "It's like giving a glass of ice water to someone in hell." The analogy was memorable, quotable, and perfectly captured his view of Windows.

### 5. Demonstrations
Don't describe — demonstrate. Jobs spent significant portions of keynotes doing live demos.
- **Case — OS X Leopard (2007):** Jobs said Leopard had 300 new features. He chose 10 to demonstrate live, including Time Machine (automated backup) and Boot Camp (run Windows on Mac). He didn't just list features — he showed them working, ensuring the media and audience focused on what he wanted them to remember.

### 6. Partners
Bring others on stage to add credibility and variety.
- **Case — Madonna on iTunes:** In September 2005, Jobs announced all of Madonna's albums would be available on iTunes. The pop star appeared via webcam and joked with Jobs. The partnership announcement became a show within a show.
- **Case — Intel at MacBook Air launch:** Jobs brought Intel CEO Paul Otellini on stage to discuss the custom Core 2 Duo processor. Otellini validated Apple's decision to switch to Intel from a technical standpoint.

### 7. Customer Evidence
Third-party validation is more persuasive than self-promotion. Jobs frequently showed customer testimonials, analyst reports, and market data.
- **Case — iPhone market share (2008):** After announcing 4 million iPhones sold, Jobs showed a slide comparing iPhone market share (19.5%) against RIM (39%), Palm, Nokia, and Motorola. He then showed that iPhone matched the combined market share of the three remaining competitors — making the achievement concrete and impressive.

### 8. Video Clips
Jobs used video not as filler but as integral storytelling devices.
- **Case — Think Different campaign:** Jobs played the "Think Different" commercial — the one narrated by Richard Dreyfuss showing icons like Einstein, Dylan, and King. Jobs later revealed that he cried the first time he saw it. The video communicated Apple's values more powerfully than any slides could.

### 9. Props
Physical objects create stronger memory anchors than digital images.
- **Case — MacBook Air envelope:** The most famous prop in presentation history. Jobs pulled the MacBook Air from a standard manila envelope. The audience's reaction was visceral — they immediately understood "world's thinnest notebook" without words.

## The "Why Should I Care" Template

This is the single most important template in the book. Before every piece of communication, ask and answer three questions:

1. **What is the benefit to the audience?** (Not what does it do — what does it do FOR THEM?)
2. **Can I explain it without jargon?** (If a reporter would need to translate it, simplify it.)
3. **Is the benefit repeated?** (Jobs repeated the core benefit at least twice in every presentation.)

**Case — Press release antipattern:** Gallo deconstructs terrible press releases that fail the "why should I care" test. Examples include: "Industries announced today that it has signed an exclusive distribution agreement for diesel exhaust fluid" and "Company has been named Pizza Chain of the Year." Nobody who reads these knows why they should care. The problem: they focus on the company, not the reader.

## The 10-Minute Rule

Cognitive research shows the brain checks out after 10 minutes of passive listening. Jobs broke every presentation into 10-minute chunks, switching modalities every 10 minutes:
- Slides (explanation)
- Demo (seeing it work)
- Video (third-party storytelling)
- Guest speaker (new voice and face)
- Another slide (new topic)

This constant novelty keeps dopamine flowing. The audience never has time to get bored.

**Case — iPhone keynote analysis:** The 2007 iPhone launch was ~90 minutes. Jobs switched between: opening problem setup (slides + talking), product reveal (slides + dramatic pause), feature demo (live), video clip (Think Different-style), partner mention, second feature demo (maps), third feature demo (music), product spec section, pricing reveal, Q&A. Never more than 10 minutes on any single format.

## Dressing Up Numbers: The SRC Framework

Jobs made numbers **Specific, Relevant, and Contextual**. Use this three-step framework for every number in your presentation:

1. **Specific:** Don't say "thousands of songs." Say "7,500 songs."
2. **Relevant:** Don't say "30 GB." Say "enough for your entire music library."
3. **Contextual:** Don't just state the number. Compare it to something familiar.

**Case — iPhone 3G speed:** Jobs didn't just say "iPhone 3G is 2.8 times faster than EDGE." He showed two screen recordings side-by-side — a website loading in 59 seconds on EDGE versus 21 seconds on 3G. The comparison made the number tangible.

**Case — 30 GB iPod:** "7,500 songs, 25,000 photos, up to 75 hours of video." Each number was specific (not "thousands"), relevant to how people actually used the device, and contextualized by the activity (listening, viewing, watching).

## Scoring on Language Complexity

Seattle Post-Intelligencer reporter Todd Bishop ran Jobs's and Bill Gates's presentation transcripts through a language complexity analyzer. The results:

| Metric | Jobs | Gates | Better |
|--------|------|-------|--------|
| Words per sentence | Lower | Higher | Jobs |
| Lexical density | Lower | Higher | Jobs |
| Hard words (3+ syllables) | Lower | Higher | Jobs |
| Fog index (education years needed) | Lower | Higher | Jobs |

**Takeaway:** Jobs used simpler language but communicated more complex ideas effectively. Simplicity is not stupidity — it's clarity.
