# Core Framework: The Three-Act Presentation

## The Three-Act Structure

Gallo's analysis reveals that every great Steve Jobs presentation followed a classic dramatic structure. This is not accidental — Jobs was heavily influenced by storytelling traditions from film, theater, and Aristotle's rhetoric.

### Act I: Create the Story

This is the planning phase. Jobs spent as much time on this as on the actual delivery — sometimes more.

**Key activities**:
- Brainstorm on paper, not on screen (Principle 1: Plan in Analog)
- Define the single most important message (Principle 2: Answer "Why Should I Care")
- Craft a headline under 140 characters (Principle 3: Twitter-Like Headline)
- Structure the presentation into three parts and announce them (Principle 4: Verbal Road Map)
- Identify the antagonist and how your solution defeats it (Principle 5: Antagonist & Hero)

### Act II: Deliver the Experience

This is the live presentation itself. Jobs treated every second on stage as theater.

**Key activities**:
- Create a multisensory experience with visuals, demos, video, and props (Principle 6)
- Build to a "holy shit" moment — the single most impressive thing in the presentation
- Bring partners and customers on stage for credibility and variety
- Use metaphors and analogies to make complex ideas instantly understandable

### Act III: Refine and Rehearse

Most presenters skip this. Jobs lived here.

**Key activities**:
- Rehearse the entire presentation standing up with slides (Principle 7)
- Record and watch yourself. Kill filler words. Adjust timing.
- Test every demo until it works flawlessly under any condition
- Plan for the unexpected — hardware failure, bad wifi, wrong version

## The 18 Scenes

Gallo identified 18 specific elements that appeared across Jobs's major keynotes. Not every presentation used all 18, but the most memorable ones covered most of them:

| Scene | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1. Headline | The single message in one sentence |
| 2. Passion Statement | Why Jobs personally loved this product |
| 3. Antagonist | The problem to be solved |
| 4. Hero | The solution |
| 5. Three-Part Road Map | The agenda announced in three sections |
| 6. Demonstration | Live product demo |
| 7. Partner Moment | Guest on stage (developer, partner, or team member) |
| 8. Customer Evidence | Testimonials, case studies, adoption numbers |
| 9. "Holy Shit" Moment | The unforgettable peak moment |
| 10. Video Clip | Short, produced video (typically 90-120 seconds) |
| 11. Analogy/Metaphor | Making the unfamiliar familiar |
| 12. Numbered List | Top 5, 3 reasons, etc. |
| 13. Comparison Chart | Before/after or us/them |
| 14. Summary & Call to Action | What to do next |
| 15. Pricing Reveal | The dramatic price announcement |
| 16. One More Thing | The surprise encore |
| 17. Media Strategy | Coordinated press materials |
| 18. Q&A | The controlled Q&A session |

## Book Cases

**Case 1: iPhone Launch (2007)** — The most analyzed presentation in history. Act I created the narrative of three separate products. Act II delivered the show — the multitouch demo remains one of the most iconic tech demonstrations ever. Act III involved weeks of rehearsal, including testing every feature of a device that was still in development. The 18 scenes are almost perfectly represented: headline ("Today Apple reinvents the phone"), antagonist (existing smartphones were terrible), hero (iPhone), three-part road map (iPod + phone + internet device), "holy shit" moment (multitouch pinching and scrolling), partner moment (Cingular/AT&T), video clip (the 1984-style ad), comparison chart (smartphones vs iPhone), pricing reveal ($499/$599), summary and call to action ("We hope you love it as much as we do").

**Case 2: MacBook Air Launch (2008)** — A masterclass in minimalism. Act I set up the problem: laptops were getting thinner but not thin enough. The headline: "The world's thinnest notebook." Act II delivered the experience — the single most memorable moment was Jobs walking to a side table, picking up a standard manila envelope, and pulling the MacBook Air out of it. No slides. No spec charts. Just an image everyone instantly understood. Act III was all rehearsal: the demo of pulling the laptop from the envelope had to be smooth, surprising, and effortless. Any fumbling would have killed the moment. The entire presentation was just 18 minutes of new content during a 90-minute keynote, proving that less is more.

**Case 3: iPod Introduction (2001)** — Jobs set up the antagonist first: existing MP3 players were "big, heavy, and expensive." The hero was the iPod — "ultra-portable, easy to use, and holds your entire music library." The headline "1,000 songs in your pocket" became the most repeated product tagline of the decade. The demonstration was simple: Jobs plugged in the iPod, scrolled through songs, and played a few tracks. The pricing reveal ($399) was a dramatic moment. This presentation followed the three-act structure even though it was relatively short.

## The Aristotle Framework

Gallo connects Jobs's approach to Aristotle's five canons of rhetoric:

1. **Invention** (finding the argument): Define the single most compelling message you need to communicate. Everything else serves it.
2. **Arrangement** (structuring the argument): The three-act structure — introduce the problem, present the solution, call to action.
3. **Style** (presenting the argument): Plain language, vivid metaphors, no jargon, emotional resonance.
4. **Memory** (rehearsing the argument): Rehearse until the presentation becomes second nature. Jobs spent days on this.
5. **Delivery** (presenting the argument): Eye contact, gestures, timing, stage presence, enthusiasm.

Jobs intuitively understood that a presentation is not a document read aloud. It is a performance. The Aristotle framework gives you the structure; the three-act structure gives you the sequence; the seven principles give you the tools.

## Pattern Summary

The core framework can be summarized as:

1. **Find the story** (who is the hero, what is the villain)
2. **Structure it in three parts** (tell them what you'll say, say it, tell them what you said)
3. **Create a single, tweetable headline**
4. **Answer "Why should I care" immediately**
5. **Show, don't tell** (demos, visuals, video, props)
6. **Create one unforgettable moment**
7. **Rehearse until it's effortless**
