# 1 — The History of Autonomous Vehicles

> Source: "Autonomy" by Lawrence D. Burns and Christopher Shulgan

## The DARPA Grand Challenge

The DARPA Grand Challenge was the crucible that created the autonomous vehicle industry. Before 2004, self-driving cars were science fiction. After 2007, they were inevitable.

> **Case: The 2004 Failure** (Chapter 1 — The Race): "The first DARPA Grand Challenge in 2004 was a debacle. The course was a 150-mile desert route from Barstow, California, to Primm, Nevada. Not a single vehicle completed the course. The furthest any vehicle got was 7.4 miles. The best vehicle got stuck on a rock and caught fire." Burns documents the humbling beginning.
> **Key takeaway**: The 2004 failure was essential. It proved the difficulty of the problem and attracted the best minds in robotics. Without this humbling start, the industry would not have developed the humility and seriousness that eventually led to success.

> **Case: Stanley's Victory** (Chapter 3 — 2005): "In 2005, Stanford's team led by Sebastian Thrun entered a modified Volkswagen Touareg named Stanley. It completed the 132-mile course in 6 hours and 53 minutes. Stanley's victory was a turning point. It proved that autonomous driving was possible. Companies and investors took notice." The 2005 race changed everything.
> **Key takeaway**: Stanley's victory was the proof of concept. It took the problem from "impossible" to "hard but solvable." The team Thrun assembled would go on to form the core of Google's self-driving car project. The DARPA challenges did not just advance the technology — they created the talent pool.
