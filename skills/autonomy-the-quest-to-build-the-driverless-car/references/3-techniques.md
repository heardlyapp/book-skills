# 3 — Google/Waymo and the Software Challenge

> Source: "Autonomy" by Lawrence D. Burns and Christopher Shulgan

## The Google Bet

Google's decision to enter autonomous driving was one of the most consequential bets in the history of transportation. The company saw that the technology had reached a tipping point.

> **Case: The Google Acquisition** (Chapter on Google): "In 2009, Google hired Sebastian Thrun and his team from Stanford. Larry Page was personally involved. He saw autonomous driving as a technology that could transform the world. Google's approach was different: they didn't try to build a car. They built the software that could retrofit any car." Google's strategy from the beginning was software-first.
> **Key takeaway**: Google's key insight: the car is a commodity. The value is in the software. This is why Waymo is the industry leader — they focused on the AI, not the hardware. The traditional automakers were focused on building cars and treated software as an afterthought.

> **Case: Waymo's First Driverless Ride** (Chapter on Waymo): "In 2015, Waymo gave its first fully driverless ride on public roads. No one in the driver's seat. No safety driver. A man named Steve Mahan, who was legally blind, was the passenger. He rode from his home to a pharmacy and back. For him, this was the first independence he had experienced in years." Burns describes the emotional milestone.
> **Key takeaway**: The first driverless ride was not for a technology demonstration — it was for someone who needed it. This is the soul of the autonomous vehicle mission: not just technology but human freedom. Burns's book is at its best when it connects the engineering to the human impact.
