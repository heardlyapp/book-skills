# 4 — Industry Hype and Regulatory Hurdles

> Source: "Autonomy" by Lawrence D. Burns and Christopher Shulgan

## Anti-Pattern: The Hype Cycle

The autonomous vehicle industry has been through multiple hype cycles. Every year since 2015, the promise has been "full autonomy in 5 years." Every year, the reality is harder than expected.

> **Case: The Timeline Trap** (Chapter on Hype): "Elon Musk predicted in 2015 that Tesla would have full autonomy within 2 years. In 2019, he said the company would have 1 million robotaxis on the road in 2020. None of this happened. The pattern repeated across the industry. CEOs promised timelines they could not keep, and the public became skeptical." Burns documents the damage of overpromising.
> **Key takeaway**: The constant overpromising has eroded public trust. When autonomous vehicles actually arrive, people may not trust them — because they've been told "they're here" too many times. The industry would have been better served by under-promising and over-delivering.

## Anti-Pattern: The Corner Case Problem

The hardest technical challenge: the 1% of unusual driving scenarios that autonomous systems cannot handle.

> **Case: The Construction Zone** (Chapter on Corner Cases): "A Waymo approaches a construction zone. The cones are in an unusual pattern. The lane markings have been repainted. A worker is waving a sign in a way the AI doesn't recognize. This is a corner case — one of millions of possible unusual scenarios. Humans handle these instantly. AI systems struggle." Burns explains the hardest unsolved problem.
> **Key takeaway**: Corner cases are the reason full autonomy has been delayed. Each corner case solved reveals ten more. The long tail of unusual scenarios means that autonomous driving will improve incrementally, not jump from 99% to 100%.
