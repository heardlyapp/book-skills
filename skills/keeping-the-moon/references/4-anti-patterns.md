# 4. Anti-Patterns — Reading Keeping the Moon Today

> Source: "Keeping the Moon" by Sarah Dessen (1999). Critiques of the novel from a modern perspective.

## Critique 1: The Weight Loss Frame

**What it looks like**: The novel presents Colie's 25-pound weight loss as the catalyst for her summer transformation. She is treated differently by everyone because she is thin now.

**Why it's problematic**: The novel unintentionally reinforces the idea that thinness is the prerequisite for a good life. Colie is only accepted by the popular crowd after losing weight. The "former fat girl" frame accepts the premise that fat people deserve mistreatment.

> **Case: The Weight Loss Backstory** (Chapter 1): "I used to be fat. That was the first thing people noticed about me. Now I'm not fat. But I still feel like I am." Colie's self-image hasn't changed even though her body has. The novel is aware of this paradox but still frames thinness as the starting point of her story.
> **Key takeaway**: Stories about body transformation should be careful not to imply that thinness is a requirement for happiness.

## Critique 2: The Mean Girls Are Cartoonish

**What it looks like**: The popular girls who torment Colie are one-dimensional villains.

**Why it's problematic**: Real bullying is more complex. The novel doesn't explore why the popular girls act the way they do. They are simply obstacles for Colie to overcome.

## Critique 3: The Romance Is Underdeveloped

**What it looks like**: The Norman subplot is introduced but not resolved.

**Why it's problematic**: The novel seems to promise a romance that never fully materializes. Some readers find the open ending unsatisfying after investing in Colie's story.

> **Case: Norman's Character** (Chapters 7-15): Norman is a sweet, awkward boy who works at a bookstore. He and Colie connect over being outsiders. But their relationship stays at the level of friendship with hints of more. The ending leaves their future ambiguous.
> **Key takeaway**: YA novels that include a love interest should either develop it fully or not introduce it at all. Half-developed romance can feel like a loose thread.

## Critique 4: Aunt Mira Is a Magical Outsider

**What it looks like**: Aunt Mira is the "magical eccentric" — a stock character who exists to dispense wisdom to the protagonist without having her own arc.

**Why it's problematic**: Mira is more a device than a character. She is defined by what she can teach Colie, not by her own desires or struggles.
