# 3. Techniques — Dessen's YA Voice

> Source: "Keeping the Moon" by Sarah Dessen (1999). Techniques drawn from Dessen's narrative craft.

## The First-Person Confessional

Dessen writes in first-person, present tense. The reader experiences everything through Colie's eyes. This creates intimacy — we are inside her self-doubt, her sarcasm, her slow transformation.

**Key technique**: The voice is authentic to a 15-year-old. Colie's language is colloquial but observant. She notices things about people that she doesn't understand — which makes the reader understand them before she does.

> **Case: Describing Aunt Mira's House** (Chapter 2): "Mira's house was a museum of broken things. Chipped plates hung on the walls. A lamp made of a wine bottle and a paper shade sat on a table with three different legs. Nothing matched. Nothing was new. But somehow, everything worked." This description is both concrete (the objects) and metaphorical (broken things can still be beautiful).
> **Key takeaway**: Description in YA should do double duty — describe the world AND reflect the protagonist's inner state.

## The Summer Structure

The novel is organized by time — "the first day of the worst summer of my life" to the final day of transformation. The limited timeframe (summer) creates urgency. Colie must change before she leaves.

## The Minor Character as Teacher

Aunt Mira, Isabel, and Morgan each represent a different lesson. Dessen uses them as mirrors for Colie to see herself more clearly.

> **Case: The Burrito as Setting** (Throughout): Most of the novel's key scenes take place at the Flying Burrito restaurant — a busy beach-town burrito joint. The setting is specific (the grill, the counter, the regular customers) and functions as a contained world where Colie can learn to interact with people in low-stakes situations before facing the high-stakes confrontation at the party.
> **Key takeaway**: A contained setting (a restaurant, a summer job) provides a safe space for transformation. The protagonist can practice being a different version of herself before the real test comes.

> **Case: Isabel's Tough Love** (Chapters 7-12): Isabel is the tough, no-nonsense waitress who becomes Colie's friend. She teaches Colie through challenge, not comfort. "Stop apologizing," she tells Colie. "You don't have to be sorry for everything." This directness — telling Colie what she needs to hear, not what she wants to hear — is a different model of friendship than Morgan's warmth.
> **Key takeaway**: Friends who challenge you are as important as friends who comfort you. Isabel's tough love is what Colie needs to hear.
