# 1. Core Framework — The Summer Arc

> Source: "Keeping the Moon" by Sarah Dessen (1999). The novel follows a classic summer structure from arrival to departure.

## Colie's Journey

The novel follows Colie Sparks through a single summer in Colby, North Carolina. The structure is circular: she arrives on a train (Chapter 1) and will leave on a train when the summer ends. What happens in between transforms her.

### The Characters

| Character | Role | What they teach Colie |
|-----------|------|----------------------|
| **Aunt Mira** | Eccentric aunt, collage artist | Everything broken has value; nonconformity is strength |
| **Isabel** | Tough waitress at the Burrito | Confidence is a choice, not a gift; stand up for yourself |
| **Morgan** | Warm, nurturing waitress | Friendship is offered, not earned; kindness matters |
| **Norman** | Awkward, kind boy | People are more complex than they appear; give people a chance |

> **Case: The Arrival** (Chapter 1): Colie's mother Kiki is a fitness celebrity — a "FlyKiki" aerobics guru. She drops Colie off at the train station in her purple workout suit. Colie is being sent away for the summer because Kiki is going to Europe. "Welcome to the first day of the worst summer of my life." This opening establishes Colie's voice (cynical, hurt) and her situation (abandoned by a self-absorbed mother).
> **Key takeaway**: The opening of a YA novel should establish voice, character, and emotional stakes. Colie's sarcasm is a shield — we immediately understand why she needs it.

> **Case: The Transformation** (Final chapters): When the popular girls mock Colie at a party, she doesn't run and cry. She fights back. She says: "I'm not afraid of you. I used to be, but I'm not anymore." This is the climax — the moment when everything the summer has taught her crystallizes into action.
> **Key takeaway**: The transformation in a coming-of-age novel must be demonstrated through action, not explained. Colie doesn't say she's changed — she shows it by acting differently.
