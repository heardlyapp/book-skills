# 4. Anti-Patterns — Reading Wasted Safely

> Source: "Wasted" by Marya Hornbacher (1998). Cautions and critiques.

## Warning 1: Extremely Triggering Content

Wasted is one of the most graphic eating disorder memoirs ever published. It includes detailed descriptions of purging methods, calorie restriction, weight loss, and physical deterioration. For readers with active eating disorders, this book can be dangerous.

> **Case: The Purging Descriptions** (Chapter 2): Hornbacher describes in graphic detail how she made herself vomit multiple times daily. The specificity could serve as instruction for someone with bulimia.
> **Key takeaway**: This is not a book to read if you are actively struggling with an eating disorder. Read with a therapist's guidance.

## Critique 2: The Literary Frame Can Romanticize

Hornbacher's beautiful prose has been criticized for making the eating disorder seem almost poetic. The language elevates the disease.

> **Case: The Persephone Frame** (Chapter 5): By framing her starvation through the Persephone myth, Hornbacher gives the disorder a mythological weight that some critics argue romanticizes it.
> **Key takeaway**: The beauty of the writing does not change the ugliness of the subject. But the two in tension can be confusing for some readers.

## Critique 3: Recovery Is Underrepresented

The main text of Wasted ends before recovery is well-established. The Afterword was added years later to provide a more hopeful perspective.

> **Case: The Afterword Addition** (Afterword): The Afterword was written for later editions. Without it, the memoir ends in ambiguity. Some early readers found the ending hopeless.
> **Key takeaway**: A memoir of illness should include the perspective of survival, not just the experience of illness. The Afterword is essential.

> **Case: The Weight Numbers** (Throughout): Hornbacher provides specific weight numbers throughout the memoir. For readers with anorexia, these numbers can become targets. The specificity is a feature of the memoir's honesty but also a potential trigger.
> **Key takeaway**: Memoirs that include specific weights and calorie counts can be dangerous for readers who may use them as benchmarks. Read with awareness of this risk.
