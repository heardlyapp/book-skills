# 2. Principles — The Psychology of Starvation

> Source: "Wasted" by Marya Hornbacher (1998). Principles drawn from her experience of severe anorexia and bulimia.

## The 7 Principles of Wasted

### Principle 1: The Disorder Is a Love Affair with Death
Hornbacher describes her eating disorder as an intimate relationship. It is the most important thing in her life. She loves it. And it is killing her.

### Principle 2: Starvation Creates Its Own Reality
At 52 pounds, Hornbacher's perception is completely distorted by starvation. The brain does not function without fuel. She cannot think clearly, see clearly, or recognize her own danger.

> **Case: The Mirror Scene** (Chapter 6): Hornbacher looks in the mirror at 52 pounds and sees a fat person. This is not vanity — it is starvation psychosis. The starving brain cannot process visual information accurately.
> **Key takeaway**: The body dysmorphia of severe anorexia is not a choice. It is a neurological symptom of starvation.

### Principle 3: The Hospital Cannot Save You — Only You Can
Hornbacher's experience of hospitalization is ambivalent. She is kept alive — fed through tubes, monitored, restrained — but she does not recover until she decides to recover.

### Principle 4: The Secret Is the Disease's Ecosystem
Like Portia de Rossi, Hornbacher becomes expert at hiding her disorder. The secret protects the disease.

> **Case: The High School Years** (Chapter 3): Hornbacher describes hiding her bulimia from friends, family, and teachers. She becomes an actress — on stage and off. The performance of normalcy is exhausting.
> **Key takeaway**: People with severe eating disorders often appear completely normal to the outside world. The disorder is invisible.

### Principle 5: Writing Is Survival
Hornbacher is a writer. The memoir is not a book she wrote after recovering. It is part of her recovery — the act of giving form to chaos.

### Principle 6: The Afterword Is the Second Story
The Afterword, written years later, is more hopeful than the main text. It shows that time and distance can change the meaning of experience.

### Principle 7: You Never Fully Leave the Underworld
Even after recovery, Hornbacher acknowledges that the disorder is present. Recovery is not the absence of the voice — it is the choice not to listen.
