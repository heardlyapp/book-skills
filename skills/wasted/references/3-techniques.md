# 3. Techniques — Literary Memoir Craft

> Source: "Wasted" by Marya Hornbacher (1998). Techniques drawn from her approach to writing about trauma.

## The Mythic Frame

Hornbacher uses the Persephone myth as a structural device. The myth gives her personal story universal resonance. It also provides distance — she is not just Marya starving; she is Persephone in the underworld.

> **Case: The Mythic Chapter Titles** (Chapters 5, 8): Chapter 5 is titled "Persephone Herself Is but a Voice" and Chapter 8 is "Dying Is an Art, Like Everything Else" (a quote from Sylvia Plath). These literary references elevate the memoir from confession to art.
> **Key takeaway**: A literary frame transforms personal testimony into universal story. The myth lets the reader see their own experience in hers.

## The Graphic Detail as Witness

Hornbacher does not spare the reader. She describes the physical realities of starvation: the lanugo hair on her body, the rotting teeth from bulimia, the heart that nearly stopped.

> **Case: The Physical Description** (Chapter 6): Hornbacher describes her body in clinical detail — the downy hair, the distended stomach, the blue extremities. She refuses to look away from what she did to herself.
> **Key takeaway**: Graphic detail is not gratuitous. It is testimony. The reader must see what the disorder does to the body.

## The Dual Time Frame

The memoir shifts between the time of the disorder (past) and the time of writing (present). The writing self watches the starving self with compassion and horror.

> **Case: The Afterword** (Afterword): Written six years after the original publication, the Afterword shows the author reflecting on her younger self. The distance is both painful and hopeful.
> **Key takeaway**: Time is the memoirist's greatest tool. Distance allows insight that the experiencing self could not have.
