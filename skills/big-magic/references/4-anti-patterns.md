# Anti-Patterns

## 1. Creature of Perfectionism

**The mistake**: Waiting until the work is perfect before showing it to anyone. Obsessing over details that don't matter. Never finishing.

**Fix**: Done > good. Set deadlines. Ship it. The imperfect work you finish is worth more than the masterpiece you never complete.

## 2. Merging Identity with Work

**The mistake**: "I am a failure because my book didn't sell." Your work's outcome ≠ your worth. When identity and work merge, every rejection is existential.

**Fix**: "You are not your work. Your work is something you do, not something you are." Separate your self-worth from your creative output.

## 3. Waiting for Permission

**The mistake**: Waiting for someone else — a publisher, a teacher, a critic — to validate your right to create.

**Fix**: Permission is not a thing you receive. It's a thing you take.

> **Case: The Woman Who Waited** (Permission): Gilbert describes a woman who spent years wanting to paint but felt she needed "one more art class" before she could start. She kept postponing. One day she realized: no one was going to give her permission. She bought canvas and paint that afternoon.
> **Key takeaway**: Permission never comes from outside. The only person who can authorize your creative life is you.

## 4. Outcome Obsession

**The mistake**: Creating for the result — money, fame, recognition — rather than for the process. When the outcome doesn't meet expectations, the creative impulse dies.

**Fix**: Create for curiosity, not for results. The process is the point. Anything that comes after is a bonus.

## 5. Starving Artist Romance

**The mistake**: Believing that suffering is necessary for great art. That creativity requires sacrifice, poverty, or emotional turmoil.

**Fix**: "Creativity is not a wound that needs to be bled." You can create from joy, from play, from curiosity. In fact, you'll probably create better work that way.
