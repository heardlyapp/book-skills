# Tiktaalik and the Hunt

> Source: Your Inner Fish, Preface, Chapter 1

## Core Framework

### The Discovery

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Fossil | Tiktaalik roseae |
| Age | 375 million years (Devonian) |
| Location | Ellesmere Island, Nunavut, Canada |
| Discovered | 2004, by Shubin, Daeschler, and Jenkins |
| Significance | Transitional form between fish and land animals |

> **Case: Finding Tiktaalik (Chapter 1)**
> Shubin describes the moment: "I first saw one of our inner fish on a snowy July afternoon while studying 375-million-year-old rocks on Ellesmere Island." Sticking out of the rocks was the snout of a fish — with a flat head. A flat head meant this fish had features that would later become useful for life on land. Over several field seasons, they excavated the full skeleton. Tiktaalik was a fish with a neck, ribs, and fin bones that look like a wrist. In the language of the Inuit, Tiktaalik means "a large freshwater fish." The discovery was front-page news around the world.
> **Key takeaway:** Tiktaalik is the most famous transitional fossil since Archaeopteryx. It is the nearest thing we have to a fish that could walk. The discovery was not luck — it was the result of years of planning based on evolutionary principles.

> **Case: The Anatomy Classroom (Preface)**
> Shubin, a paleontologist, found himself teaching human anatomy to medical students. He realized: the best way to understand the human body is to look at simpler animals. The nerves in the human head? A shark has the same pattern — simpler, easier to see. The bones of the human arm? A fish fin has the same pattern — easier to trace. His inner fish became his teaching tool, and eventually, his book.
> **Key takeaway:** Shubin's insight — that comparative anatomy is the key to understanding human anatomy — is the book's guiding principle. You cannot understand the human body unless you understand where it came from.
