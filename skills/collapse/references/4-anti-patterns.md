# Anti-Patterns — Modern Collapses in Progress

> Source: Collapse, Chapters 10, 11, 12, 13, 15

## Four Modern Case Studies

### 1. Rwanda — Overpopulation and Ethnic Conflict
**Pattern**: High population density + scarce land + drought → resource competition → ethnic violence → genocide.
**Lesson**: Environmental stress makes existing conflicts more dangerous. Sustainable resource management is a peace-building strategy.

### 2. China — Top-Down Environmental Decisions
**Pattern**: The government can make big decisions quickly — but can also ignore local costs. Three Gorges Dam displaced millions. Air pollution kills 1.5 million people per year.
**Lesson**: Concentrated power can solve some problems but creates others. Top-down decisions often ignore local knowledge.

### 3. Australia — Mining and Water Depletion
**Pattern**: A wealthy democracy that is over-exploiting its resources: mining (non-renewable), water (Murray-Darling Basin depleted), and soil (salinity from irrigation).
**Lesson**: Even democratic societies with freedom of the press can fail to address long-term resource problems.

### 4. Montana — The American Pattern
**Pattern**: Partisan gridlock prevents action. Logging, mining, and energy interests block environmental regulation. The "wise use" movement opposes conservation.
**Lesson**: Democracy only works if the public demands action — and the public is divided and skeptical of science.

> **Case: The Failure of American Environmental Policy** (Chapter 1, Montana)
> Diamond opens the book with Montana — a modern American case study. Montana has abundant natural resources, a small population, and a strong tradition of independence. Yet it faces the same environmental problems as the rest of the world: deforestation, water depletion, soil loss, and partisan paralysis. Diamond notes that Montana's environmental problems are not as severe as Rwanda's or China's — but the pattern of ignoring long-term problems for short-term gain is the same.
> **Key takeaway**: The failure to act is not limited to poor or authoritarian societies. Rich democracies also fail — because the political system rewards short-term thinking and powerful interests block change. The most important variable is not wealth or technology but political will.

> **Case: The Polder Model — Success Is Possible** (Chapter 16)
> Diamond's hopeful conclusion: the Netherlands (polders) shows that societies can succeed. The Dutch faced an existential threat (the sea) and chose collective action to manage it. They built dikes, created water management boards, and cooperated across political divisions. The "polder model" — decision-making by consensus, with all stakeholders included — is the opposite of the behavior that causes collapse. Diamond's point: it is possible to organize a society to solve its environmental problems. The Netherlands did it. So can we.
> **Key takeaway**: The Netherlands proves that collective action on environmental threats is possible — if the society chooses to act. The polder model is not a Dutch cultural peculiarity — it is a set of institutional arrangements that can be replicated. The key: include all stakeholders, require consensus, and enforce compliance.
