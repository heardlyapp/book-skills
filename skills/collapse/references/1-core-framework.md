# Diamond's Five-Point Collapse Framework

> Source: Collapse, Prologue, Chapters 1, 14

## Core Framework

Diamond synthesizes decades of research into a framework for understanding why societies collapse. The framework applies equally to past civilizations and modern nations.

### The Five Factors

| Factor | Definition | Example |
|--------|------------|---------|
| Environmental damage | A society damages its resource base (deforestation, soil erosion, water depletion, overhunting) | Easter Island cut down all its trees |
| Climate change | Natural or human-caused shifts in climate | Maya drought (prolonged dry period) |
| Hostile neighbors | Attack or invasion by enemies | Norse Greenland attacked by Inuit |
| Loss of trading partners | Economic isolation deprives the society of essential resources | Norse Greenland lost access to European ships |
| Failure to perceive or solve | The society fails to notice the problem or fails to solve it once noticed | Maya elites ignored warning signs |

### The Most Critical Factor

The fifth factor — failure to perceive or solve — is the most important because it explains why societies with visible problems do nothing. Diamond identifies four reasons for this failure:
1. The problem may not be noticed (landscape amnesia, creeping normalcy)
2. The problem may be noticed but not addressed (conflicting values, elite self-interest)
3. Attempted solutions may fail
4. The society may not have the resources to solve the problem

> **Case: Easter Island — The Icon of Collapse** (Chapter 2)
> Easter Island (Rapa Nui) is Diamond's most famous case. The Polynesian inhabitants arrived around 900 AD and built a society that erected hundreds of giant stone statues (moai). Over centuries, they cut down all the island's trees — for moving statues, building houses and canoes, and clearing land for farming. Deforestation led to soil erosion, loss of fishing boats, crop failures, and social collapse. By the time Europeans arrived in 1722, the population had crashed, and the society had collapsed into civil war and cannibalism. The most haunting detail: the islanders cut down the last tree knowing it was the last one.
> **Key takeaway**: Easter Island is a parable for our planet. The islanders had no place to go when they destroyed their environment. Neither do we. The difference: they did not know what they were doing. We do.

> **Case: The Maya Collapse** (Chapter 5)
> The Classic Maya civilization collapsed in the 8th-9th centuries after centuries of growth. Diamond argues that the collapse was caused by a combination of factors: deforestation (for building materials and cooking fires), soil erosion (from intensive farming on slopes), and a prolonged drought (documented by lake sediment cores). Maya kings responded by building more temples and waging more wars — trying to propitiate the gods instead of addressing the environmental crisis. Their failure to adapt doomed them.
> **Key takeaway**: The Maya response to crisis was to do more of what had worked in the past — but the past conditions no longer applied. This is the most common form of failed adaptation: applying old solutions to new problems.
