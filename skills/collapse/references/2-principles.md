# Past Collapses — Easter Island, Maya, Norse Greenland

> Source: Collapse, Chapters 2-8

## Key Case Studies

### Easter Island (c. 900-1722)
**What happened**: Deforestation → soil erosion → crop failure → starvation → war → cannibalism → population crash from ~15,000 to ~3,000.
**Why it matters**: It is the clearest example of a society that destroyed its own resource base. The islanders cut down the last tree knowing it was the last.

### Maya (c. 250-900 AD)
**What happened**: Deforestation + drought + overpopulation → food shortages → political collapse → abandonment of cities.
**Why it matters**: Maya kings responded with more monuments and wars — the opposite of what was needed.

### Norse Greenland (c. 985-1450 AD)
**What happened**: Norse settlers established farms in Greenland during a warm period. When the climate cooled, they refused to adopt Inuit survival techniques (seal hunting, ice fishing). They starved.
**Why it matters**: Cultural inflexibility killed them. They would rather starve as Christians than eat seal meat like the "pagan" Inuit.

> **Case: Norse Greenland's Refusal to Adapt** (Chapters 7-8)
> The Norse settled Greenland during the Medieval Warm Period. They brought their European lifestyle: cows, sheep, churches, and a hierarchical society. When the climate cooled in the 14th century, growing seasons shortened, and pastures failed. The Norse had a simple alternative: learn from the Inuit, who had adapted perfectly to the Arctic environment — seal hunting, kayaks, warm clothing. The Norse refused. They considered the Inuit backward and pagan. They clung to their European identity and starved.
> **Key takeaway**: Cultural inflexibility — the refusal to learn from others — can be fatal. The Norse chose their identity over their survival. The lesson: adaptation requires humility, not pride.

> **Case: The Anasazi of the American Southwest** (Chapter 4)
> The Anasazi built elaborate cliff dwellings in the American Southwest, thriving for centuries. They developed sophisticated water management systems and traded across the region. In the late 13th century, they abandoned their settlements. The cause: a prolonged drought combined with population pressure and deforestation. The Anasazi did not starve — they dispersed. People moved to other areas, joined other communities, and survived. The society collapsed, but the people adapted.
> **Key takeaway**: The Anasazi showed that collapse does not have to mean extinction. Sometimes the answer is not saving the society but saving the people — dispersing, migrating, adapting. The modern equivalent: when a region becomes uninhabitable, people will move.
