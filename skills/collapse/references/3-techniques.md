# Why Societies Ignore Problems — The Perception Failures

> Source: Collapse, Chapter 14

## The Four Reasons Problems Are Not Solved

Diamond identifies four reasons why societies fail to respond to threats.

### 1. Failure to Perceive
The problem may not be noticed. Reasons:
- **Landscape amnesia**: Each generation forgets what the environment used to look like. The forest that disappeared over 200 years is not missed because no one remembers it.
- **Creeping normalcy**: Slow, gradual decline is not noticed because each year is only slightly worse than the last.
- **Shifting baseline**: What is considered "normal" shifts over generations.

### 2. Failure to Act After Perception
The problem is noticed but not addressed. Reasons:
- **Conflict of values**: The solution conflicts with deeply held beliefs (Norse refusing to eat seal meat).
- **Elite self-interest**: Those who benefit from the status quo oppose change.
- **Psychological denial**: The problem is too painful to acknowledge.
- **Short-term vs long-term**: Immediate costs of action outweigh future benefits in political calculations.

### 3. Failure of Solutions
Attempted solutions fail because they address symptoms, not causes, or because circumstances have changed.

### 4. Runaway Feedback
Attempts to solve the problem make it worse (the "fix" backfires).

> **Case: The "Rational" Bad Behavior of Elites** (Chapter 14)
> Diamond discusses the most depressing pattern: elites who know the problem exists but have no incentive to solve it. The Maya kings, the Easter Island chiefs, the Norse chieftains — all were rational actors pursuing their own interests. The kings built more monuments to legitimate their rule. The chiefs competed to erect the largest statues. The chieftains maintained their European identity. In each case, the elites benefited from the status quo, and the costs of collapse were borne by the common people — who had no power to change the system.
> **Key takeaway**: The most tragic collapses are those where the problem is known but the people with power to solve it have no incentive to act. This is "rational bad behavior" — rational for the individual, catastrophic for the society. Modern equivalents: fossil fuel executives who know about climate change but resist action.

> **Case: The Rwanda Genocide — Overpopulation and Environmental Stress** (Chapter 10)
> Diamond argues that the 1994 Rwanda genocide was partially caused by environmental stress. Rwanda had the highest population density in Africa. Land was scarce. Farmers were desperate. When drought hit, competition for resources intensified. The Hutu-Tutsi ethnic conflict was real — but it was triggered and intensified by resource scarcity. Diamond is not excusing genocide — he is explaining that societies under environmental stress are more likely to collapse into violence.
> **Key takeaway**: Environmental stress does not directly cause violence — but it makes violence more likely. When resources are scarce and populations are desperate, existing ethnic, religious, or political divisions become lethal. The lesson: environmental stability is a precondition for social peace.
