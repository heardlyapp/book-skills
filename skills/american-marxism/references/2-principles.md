# 2 — Cultural Marxism and the Frankfurt School

> Source: "American Marxism" by Mark R. Levin

## The Shift to Culture

Classical Marxism focused on economics and the working class. When the revolution didn't come, Marxist intellectuals shifted their focus to culture — a move that would prove far more effective.

> **Case: The Frankfurt School** (Chapter on Cultural Marxism): "In the 1920s and 1930s, a group of German Marxist intellectuals formed the Institute for Social Research — later known as the Frankfurt School. They applied Marxist analysis to culture, psychology, family, and religion. Their goal: to deconstruct Western civilization from within." Levin traces the intellectual origins of cultural Marxism.
> **Key takeaway**: The Frankfurt School's innovation was to apply Marx's critique of capitalism to all of Western culture — not just its economic system but its art, religion, family structure, and psychology. Everything was a weapon of oppression. Everything had to be deconstructed.

> **Case: Gramsci's March Through Institutions** (Chapter on Long March): "Antonio Gramsci, an Italian Marxist, developed the theory of cultural hegemony. He argued that revolutionaries must first control the institutions of civil society — schools, universities, media, churches — before they can take control of the state. This 'long march through institutions' became the strategy of choice for cultural Marxists." Levin explains Gramsci's enduring influence.
> **Key takeaway**: The Gramscian strategy explains why modern Marxists focus on education, media, and culture rather than factory workers and revolution. They are playing the long game. Control the institutions, control the narrative. Control the narrative, control the society. This is happening now.
