# Core Framework — Dreamstate: A Conspiracy Theory

## The Central Premise

"The central premise of this book is the admittedly ridiculous but incontrovertibly true assertion that the universe does not exist." Everything you perceive as reality is a dream. We are asleep on a dead planet, dreaming a rich dreamworld to avoid the boredom of existence. "This dead rock floating in infinite nothingness would drive anyone batshit crazy in thirty seconds, but you have come up with a very clever solution: You sleep, you dream of a better world, and you inhabit it."

## The Three States of Being

**State 1 — Sub-Lucid (Eyes Closed in the Dreamstate):** Almost everyone. You believe the dream is real. You are "born of the flesh." This is Human Childhood or the Segregated State.

**State 2 — Lucid (Eyes Open in the Dreamstate):** Very few. You know you're dreaming. "Reborn of the spirit." Human Adulthood or the Integrated State. This is the natural and universally desirable condition for all humans past puberty, but almost no one reaches it because "our failure to develop naturally is actually the success of the mechanism that keeps the dreamstate amusing."

**State 3 — Awake from the Dreamstate:** The author's claimed state. You are awake on a dead rock in the middle of nowhere. "It's about as much fun as it sounds." This is the only state that merits the term spiritual enlightenment. "All other states being imaginary."

## The Two Things That Exist

In truth, only two things exist, one of which doesn't: **awareness** and **appearance**. Awareness is consciousness. Appearance is the content of consciousness. Consciousness is true. The content of consciousness is not. "It doesn't get much simpler than that."

## I-Am/Consciousness as the Only Knowledge

"Beyond I Am, there is no knowledge to be found in the dreamstate, only anti-knowledge." All supposed knowledge is really belief masquerading as knowledge. "I-Am/Consciousness is the weapon that destroys the universe." This kills God, gods, science, philosophy, and religion — all claimants to knowledge.

## The Journey to No-Self

"Self itself is but a dream." The spiritual journey is not about finding your true self — it's about discovering there is no self. "Who is all this for? Who's dream is this, and who wants to wake up?"

## Spirituality vs. Waking Up

"Spirituality is about staying comfortably asleep in the dreamstate." Popular spiritual belief systems are popular because "we secretly want to be seduced and saved from ourselves." Buddhism produces Buddhists, not Buddhas. "Ditto all -isms." If you want to wake up, you're on your own.

## The Narrative Container

The philosophical content is wrapped in a fictional narrative: Jed McKenna lives in a remote Appalachian cabin with his dog Maya. He cuts firewood, reads (Solzhenitsyn, Melville, Beckett), writes plays, and drinks cheap red wine. Visitors — Lisa's daughter Maggie, who wants to become a filmmaker — provide the human context for the philosophical explorations.

## Key Stories and Analogies

**The Dead Planet:** The foundational analogy. "Picture a dead planet; lifeless, featureless, empty. This is your homeworld, and you never left. Living on this dead rock would drive anyone batshit crazy in thirty seconds, but you have come up with a very clever solution: You sleep, you dream of a better world, and you inhabit it."

**The Sleepwalking Cliff:** "It's like you're asleep on top of a cliff. As long as you stay asleep, you're safe, but as soon as you get antsy and start sleepwalking, things can change. You walk off the edge and that's it — there's no undo button, no do-overs, no going back."

**The House of Mirrors:** "Wherever we turn in this house of mirrors, it all reflects back so bendy and distorted that no matter how hard we focus or how well we think, we can never make it make sense. We assume the mirrors are reflecting something real and if we could just get steady, the images would snap into focus. But we can never see things as they actually are because nothing is as it actually is."

**The Party at the House in the Evening:** The surreal opening chapter where Jed attends a party where everyone has "win" appended to their names (Jedwin, Lisabelle, Bradwin, Fredwin) and nothing makes linear sense — this is a demonstration of the dreamstate.
