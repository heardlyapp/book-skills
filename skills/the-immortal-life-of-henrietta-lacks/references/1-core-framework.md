## The HeLa Story — A Century in Three Parts

### Part One: Life (1920-1951)

Henrietta Lacks was born Loretta Pleasant in Roanoke, Virginia, on August 1, 1920. After her mother died giving birth to her tenth child, Henrietta was sent to live with her grandfather in Clover, Virginia, in a four-room log cabin that had once been slave quarters. She grew up working tobacco fields alongside her cousin David "Day" Lacks, who would become her husband. They had five children together.

On January 29, 1951, Henrietta went to Johns Hopkins Hospital — the only major hospital within miles that treated black patients — because she felt "a knot on her womb." Dr. Howard Jones found a hard, purple tumor on her cervix, unlike anything he'd seen. Without telling Henrietta, he cut two samples from her tumor — one for diagnosis, one for Dr. George Gey's tissue culture research.

> **Case: The Biopsy** (Chapter 1): Jones removed two samples of Henrietta's cervix. The first was for pathology (diagnosis). The second went to Dr. George Gey's lab at Hopkins. Henrietta was never asked for permission. On the pathology form, Gey's technician wrote "HeLa" — using the first two letters of Henrietta's first and last names. Neither Henrietta nor her family would know about the cells for another 25 years.
> **Key takeaway**: The line between diagnosis and research was crossed without consent. The very naming of "HeLa" erased Henrietta's identity from the start.

### Part Two: Death (1951-1973)

Henrietta died on October 4, 1951, at age 31. But her cells didn't die. The sample George Gey received was extraordinary — while every other human cell culture died within days, HeLa cells doubled every 24 hours and never stopped. Gey began distributing HeLa cells to researchers around the world.

HeLa cells became the workhorse of modern medicine. They were used to develop the polio vaccine (Jonas Salk tested it on HeLa). They went into space to test zero gravity effects. They helped develop chemotherapy, gene mapping, cloning, and in vitro fertilization. By the 1970s, HeLa cells were a multi-million-dollar industry.

> **Case: The Tuskegee Context** (Chapters 16-17, 21): The Lacks family discovered the truth about Henrietta's cells in the 1970s — an era when the Tuskegee Syphilis Study (1932-1972) had just been exposed. The study had left 400 black men with syphilis untreated for 40 years. This created a deep distrust: when scientists approached the Lacks family for blood tests, Deborah feared they were being used as guinea pigs. "I think they was trying to kill us," she told Skloot.
> **Key takeaway**: Historical context matters. The Lacks family's suspicion was not paranoia — it was the rational response of a community repeatedly betrayed by medicine.

### Part Three: Immortality (1973-Present)

When scientists from Hopkins contacted the Lacks family in 1973 asking for blood samples to study Henrietta's genetics, the family learned for the first time that Henrietta's cells were still alive. The revelation sent the family into crisis. Deborah, Henrietta's daughter, became obsessed with understanding who her mother was and what had happened to her cells. Zakariyya, the son, became bitter and distrustful.

In 2013, the NIH reached an agreement with the Lacks family giving them a role in reviewing access to the HeLa genome sequence — the first time a family has had such control over a cell line derived from a relative.

> **Case: The Genome Publication** (Chapter 33, Afterword): In 2013, a German research team published the full HeLa genome sequence without consulting the Lacks family. The sequence revealed intimate genetic information about Henrietta and her children — including disease risks and family relationships. Deborah had died in 2009, but her brothers were still alive. The NIH eventually brokered an agreement requiring researchers to apply for permission to use HeLa genomic data, with two Lacks family members on the review board.
> **Key takeaway**: Privacy violations don't end with death. Genetic information is shared across generations. Informed consent must account for the family, not just the individual.

## The Four Key Relationships

1. **Henrietta and Deborah**: Henrietta died when Deborah was an infant. Deborah spent her life trying to know her mother — through medical records, family stories, and eventually Skloot's research.
2. **Henrietta and John Hopkins**: A relationship of unequal power. Hopkins was the only hospital that would treat Henrietta — but it was also the institution that took her cells and distributed them without consent.
3. **HeLa and Science**: The most productive relationship in medical history — HeLa cells enabled discoveries that won Nobel Prizes, eradicated polio, and mapped the human genome — but built on a foundation of exploitation.
4. **Skloot and Deborah**: The author became a participant in the story. Deborah and Skloot formed a deep bond over a decade of research, with Deborah believing Henrietta's spirit had chosen Skloot to tell her story.
