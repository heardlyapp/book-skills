> Source: The Immortal Life of Henrietta Lacks, Prologue through Afterword — 6 recurring anti-patterns in medical research and storytelling about marginalized communities

## Anti-Pattern 1: Treating People as Specimens

The most fundamental anti-pattern: viewing patients as sources of biological material rather than as human beings with rights, feelings, and families. Henrietta Lacks became "HeLa" — a cell line, not a woman. Her tumor sample was labeled with a code, not her name. Her medical records were kept, but her personhood was discarded.

> **Case: The Labeling of HeLa** (Chapter 4): "HeLa" was created from the first two letters of Henrietta's first and last names — He-nrietta La-cks. This anonymized code served the purposes of science but erased the person. For decades, students learning about HeLa cells never knew the name of the woman behind them. Skloot's instructor in 1988 mentioned her name only once: "She was a black woman." Then he erased it.
> **Key takeaway**: When you label a person as a specimen code, you have already begun the process of dehumanization.

## Anti-Pattern 2: Ends Justifying Means in Science

The most common defense of what happened to Henrietta is: "But her cells saved millions of lives." This is the ends-justify-the-means argument. It sounds reasonable — until you apply it consistently. If the benefits justified the violation, then no one would ever need to consent to anything that might advance science. The argument also conveniently ignores that ethical science — with consent — could have achieved the same results.

> **Case: George Gey's Dilemma** (Chapters 4, 7): Dr. George Gey was not a villain. He was a dedicated scientist who had spent decades trying to grow human cells in culture. He believed HeLa cells could cure disease. But he never asked Henrietta for permission. When Skloot asked Gey's colleagues why, they said: "It wasn't done in those days." The question Skloot asks: If it wasn't done, why did Gey disguise Henrietta's identity with a code?
> **Key takeaway**: People know when they are doing something wrong — even if they don't admit it to themselves. The "it was standard practice" defense is often a cover for a bad conscience.

## Anti-Pattern 3: Extracting from Communities without Giving Back

Scientists went to Turner Station, the Lacks family's neighborhood, and took blood samples from dozens of relatives — ostensibly to study the genetics of Henrietta's cancer. The family was told the tests would help them know if they were at risk. But the research was published, the scientists advanced their careers, and the family never heard the results. No one came back to explain.

> **Case: The Family Blood Draws** (Chapters 23-24): In 1973, researchers from Hopkins called the Lacks family, asking for blood samples. Henrietta's husband, Day, was told the tests would help his children. He agreed. The researchers drew blood from Deborah, Lawrence, Sonny, Joe, and Zakariyya. The family was never told the results. Years later, Deborah discovered that their DNA had been used to map genetic markers — without their knowledge.
> **Key takeaway**: Taking samples from a community without returning results is a form of extraction. Research should benefit the participants, not just the researchers.

## Anti-Pattern 4: Publishing Genetic Data without Consent

The 2013 publication of the HeLa genome sequence was a textbook violation of privacy. The researchers argued that the sequence was "anonymized" — but with whole-genome data, true anonymization is impossible. The sequence revealed information about Henrietta's children, grandchildren, and family medical history. The family learned about it from a reporter.

> **Case: The German Genome** (Afterword): A team in Germany sequenced the full HeLa genome and published it online in 2013. They didn't contact the Lacks family. They didn't consult the NIH. When the story broke, Deborah's son Alfred said: "They're always doing things to us without asking. When is it going to stop?" The NIH removed the data from public access and negotiated the first-ever family agreement for genomic data.
> **Key takeaway**: Genetic information is not just about one person — it is about an entire family and future generations. Publishing it without consent is a violation with ripple effects across decades.

## Anti-Pattern 5: Assuming Distrust Is Irrational

When the Lacks family reacted with fear and suspicion to requests for blood samples, the researchers dismissed it as ignorance. But the family's distrust was not irrational — it was a logical response to a long history of medical abuse. From the Tuskegee Syphilis Study to the unauthorized harvesting of Henrietta's cells, the black community had ample reason to mistrust medical institutions.

> **Case: The Night Doctors** (Chapter 21): Deborah's grandmother told her stories of "night doctors" who would kidnap black people for medical experiments. These stories had roots in reality — during the Jim Crow era, black bodies were routinely used for dissection, experimentation, and teaching without consent. When Deborah said she was afraid to give blood, she wasn't being paranoid. She was being rational.
> **Key takeaway**: Before calling someone's distrust "paranoid," ask what their community has experienced. Often, the distrust is earned.

## Anti-Pattern 6: Telling Stories about Communities without Their Participation

Before Skloot, journalists who wrote about the Lacks family did so without building relationships with them. They quoted family members but didn't explain the science. They reported the controversy but didn't stay long enough to understand the pain. The family felt used. Deborah told Skloot: "Every reporter who's come here has lied to us."

> **Case: The 1976 Jet Article** (Prologue): In 1976, Jet magazine published a story about Henrietta Lacks and her family. The article included photos of the family and reported their anger. But the family felt the article didn't tell their full story. It was a drive-by journalism — in and out in a day, leaving the family to deal with the aftermath. When Skloot arrived years later, the family's first instinct was to shut the door.
> **Key takeaway**: Ethical storytelling requires relationship, not just access. If you can't stay for the difficult parts, don't start.
