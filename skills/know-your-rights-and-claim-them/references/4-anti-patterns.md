# 4 — Anti-Patterns

> Source: *Know Your Rights and Claim Them* — Parts 1-3.

## Common Errors

### 1. The "My Rights Will Be Respected Automatically" Fallacy

**The trap:** "The Convention exists. Governments signed it. Therefore my rights are protected."

**Why it fails:** The entire book is about the gap between principle and reality. "If all governments kept their word, there would be no need for this book." Rights must be claimed, defended, and fought for. Passivity is the enemy.

### 2. The "I'm Too Young to Make a Difference" Trap

**The trap:** "Adults have all the power. No one will listen to a child."

**Why it fails:** The case studies in this book are all children and young people. Greta was 15. Autumn was 13. Nkosi was 11. Aisha was 15. The Glasgow Girls were 15. Age is not the barrier — lack of knowledge and organization is.

### 3. The "It's Just One Person's Problem" Isolation

**The trap:** "This is happening to me. No one else cares."

**Why it fails:** Discrimination and rights violations always affect more people than you think. Zulaikha's hair protest at Pretoria High School started with a small group of Black students. It ended with a national conversation about racism in South African schools.

### 4. The "Rights End Where the US Border Is" Confusion

**The trap:** "The US didn't ratify, so I have no rights."

**Why it fails:** The US has signed the Convention, helped draft it, and ratified other treaties. Many rights apply through *jus cogens*, the Bill of Rights, and state laws. It's more complex but not hopeless.

### 5. The Unsafe Activism Trap

**The trap:** "I have to speak out right now, whatever the cost."

**Why it fails:** In some countries, speaking out can lead to arrest, violence, or worse. The book's first instruction is to check your physical security. A martyr is less useful than a survivor who can keep fighting.
