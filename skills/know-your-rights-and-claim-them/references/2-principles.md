# 2 — Key Principles

> Source: *Know Your Rights and Claim Them* — Parts 1-3.

## The 7 Principles

### 1. Your Rights Begin at Birth

"You have had them since the moment you were born." Rights are not granted by governments or parents. They are inherent in being human. The Convention merely recognizes what already belongs to you.

### 2. Knowledge is the First Defense

"The best way forward is to educate yourself and others about rights." The single most effective protection against rights violations is knowing what your rights are. Abusers and oppressive systems rely on ignorance.

### 3. The Gap is Where Action Lives

> **Case: Nkosi Johnson** (Part 2, "Life, Dignity, and Health"): Born with HIV in South Africa in 1989, Nkosi was denied admission to school because of his HIV status. He and his foster mother campaigned. New anti-discrimination policies were put in place. At age 11, he spoke at the International AIDS Conference: "Care for us and accept us — we are all human beings." His speech changed public opinion in South Africa and worldwide.
> **Key takeaway**: When the law says one thing but reality says another, the gap is where you act. Nkosi didn't just accept the gap — he used it as leverage for change.

### 4. Solidarity is Power

"Standing up for others is also called solidarity." When you join with others who share your concern, your individual rights become collective power. The Glasgow Girls started as friends; they ended by changing national law.

### 5. Safety is Non-Negotiable

"If it motivates you to take action, first check if it is safe for you to do so." The book begins with this warning. Before any campaign, assess your physical and digital security. No cause justifies your harm.

### 6. Participation is a Right, Not a Privilege

"You have the right to be heard and to participate in all decisions affecting you." This is a legally binding right under the Convention. Schools, hospitals, courts, and governments must listen to children.

### 7. The United States Has Not Ratified — But You Still Have Rights

The US helped draft the Convention but never ratified it. However, many child rights apply through other federal and state laws, the Bill of Rights, and *jus cogens* (compelling international law).
