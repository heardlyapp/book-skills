# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *Know Your Rights and Claim Them* — Part 3.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The Physical Security Check

**Before any activist action, assess:**
- Can I be identified? If so, what are the risks?
- Will I face repercussions at home, school, or with authorities?
- Do I have trusted adults or allies who support this action?
- Do I have an exit plan if things go wrong?
- Is there a safer way to achieve the same goal?

### 2. The Know-Skill-Act Audit

**For any issue you want to address:**
- **Know:** What rights are at stake? What does the law say? Who is responsible?
- **Skill:** Can I research this? Speak about it? Organize others? Stay digitally safe?
- **Act:** What's the smallest effective action I can take today? This week?

### 3. The Campaign Escalation Ladder

Start small, escalate only as needed:
1. Write a letter (to principal, local official, newspaper)
2. Start a petition (online or paper)
3. Organize a meeting (with decision-makers)
4. Public action (protest, walkout, banner)
5. Media engagement (contact journalists, social media)
6. Legal action (file complaint, go to court)
7. International bodies (UN Committee via OP3)

> **Case: Aisha Saleh on Period Poverty** (Part 2, "Equality"): Aisha started by speaking to her community about the taboo on menstruation. Then she urged the Nigerian government to provide free menstrual products. She used social media to raise awareness, organized donations, and distributed pads to girls across Nigeria. She escalated from local community action to national advocacy over months.
> **Key takeaway**: Start where you are. Move up only when the first step isn't enough.

### 4. The Digital Security Baseline

- Use strong passwords and two-factor authentication
- Know your platform's privacy settings
- Be aware that social media platforms collect data
- If you're at risk, use encrypted messaging apps
- Share information on a need-to-know basis

### 5. The OP3 Complaint Route

If your national legal system has failed and your country has ratified OP3:
1. Exhaust domestic remedies first (try courts in your country)
2. Document everything — every violation, every failed attempt at justice
3. Find a legal organization to help submit the complaint
4. Submit to the UN Committee on the Rights of the Child

### 6. The Legal Navigation Checklist

Before going to court:
1. Identify the specific right violated (which Article?)
2. Identify who violated it (state or individual?)
3. Research national laws that incorporate the Convention
4. Find legal aid or advocacy organizations
5. Gather evidence (documents, witness statements, records)
