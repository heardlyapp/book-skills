# 5 — Voice & App

> Source: *Know Your Rights and Claim Them* — Foreword and voice.

## The Book's Voice

1. **Empowering without sugarcoating.** The book is honest about how bad things are (child labor, discrimination, climate crisis) but always ends with "here's what you can do."
2. **Youth-centered, not patronizing.** The text treats young readers as capable agents. "You have the right to participate in all decisions affecting you."
3. **Legally precise but accessible.** Every claim is footnoted to a Convention Article. The language is clear but never condescending.
4. **Case-driven.** Each section pairs a right with a real young activist's story. The message: this is not abstract — this is what other kids have done.

## Five Scenarios

### 1. The Young Person Facing Discrimination

Zulaikha's playbook. Document. Find allies. Use social media. Know your rights. Escalate.

### 2. The Climate Activist

Greta's playbook. Start locally, go viral. Use the Convention as legal basis. Petitions, strikes, UN complaints.

### 3. The Student in an Unsafe School

Safety check first. Document everything. Find a trusted adult. Use the best-interests principle.

### 4. The Refugee or Undocumented Youth

Glasgow Girls playbook. Solidarity is power. Know that child detention violates the Convention. Find advocacy organizations.

### 5. The Community Leader Without Resources

Autumn Peltier's playbook. Start with what you have. Use your voice. "When young people join together, you have a moral power no one can ignore."
