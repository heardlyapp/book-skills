# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *Know Your Rights and Claim Them* by Amnesty International/Angelina Jolie/Geraldine Van Bueren — Parts 1-3.

## The Three Frameworks

### Framework A: The Four General Principles (Part 1)

The UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (UNCRC) has 54 Articles, but they rest on four General Principles that act as the lens for understanding all rights:

1. **The right to life, survival, and development** — The foundational right. Without this, nothing else matters. Includes food, water, shelter, healthcare, and a clean environment.
2. **Equality and non-discrimination** — All children have the same rights regardless of race, gender, sexuality, religion, disability, wealth, or status.
3. **The right to be heard and to participate** — Your views matter in decisions affecting you. Courts, schools, and families must listen.
4. **The best interests of the child** — Every decision affecting children must put their welfare first. Not "what's convenient" but "what's best."

> **Case: Greta Thunberg and the Climate Petition** (Part 2, "Life, Dignity, and Health"): In 2019, Greta and 14 other child activists petitioned the UN Committee on the Rights of the Child, protesting their governments' failure to act on climate change. They used the Convention (right to life, health) as their legal basis. They argued that inaction on climate violates children's right to survival and development. The petition was a direct application of the General Principles to a real-world crisis.
> **Key takeaway**: The Convention is not abstract theory. It can be used as a legal weapon against governments that fail to protect children.

### Framework B: The Gap Between Principle and Reality (Part 2)

The book's central insight: rights in principle mean nothing if they're not enforced in practice. Part 2 takes each cluster of rights and shows both what the Convention says and what actually happens.

**Core themes examined:** Life/health, equality, participation, identity, safe place, protection from harm, bodily integrity, armed violence, criminal justice, privacy, minority rights, education, play, freedom of thought, protest.

> **Case: Autumn Peltier, The Water Warrior** (Part 2, "Life, Dignity, and Health"): As a young Anishinaabe girl in Canada, Autumn learned that over 61 Indigenous communities couldn't drink their water — some for over 20 years. Canada is one of the world's wealthiest nations. But regulations don't apply on First Nations reserves. Autumn spoke at the UN World Water Day at age 13 and became a water protector for her community.
> **Key takeaway**: Even in wealthy countries with strong laws, marginalized communities get excluded. Rights exist on paper. The reality depends on who has power.

### Framework C: The Know-Skill-Act Cycle (Part 3)

The activist method: three overlapping circles.

- **Know:** Knowledge of your rights, your situation, and the system
- **Skill:** Research, public speaking, organizing, digital security, legal literacy
- **Act:** From writing a letter to leading a campaign to filing a legal complaint

> **Case: The Glasgow Girls** (Part 2, "Safe Place"): Six teenage girls at Drumchapel High School organized a petition when their classmate Agnesa was seized in a dawn raid and threatened with deportation. They grew the campaign, brought it to the Scottish Parliament, and won — not just for Agnesa but eventually changed UK national law to end child detention in immigration centers.
> **Key takeaway**: The cycle works: they knew Agnesa's rights, they built organizing skills, and they acted. The result was legal change that went far beyond their original goal.
