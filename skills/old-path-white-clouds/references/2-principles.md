# 2 — Core Buddhist Teachings

> Source: "Old Path White Clouds" by Thich Nhat Hanh

## The Four Noble Truths

1. **Dukkha**: Suffering exists — birth, aging, sickness, death, separation from what we love, being with what we dislike, not getting what we want.
2. **Samudaya**: Suffering has a cause — craving, attachment, ignorance.
3. **Nirodha**: Suffering can end — nirvana, the cessation of craving.
4. **Magga**: The path to the end of suffering — the Eightfold Path.

## The Eightfold Path

Right View | Right Thought | Right Speech | Right Action | Right Livelihood | Right Effort | Right Mindfulness | Right Concentration

## Dependent Co-Arising

All phenomena arise in dependence on causes and conditions. "This is, because that is. This is not, because that is not. This ceases, because that ceases."

> **Case: The First Sermon** (Chapter 22): After his enlightenment, the Buddha gave his first sermon at Deer Park to five ascetics. He taught the Middle Way — avoiding extremes of luxury and self-mortification. He taught the Four Noble Truths. Kondanna, the first of the five, became the first disciple.
> **Key takeaway**: The Buddha's first teaching was not complex philosophy. It was simple: there is suffering, there is a cause, there is an end, there is a path. This remains the core of Buddhist practice.

> **Case: The Tangerine of Mindfulness** (Chapter 19): The Buddha taught mindfulness using a tangerine. "When you eat a tangerine, be fully present. Taste each segment as if it is the only thing in the universe. When you are truly present, the tangerine becomes miraculous."
> **Key takeaway**: Mindfulness is not just for meditation — it's for eating, walking, talking, living. Every ordinary activity is an opportunity to be fully present.
