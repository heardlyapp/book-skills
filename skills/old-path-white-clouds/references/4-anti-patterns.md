# 4 — The Roots of Suffering

> Source: "Old Path White Clouds" by Thich Nhat Hanh

## The Three Poisons

The Buddha identified three root causes of suffering: **Craving** (grasping for pleasure, existence, non-existence), **Aversion** (anger, hatred, fear), and **Ignorance** (not seeing reality as it is).

## Anti-Pattern: The Extremes

The Buddha taught the Middle Way — avoiding the extremes of sensual indulgence and severe asceticism.

> **Case: The Six Years of Asceticism** (Chapters 13-18): Before enlightenment, Siddhartha practiced extreme self-mortification. He ate so little he could touch his stomach and feel his spine. He nearly died. He realized: starving the body does not free the mind. This led to the Middle Way.
> **Key takeaway**: You don't need to punish yourself to grow spiritually. The path is balance, not extremes. Self-care is not a distraction from practice — it's part of it.

> **Case: Mara's Temptation** (Chapter 19): During the night of enlightenment, Mara — the embodiment of temptation — attacked the Buddha with desire, fear, and doubt. The Buddha did not fight. He touched the earth. "I am here, on this earth, with all beings. I am not separate." Mara fled.
> **Key takeaway**: The way to overcome inner demons is not to fight them, but to ground yourself in reality. Touch the earth. Connect with what's real. The demons cannot survive there.
