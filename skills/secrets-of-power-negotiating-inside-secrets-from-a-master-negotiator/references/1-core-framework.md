## The Core Gaming Engine: Gambits Framework

The essence of Power Negotiating is treating negotiation as a strategic game played by defined rules. Roger Dawson calls these moves "Gambits" — borrowing from chess terminology for strategic moves that carry risk but yield advantage when executed correctly. The entire book is structured around three phases of Gambits.

### Phase 1: Beginning Gambits (Set the Range)

**Ask for More Than You Expect to Get (Chapter 1):** The cardinal rule. Your opening should be your Maximum Plausible Position (MPP) — the most you can ask for while still having the other side see some plausibility. Henry Kissinger said, "Effectiveness at the conference table depends upon overstating one's demands." Five reasons: you might get it, it gives negotiating room, it raises perceived value, it prevents deadlock, and it creates a climate where the other side feels they won.

**Bracketing (Chapter 1):** If you expect to end up splitting the difference (and in Western culture, you usually will), start equidistant from your goal on the opposite side of their opening. The car dealer asks $15,000, you want $13,000 — offer $11,000. The 1982 Mexico loan negotiation: U.S. asked for $100M negotiating fee, Mexico offered $0. They settled at $50M.

**Never Say Yes to the First Offer (Chapter 2):** Saying yes immediately triggers two automatic thoughts: "I could have done better" and "Something must be wrong." Even if the offer is better than expected, never jump at it.

**Flinch (Chapter 3):** React with visible shock and surprise to any proposal. Most people are visual — what they see overrides what they hear. A flinch often triggers an immediate concession. A speaker friend flinched at a $1,500 offer and got $2,500 — $1,000 more in 15 seconds.

**Reluctant Seller/Buyer (Chapter 5):** If you're selling, act reluctant to part with it. When Donald Trump needed to sell the St. Moritz hotel desperately, he played Reluctant Seller and got $160M from Alan Bond — double what he'd paid. If you're buying, act uninterested. The other side typically gives away half their negotiating range just because you use this.

**Vise Technique (Chapter 6):** Simply say, "You'll have to do better than that" — then shut up. A client used this by scrawling it on a bid and got $14,000 off without any further negotiation ($420,000/hour profit rate).

> **Case: The Beijing Pedishaw** (Chapter 1): When Roger Dawson was in Beijing, pedishaw drivers gathered around to advise the lucky driver how much to charge the American. They agreed on $50. Dawson eventually gave him $1 — more than a day's wages — and the driver was very happy. The initial demand was extreme ($50 vs actual value ~$0.50), but Dawson let it set the range and negotiated down.
> **Key takeaway**: The initial demand in any negotiation is an opening position, not a statement of value.

> **Case: John Broadfoot's 23 Paragraphs** (Chapter 1): An attorney representing a buyer of real estate tested the "Ask for More" rule by dreaming up 23 paragraphs of requests, some ridiculous. The seller took strong objection to only one sentence in one paragraph. Broadfoot held out for two days, then reluctantly conceded. He got 22.5 out of 23 requests — and the seller still felt he'd won.
> **Key takeaway**: Ask for far more than you expect. You'll be surprised how much sticks. The seller's ego satisfaction at "winning" one point made the entire deal feel fair to them.

### Phase 2: Middle Gambits (Maintain Momentum)

**Higher Authority (Chapter 7):** Always maintain that you must check with someone else (a vague entity like "the committee" or "the board," never a specific person). This applies pressure without confrontation. The author created "Plaza Properties" as a vague entity to handle tenant complaints. When tenants asked for carpet replacement, Dawson would say "I don't think I can get the owners to do that."

**Declining Value of Services (Chapter 8):** Any concession you make loses value rapidly in the other person's mind after you've made it. Always ask for a reciprocal concession immediately.

**Never Offer to Split the Difference (Chapter 9):** Let the other person suggest it. Then you can reluctantly agree to their proposal, making them feel they won. By splitting twice or thrice, you shift the split from 50/50 to 75/25 in your favor.

**Trade-Off (Chapter 13):** Every time you're asked for a concession, say "If I can do that for you, what can you do for me?" This stops the grinding-away process. A Fortune 50 sales VP called this "the most valuable lesson I've ever learned in any seminar."

### Phase 3: Ending Gambits (Close While They Feel They Won)

**Good Guy/Bad Guy (Chapter 14):** One side plays tough, the other reasonable. Used since Charles Dickens' Great Expectations. Dawson used it with a landlord: "I agree with you, but the board of directors will tell me to close the office." The landlord actually asked Dawson to negotiate for him with his own board.

**Nibbling (Chapter 15):** After agreement, ask for a little more. A person's mind reinforces decisions just made — they're most vulnerable right after saying yes. Dawson's daughter Julia got a Europe trip, then $1,200 spending money, then new luggage, each asked separately.

**Withdrawing an Offer (Chapter 17):** When the other side keeps grinding, pretend to withdraw your last concession. "We were talking $1.72 yesterday, but we can't even sell it for that anymore — $1.73 is the lowest." Instantly, the buyer forgets $1.71 and wants $1.72.

**Positioning for Easy Acceptance (Chapter 18):** Make a small concession at the very last moment. The size doesn't matter — it's the timing. A tiny extra sweetener lets the other person tell themselves they traded off, not surrendered.

**Always Congratulate the Other Side (Chapter 33):** However poorly they did, congratulate them. After the Falklands War, the British admiral invited the Argentine admiral to dine and congratulated him on a "splendid campaign." This ensures they leave feeling they won and want to deal with you again.

> **Case: The Magazine Salesman** (Chapter 2): Dawson negotiated a magazine ad from $2,000 to $800, then used Higher Authority to claim his "board of directors" wouldn't approve. He got it down to $500. But because the salesman said yes too quickly every time, Dawson still felt cheated — he wondered how much lower he could have gone. Years later the salesman attended Dawson's seminar and thanked him for explaining why jumping at deals was hurting him.
> **Key takeaway**: Never say yes to the first (or second, or third) offer. A fast yes triggers buyer's regret in the other party — even when they got a great deal.
