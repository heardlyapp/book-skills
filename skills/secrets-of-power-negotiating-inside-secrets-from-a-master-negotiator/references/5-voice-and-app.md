## Author Voice + Power Dynamics + Application

### Roger Dawson's Voice and Style

Roger Dawson writes with the direct, no-nonsense tone of a veteran seminar leader who has trained thousands of executives worldwide. His style is anecdotal and personal — every concept comes with a real story from his career as a real estate company president, seminar leader, or international negotiator. Key characteristics:

- **Conversational directness**: "I'm sure you can see why..." "Let me prove this to you..." "Here's the point..."
- **Skeptical of theory, committed to practice**: He dismisses the "two people with one orange" story as unrealistic and instead teaches Power Negotiating as a tactical game.
- **Cross-cultural humility**: Having taught in Kuwait, Nigeria, China, New Zealand, and Iceland, he acknowledges cultural differences without stereotyping.
- **Self-aware about gender use**: He openly addresses the challenge of gendered language and chooses to use "he" with apologies rather than create awkward text.
- **Key phrases**: "Power Negotiators know..." (identifies insiders), "Look out for..." (warns about gambits), "Here's the counter..." (provides immediately actionable counters).

### The 11 Types of Power (Chapters 53-63)

Understanding power is critical to knowing who has leverage. Dawson identifies 11 distinct sources:

| Power Type | Source | How to Use | How to Counter |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Legitimate** | Title, position, rank | Use your official capacity | Question whether they truly have authority |
| **Reward** | Ability to give benefits | Offer bonuses, referrals, future business | Don't be swayed by promises of future favors |
| **Coercive** | Ability to punish | Subtle pressure (not threats) | Don't show fear; have alternatives |
| **Reverent** | Association with revered figures | Use testimonials, endorsements | Question relevance to current deal |
| **Charismatic** | Personal magnetism | Build rapport, use charm | Separate likability from deal terms |
| **Expertise** | Knowledge, credentials | Demonstrate mastery | Ask detailed questions to test depth |
| **Situation** | Circumstantial advantage | Control venue, timing | Change the venue or timing |
| **Information** | Having better data | Prepare more than they do | Gather your own intel |
| **Risk Sharing** | Spreading risk across parties | Offer partnerships, guarantees | Analyze true shared risk vs shifted risk |
| **Confusion** | Using complexity to advantage | Simplify for them; don't get lost | Take time to understand fully |
| **Communicating Options** | Having alternatives | Show them you have other choices | Develop your own alternatives |

> **Case: The Signal Hill Apartment Complex** (Chapter 32): Dawson wanted to buy 18 four-unit buildings on Signal Hill for $1.2M — $600K below the $1.8M asking price. The real estate agent presented the offer to all principals plus their attorney, CPA, and broker. They stormed out insulted. But on their way out, they said they wouldn't go below $1.5M. The agent felt devastated. Dawson saw the win: in 5 minutes, the seller dropped from $1.8M to $1.5M — a $300K concession. The agent had lost perspective because he couldn't separate personalities from issues.
> **Key takeaway**: Concentrate on the movement of concessions, not the emotional drama. The $1.5M counter was a win, not a failure.

### Cross-Cultural Negotiation (Chapters 44-47)

Americans are direct communicators (low-context culture). Non-Americans often use high-context communication where what's unsaid matters more.

**American negotiating style**: Direct, resist outrageous initial demands, likely to negotiate alone, uncomfortable with emotional displays, expect short-term profits, unlikely to speak foreign languages, uncomfortable with silence.

**British**: Polite, indirect, value "fair play" rhetoric. Don't mistake politeness for weakness.

**French**: Strongly logical, argumentative, highly confrontational. They enjoy intellectual combat — don't take it personally.

**German**: Thorough, methodical, hierarchical. Decisions take time. They value expertise and preparation.

**Asian (Japanese/Chinese)**: Silence is a negotiation weapon. They use it on Americans who hate silence. High-context — "yes" may mean "I hear you," not agreement. Face-saving is paramount.

**Russian**: Combative, patient, willing to walk away. They'll use emotional outbursts as tactics. Stay calm.

**Middle Eastern**: Relationship-first negotiators. Business happens after trust and personal connection are established. Haggling is expected — your MPP should be much higher.

### Application Scenarios

**Salary Negotiation**: Ask for more than you expect (MPP). Use Higher Authority: "Let me check with my family." Get them to name the range first. Never say yes to the first offer. Taper your demands.

**Vendor Negotiation**: Play Reluctant Buyer. Flinch at their first price. Use Vise: "You'll have to do better than that." If they ask for something, Trade-Off: "If I can do that for you, what can you do for me?"

**Real Estate**: Play Reluctant Buyer or Seller. Use the 80/20 rule — 80% of movement happens in last 20% of time before closing. Write the contract yourself. Get everything in writing.

**Conflict Resolution**: First contain the situation. Let the angry person vent. Look for the hurt behind the anger. Ask "What exactly do you want?" Focus on mutual interests, not positions. Use the Power Negotiator's Creed.

**Mediation/Arbitration**: Mediation (facilitate) vs Arbitration (impose). Mediator must be neutral. Process: initial joint meeting → first private meeting → second private meeting → resolution. The NYPD hostage negotiation model: contain, communicate, earn trust, offer minor concessions, let time work for you.

> **Case: The NYPD Hostage Negotiation Model** (Chapter 36): After the disastrous Attica Prison riot (39 killed) and Munich Olympic massacre (11 athletes killed), the NYPD developed a new crisis response. Key principles: (1) Time is on your side — every passing hour brings peaceful resolution closer. (2) Don't lie to hostage-takers — it destroys future credibility. (3) Let them vent, then channel anger into reasoned demands. (4) Small concessions for small reciprocal moves. (5) Bring in trusted third parties only when needed. Results: 98% of child hostages released unharmed, and "Police Assisted Suicides" now account for 25% of police shootings of hostage-takers (indicating improved non-lethal resolution).
> **Key takeaway**: Hostage negotiation principles translate directly to everyday conflict: isolate the situation, don't lie, let them vent, use time as pressure, and offer small trade-offs to build momentum toward resolution.
