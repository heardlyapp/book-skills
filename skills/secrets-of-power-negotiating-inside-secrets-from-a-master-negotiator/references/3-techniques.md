## Tactical Techniques and Pressure Points

### Gambits for Getting to Yes

**1. The Trade-Off Gambit (Chapter 13):** Any time the other side asks for a concession, say "If I can do that for you, what can you do for me?" This stops the grinding-away process where they keep asking for more and more. Jack Wilson used this when a TV studio asked to borrow his camera operator for a day. He said "If I do that for you, what will you do for me?" They offered to waive future overtime charges — thousands of dollars he'd never have thought to ask for.

**2. Splitting the Difference (Chapter 9):** Never offer to split it yourself. Get the other side to suggest it. If they say "Why don't we split the difference at $82,000?" act surprised: "So you'd come up to $82,000? Let me take that to my partners." Then come back the next day: "They were tough — we're still $2,000 apart. Surely we're not going to let it fall apart over $2,000?" Often they'll split again. Dawson used this with a bank holding a blanket encumbrance: he got them to split four times, from $30,000 down to $28,250.

**3. Nibbling (Chapter 15):** After initial agreement, ask for a little extra. It works because the mind reinforces decisions just made. The racetrack study: gamblers before placing bets were uptight and anxious; after placing them, they wanted to double down. Dawson's daughter Julia used this masterfully: first closed him on the Europe trip, then asked for $1,200 spending money showing "recommended amount," then nibbled for new luggage at the last minute.

**4. Tapering Concessions (Chapter 16):** Start with a reasonable concession, then make each subsequent one smaller. If you have $1,000 to give away: first $400, then $300, then $200, then $100. By reducing the size, you subliminally communicate you've reached your limit. The wrong way: equal-sized concessions ($250 each) just trains them to keep pushing; or a big final concession ($600 then $400) creates hostility when you refuse the next $100.

**5. Withdrawing an Offer (Chapter 17):** When someone grinds you down, pretend to withdraw. A cabin seller said "$20,000." Buyer played hardball. Next day the seller came back: "The committee decided they won't sell for less than $24,000." The buyer panicked and was thrilled to get back to $20,000. Applied in sales: "I know we were talking about waiving installation, but my manager says at this price we just can't do that."

> **Case: The Horse Trailer Negotiation** (Chapter 13): A student of Dawson's was buying a horse trailer listed at $5,000. Just before writing the check, the seller said "That includes the feed bags, doesn't it?" The student (instinctively using the Trade-Off counter) said "If I give you the feed bags, what will you do for me?" The seller said "I'll throw in the hay fork and halter too." This simple Trade-Off at the last moment got him hundreds of dollars in extras he'd never have asked for.
> **Key takeaway**: Nibbling and Trade-Off work together. When someone nibbles, don't say yes — trade off.

### The Four Pressure Points

**1. Time Pressure (Chapter 37):** Under time pressure, people become flexible. Pareto's 80/20 rule: 80% of concessions happen in last 20% of time. Never reveal your deadline. If they know you have a 6pm flight, tell them you have a 9pm backup too. When both sides face the same deadline, the side with more options (walk-away power) can use time pressure; the weaker side should negotiate early.

**2. Information Power (Chapter 38):** Gather intelligence aggressively before negotiating. Six rules: (1) Don't be afraid to admit you don't know, (2) Ask the question directly, (3) Use open-ended questions (What/Why/When/How/Where/Who), (4) Location matters for asking, (5) Ask other people not in the negotiation, (6) Ask for reasons. Governments spend $44B/year on intelligence gathering — your negotiation deserves real preparation too.

**3. Walk-Away Power (Chapter 39):** Your strongest position is being willing to walk away. Develop alternatives before negotiating. If you have other options, you have power. If you don't, you're negotiating from desperation. Donald Trump shelved his $100M Television City project when he couldn't get the right tax concessions — he always looks at the deal as it stands now, ignoring sunk costs.

**4. The Fait Accompli (Chapter 42):** Presenting the other side with an already-accomplished action. If you're a subcontractor, you might install the equipment before the paperwork is complete, making it harder for the client to renegotiate. Risky but effective.

### Handling Negotiation Crises

**Set-Aside Gambit (Chapter 10):** When you hit an impasse on one issue, set it aside. "I understand that's important. Let's set that aside for a minute and talk about the other issues." Resolving minor issues first creates momentum that makes big issues easier.

**Changing Dynamics for Stalemates (Chapter 11):** If negotiations stall, change one element: change the people, the venue, remove an irritating team member, ease the tension with small talk, explore financing changes, or suggest sharing risk.

**Bringing in a Third Party (Chapter 12):** Only way to resolve a true deadlock is a mediator (facilitates a solution) or arbitrator (imposes a solution). The third party must be perceived as neutral. If they aren't, have them make a small early concession to the other side to establish neutrality.

> **Case: The Montana Real Estate Agent** (Chapter 37): An agent listed a $600K home at 6% commission ($36,000). When the seller balked at the fee, she said "If you have to come down much on price, we'll work with you on the commission." Months later, just before closing, the seller came back: "I've decided $5,000 would be a fair commission." She'd left a detail to "we can work that out later" — under time pressure, it became a huge problem. Had she tied up the commission in the listing agreement, this wouldn't have happened.
> **Key takeaway**: Never leave anything to "we can work it out later." Unresolved details become leverage under time pressure. Tie everything up in writing upfront.
