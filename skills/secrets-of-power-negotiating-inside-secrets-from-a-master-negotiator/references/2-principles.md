## Design Principles of Power Negotiating

### 1. Avoid Confrontational Negotiation

The first few moments of a negotiation set its entire climate. Don't argue — arguing intensifies the other person's desire to prove themselves right. Use the **Feel, Felt, Found** formula instead:

- **Feel**: "I understand exactly how you feel about that."
- **Felt**: "Many other people have felt exactly the same way."
- **Found**: "But what we have always found is..."

When the other side says "Your price is way too high," don't push back. "I understand how you feel. Many others have felt the same when they first hear our price. What they've always found is that we offer the best value in the marketplace."

> **Case: Attorneys as Confrontational Negotiators** (Chapter 4): Dawson conducted a seminar for 50 medical malpractice attorneys. Despite their reluctance to attend, they got into a negotiation workshop. The attorneys were so confrontational that most started with vicious threats and became more abusive. Dawson had to stop the exercise — arguing only intensified each side's desire to prove the other wrong.
> **Key takeaway**: Confrontation escalates hostility. The Feel/Felt/Found formula diffuses competitive spirit and opens the door to collaboration.

### 2. Get the Other Side to Commit First (Chapter 26)

You're almost always better off having the other side state their position first. Reasons: (1) their offer may be better than you expected, (2) it gives you information, (3) it lets you bracket their proposal.

**Brian Epstein's $140M mistake**: When United Artists wanted the Beatles to make their first movie, the producer was reluctant to name a price. He offered $25,000 upfront and asked Epstein what profit percentage he thought was fair. Instead of playing Reluctant Buyer, Epstein's ego made him state "7.5%." The movie became a worldwide hit — that single tactical error cost the Beatles millions.

When you must state first, modify their expectations first: "It would have to be a giveaway price," or "We've already turned down an offer for $10,000."

### 3. Act Dumb Is Smart (Chapter 27)

Acting as if you know less than everybody else diffuses the other side's competitive spirit. Most people want to help those they see as less informed. The TV detective Columbo (Peter Falk) was the perfect model — shabby raincoat, mental fog, chewing on a cigar butt. His helpless demeanor made murderers almost want him to solve the case.

Practical applications: Ask for definitions of words. Ask them to explain something again. Say "I don't know — what do you think?" This makes the other side stop fighting you and start helping you.

> **Case: Columbo's Method** (Chapter 27): The TV show Columbo featured a detective who stumbled through investigations in an old raincoat, confused and forgetful. In reality, his success was directly attributable to how smart he was — by acting dumb. His demeanor was so disarming that suspects came close to wanting him to solve his cases. The egotistical negotiator who "doesn't need time to think" or "doesn't have to check with anyone" loses this advantage.
> **Key takeaway**: Acting dumb retains options — time to think, deferring to authority, pleading for concessions. Smart looks like a fast decision-maker, but that closes doors.

### 4. Don't Let the Other Side Write the Contract (Chapter 28)

Whoever writes the contract has a tremendous advantage. They'll think of half a dozen things that didn't come up during verbal negotiations and write them to their advantage. Even a one-paragraph counter-offer can be slanted. If the listing agent writes it, they add "additional deposit upon acceptance." If the selling agent writes it, they add "additional $5,000 added to the seller-carried note."

### 5. People Believe What They See in Writing (Chapter 31)

The printed word has extraordinary power. A sign on a road next to a golf course that said "Delaware Closed" caused drivers to stop and ask "How long is it going to be closed for?" Hotels get 97% compliance with checkout times simply by posting a sign on the back of the door.

Always present proposals in writing. If you're a salesperson announcing a price increase, show them the memo. Use presentation binders and professional proposals.

> **Case: The $49,000 Bid** (Chapter 31): After a fire damaged Dawson's California home, three contractors bid on repairs. Two scrawled handwritten bids around $24,000. The third prepared a comprehensive computer-generated bid at $49,000 — more than double. Dawson accepted the higher bid because "the power of the printed word was so great that I just didn't trust the handwritten bids."
> **Key takeaway**: Presentation quality and written documentation directly drive perceived value. A well-presented higher bid can beat a sloppy low bid.

### 6. Concentrate on the Issues, Not the Personalities (Chapter 32)

The only thing that affects the outcome of a negotiation is the movement of concessions across the table — like the ball in tennis. Emotional reactions to the other side's behavior are distractions. A real estate agent once presented Dawson's $1.2M offer on an $1.8M property. The sellers stormed out insulted — but in five minutes they'd dropped to $1.5M. The agent felt terrible; Dawson saw a $300K win.

### 7. The 80/20 Rule of Negotiation (Chapter 37)

80% of concessions occur in the last 20% of available time. People become flexible under time pressure. Never reveal your deadline. The Vietnamese negotiator in the Paris peace talks rented a villa for 2.5 years while American negotiators rented a hotel room weekly — putting incremental time pressure on the U.S. side.

### 8. Information Power (Chapter 38)

Gather information aggressively. Six rules: (1) Admit you don't know, (2) Ask the question, (3) Ask open-ended questions (What/Why/When/How/Where/Who), (4) Location matters, (5) Ask other people, (6) Ask for reasons, not just data.

> **Case: The Franklin Stove Complaint** (Chapter 38): An elderly couple complained a Franklin stove had blackened their walls and burned a hole in their carpet. Everyone assumed an expensive settlement. Dawson drove out and asked, "Exactly what do you think we should do?" The husband just wanted a scatter rug to cover the hole. Dawson got them a rug, they signed a release, and the VP praised his "masterful negotiating." He'd simply asked what nobody had the courage to ask.
> **Key takeaway**: Most negotiators are too afraid to ask direct questions. The answer is often much smaller than you fear. Asking "What do you want?" is the simplest powerful move in negotiation.
