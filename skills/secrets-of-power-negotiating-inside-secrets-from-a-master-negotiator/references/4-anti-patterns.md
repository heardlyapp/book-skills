## Anti-Patterns: What NOT to Do + Recognizing Manipulation

### Unethical Gambits Used Against You

**1. The Decoy (Chapter 19):** The other side raises a phony issue so they can trade it off for something real. A buyer claimed he needed custom gears in 90 days (impossible 120-day lead time), creating a crisis. Only when the seller was frantic did the buyer reveal the solution: waive the $22,000 engineering fee and pay air freight. Six months later, the seller learned the buyer always works 6 months out — the 90-day deadline was a complete fabrication.

**Defense:** Stay focused. Isolate the objection: "Is that the only thing that's bothering you?" If it's a genuine deal-breaker, explore solutions. If it's a Decoy, they'll pivot when pressed.

**2. The Red Herring (Chapter 20):** Similar to Decoy but even more deceptive — the other side creates a demand they plan to withdraw, then trades that withdrawal for a real concession. The North Koreans during the Korean War armistice talks: they agreed to neutral-country negotiators, chose Poland and Czechoslovakia, then suggested the Soviet Union as their third. The international outcry was predictable. After months of debate, they "graciously" withdrew the Soviet demand — in exchange for the U.S. waiving the restriction on rebuilding airstrips.

**Defense:** Keep your eye on real issues. Don't let them link the Red Herring to a concession you're reluctant to make. Stay focused on what matters.

> **Case: John Portman's Peachtree Hotel** (Chapter 19): Dawson negotiated a room at the Peachtree Hotel in Atlanta. He used the Decoy — pretending the primary issue was the bed size (twin vs queen) rather than the price. The desk clerk said only twin rooms were available. Dawson said he wouldn't accept a twin. "If I do that for you [settle for a twin], what will you do for me?" The manager offered half price ($37.50 instead of $75). Then they "found" a queen room. Dawson got a $135 room for $37.50.
> **Key takeaway**: The Decoy can work in your favor too — but recognize when it's used against you. Any sudden "crisis" issue (ship date, bed size, delivery terms) may be a distraction from the real negotiation.

**3. The Deliberate Mistake (Chapter 22):** The seller "accidentally" under-prices or omits a feature. If the buyer jumps on it hoping to get a steal, they become emotionally invested and sloppy. Then the seller "discovers" the mistake and shames the buyer into paying full price.

**Defense:** Never try to get away with obvious errors. Point them out: "I assume you're not charging me for X because you want me to decide now?"

**4. The Default (Chapter 23):** Unilateral assumption that benefits the party making it. A company sends payment deducting 2.5% saying "All our other vendors discount for early payment." Or a salesperson writes "Because I haven't heard, I'll ship the deluxe model unless I hear within 10 days." Preys on busy or lazy people.

**Defense:** Call it out immediately. "That's not what we agreed. Please remit the full balance."

**5. Escalation (Chapter 24):** Raising demands after both sides have reached agreement. A franchise seller agreed to a price, then "heard voices" telling him he wasn't getting enough. The next morning he demanded another $500,000 — and got it. The corporation paid rather than cancel the press conference.

**Defense:** (1) Tie up all details up front in writing. (2) Get large deposits. (3) Build personal relationships — it's harder to be ruthless with friends. (4) If they escalate, escalate back: "We've been having second thoughts too — our price has gone up."

> **Case: The Rancher's Integrity** (Chapter 24): A Florida rancher shook hands on a deal to sell his land. That day, the Orlando Sentinel broke the news that Walt Disney was secretly buying land for Walt Disney World. The rancher could have held out for millions more. But his sense of integrity stopped him. Similarly, Henry Hollis sold the Palmer House to Conrad Hilton after shaking hands on $19,385,000 — and refused higher offers. Hilton called him "a master in the greatest traditions of American business."
> **Key takeaway**: Integrity in negotiation is a long-term asset. The rancher and Hollis gained reputations worth far more than the extra millions. Contrast with the franchise seller who extorted $500K — would anyone trust him again?

**6. Planted Information (Chapter 25):** Information you "discover" surreptitiously carries more weight than information given directly. A salesman presenting to a board of directors saw a note: "Universal's price is $762,000." He cut his price by $58,000. The note was planted — but he couldn't complain because he obtained it surreptitiously.

**Defense:** Any time you're negotiating based on information the other side "let you discover," be skeptical. Verify independently.

### Common Traps to Avoid

**7. Going in with Your Best Offer Up Front:** This gives you no room, raises no perceived value, creates no win feeling for the other side, and often deadlocks the negotiation.

**8. Revealing Your Deadline:** If the other side knows you're under time pressure, they'll delay negotiations until the last 20% of your available time and extract maximum concessions.

**9. Confrontational Opening:** Arguing up front makes the other side defend their position. Use Feel/Felt/Found instead.

**10. Letting Ego Drive:** Smart is dumb and dumb is smart. The fast-talking "expert" who has all the answers loses negotiating flexibility. The person who asks "I don't understand — could you explain that again?" retains all options.

**11. Making Concessions Without Trading Off:** This teaches the other side that asking for more works. Always ask for something in return.

**12. Leaving Details Unresolved:** "We can work that out later" is a promise of future trouble. The Montana real estate agent lost $31,000 of her commission because she didn't tie up a vague "we'll work with you" statement.

### The Power Negotiator's Creed

The most important thought you can have in any negotiation: **"What can I give them that would not take away from my position, but may be of value to them?"**

This shifts the game from zero-sum to creative collaboration. Instead of fighting over a fixed pie, you look for ways to expand it — the orange story (one wants juice, the other wants rind) comes to life when you genuinely seek mutual benefit.
