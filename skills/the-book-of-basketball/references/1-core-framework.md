# Core Framework — The Book of Basketball

## The Premise

Bill Simmons, "The Sports Guy," spent his childhood inside Boston Garden thanks to his father's $4 season ticket purchase (proceeds from an income tax refund, after his mother vetoed buying a motorcycle). He witnessed Larry Bird's entire career from five rows behind the visitors' bench. This book is his attempt to capture "that noise" — the memories, stories, and arguments that make NBA fandom great — before it fades.

## The Secret (Chapter 1)

Isiah Thomas revealed it by a Vegas topless pool: **"The secret of basketball is that it's not about basketball."** Winning comes from chemistry, unselfishness, knowing roles, and sacrificing stats.

**The Dantley trade:** In 1989, the Pistons traded Adrian Dantley (a scorer) for Mark Aguirre because Dantley wouldn't accept Rodman taking his crunch-time minutes. "It was a people trade, not a basketball trade." Result: back-to-back championships.

**The '89 Pistons:** First team in history to win a title without a single player averaging 20 points. Isiah: "We got 12 guys who are totally committed to winning. Every night we found a different person to win it for us."

**The framework:** (1) Build around one great leader. (2) Surround with 1-2 elite sidekicks who accept hierarchy. (3) Fill with role players who don't threaten the culture. (4) Stay healthy and catch breaks.

**The Bill Russell quote:** "Star players have an enormous responsibility beyond their statistics — the responsibility to pick their team up and carry it. I always thought that the most important measure of how good a game I'd played was how much better I'd made my teammates play."

## The Hall of Fame Pyramid (Chapters 6-11)

Simmons ranks the top 96 NBA players in a 6-tier pyramid structure:

**Pantheon (6 players):** Michael Jordan, Bill Russell, Kareem Abdul-Jabbar, Magic Johnson, Larry Bird, Wilt Chamberlain. These are the untouchable greats — the players who define the sport.

**Level 4 (24-7):** The near-Pantheon greats who peaked for sustained periods. Includes: Tim Duncan (arguably belongs in Pantheon — Simmons acknowledges this), Hakeem Olajuwon, Shaquille O'Neal, Oscar Robertson, Jerry West, Elgin Baylor, Moses Malone, Julius Erving, Kobe Bryant, Kevin Garnett, Karl Malone, David Robinson, Charles Barkley, John Havlicek, Dirk Nowitzki, Bob Pettit, Kevin McHale, Isiah Thomas, Bob Cousy, Dwyane Wade, Allen Iverson, Patrick Ewing, Rick Barry, Scottie Pippen.

**Level 3 (48-25):** Multiple All-Stars, Hall of Famers, players who carried teams. Includes: Dominique Wilkins, Steve Nash, Jason Kidd, Gary Payton, James Worthy, Dennis Rodman, Reggie Miller, Walt Frazier, Willis Reed, Clyde Drexler, George Gervin, Pete Maravich, Bill Walton, Dave Cowens, Wes Unseld.

**Level 2 (72-49) and Level 1 (96-73):** Solid Hall of Famers, stars who were great but not transcendent. Deep cuts and role players who made an impact.

## The What-If Game (Chapter 4)

Simmons explores the pivotal what-ifs of NBA history:

**Len Bias dying of a cocaine overdose (1986):** Two days after being drafted by Boston. Would have extended Bird's career, likely won more titles. "When Lenny Bias overdosed two days after the 1986 draft, Bird lost the young teammate who would have extended his career."

**Portland drafting Sam Bowie over Michael Jordan (1984):** "Portland landed Greg Oden when they had 5.3 percent odds of getting the first pick." But the real killer was passing on Jordan for Bowie because they already had Clyde Drexler. "Portland could have had Jordan and Drexler."

**The coin flip (1966):** A literal coin flip determined whether the Celtics or the Bulls would win the #1 pick. The Bulls won it and took Elvin Hayes. What if Hayes had landed in Boston?

**Suns vs. Celtics Game 5, 1976 Finals:** The triple-overtime classic. Simmons slept through the first two OTs as a 6-year-old, waking up just in time for Havlicek's bank shot.

## Russell vs. Wilt (Chapter 2)

The defining rivalry of basketball's early era. Russell: 11 titles in 13 years, the greatest winner in sports history. Wilt: 2 titles, 100-point game, 50.4 PPG season, but his teams underachieved until he accepted a lesser role.

Simmons argues Russell's 11 > Wilt's stats. "Russell still ritually puked before big games in his 13th season." The ultimate teammate vs. the ultimate individual.

## Most Valuable Chapter (Chapter 5)

Simmons deconstructs MVP voting history — the snubs, the biases, the "narrative" wins. Key insight: MVP voting penalizes players on great teams (voter fatigue) and overvalues players on surprisingly good teams. Derrick Rose won in 2011 because his narrative was better than LeBron's.

## The Wine Cellar (Chapter 13)

Simmons' favorite NBA moments — the games, plays, and memories that define his fandom:

**Bird's 60-point game vs. Atlanta (1985):** "There were four times when the Hawks subs jumped up in delight, fell on top of each other in disbelief, or slapped palms."

**Bird-Dominique duel, Game 7, 1988 Eastern Semis:** "Nique drops 47 but Bird explodes for 20 in the final quarter, saving the game."

**Larry's steal from Isiah (Game 5, 1987 Eastern Finals):** "The loudest I ever heard the Garden in my life."

**Magic's baby skyhook (Game 4, 1987 Finals):** "Spine-crushing."

**Isiah's 25-point quarter on a sprained ankle (Game 6, 1988 Finals):** "Pantheon-level stuff."

**Bird's near-miss (Game 4, 1987 Finals):** "That moon shot soaring through the air on a direct line — I would have bet my life it was going in. It didn't go in. It couldn't have been closer."
