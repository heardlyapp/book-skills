# Key Principles — The Book of Basketball

## I. The Secret: It's Not About Basketball

Isiah Thomas by the Wynn's topless pool: "The secret of basketball is that it's not about basketball." Winning comes from chemistry, sacrifice, and knowing your role — not from stats, talent, or money. The '89 Pistons had no 20-PPG scorer and won back-to-back titles. The '08 Celtics went from worst to first because of Garnett's Ubuntu culture. The Spurs won 70% for two decades by drafting high-character players who "have gotten over themselves."

## II. Chemistry Beats Talent (Every Time)

"The 2008 Lakers were 3-to-1 favorites over Boston and lost the Finals." Blew a 24-point lead in Game 4. Simmons: "Talent-wise, the Lakers were better. Team-wise, the Celtics were better." The '75 Warriors imploded because Rick Barry was a prick. The '86 Lakers lost to Houston because Kareem wouldn't defer and Magic wouldn't assert. "Year after year, at least one contender fell short for reasons that had little or nothing to do with basketball."

## III. The Pyramid Measures Legacy, Not Peak

Simmons' 6-tier ranking system considers: peak performance, longevity, playoff success, impact on winning, and "basketball immortality." Jordan at #1 (6-0 Finals, 6 MVPs, defined an era). Russell at #2 (11 rings, greatest winner ever). Kareem at #3 (most career points, 6 MVPs, 6 rings). Magic at #4, Bird at #5, Wilt at #6. The ranking is designed to spark arguments — "this book is supposed to start arguments."

## IV. The What-If Game Proves How Random History Is

Len Bias dies = Celtics dynasty ends. Portland drafts Jordan = Celtics never have Bird vs Magic rivalry? The 1966 coin flip determines whether Elvin Hayes goes to Boston. "You need luck in the lottery, luck with young players, luck with trades, luck with everything." Simmons' father nearly bought a motorcycle instead of Celtics tickets — the entire book rests on a coin flip of a decision.

## V. Greatness Fades from Memory — You Must Fight It

Goldman: "The greatest struggle an athlete undergoes is the battle for our memories." Havlicek was 3rd in scoring, 2nd in minutes, top 20 all-time — and fans under 30 can't spell his name. Simmons wrote the book because "the depreciation of sports memories bothers me more than I ever thought it would."

## VI. Statistics Can't Measure Greatness

"You can only play five players at a time. Those players can only play 240 minutes. How those players coexist, how they make each other better, how they accept their portion of that 240-minute pie — that's basketball." Amar'e Stoudemire scored 22-25 PPG but "doesn't make anyone else better, doesn't create shots for anyone else." Duncan had "subpar-for-him numbers" yet carried San Antonio for two decades. "Where's the statistic for that?"

## VII. Being a Fan Is a Privilege, Not a Right

Simmons grew up inside the Garden — $4/ticket, first class seats, watching Bird's every move. His father renewed season tickets through two decades of losing because "you can't walk away from the potential of more Bailey's moments." The book was "capturing that noise" before it fades. "That's the funny thing about noise: eventually it stops."
