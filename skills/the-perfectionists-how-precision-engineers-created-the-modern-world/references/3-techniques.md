# Techniques: Time, Navigation, and Modern Precision

> Source: The Perfectionists, Chapters 6-8

## Harrison's Chronometer

John Harrison spent decades building a clock accurate enough to keep time at sea — essential for solving the longitude problem. The British government offered a £20,000 prize. Harrison's H4 chronometer was the solution: a clock that lost only 5 seconds over 81 days at sea on a voyage to Jamaica.

> **Case: The Longitude Prize** (Chapter 8): Parliament offered £20,000 for a method to determine longitude at sea — one of the biggest rewards in scientific history. Harrison's H4 worked perfectly, but the Board of Longitude refused to pay for years. It took decades of bureaucratic battles for him to receive the prize.

> **Key takeaway**: The most precise machine of its era was a clock. Time and position are the same problem — you can't know where you are without knowing what time it is.

## GPS and Relativity

GPS satellites carry atomic clocks accurate to nanoseconds. Without relativistic corrections, GPS would drift by 10 km per day. The correction is 38 microseconds per day — a direct application of Einstein's theories in everyday life.

> **Case: The Relativity Correction** (Chapter 8): Special relativity: fast-moving clocks run slower. General relativity: weaker gravity means clocks run faster. For GPS satellites, the net is +38 microseconds/day. Every GPS device applies this correction. It's the most widespread use of Einstein's relativity.

From microscopes to microchips, the drive for smaller features has driven precision to the atomic level.
