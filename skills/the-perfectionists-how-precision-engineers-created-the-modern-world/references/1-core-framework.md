# Core Framework: The Precision Chain

> Source: The Perfectionists, Prologue - Chapter 2

## John Wilkinson's Cannon Borer

The Industrial Revolution began with a hole. John Wilkinson's cannon boring machine could drill a cylinder accurate to 1/16 inch — unheard of precision for 1775. This single invention made James Watt's steam engine possible, which in turn powered the entire Industrial Revolution.

> **Case: The Boulton & Watt Cylinder** (Chapter 1): Watt couldn't make his steam engine work because existing cylinders were too inaccurate — steam leaked past the piston, wasting energy. Wilkinson solved this by adapting his cannon borer to bore steam engine cylinders. The first successful engine was installed at Wilkinson's own ironworks. Without Wilkinson, Watt's engine would have remained a theoretical curiosity.

> **Case: The Royal Navy's Standard** (Chapter 1): Wilkinson's boring machine was originally designed for cannons. The Royal Navy needed consistent bore diameters so cannonballs would fit. Before Wilkinson, each cannon was essentially unique, requiring hand-fitted ammunition.

## Maudslay's Micrometer

Henry Maudslay created the first precision measuring tool — the micrometer — allowing measurements to 1/1,000 of an inch. He also standardized screw threads, making interchangeable parts possible.

> **Key takeaway**: Better measurement enables better manufacturing. The micrometer made the Industrial Revolution possible by allowing engineers to measure what they made.
