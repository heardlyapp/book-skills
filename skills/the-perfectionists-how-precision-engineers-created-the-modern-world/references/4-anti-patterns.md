# Anti-Patterns: The Perils of Perfectionism

> Source: The Perfectionists, Chapters 9-10

## Perfectionism as Paralysis

The pursuit of precision can become an obsession that prevents completion. Some of history's greatest precision engineers were difficult, obsessive people who sacrificed relationships and health for ever-smaller tolerances.

> **Case: The Obsessive Engineer** (Chapter 9): Winchester profiles engineers who spent decades chasing impossibly small tolerances. Their work advanced human knowledge, but often at great personal cost. The drive for perfection can become a trap that prevents shipping anything at all.

> **Key takeaway**: More precision is not always better. Diminishing returns apply. Knowing when to stop is as important as knowing how to measure.

## Mistaking Precision for Truth

A measurement can be precise (consistent) without being accurate (correct). An engineer can measure to 1/10,000 inch but be measuring the wrong thing.

> **Case: The Hubble Space Telescope** (throughout): The Hubble mirror was polished to incredible precision — one of the most precisely shaped objects ever made. But it was the wrong shape. Precision without accuracy is useless. The fix required a subsequent servicing mission.

## Equipoise — Balance

The book ends with a call for balance. The Japanese concept of wabi-sabi — finding beauty in imperfection — is the necessary counterweight to the engineer's obsession with accuracy.

> **Key takeaway**: Precision is a tool, not a goal. The goal is to make something that works.
