## The Jaynes Framework: Bicameral Mind → Breakdown → Consciousness

Jaynes' framework is a three-stage history of the human mind: Stage 1 — Bicameral (pre-conscious, voice-guided), Stage 2 — Breakdown (the voices fade, consciousness emerges), Stage 3 — Modern (consciousness is dominant, vestiges remain).

**The Key Insight**: Subjective consciousness is a historical development, not a biological given. It arose in specific times and places as a response to specific pressures.

> **Case: The Iliad's Mindless Warriors** (Chapter 3): In the Iliad, warriors do not make decisions. Agamemnon does not "decide" to take Briseis. He is driven by the goddess. Achilles does not "decide" to withdraw. Hera prompts him. There is no introspection, no deliberation, no inner conflict as we understand it. The characters act — but they do not "think about" acting.
> **Key takeaway**: The Iliad represents a mind without subjectivity. The characters are agents without interiority — and this is not poetic convention, Jaynes argues, but a reflection of how the bicameral mind worked.

> **Case: The Mesopotamian Theocracy** (Chapter 7): In the earliest Mesopotamian city-states, the king claimed to receive instructions directly from the gods. The laws of Hammurabi were given by the god Shamash. The statues of gods were physically present in temples — they were fed, clothed, and consulted. The entire society was organized around the voices of gods.
> **Key takeaway**: The bicameral society was a theocracy because the voices of gods were real — they were the voice of the king's right brain that he and his subjects experienced as divine authority.
