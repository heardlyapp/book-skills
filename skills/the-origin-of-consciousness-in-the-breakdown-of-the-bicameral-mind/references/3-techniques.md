## Practical Techniques

### 1. The Consciousness Audit

Track moments of true consciousness vs. autopilot. Jaynes argues most behavior is non-conscious. Notice when you are actually introspecting — and when you are just reacting.

### 2. The Voice Identification

When you hear an inner voice (intuition, guidance, the "should" voice), ask: "Who is speaking?" Jaynes would say: your right hemisphere, disguised as authority.

> **Case: The Schizophrenic Voice** (Chapter 2): Jaynes notes that schizophrenics hear voices that are often critical, commanding, and external — exactly what the bicameral mind would have experienced as the voice of a god. The difference: modern minds do not have a framework to interpret these voices as divine.
> **Key takeaway**: The voices are not the pathology — the inability to integrate them is. Schizophrenia may be a bicameral vestige in a conscious world.

### 3. The Hypnosis Experiment

Jaynes argues hypnosis is a bicameral vestige — the voice of the hypnotist replaces the voice of the god. The subject obeys without internal resistance.

### 4. The Poetry Test

Write spontaneously without planning. Jaynes would say the best poetry comes from the bicameral mind — from the voice of the muse, not from conscious crafting.

> **Case: The Muse in Action** (Vestiges): Jaynes points to poets who describe their work as "dictated" by an external source. John Milton dictated Paradise Lost to his daughters. William Blake claimed to write from visions. The bicameral mind never fully disappeared — it just became poetry.
> **Key takeaway**: Creative inspiration — the sense that ideas come from outside — is a vestige of the bicameral mind.
