# Reference: Zero Marginal Cost

## Overview
The zero marginal cost society is Rifkin's most provocative concept. As technology advances, the cost of producing additional goods approaches zero.

## Case: Digital Goods
Music, movies, software, and books can be reproduced at almost zero cost. The music industry was transformed by file sharing. The publishing industry is being transformed by ebooks. Information wants to be free.

## Case: Physical Goods
The trend extends to physical goods through 3D printing, autonomous manufacturing, and renewable energy. Solar panels generate electricity at near-zero marginal cost after installation. 3D printers can produce objects at minimal cost.

## Case: The Collaborative Commons
When marginal costs approach zero, the market economy gives way to the collaborative commons. People share rather than own, produce rather than consume, and participate rather than purchase. Wikipedia, open source software, and Airbnb are early examples.

## Case: The Prosumer
The prosumer is both producer and consumer. In the collaborative commons, individuals generate their own solar power, share their cars, and produce their own content. The line between producer and consumer blurs.

## Case: The Abundance Paradox
As goods become abundant (zero or near-zero cost), society faces a paradox: the economy that depends on scarcity cannot function in an era of abundance. This is the fundamental challenge of the zero marginal cost society.

This paradox requires rethinking capitalism itself.
