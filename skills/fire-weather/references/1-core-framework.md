# The Fort McMurray Fire

> Source: Fire Weather, Part One (Chapters 1-4), Part Two (Chapters 5-12)

## Core Framework

### The Timeline

| Day | Event |
|-----|-------|
| May 1, 2016 | Small fire starts 5 miles from Fort McMurray |
| May 2 | Fire grows to 2,000 acres in one day |

| May 3, 12:00 PM | Fire jumps the Athabasca River |

> **Case: The Jump** (Chapters 3-4)
> The Athabasca River is Fort McMurray's natural firebreak — a wide, powerful river that should stop any fire. On May 3, 2016, the fire jumped it. Not with a single ember — with a wall of flame. The fire was so intense that it created its own wind, which carried burning embers across the river. The fire was now on both sides of the river, and the city had lost its only natural defense.
> **Key takeaway:** When a fire jumps a river the size of the Athabasca, the normal rules of fire behavior have been suspended. The fire was not just burning — it was creating conditions that had never been seen before.

> **Case: The Evacuation** (Chapters 5-6)
> 90,000 people were evacuated in a single day — the largest single-day fire evacuation in modern history. There was one highway out of town. The fire was on both sides of it. People drove through walls of flame on both sides of the road. Cars overheated. Gas stations burned. Cell towers failed. The evacuation worked — only two people died directly from the fire — but only barely. The traffic jam stretched for miles, and families were separated, children lost, pets abandoned.
> **Key takeaway:** The evacuation of Fort McMurray is a case study in both heroism and luck. It succeeded because the wind shifted at the right moment — and because the fire did not directly hit the highway at the worst possible time. Next time, we may not be so lucky.
