# Anti-Patterns — The Irony of Fort McMurray

> Source: Fire Weather, all chapters

## The Five Fatal Assumptions

### 1. The Oil Sands Paradox
**Error**: "Our industry drives the economy. We are building prosperity."
**Irony**: Fort McMurray existed to extract the oil whose combustion heats the planet — heating that created the conditions for the fire that destroyed the city. The fire burned down the house built on the fuel that caused the fire.

### 2. The Forest Fire Myth
**Error**: "Forest fires are natural. They're part of the ecosystem."
**Correction**: The Fort McMurray fire was not natural. It was a fire burning through a city, fueled by houses, cars, and industrial infrastructure. It released toxins unknown in natural fires.

### 3. The "It Can't Happen Here" Fallacy
**Error**: "Fort McMurray is in the boreal forest. It's wet. It's too far north."
**Correction**: It happened. The boreal forest, the wettest major ecosystem, burned like tinder.

### 4. The Firebreak Fallacy
**Error**: "The river will stop it."
**Correction**: The Athabasca River — a massive, powerful river — was jumped by the fire. Nothing would stop it.

### 5. The "We Can Fight It" Fallacy
**Error**: "We have trained firefighters. We have equipment. We can handle it."
**Correction**: They couldn't. The fire was beyond the capacity of any municipal fire department. The textbook was useless.

> **Case: The Firefighters' Helplessness** (Chapters 7, 11)
> Firefighters in Fort McMurray faced something they had never seen: a fire that could not be fought with standard methods. Hoses evaporated before reaching the flames. Bulldozers burned. Firefighters lost their own homes while trying to save others'. One firefighter described it: "Send a fire department to a fire like this — it's like sending plumbers to confront a bursting dam." The Blue Card Command system — the standard protocol — had no chapter for what they faced.
> **Key takeaway:** The Fort McMurray fire exposed the limits of human response to climate-amplified disasters. We have built our emergency services for the world of the past. That world no longer exists.

> **Case: The Carbon Footprint of the Disaster Itself** (Chapter 15)
> The Fort McMurray fire released 100 million tons of CO2 — equivalent to the annual emissions of a small industrialized country. The irony: much of that carbon came from burning the cars and houses of people who worked in the oil industry — the industry that produced the fuel that caused the warming that amplified the fire.
> **Key takeaway:** The fire was not just a natural disaster. It was a convergence of nature and industry. The boundaries between natural and man-made catastrophe have dissolved. We are living in a hybrid disaster.
