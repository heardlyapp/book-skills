# Fire Weather — How Fires Create Their Own Weather

> Source: Fire Weather, Part Two (Chapters 5-12), Part Three (Chapters 13-15)

## The Science

### The Pyrocumulus Effect

| Normal fire | Fire weather fire |
|-------------|------------------|
| Burns at 800-1,200°F | Burns at 2,000°F+ |
| Creates smoke plume | Creates thunderhead visible from space |
| Affects local air quality | Affects global atmosphere |
| Wind drives the fire | The fire creates its own wind |

> **Case: The Pyrocumulus Cloud** (Chapter 8)
> On May 3, the Fort McMurray fire generated a pyrocumulus cloud that towered 40,000 feet into the stratosphere. These clouds are typically found over erupting volcanoes. The fire's heat was so intense that it created its own weather system: the rising column of superheated air pulled in more oxygen, creating hurricane-force winds at ground level, and generated lightning that started new fires miles away. The fire was no longer just burning — it was creating thunderstorms.
> **Key takeaway:** When a fire generates pyrocumulus clouds, it has crossed a threshold. It is no longer a fire that responds to weather — it is a fire that IS weather. Traditional firefighting methods are useless against such a fire.

> **Case: The Fire Tornado** (Chapter 10)
> Firefighters and residents reported fire tornadoes — rotating columns of flame that ripped through neighborhoods. These are extreme manifestations of the pyrocumulus effect: the rising column of superheated air creates a vacuum, which spins into a tornado-like vortex. One bulldozer operator described it: "The fire was creating its own weather system. The wind would just switch on us, and the fire would pick — it was like it was choosing which house to burn."
> **Key takeaway:** Fire tornadoes are the ultimate expression of the fire's autonomy. The fire is not just burning — it is behaving intelligently, finding new fuel sources, creating new pathways. This is what Vaillant calls "the fire becoming a beast."
