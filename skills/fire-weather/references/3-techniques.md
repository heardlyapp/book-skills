# Climate Change and the Boreal Forest

> Source: Fire Weather, Part One, Chapter 2, Part Three, Chapter 15

## The Climate Connection

### The Boreal Carbon Bomb

| Ecosystem | Carbon stored |
|-----------|--------------|
| Boreal forest | 1.2 trillion tons — more than all tropical forests combined |
| Peatlands (boreal) | 500 billion tons — accumulated over millennia |
| Permafrost | 1.5 trillion tons — frozen organic matter |

> **Case: The Chinchaga Fire (1950) — A Preview** (Chapter 1)
> The Chinchaga Fire of 1950 burned 4 million acres across northern Alberta. It was the largest fire ever recorded in North America. Its smoke plume rose 40,000 feet, circled the Northern Hemisphere, and created lavender suns and blue moons — effects not seen since the eruption of Krakatoa in 1883. Carl Sagan studied the Chinchaga Fire to understand what nuclear winter might look like. The fire was a natural event, but it showed what the boreal forest could do when it burned at scale. Now, climate change has made Chinchaga-scale events the new normal.
> **Key takeaway:** The Chinchaga Fire was a freak event in 1950. Today, fires of that scale are happening every year in California, Australia, Canada, and Siberia. Climate change has normalized the abnormal.

> **Case: The Feedback Loop** (Chapter 15)
> The Fort McMurray Fire released 100 million tons of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere — from burning cars, houses, and forest. This carbon stays in the atmosphere, traps more heat, dries out more forests, and creates conditions for more megafires. This is the climate-fire feedback loop: more fire → more carbon → more warming → more fire. The fire itself becomes an engine of climate change.
> **Key takeaway:** The feedback loop is the most dangerous aspect of the new pyrosphere. Each megafire makes the next megafire more likely. We are not just fighting fires — we are fighting a self-reinforcing cycle that may be impossible to break at current emissions levels.
