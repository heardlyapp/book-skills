## Practical Techniques

### 1. The 4-Step Annual Financial Checkup

**Step 1**: Review your net worth — assets minus liabilities. Is it growing year over year?
**Step 2**: Review your saving rate — what percentage of income are you saving?
**Step 3**: Rebalance your portfolio — sell assets that have grown beyond your target allocation and buy those that have fallen.
**Step 4**: Check your fees — are you paying more than 0.20% in expense ratios? If so, switch to lower-cost funds.

### 2. The Emergency Fund Rule

Keep 3-6 months of essential expenses in a high-yield savings account or money market fund. This is not "wasted" money — it is insurance against having to sell investments at a loss during an emergency.

### 3. The Dollar-Cost Averaging Strategy

Invest a fixed amount at regular intervals — monthly, quarterly, or with every paycheck. This eliminates the need to time the market. When prices are low, your fixed amount buys more shares. When prices are high, it buys fewer. Over time, this reduces the average cost of your investments.

> **Case: The Steady Investor** (Chapter 4): Tobias compares two investors. One invests $12,000 once per year. The other invests $1,000 per month. Both invest $12,000 per year. The monthly investor benefits from dollar-cost averaging — buying at lower prices during market dips. Over 20 years, the monthly investor ends up with significantly more.

### 4. The Insurance Rule of Thumb

Buy term life insurance only — never whole life, universal life, or variable life. Term insurance is pure protection. The other types bundle insurance with investments — and the investment portion is almost always overpriced and underperforms.

> **Case: The Whole Life Trap** (Chapter 8): Tobias calculates that a 30-year-old buying term life insurance and investing the difference would have 3-5x more money at age 65 than someone who bought whole life insurance with the same premium.

### 5. The 4% Rule

A common rule of thumb for retirement withdrawals: in your first year of retirement, withdraw 4% of your portfolio. In subsequent years, adjust that amount for inflation. This withdrawal rate has historically allowed portfolios to last 30 years.
