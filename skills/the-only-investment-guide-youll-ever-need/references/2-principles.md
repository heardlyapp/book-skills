## 7 Principles of Personal Finance

### Principle 1: Spend Less Than You Earn

There is no substitute for this. No investment strategy can overcome a negative saving rate. The single most important financial decision you will make is how much you save, not what you invest in. Save at least 15-20% of your income from your first paycheck.

### Principle 2: Start Early

Compound interest is the eighth wonder of the world. A dollar invested at age 22 will grow to roughly $16 by age 65 at 7% annual return. The same dollar invested at age 32 will grow to only $8. Starting 10 years earlier doubles the final amount.

> **Case: The Barista vs The Banker** (Chapter 1): The barista who started investing $50/month at 22 ended up with more than the banker who started investing $500/month at 40. Starting early beats starting big.

### Principle 3: Use Tax-Advantaged Accounts

401k, IRA, and HSA accounts provide immediate tax deductions, tax-free growth, or both. Max them out before investing in taxable accounts. The tax savings alone can boost your effective returns by 30% or more over time.

### Principle 4: Invest in Low-Cost Index Funds

Most active fund managers underperform the market after fees. The average actively managed mutual fund charges 1.2%. The average index fund charges 0.05%. Over 30 years, that difference compounds to hundreds of thousands of dollars. Buy the whole market and hold.

> **Case: The Fund Manager vs The Market** (Chapter 3): Tobias shows that over any 10-year period, 80-90% of actively managed funds underperform their benchmark index. The few that outperform in one period rarely outperform in the next. Past performance does not predict future results.

### Principle 5: Diversify

Own a mix of US stocks, international stocks, and bonds. The specific allocation matters less than being diversified across asset classes. A 60/40 portfolio (60% stocks, 40% bonds) has historically provided steady returns with lower volatility than all-stock portfolios.

### Principle 6: Keep Costs Low

A 1% fee consumes roughly 25% of your investment returns over 30 years. A 2% fee consumes nearly 40%. Every basis point you pay in fees is a basis point you don't earn. This is the single factor most under the investor's control.

### Principle 7: Stay the Course

The market goes up and down. The only way to lose permanently is to sell when the market is down. History shows that markets recover from every crash. The investor who stays invested through downturns is rewarded. The investor who panics and sells locks in losses.
