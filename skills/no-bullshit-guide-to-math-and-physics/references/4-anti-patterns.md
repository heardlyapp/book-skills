## 6 Anti-Patterns of STEM Learning

### 1. "I'll Just Memorize the Formulas"

Memorization without understanding fails when problems require adaptation. Understand the derivation. Know where formulas come from and when they apply.

### 2. "I'll Read the Theory and That's Enough"

Reading is passive. Math and physics require active problem-solving. You have not learned it until you can solve problems without looking at the solution.

### 3. "I Need to Be Good at Math First"

Physics helps you understand math. Math helps you do physics. Learn them together — the synergy is powerful.

> **Case: The Physics That Made Calculus Click** (Preface): Savov notes that many students struggle with calculus until they see it applied in physics. The derivative as velocity makes sense when you are tracking a moving object. The integral as distance traveled makes sense when you sum velocity over time.
> **Key takeaway**: Abstract math concepts become concrete when applied to physical problems. Use physics to build math intuition.

### 4. "Skipping Steps Is Faster"

Skipping steps leads to mistakes. Work through each step methodically. Speed comes from fluency, not shortcuts.

### 5. "I'm Just Not a Math Person"

Math is a skill, not a talent. With consistent practice, anyone can learn it. The "I'm not a math person" belief is a self-fulfilling prophecy.

### 6. "The Answer Is All That Matters"

The process is more important than the answer. A correct answer with the wrong reasoning teaches nothing. A wrong answer with the right reasoning can be corrected.

> **Case: The Skipped Steps Mistake** (Chapter 4): A student solving 3(x+2) = 9 wrote: x+2 = 3, x = 1. Correct. They skipped the step: 3x+6=9, 3x=3, x=1. When the equation changed, they could not adapt.
> **Key takeaway**: Skipping steps hides logic. Show every step.
