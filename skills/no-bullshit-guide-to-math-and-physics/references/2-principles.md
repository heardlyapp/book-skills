## 7 Principles of STEM Self-Study

### 1. Foundation Before Advanced

Math builds linearly — algebra before trig, trig before calculus, calculus before physics. Skipping foundations leads to confusion.

### 2. Practice, Don't Just Read

Reading theory gives illusion of understanding. Real understanding comes from solving problems. Work through every example with pencil and paper.

### 3. Diagrams Are Essential

A picture can replace 100 equations. Draw free body diagrams for physics, graphs for functions, and vectors for problems involving direction.

> **Case: The Free Body Diagram** (Chapter 8): Savov always starts force problems with a free body diagram — identify all forces acting on an object, draw them as vectors, then apply F=ma. The diagram is not optional — it is the first step of the solution.
> **Key takeaway**: In physics, drawing the problem is half the solution. Never skip the diagram.

### 4. Units Don't Lie

If your answer has the wrong units, it is wrong — even if the number is correct. Checking units catches a large fraction of errors.

### 5. Equations Are Tools, Not Rules

Equations describe relationships. Understanding the concept behind the equation is more important than memorizing the formula.

### 6. Teach to Learn

If you can explain a concept to someone else, you understand it. If you cannot, you do not.

### 7. Consistency Beats Intensity

20 minutes daily > 5 hours on the weekend. Regular practice builds durable knowledge. Cramming produces fragile recall.

> **Case: The Chain Rule Practice** (Chapter 5): Savov shows the chain rule with multiple examples of increasing complexity: d/dx[sin(2x)] = 2cos(2x), d/dx[(x²+1)³] = 3(x²+1)²·2x. Each example builds on the previous. By the end, the pattern is clear.
> **Key takeaway**: Practice problems should progress from simple to complex. Each step should reinforce the pattern.
