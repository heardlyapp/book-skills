## The Savov Framework: Concise STEM Self-Study

Savov's framework is built for self-directed learners: each topic starts with a concise theory explanation, followed by worked examples, followed by problem sets. The book assumes the reader wants to understand the material, not just pass a test.

**The Approach**: "No bullshit" means the author skips the fluff and gets to the point. Each section is designed to be readable in one sitting, with enough depth to solve problems.

> **Case: The Worked Derivative Example** (Chapter 5): Savov walks through finding the derivative of f(x) = x³ + 2x² - 5x + 3 using the power rule. Step-by-step: f'(x) = 3x² + 4x - 5. He then shows the tangent line at x=1: slope = 3(1)² + 4(1) - 5 = 2. This connects the abstract rule to a concrete result.
> **Key takeaway**: The worked examples bridge theory and practice. Each example is designed to show not just the answer but the reasoning process.

> **Case: The Kinematics Problem** (Chapter 7): A car accelerates from rest at 4 m/s² for 5 seconds. How far does it travel? Given a=4, t=5, v₀=0. Using Δx = v₀t + ½at²: Δx = 0 + ½(4)(25) = 50 meters. Then: what is its final velocity? v = v₀ + at = 0 + 4(5) = 20 m/s.
> **Key takeaway**: Physics problems are systematic — identify the equation that connects knowns to unknowns, then solve.

The book covers roughly 3 years of high school + 1 year of university STEM in about 400 pages. The organization: math builds up through calculus, then physics applies that math to real-world problems. This integration is the book's strength — you see why calculus matters the moment you get to kinematics.
