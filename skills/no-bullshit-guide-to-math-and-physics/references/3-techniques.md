## Practical Techniques

### 1. The Isolation Method

When solving an equation, isolate the unknown step by step. Do not skip steps — each step is a chance to catch errors.

### 2. The Dimensional Analysis Check

Before plugging in numbers, check that the units work out. If the left side is meters and the right side is meters/second, something is wrong.

> **Case: The Unit Mismatch** (Chapter 7): A student solving a kinematics problem got Δx = ½(2)(5) = 5. But the answer was meters? Actually: a=2 m/s², t=5 s. ½·(2 m/s²)·(5 s)² = ½·2·25 = 25 m. Skipping the units hid the fact that t needed to be squared.
> **Key takeaway**: Units force you to use equations correctly. Algebra mistakes are harder to catch without unit discipline.

### 3. The Problem Decomposition

Break complex problems into smaller steps. Find the intermediate unknown that connects what you know to what you need.

### 4. The Answer Check

Does the answer make physical sense? A car cannot travel 1000 meters in 5 seconds (that's 720 km/h). A negative mass is impossible. Sanity-check your results.


> **Case: The Multi-Step Kinematics** (Chapter 7): A ball is thrown upward at 20 m/s. How high does it go? Step 1: At max height, v=0. Step 2: Use v² = v₀² + 2aΔy. Step 3: 0 = 400 + 2(-9.8)Δy. Step 4: Δy = 400/19.6 ≈ 20.4 m. The problem is broken into identifying the key point (v=0 at top), choosing the equation, and solving.
> **Key takeaway**: Decomposition turns a confusing problem into a series of simple steps. Each step uses what you learned in the previous step.

### 5. The Table Method for Derivatives

For complex derivatives, make a table: function, outer derivative, inner derivative, chain. This organizes the chain rule application into manageable pieces.
