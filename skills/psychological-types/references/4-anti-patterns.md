# 4 — Anti-Patterns

> Source: "Psychological Types" by C.G. Jung

## Anti-Pattern 1: Type Fixation

**The mistake:** Treating your type as an identity rather than a description of your current tendencies. "I'm an INTJ" becomes a cage instead of a starting point.

## Anti-Pattern 2: One-Sided Development

**The mistake:** Overdeveloping your dominant function while neglecting your auxiliary and inferior functions. This creates an imbalanced personality.

## Anti-Pattern 3: Pathologizing Difference

**The mistake:** Thinking your type is "right" and other types are "wrong." Feeling types aren't less logical. Intuitives aren't less practical. They are different.

## Anti-Pattern 4: Ignoring the Shadow

**The mistake:** Denying the parts of yourself that don't fit your type identity. The shadow doesn't disappear because you ignore it. It grows and erupts.

> **Case: The Danger of Identification** (General): Jung warns against identifying too strongly with any psychological description. "The type is a tool, not a truth. It describes tendencies, not fixed laws." When people say "I can't do that because I'm an introvert" or "I'm a feeler so I'm not logical" — they've confused description with identity.
> **Key takeaway**: Type is descriptive, not prescriptive. It tells you where you start, not where you end. Don't use type as an excuse for avoiding growth.

## Anti-Pattern 5: Projection Without Withdrawal

**The mistake:** Seeing your inferior function in others and reacting emotionally, without recognizing it as your own unconscious material.
