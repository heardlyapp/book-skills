# Core Framework: The Westphalian System and Its Alternatives

Kissinger begins by identifying the foundation of modern international order: the Peace of Westphalia (1648), which established the principle of state sovereignty. Each nation has authority over its own territory; no external power should interfere in another state's internal affairs. This system, born in Europe, has become the global norm.

## The Three Elements of Order

1. **A defined set of rules** — Accepted principles governing international behavior
2. **A balance of power** — Preventing any one nation from dominating others
3. **Legitimacy** — The rules must be accepted as fair by participants

> **Case: The Congress of Vienna** (Introduction): After Napoleon's wars, the great powers of Europe assembled in Vienna in 1815 to create a new order. Led by Metternich and Castlereagh, they established a "Concert of Europe" — a system where great powers would meet regularly to manage crises. This system maintained peace in Europe for nearly a century. It worked because it combined power (great powers could enforce decisions) with legitimacy (all major states agreed to participate).
> **Key takeaway:** A durable order needs both power and legitimacy. Power without legitimacy creates resistance. Legitimacy without power is unenforceable.

## Different Regional Conceptions of Order

Kissinger argues that Europe, the Middle East, Asia, and America each have distinct concepts of world order. Europe emphasizes international law and multilateral institutions. The Middle East is shaped by Islamic conceptions of universal religious community. Asia, particularly China, views order through hierarchy and cultural influence. America sees itself as the defender of universal democratic values.

> **Case: China's Tribute System** (Chapter 5, "The Multiplicity of Asia"): For centuries, China's foreign policy was based on the tribute system — neighboring states would acknowledge China's cultural supremacy through ceremonial tributes, and China would grant them trading rights and protection. This was not a balance-of-power system but a hierarchical one based on cultural superiority. When European powers arrived demanding equal treatment, China could not comprehend a system of sovereign equals. The clash of these two worldviews shaped Asian geopolitics for centuries.
> **Key takeaway:** Different civilizations have fundamentally different concepts of international order. Understanding these differences is essential for effective diplomacy.


## The Challenge of Our Time

Kissinger argues that we are living through a period of unprecedented change in world order. The postwar system built by the United States is under strain. New powers are rising. Technology is creating new domains of conflict. And the international institutions designed for an earlier era are struggling to adapt. The question of our time: can we build a new order before the old one collapses entirely?

> **Case: The Cyber Domain** (Chapter 9, "Technology, Equilibrium, and Human Consciousness"): Kissinger analyzes how cyber warfare challenges traditional concepts of sovereignty. A cyber attack can cross borders instantly, anonymously, and without traditional military force. The attacker may be a state, a criminal group, or a lone actor. Existing international law has no framework for this. The Westphalian system assumes territorial control. Cyber knows no territory.
> **Key takeaway:** Technology is creating forms of conflict that our existing international frameworks cannot address. This is the most urgent challenge for world order in the 21st century.
