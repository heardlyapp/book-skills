# Core Framework: Separated Institutions Sharing Powers

## The Constitutional Design

The Founders invented the presidency at the Constitutional Convention of 1787. They debated whether to have a single or plural executive, how to select the president, and what powers to grant. The result was Article II: a single executive elected indirectly through the Electoral College, with powers that were deliberately vague compared to Congress's enumerated powers in Article I.

## Separated Institutions Sharing Powers

Jones' key insight is that the Founders did not create a system of separated powers in the way we usually think. They created separated institutions that SHARE powers. The president cannot make law without Congress. Congress cannot execute law without the president. The courts cannot act without either. All three branches must cooperate to govern.

> **Case: The Louisiana Purchase** (Chapter 2): President Jefferson faced a constitutional dilemma. The Constitution did not explicitly authorize the president to acquire territory. Jefferson believed in strict constitutional interpretation, but the opportunity to buy Louisiana was too good to pass up. He set aside his constitutional scruples and made the purchase, later securing congressional approval. This case illustrates how presidents expand power through practice.
> **Key takeaway:** The Constitution is a framework, not a straitjacket. Presidential power evolves through practice and necessity, not just constitutional text.

## The Perpetual Ordeal

The presidency is what Jones calls a perpetual ordeal. Presidents are held responsible for everything that happens in government — the economy, foreign policy, natural disasters — yet they have limited constitutional tools to address these challenges. The gap between responsibility and authority is the central tension of the office.

> **Case: President Truman and the Korean War** (Chapter 6): Truman committed US troops to Korea without congressional authorization, citing his authority as Commander in Chief. The Supreme Court later ruled that Truman's seizure of steel mills during the war was unconstitutional. The tension between presidential action in foreign affairs and constitutional constraints illustrates the perpetual ordeal.
> **Key takeaway:** Presidents push the boundaries of their power in times of crisis. The courts and Congress push back. This push-and-pull is the normal operation of the separated system.
The presidency is designed to be constrained. The Founders feared both executive tyranny and legislative supremacy. Their solution was to create an independent executive with limited powers, a powerful legislature with the purse and the power to declare war, and an independent judiciary with the power of judicial review. Each branch checks the others. No branch can act alone. This design is not a bug. It is the point.

The president has constitutional powers: veto legislation, command the military, appoint officials, negotiate treaties, grant pardons, and deliver the State of the Union. But each of these powers is checked. Vetoes can be overridden. Military commands require funding. Appointments require Senate confirmation. Treaties require Senate ratification. Pardons are the most unchecked power, but even they have limits.

Congress has powers of its own: the power of the purse, the power to declare war, the power to investigate, the power to confirm or reject appointments, the power to impeach and remove. These powers make Congress a co-equal branch, not a subordinate one. Presidents who ignore Congress find themselves unable to govern.
