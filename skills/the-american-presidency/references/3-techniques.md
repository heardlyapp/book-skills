# Techniques: How Presidents Govern

## Technique 1: Transition Management

The 11 weeks between election and inauguration are the most important period of a presidency. The president-elect must build a White House staff, select cabinet members, review policy options, and prepare a budget. A smooth transition sets the foundation for success. A chaotic transition wastes the first months of the term.

## Technique 2: Strategic Appointments

Cabinet secretaries and White House staff shape presidential outcomes. Presidents must balance competing considerations: policy expertise, political loyalty, demographic representation, and Senate confirmability. The most successful presidents appoint people who are both competent and loyal.

> **Case: The Bush 43 Transition and Cabinet** (Chapter 5): The 2000 election was decided by the Supreme Court, leaving Bush with a compressed transition. Despite this, his transition team prepared extensively during the campaign. The result was one of the most organized transitions in modern history. His cabinet included experienced figures like Colin Powell, Donald Rumsfeld, and Condoleezza Rice.
> **Key takeaway:** A well-planned transition, even in challenging circumstances, sets the stage for effective governance. Preparation during the campaign phase is critical.

## Technique 3: The Legislative Liaison

Presidents maintain a White House legislative liaison office to manage relations with Congress. The key is personal engagement: phone calls, White House meetings, and careful attention to congressional priorities. Presidents who ignore Congress find that Congress ignores them.

## Technique 4: Crisis Management

Crises define presidencies. The response to a crisis can make or break a president's reputation. Effective crisis management requires: rapid information gathering, clear communication, decisive action within constitutional bounds, and post-crisis accountability.

## Technique 5: The Budget as a Governing Tool

The federal budget is the most important legislative vehicle of any presidency. It sets priorities, allocates resources, and forces Congress to engage with the president's agenda. Mastery of the budget process is essential for presidential effectiveness.

> **Case: Reagan and the 1981 Tax Cut** (Chapter 4): Reagan entered office with a mandate for tax cuts and spending reductions. He focused his political capital on this single priority, working closely with congressional leaders and making strategic compromises. The result was one of the most significant legislative achievements of the modern presidency. His success came from clear priorities, strategic bargaining, and timely action.
> **Key takeaway:** Focus on a few clear priorities. Spend political capital strategically. Compromise on details without abandoning core principles.
The legislative liaison office is the nerve center of presidential-congressional relations. It tracks every bill, every vote, every congressional hearing. It manages the presidents relationships with committee chairs, party leaders, and rank-and-file members. An effective liaison office is worth more than a dozen executive orders.
The transition period is the moment when potential becomes reality. The decisions made in those 11 weeks shape the entire presidency. Staff appointments, policy priorities, budget proposals, and legislative strategy are all set during this period. A well-executed transition is the foundation of an effective presidency.
A chaotic transition wastes the first months of a presidency. Missed appointments, stalled policy reviews, and unclear lines of authority create an impression of incompetence that can last for years.
