# Anti-Patterns: What Makes Presidencies Fail

## ❌ The Imperial Presidency

The belief that the president has unlimited unilateral power is the most dangerous anti-pattern. Presidents who act without regard for Congress or the courts invite institutional backlash. The Nixon presidency is the classic example: expansive claims of executive power led to impeachment and resignation.

> **Case: Nixon and Watergate** (Chapter 7): Nixon claimed broad executive privilege to withhold evidence from Congress. He asserted inherent presidential power to authorize break-ins and wiretaps. The courts rejected these claims, and Congress moved toward impeachment. Nixon resigned before the full House could vote. His expansive claims of executive power destroyed his presidency.
> **Key takeaway:** Claims of unlimited presidential power are both unconstitutional and politically unsustainable. The separated system has multiple checks that will eventually be enforced.

## ❌ Going It Alone

Presidents who try to govern without Congress inevitably fail. Executive orders can substitute for legislation in limited circumstances, but they are fragile. A future president can reverse them with the stroke of a pen. Lasting policy changes require congressional legislation.

## ❌ Misreading the Mandate

Presidents who believe their election victory gives them a sweeping mandate to govern are often disappointed. Electoral coalitions are fragile and issue-specific. A president elected on one set of priorities cannot assume public support for unrelated initiatives.

## ❌ The Permanent Campaign

Modern presidents never stop campaigning. They raise money, give speeches, and court voters continuously. This permanent campaign distracts from governing and erodes public trust. The most effective presidents balance campaigning with the actual work of governance.

## ❌ Bureaucratic Isolation

Presidents who surround themselves with loyalists and ignore the permanent government lose the ability to implement policy. The bureaucracy has its own culture, expertise, and priorities. Presidents must engage with career officials, not just political appointees.

The Watergate scandal is the classic example of the imperial presidency trap. Nixon asserted broad executive privilege, claimed inherent presidential power to authorize surveillance, and treated Congress as an adversary rather than a partner. The result was impeachment proceedings and resignation. The case demonstrates that the system of separated powers has multiple checks that will eventually be enforced, even against a popular president.

The going it alone trap is common in modern presidencies. Presidents increasingly use executive orders to bypass Congress. But executive orders are fragile. They can be reversed by the next president. They are vulnerable to court challenges. And they often fail to achieve lasting policy change because they lack the stability of legislation. Presidents who rely too heavily on executive orders are building on sand.

> **Case: Trumans Steel Seizure** (Chapter 6): During the Korean War, Truman ordered the seizure of steel mills to prevent a strike that would disrupt war production. The Supreme Court ruled the seizure unconstitutional in Youngstown Sheet and Tube Co. v. Sawyer. The case established that even war powers have limits. The presidents inherent powers do not include the authority to seize private property without congressional authorization.
> **Key takeaway:** Even in national security emergencies, presidential power has constitutional limits. The courts will enforce those limits.
