# 4 — Anti-Patterns: What Presidencies to Avoid

## Anti-Pattern 1: The Imperial Presidency

The most dangerous pattern in presidential history is the belief that the president can — and should — act unilaterally, particularly in foreign and national security policy.

> Source: The American Presidency, Chapter 7

**Warning signs:**
- Claiming inherent or exclusive presidential authority without constitutional basis
- Circumventing congressional authorization for military action
- Asserting executive privilege to block legitimate inquiries
- Treating the other branches as obstacles rather than partners

**Historical examples:**

The Nixon presidency is the cautionary tale. The claim of executive privilege to withhold Oval Office tapes led to *United States v. Nixon* (1974) — a unanimous Supreme Court decision against the president. The tapes revealed abuse of power, and Nixon resigned rather than face impeachment.

The pattern often begins in foreign policy, where presidents have greater discretion. Overreach in foreign policy (secret wars, unauthorized surveillance, unchecked military commitments) eventually spills into domestic politics. Arthur Schlesinger Jr.'s term "imperial presidency" was coined during Nixon's time, but the impulse recurs. Bush 43's warrantless surveillance program and military tribunals for suspected terrorists both faced Supreme Court challenges (*Hamdan v. Rumsfeld*, 2006).

**Why it fails:**
- The Founders built a system of checks that eventually corrects overreach
- Judicial review provides a final backstop
- Congress has the power of the purse, investigation, and (ultimately) impeachment
- Public opinion eventually turns against perceived lawlessness

## Anti-Pattern 2: Overpromising on a Narrow Mandate

More than half of presidents since 1856 won by narrow margins. A plurality win with less than 50% of the popular vote does not authorize transformative change. Yet presidents repeatedly make this mistake.

> Source: The American Presidency, Chapter 3

**The failure pattern:**

1. **Narrow win** — The president enters with limited political capital
2. **Ambitious agenda** — Sweeping proposals are announced that require broad public support
3. **Congressional resistance** — The opposition party, often controlling one or both houses, blocks the initiative
4. **Failure** — The proposal dies, the president loses momentum, and the rest of the agenda suffers

**Examples:**
- **Carter's energy program**: Won with 50.1% of the vote in 1976 against an unpopular incumbent. Proposed a comprehensive energy package that Congress dismantled.
- **Clinton's health care plan**: Won with 43% in 1992 (a three-way race). Put national health care at the center of his agenda. It died in a Democratic Congress.
- **Bush 43's Social Security reform**: Won reelection with 51% in 2004. Proposed private accounts as a major reform. The plan never reached a vote in either house.

**The correct approach:**
- Read the election results honestly — what does the margin mean?
- Start with achievable priorities that build momentum
- Save transformative proposals for when political capital is higher (or don't attempt them)
- Understand that a close election is a license to govern, not a mandate to transform

## Anti-Pattern 3: Mistaking Campaigning for Governing

The permanent campaign is now a reality of presidential leadership. But treating governing as a continuous campaign is a recipe for failure.

> Source: The American Presidency, Chapter 4

**How they differ:**

| Campaign | Government |
|----------|------------|
| "Us versus them" | We need 60 votes |
| Day certain for decision | Indefinite timeline |
| Hail Mary passes possible | Slow, steady progress |
| One-ring circus | Thousand-ring circus |
| Victory is clear | "Victory" is ambiguous |

**Failure examples:**

Clinton's 1993 health care speech is a masterclass in campaign-mode thinking. In the State of the Union, he waved his pen and threatened to veto any bill that didn't guarantee universal coverage. He later admitted: "It was an unnecessary red flag to my opponents in Congress. Politics is about compromise and people expect Presidents to win, not posture for them."

**The correct approach:**
- Govern in campaign mode when building public support, but switch to legislative mode when working with Congress
- Know when to threaten a veto and when to signal openness to compromise
- Recognize that an all-or-nothing posture on complex legislation invites failure

## Anti-Pattern 4: Ignoring Congress

Some presidents have treated Congress as an inconvenience rather than a co-equal branch. This always backfires.

> Source: The American Presidency, Chapter 6

**Nixon's approach** is illustrative. With six years in Congress but no leadership experience, he preferred foreign policy and avoided regular contact with members. A House Republican said: "I pretty well concluded that there was almost no way to contact him except if you had a personal relationship." This distance made it easier for Congress to turn against him during Watergate.

**Carter's approach** was different but equally problematic. He was skeptical of the "politics of compromise" that defined Washington. His view: the president represents all the people and should do what's right, regardless of political bargains. The result was a famously difficult relationship with his own party's congressional leaders.

**The correct approach:**
- Invest in regular communication with congressional leaders of both parties
- Respect the institutional prerogatives of committees and leadership
- Understand that members of Congress face different constituencies and electoral pressures
- Build relationships before you need them

## Anti-Pattern 5: Overreaching in the Second Term

The second term carries unique risks. The Twenty-second Amendment creates a "lame duck" dynamic, and the temptation to pursue transformative change can lead to overreach.

> Source: The American Presidency, Chapters 4, 7

**The second-term failures:**

- **Nixon (1973–1974)**: A landslide reelection in 1972 was followed by a catastrophic second term — Watergate investigations, resignation of the vice president, and finally Nixon's own resignation.
- **Reagan (1985–1989)**: Iran-Contra scandal in 1986-87 severely damaged the administration, though Reagan's job approval recovered.
- **Clinton (1997–2001)**: The Lewinsky scandal led to impeachment in 1998, though the Senate acquitted and his approval ratings remained high.

**Why second terms are vulnerable:**
- Cabinet and staff turnover creates instability (Bush 43 set a record with eight changes at the start of his second term)
- The president is a known quantity — both successes and failures are on the record
- The opposition party plans aggressively for the next election
- Scandal or fatigue can set in

**The correct approach:**
- Use cabinet renewal strategically but carefully
- Focus on achievable legacy items rather than sweeping transformation
- Maintain engagement with Congress — lame duck status is partly self-fulfilling
- Understand that historical judgment will be shaped by second-term performance

## Anti-Pattern 6: Failing to Adapt to Split-Party Government

Split-party government has been the norm in the post-WWII era — eight of eleven presidents faced opposition-party control of one or both houses at some point. Presidents who cannot operate under these conditions are doomed to gridlock.

> Source: The American Presidency, Chapter 3

**Common mistakes:**
- Blaming the opposition party rather than working with them
- Over-relying on party-line votes when the party lacks a majority
- Going public too aggressively, alienating potential cross-party allies
- Refusing to compromise on principle, leading to legislative paralysis

**The correct approach:**
- Acknowledge that split-party government is constitutional, not pathological
- Major legislation can and does pass under split-party conditions (there's no significant difference in major law output between same-party and split-party congresses)
- Build relationships across the aisle
- Choose battles carefully — not every issue needs a confrontation

## Case Studies in Failure

### Case 1: Johnson and Vietnam
Lyndon Johnson was a master of domestic legislative leadership, passing the Great Society programs with historic majorities. Yet he applied the same approach to Vietnam — escalating commitment without congressional authorization, expecting that his influence and persistence would produce results. The failure destroyed his presidency.

### Case 2: Nixon and Watergate
Watergate is the ultimate example of the imperial presidency anti-pattern. The break-in was bad enough; the cover-up was catastrophic. Nixon's claim of executive privilege, his refusal to cooperate with investigators, and his eventual resignation taught the permanent lesson: the system checks presidents who place themselves above the law.

### Case 3: Clinton's Health Care Failure
The 1993–1994 health care reform effort is a textbook case of overreaching on a narrow mandate. Clinton had won with 43% of the vote, yet proposed the most sweeping social program since Medicare. The plan was developed in secret by a task force led by the First Lady. Congress was neither consulted nor included. The bill died without a floor vote in either house, and Democrats lost their congressional majorities in the 1994 midterms.

> Source: The American Presidency, Chapters 4, 6–7

## Key Takeaway

The history of the presidency is filled with failure patterns that recur across administrations and eras. The most reliable protection against these anti-patterns is a clear-eyed understanding of the separated system: presidents are not the government, their power depends on influence not command, and the system is designed to check those who forget these truths.
