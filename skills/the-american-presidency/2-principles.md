# 2 — Principles of Presidential Leadership

## Principle 1: Influence Over Command

The most fundamental principle of presidential leadership is that power flows from influence, not authority. The separated system is designed so that no single actor can command. Presidents who try to govern by fiat will be checked by Congress, the courts, the bureaucracy, or the public.

> Source: The American Presidency, Chapter 1

E. Pendleton Herring captured this perfectly: "We have created a position of great power but have made the full realization of that power dependent upon influence rather than legal authority."

**In practice this means:**
- A president's political capital is earned, not granted by the Constitution
- The veto is a negative tool, not a positive one — it stops things, it doesn't start them
- Executive orders can be reversed by successors; legislation and treaties require congressional concurrence
- Public approval is an asset that must be carefully managed and protected

## Principle 2: Agenda-Setting is the Core Power

The president's most significant constitutional advantage is the power to set the national agenda. Three clauses in Article II enable this:

1. The right to require written opinions from department heads (creating the basis for policy development)
2. The duty to give Congress information on the State of the Union
3. The power to recommend measures for congressional consideration

> Source: The American Presidency, Chapter 1

These provisions have evolved into the annual State of the Union address — a "national moment" that sets the policy agenda for the year. But agenda-setting is about more than one speech. It requires:

- **Selection**: Choosing which issues deserve national attention
- **Prioritization**: Deciding the order and intensity of focus
- **Framing**: Defining problems in ways that build support for solutions
- **Timing**: Knowing when to push and when to wait

Presidents who fail to set the agenda find others doing it for them. House Speakers like Newt Gingrich were able to dominate the policy conversation when President Clinton's political status was weak.

## Principle 3: Know Your Political Capital

Not all presidents are created equal in the political resources they bring to office. The circumstances of election, the state of the economy, the partisan composition of Congress, and the national mood all shape a president's available capital.

> Source: The American Presidency, Chapter 3

**Key metrics of political capital:**

- **Electoral margin**: More than half of all presidential elections since 1856 have been close. A narrow win (plurality or near-plurality) signals limited mandate.
- **Split-party government**: Eight of eleven post-WWII presidents faced opposition-party control of one or both houses at some point.
- **Coattails**: Presidential popularity that carries congressional candidates is rare. Most members of Congress win independently.
- **Job approval**: Tested hundreds of times per year, approval ratings fluctuate and constrain options.

The lesson is clear: presidents who overestimate their capital — proposing sweeping changes on the strength of a narrow victory — repeatedly fail. Carter's energy program, Clinton's health care plan, and Bush 43's Social Security reform all foundered on this miscalculation.

## Principle 4: Campaigning and Governing Are Different Games

A Reagan aide contrasted the two: "Campaigning is us versus them. That's not the same as governing. There aren't many Hail Mary passes in governing. It's chewing up five or ten yards at a time."

> Source: The American Presidency, Chapter 4

**Campaigning** is a "one-ring circus" — focused on the candidate, with a single day when a winner is declared. **Governing** is a "thousand-ring circus" — involving countless actors, interests, and institutions over an indefinite period.

Yet the two are increasingly intertwined. Modern presidents "go public" — campaigning for policy as aggressively as they campaigned for office. The permanent campaign is now a defining feature of presidential leadership, with pollsters and political consultants embedded in the White House.

The danger is treating governing like campaigning. Campaigning promises change; governing requires compromise. Presidents who cannot shift gears — from us-versus-them to we-need-60-votes — will alienate potential allies.

## Principle 5: Build for the Second Term While Managing the First

First and second terms are fundamentally different. The pressures of the first term center on staffing, learning, and establishing a track record. The second term offers more flexibility — but also carries a built-in "lame duck" dynamic that erodes influence.

> Source: The American Presidency, Chapter 4

**First-term realities:**
- Appointments must satisfy campaign workers, party factions, interest groups, and Congress
- The transition sets the tone — smooth transitions (Reagan, Bush 43) tend to produce effective starts
- Learning on the job is mandatory; previous Washington experience is rare

**Second-term realities:**
- Cabinet turnover is substantial (45% average for recent reelected presidents)
- The Twenty-second Amendment replaces uncertainty with certainty — the president cannot run again
- New candidates begin campaigning immediately, often from within the Senate
- Scandals are not inevitable, but "lame duck" perception is

Truman's experience illustrates the challenge: succeeding FDR with huge shoes to fill, working with a Republican Congress after 1946, and winning election in his own right in 1948 only to face the Korean War and steel seizure crisis. The perpetual ordeal is indeed perpetual.

## Principle 6: Adapt to Events

Events matter more than reforms. The greatest turning points in presidential history have been driven by external circumstances — the Great Depression, World War II, 9/11 — not by institutional changes.

> Source: The American Presidency, Chapter 7

**The lesson:** Presidents can shape events but they cannot choose them. Those who serve during crises (Truman, Johnson, Reagan, Bush 43) are ranked higher by historians, not because they were better presidents, but because the stakes were higher.

What presidents *can* control:
- How they frame the crisis and its meaning
- The speed and effectiveness of their response
- Whether they seek congressional authorization or act unilaterally
- How they communicate their decisions to the public

## Case Studies

### Case 1: Reagan's Agenda Dominance (1981)
Reagan entered office with a clear mandate (decisive electoral win) and a focused agenda (tax cuts, reduced government). His limited set of priorities concentrated political capital effectively. Facing a Democratic House, he still won passage of major tax and budget legislation by building a cross-party coalition and using his communication skills to rally public support.

### Case 2: Clinton's Recovery (1995–1996)
After losing Democratic majorities in 1994, Clinton's political capital was severely depleted. He adapted by triangulating — co-opting Republican issues like welfare reform and deficit reduction while defending popular Democratic programs. His successful standoff over the 1995 government shutdown restored his preeminence in agenda-setting and set up his 1996 reelection.

### Case 3: Bush 43's 9/11 Transformation
Bush entered office with a narrow, contested victory. The 9/11 attacks transformed his presidency overnight. His job approval soared to record levels, and he quickly secured bipartisan support for the war in Afghanistan and the Patriot Act. The lesson: crisis can dramatically expand presidential authority, but it cannot be manufactured or predicted.

> Source: The American Presidency, Chapters 3–4, 6

## Key Takeaway

Effective presidential leadership requires a clear-eyed understanding of one's place in the separated system. Influence is earned through strategic agenda-setting, realistic assessment of political capital, and adaptation to events. Presidents who mistake the office for the government — or commanding for leading — will be humbled by the system the Founders built.
