## 6 Anti-Patterns of Industrial-Age Thinking

### 1. "The Government Will Take Care of It"

The state's capacity to provide security, welfare, and infrastructure is declining. Assuming the state will solve problems is a risky bet.

### 2. "My Job Is Safe"

Lifetime employment was a product of industrial age. The information age favors flexible, portable skills. Job security is an illusion.

> **Case: The Factory Worker** (Chapter 6): The authors use the example of a steelworker whose entire community depended on one factory. When the factory closed, the community collapsed. The information age will create similar dislocations — but faster.
> **Key takeaway**: Dependence on a single employer, industry, or location is a risk. The sovereign individual diversifies their income streams.

### 3. "Democracy Will Fix It"

Democracy works within the nation-state framework. When the state's power declines, democracy's effectiveness declines with it. Voting is less powerful than exit.

### 4. "My Identity Is Fixed"

In the information age, identity becomes flexible. The sovereign individual can have multiple identities, multiple citizenships, multiple legal personas.

### 5. "Taxes Are Inevitable"

Taxes are increasingly optional for those who know how to organize their affairs. The state can only tax what it can see and compel.

### 6. "This Is Too Extreme"

What sounds extreme in 1997 sounds obvious in 2026. The predictions of The Sovereign Individual have been validated repeatedly. The only question is speed.

> **Case: The Welfare Trap** (Chapter 7): The authors argue that welfare state benefits create dependency that is hard to escape. Those who rely on state benefits are most vulnerable when the state's capacity to deliver declines.
> **Key takeaway**: Welfare is a trap disguised as a safety net. The sovereign individual builds their own safety net.
