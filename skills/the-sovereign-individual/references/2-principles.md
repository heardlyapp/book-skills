## 7 Principles of the Megapolitical Shift

### 1. Technology Drives History

Changes in the technology of violence and production cause the deepest transformations in human society. The microprocessor is the latest — and most powerful — such change.

### 2. The Nation-State Is an Industrial Product

The state as we know it — central government, standing army, welfare, mass taxation — is designed for the industrial age. It is poorly adapted to the information age.

### 3. You Can Vote with Your Feet (and Your Wealth)

The Internet makes exit possible. You can choose where to live, where to bank, where to incorporate, and even who to be. The sovereign individual takes advantage of this.

> **Case: The Crypto Exit** (Chapter 8, prophetic): The authors predict that digital currency will allow individuals to hold wealth outside the state's reach. They wrote this in 1997 — 12 years before Bitcoin. The prediction was considered absurd at the time.
> **Key takeaway**: The ability to exit the state's financial system is the foundation of sovereignty. Without economic exit, sovereignty is incomplete.

### 4. Violence Is Becoming Private

The state's monopoly on force is eroding. Private security, cyber warfare, and decentralized violence are growing.

### 5. Mass Democracy Is in Crisis

Democracy in the nation-state was a product of industrial mass society. When society fragments into networks, mass democracy becomes unstable.

### 6. The Transition Will Be Painful

The losers of the industrial-information transition will resist. Periods of megapolitical change are always violent.

> **Case: The Luddites (Part II)**: The authors compare the resistance to the information revolution to the Luddite rebellion against industrialization. The Luddites were not against technology — they were against the destruction of their way of life. The same dynamic will play out today. The losers will fight.
> **Key takeaway**: Megapolitical transitions are never peaceful. Those who lose power do not go quietly. Expect conflict.

### 7. The Sovereign Individual Is the Future

The human being adapted to the information age: self-sufficient, globally mobile, cryptographically secure, and free from mass institutions.
