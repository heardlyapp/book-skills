## Core Framework Quick Reference

1. **Megapolitics**: The study of large-scale historical change driven by transformations in the technology of violence and production. Each megapolitical shift creates new forms of social organization and destroys old ones.
2. **The Four Megapolitical Shifts**: Agricultural revolution (10,000 BCE) → Classical era → Industrial revolution (1750) → Information revolution (2000).
3. **The Information Revolution's Impact**: Will do to the nation-state what the printing press did to the Church — undermine its authority, make its monopoly on power obsolete, and empower individuals to bypass it.
4. **The Sovereign Individual**: The new human archetype — someone who controls their own security, their own economic life, and their own identity in the digital age. The sovereign individual is not subject to mass society.
5. **The End of Mass Democracy**: Democracy in the nation-state was a product of the industrial age. In the information age, politics will return to a more competitive, market-based model.
6. **Cryptocurrency Prediction (1997!)**: Davidson and Rees-Mogg predicted the rise of digital currencies that governments could not control — written 12 years before Bitcoin.

## Key Principles

1. Technology determines the structure of society. Changes in the technology of violence and production cause megapolitical shifts.
2. The nation-state is a product of the industrial age. Its power peaked in the 20th century and is now declining.
3. The sovereign individual will be the dominant social actor of the information age — not the citizen, not the worker, not the voter.
4. Taxation will become voluntary as the Internet makes it easy to move capital, identity, and even residence.
5. Cryptocurrencies will enable economic activity outside state control.
6. Violence will be privatized — the state's monopoly on force will erode.
7. The transition will be painful for those who depend on the old system and liberating for those who adapt.

## The Transition's Winners and Losers

**Winners**: Those who develop portable skills, embrace technology, reduce dependence on state services, and think globally.

**Losers**: Those who depend on welfare state benefits, work in non-portable jobs, trust traditional institutions, and rely on government-provided security.
