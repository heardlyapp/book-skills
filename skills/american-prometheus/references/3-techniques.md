# 3 — Post-War Politics and the H-Bomb

> Source: "American Prometheus" by Kai Bird and Martin J. Sherwin

## The Scientist as Statesman

After the war, Oppenheimer became the most powerful scientist in America — and then the most controversial. His opposition to the hydrogen bomb set him on a collision course with Edward Teller and Lewis Strauss.

> **Case: The Oppenheimer-Teller Split** (Part IV — The Postwar Years): "Edward Teller wanted to build the hydrogen bomb, a weapon a thousand times more powerful than the atomic bomb. Oppenheimer opposed it — not on technical grounds but on strategic and moral ones. He believed it was a weapon of genocide with no military utility. Teller never forgave him." The authors detail the growing rift.
> **Key takeaway**: The H-bomb debate was not just scientific — it was strategic and moral. Oppenheimer believed that nuclear weapons were already powerful enough. The H-bomb was a bridge too far. Teller disagreed. This disagreement, combined with personal ambition and political calculation, destroyed Oppenheimer's career.

> **Case: The AEC Years** (Part IV — Influence): "As chair of the AEC's General Advisory Committee, Oppenheimer was the most influential scientific voice in American nuclear policy. He shaped the debate on international control of atomic energy, nuclear testing, and the arms race. His influence was unmatched — and it made him enemies." The authors describe his peak influence.
> **Key takeaway**: Oppenheimer's influence was both a blessing and a curse. It allowed him to shape policy in ways he believed were right. But it also made him a target. His enemies — Teller, Strauss — were patient and strategically placed. They waited for the political moment to strike.
