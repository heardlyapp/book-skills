## Practical Techniques

### 1. The Swing Audit

Assess your team: are they in sync? If not, identify the person or process that is off. In rowing, the stroke rower sets the rhythm. Every team has a stroke — the person whose pace others follow.

### 2. The Shared Suffering Practice

Build team bonds through shared challenge — not trust falls or happy hours, but real difficulty that requires collective effort. Physical, mental, or professional. The bond comes from getting through it together.

> **Case: The Rain Practice** (Chapter 8): Ulbrickson did not cancel practice in bad weather. The worst conditions built the strongest team. Every man who showed up in the rain earned the trust of the others.
> **Key takeaway**: The best team-building happens organically through shared difficulty. Do not protect your team from hardship — guide them through it.

### 3. The Egoless Role

Find the role where you can contribute most — not the one that gives you the most visibility. The bow rower is the most powerful but least visible. The stroke rower sets the rhythm but gets no credit. True teamwork means finding your place and doing your job.

### 4. The Rhythm Find

When the team is struggling, stop and find the rhythm. Not faster — smoother. Speed comes from efficiency, not force. A team that is synchronized is faster than a team that is trying hard but out of sync.

> **Case: The Final 500 Meters** (Chapter 18): In the Olympic final, the American crew did not panic when the Germans led. They focused on their rhythm — stroke by stroke. In the last 500 meters, they found swing. The boat lifted. They passed the Germans. The rhythm — not the effort — won the race.
> **Key takeaway**: When the pressure is highest, do not try harder — get in rhythm. Speed follows synchrony.
