## 7 Principles of Teamwork

### 1. Ego Has No Place in a Boat

A rowing shell has no room for individual glory. If you are rowing for yourself, you are slowing down everyone else. The team must come first.

### 2. Suffering Builds Trust

The boys trained in freezing rain, before dawn, through pain and exhaustion. Shared suffering created bonds that lasted a lifetime. Comfort does not build teams — challenge does.

> **Case: The Winter Training** (Chapter 8): The UW crew trained through the Pacific Northwest winter — icy water, rain, darkness. They rowed until their hands bled. They vomited from exhaustion. No one quit. The suffering was not punishment — it was the forge that welded them together.
> **Key takeaway**: Teams that go through hard things together develop a bond that cannot be broken. Comfort is the enemy of deep trust.

### 3. The Right Coach Creates Conditions, Not Commands

Al Ulbrickson was demanding but fair. He selected the crew carefully, pushed them hard, and got out of their way when they were ready.

### 4. The Right Equipment Matters

Pocock's boats were works of art. He built each shell by hand, selecting cedar planks for their grain, steaming and bending them into shape. The boat was not a tool — it was a partner.

### 5. The Current Cannot Be Controlled

At the Olympics, the American crew drew the worst lane — choppy water, cross-currents. They did not complain. They adapted. External conditions cannot be controlled, but the response to them can.

> **Case: The Berlin Lane Draw** (Chapter 17): The Americans drew lane 6 — the roughest water on the course. The Germans had lane 3 — calm water. The Americans did not panic. They focused on their rhythm. In rough water, synchronization becomes even more important. The rough water made them stronger.
> **Key takeaway**: Adversity is not an excuse — it is a signal to focus on what you can control: your effort, your rhythm, your trust in each other.

### 6. The Italians Cheered

After the race, the Italian silver medalists — who had come from behind to finish third — rowed over to the Americans and raised their oars in salute. Even competitors recognized greatness.

### 7. The Victory Was Not the Point

The boys remembered the training, the mornings on the water, the bond between them — more than the gold medal. The journey, not the destination, was the true reward.
