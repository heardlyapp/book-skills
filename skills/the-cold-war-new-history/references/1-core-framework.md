# Core Framework: Containment and the Division of Europe

## The Origins

The Cold War began before WWII ended. Stalin's determination to control Eastern Europe clashed with Roosevelt and Churchill's vision of self-determination. By 1947, Europe was divided by what Churchill called an Iron Curtain. The US responded with the Truman Doctrine (support for Greece and Turkey) and the Marshall Plan (economic reconstruction of Western Europe).

## Containment

George Kennan's Long Telegram outlined the strategy of containment: prevent Soviet expansion without provoking war. This became the foundation of US foreign policy for four decades. Containment was not about rolling back communism. It was about holding the line and waiting for the Soviet system to collapse under its own weight, which it eventually did.

> **Case: The Berlin Blockade** (Chapter 2): Stalin blockaded West Berlin in 1948, hoping to starve the Western allies out. The US responded with the Berlin Airlift, flying supplies into the city for 11 months. Stalin backed down. The crisis demonstrated that containment worked. The US could resist Soviet pressure without triggering war.
> **Key takeaway:** Containment required patience and creativity. The airlift was a logistical miracle that showed the West could resist without fighting.

## The Division of Europe

NATO was formed in 1949 as a military alliance of Western democracies. The Soviet Union responded with the Warsaw Pact in 1955. Germany was divided, with East Germany becoming a Soviet satellite and West Germany a democratic state integrated into Western Europe. The division of Europe would last 40 years.
The atomic bomb gave the US a temporary monopoly, but by 1949 the USSR had tested its own bomb. The nuclear arms race had begun. Both sides built massive arsenals of nuclear weapons and delivery systems. By the 1960s, each side had enough weapons to destroy the other many times over. This was the foundation of Mutually Assured Destruction. The paradox of the Cold War was that the most destructive weapons in history prevented a direct war between the superpowers.

The division of Germany was the most visible symbol of the Cold War. Berlin, located deep inside East Germany, was divided into four sectors controlled by the US, UK, France, and USSR. In 1961, East Germany built the Berlin Wall to stop its citizens from fleeing to the West. The Wall became the most powerful symbol of the Cold War: a physical barrier between freedom and oppression. It stood for 28 years before falling in 1989.

The Cold War was a global conflict, not just a European one. The Korean War (1950-1953) was the first major hot war of the Cold War. It ended in a stalemate that continues to this day. The Vietnam War (1955-1975) was the longest and most costly. Cuba became a flashpoint in 1962. Afghanistan became the Soviet Union Vietnam. Every continent felt the effects of the US-Soviet rivalry.

> **Case: The Marshall Plan** (Chapter 1): The US spent billions rebuilding Western Europe after WWII. The Marshall Plan was not charity. It was strategic. By rebuilding Western European economies, the US created prosperous democratic allies who would resist Soviet influence. The plan worked. Western Europe recovered, became a bulwark against communism, and a major market for American goods.
> **Key takeaway:** Economic aid is a form of statecraft. The Marshall Plan was one of the most successful foreign policy initiatives in history. It rebuilt allies while containing Soviet expansion.
