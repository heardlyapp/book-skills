# 3 — Nuclear Strategy

> Source: "The Cold War: A New History" by John Lewis Gaddis

## MAD — Mutually Assured Destruction

The strategy that kept the Cold War cold: if both sides can destroy each other, neither can win a war. The paradox: the ultimate weapon made the ultimate war unthinkable.

## The Arms Race

Both sides built tens of thousands of nuclear weapons. The US and USSR competed to develop more, bigger, and more accurate weapons.

> **Case: The Nuclear Taboo** (Chapter 7): Gaddis notes that despite the enormous arsenals, nuclear weapons were never used after 1945. A "nuclear taboo" developed. Even in the most desperate moments — Korea, Vietnam, Afghanistan — neither side used nuclear weapons. The taboo was not guaranteed; it was created by leadership and luck.
> **Key takeaway**: The fact that nuclear weapons weren't used is not inevitable. It was a choice, reinforced by norms and institutions. We cannot take the taboo for granted.

> **Case: Star Wars (SDI)** (Chapter 6): Reagan's Strategic Defense Initiative proposed a missile shield. The Soviets feared it would give the US a first-strike capability. "SDI was a technological fantasy, but as a bargaining chip, it was brilliant." Gaddis argues that SDI pushed the Soviets to negotiate because they feared an expensive new arms race they couldn't afford.
> **Key takeaway**: Military strategy is as much about psychology and economics as it is about technology. SDI never worked, but it helped end the Cold War.
