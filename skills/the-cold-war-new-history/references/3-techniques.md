# Techniques: Nuclear Strategy and Arms Control

## Technique 1: Deterrence through MAD

Mutually Assured Destruction was the foundation of Cold War strategy. Both sides maintained a second-strike capability — the ability to retaliate even after being attacked. This ensured that any nuclear attack would be suicidal. The strategy worked. No nuclear weapons were used after 1945.

## Technique 2: Arms Control Agreements

The superpowers negotiated limits on nuclear weapons. SALT I (1972) froze the number of missile launchers. SALT II (1979) set further limits. The INF Treaty (1987) eliminated an entire class of intermediate-range missiles. Arms control reduced the risk of accidental war.

> **Case: The Cuban Missile Crisis** (Chapter 3): For 13 days in October 1962, the US and USSR faced off over Soviet missiles in Cuba. Kennedy imposed a naval quarantine. Khrushchev agreed to remove the missiles in exchange for a US pledge not to invade Cuba and a secret deal to remove US missiles from Turkey. The crisis led to the establishment of the Moscow-Washington hotline and the Limited Test Ban Treaty. It was the turning point of the Cold War.
> **Key takeaway:** Crisis management requires both firmness and flexibility. Kennedy showed resolve publicly while offering private off-ramps. Both leaders understood that nuclear war was unacceptable.

## Technique 3: Intelligence and Espionage

Both sides invested heavily in intelligence. The CIA and KGB fought a shadow war of spies, defectors, and covert operations. Intelligence shaped decisions: U-2 spy plane photos revealed Soviet missile deployments in Cuba.

## Technique 4: Covert Operations

Both sides engaged in covert operations to influence foreign governments. The CIA overthrew governments in Iran (1953) and Guatemala (1954). The KGB supported communist movements worldwide. Covert action was a way to fight without triggering a direct superpower confrontation.

## Technique 5: The Hotline

After the Cuban Missile Crisis, the US and USSR established a direct communications link known as the hotline. It allowed leaders to communicate directly in a crisis, reducing the risk of miscommunication leading to war. The hotline was used during subsequent crises and remains operational today.

The arms race was not just about numbers of weapons. It was about technology, delivery systems, and perceptions. Each new technology — intercontinental ballistic missiles, submarine-launched missiles, multiple independently targetable reentry vehicles (MIRVs) — changed the strategic balance. Each side had to anticipate what the other was developing and respond. This dynamic drove an ever-escalating cycle of innovation and counter-innovation.

> **Case: The U-2 Incident** (Chapter 3): In 1960, a US U-2 spy plane was shot down over the USSR. The pilot, Gary Powers, survived and was captured. The US initially denied the mission, then was forced to admit it when the USSR produced the pilot and wreckage. The incident destroyed a summit meeting between Eisenhower and Khrushchev. But both sides learned from the crisis and improved protocols for intelligence operations.
> **Key takeaway:** Espionage was a necessary part of Cold War management, but when exposed, it could disrupt diplomacy. Both sides learned to compartmentalize intelligence operations from diplomatic engagement.
