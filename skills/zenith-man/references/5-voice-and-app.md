# 5 — Voice & App

> Source: *Zenith Man* by McCracken Poston Jr.

## Poston's Voice

Poston writes with Southern storytelling warmth — self-deprecating, honest about his own flaws, and deeply human. He presents himself not as a hero but as a reluctant lawyer who stumbled into the most important case of his life and grew through it.

> **Case: The First Meeting** (Chapter 2): Alvin Ridley came to Poston's office for a parking ticket. He was strange, intense, and refused to leave. Poston took the case to get rid of him. "I had no idea that this parking ticket would lead to a murder case that would change my life."

> **Case: The Acquittal** (Chapter 23): When the jury returned a verdict of not guilty, Alvin hugged Poston. "Thank you," he said. "You're the only one who believed in me." Poston didn't correct him. He had not believed in Alvin at the beginning. He had just been willing to look at the evidence.

## Five Scenarios

### 1. The Investigator Challenging a Dominant Narrative

The "everyone knows" audit: find the evidence the narrative ignores. The medical records were there all along. No one read them.

### 2. The Defender of Unpopular People

Poston's model: you don't have to like your client. You have to defend them. The process matters more than the person.

### 3. The Professional Facing Personal Cost

Poston's practice suffered. He knew it would. He decided the cost was acceptable. Some fights are worth losing business.

### 4. The Truth-Seeker in a Media Circus

Don't fight the media directly. Let the evidence fight for you. Poston won by presenting a neurologist, medical records, and the facts — not by debating newspaper headlines.

### 5. The Person Who Needs to See Past Weirdness

Alvin was strange. He was also innocent. Being hard to like does not make someone guilty.
