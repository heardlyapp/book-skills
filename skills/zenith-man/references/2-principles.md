# 2 — Key Principles

> Source: *Zenith Man* by McCracken Poston Jr.

## The 7 Principles

### 1. The Facts Are Always More Complicated Than the Headlines

The media, the sheriff, the neighbors — everyone was wrong about Alvin Ridley. The truth was found in medical records no one had bothered to read.

### 2. Every Client Deserves a Defense

Not because they are innocent — but because even the guilty deserve due process. And sometimes, the person everyone is sure is guilty is innocent.

> **Case: Poston's Reluctance** (Chapter 2): Poston did not want to take the case. Alvin had been harassing him for months. He was an unpaid parking ticket client. But when Poston heard the sheriff's press conference, describing Alvin as a monster, he knew: "I had to take the case. Not because I wanted to — but because no one else would."

### 3. The Truth Is in the Details

The medical records, the phone logs, the neurologist's testimony — these told a story that the media narrative couldn't accommodate. The details always matter.

### 4. Don't Abandon a Client Because They Are Hard to Like

Alvin Ridley was paranoid, angry, rude, and exhausting. He was also innocent. Poston almost quit multiple times. He stayed.

> **Case: The 26-Hour Bond** (Chapter 5): Poston got Alvin out of jail in 26 hours. Alvin's first phone call was to scream at Poston that it had taken "26 hours, not 24." Most lawyers would have quit. Poston set boundaries: no calls at night, drop-in welcome during business hours.

### 5. Doing the Right Thing Often Costs You

Poston lost clients, his wife was harassed, his reputation suffered. He would do it again.

### 6. Small Communities Have Long Memories

Poston paid for defending Alvin years after the trial. But he also earned the respect of people who mattered — including Alvin himself.

### 7. Redemption Is Possible on Both Sides

Poston saved Alvin. Alvin saved Poston from a life of comfortable mediocrity. The lawyer who took the unpopular case became a different person than the one who started.
