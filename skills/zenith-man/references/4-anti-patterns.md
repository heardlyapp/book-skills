# 4 — Anti-Patterns

> Source: *Zenith Man* by McCracken Poston Jr.

## Common Errors

### 1. Trusting the Consensus

**The trap:** "Everyone knows it's true, so it must be true."

**Why it fails:** The sheriff, the media, and the whole community "knew" Alvin was guilty. They were wrong. Consensus is not evidence.

> **Case: The Sheriff's Certainty** (Prologue, Chapter 1): Sheriff Phil Summers told the press that Alvin had killed his wife and had been "holding her prisoner for decades." He was completely wrong. His certainty was the only thing that was real. The evidence was never examined.
> **Key takeaway**: Certainty without evidence is just conviction. It's not the same as truth.

> **Case: The Tabloid Headline Factory** (Chapter 4): The National Examiner ran: "SICKO HOLDS HIS WIFE HOSTAGE FOR 30 YEARS THEN KILLS HER." The story was built entirely on allegations from Virginia's estranged sister — who had not spoken to her in 30 years. The media printed the accusation as fact. It was not.
> **Key takeaway**: The more sensational the claim, the less likely it is to be checked before publication.

**The trap:** "He's so difficult and unpleasant, he must be guilty."

**Why it fails:** Alvin was a difficult, paranoid, unpleasant man. None of that made him a murderer. Poston separated the person from the case.

### 3. Believing the Media Narrative

The National Examiner, local news, and word-of-mouth spread a story that had almost no basis in fact. The more dramatic the story, the less likely it is to be true.

### 4. Defending at All Costs

Poston never lied or suppressed evidence. He let the truth — hidden medical records — speak for itself. The best defense is not theatrics. It's evidence.

### 5. Misjudging the "Weird" Client

Alvin collected newspapers, lived in a house full of cats, and hoarded electronics. This strangeness was used against him in the press and in court. It had nothing to do with whether he killed his wife. "He was weird, not a murderer."
