# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *Zenith Man* by McCracken Poston Jr.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The "Everyone Knows" Audit

When "everyone knows" something is true, ask: what is the evidence? Who actually examined it? The sheriff "knew" Alvin was guilty — but had never read Virginia's medical records.

> **Case: The Medical Records Nobody Read** (Chapter 14): The sheriff had spent months building a case against Alvin. He had never subpoenaed Virginia's medical records. "If you don't read the medical records, you don't know what happened."
> **Key takeaway**: The most important evidence is often the evidence nobody looked at.

### 2. The Difficult Client Boundary Protocol

Set clear rules: (1) No calls at night. (2) Drop-in welcome during business hours. (3) No exceptions. This gives the difficult client access while protecting your own sanity.

### 3. The Counter-Narrative Building

To challenge a dominant narrative: (1) Find the evidence the narrative ignores. (2) Find an authoritative source to present it (neurologist, medical records). (3) Let the evidence speak — don't argue the narrative directly.

### 4. The Pre-Trial Media Management

Poston did not fight the media directly. He let the evidence fight for him. The trial itself was the place to demolish the narrative, not the newspaper.

### 5. The Personal Cost Calculation

Before taking an unpopular case: calculate the potential loss. Poston knew he would lose clients and reputation. He decided that losing was acceptable if justice was served.

> **Case: The Empty Office** (Epilogue): After the trial, Poston's caseload dropped. Clients who had come to him for other matters had seen the publicity and assumed he was guilty by association. "Defending Alvin cost me. I knew it would. I did it anyway."
> **Key takeaway**: Some costs are worth paying. Calculate them in advance, then pay them without complaint.
