# Engineering Methods — Techniques

## Technique 1: The Trade-Off Matrix

Sutter's team used trade-off analysis to make design decisions. Speed vs. efficiency. Range vs. payload. Weight vs. strength.

**How to do it:** When facing a design decision, list all the variables. Score each option. Choose the one that best balances competing priorities.

**Case: The Fuselage Width.** The 747 could have been wider or narrower. A wider fuselage would be more comfortable but heavier and more drag. A narrower fuselage would be more efficient but less comfortable and less flexible for cargo conversion. The final width was a compromise.

**Case: The Wing Sweep.** More wing sweep reduced drag at high speed but reduced lift at low speed. Less wing sweep was better for takeoff and landing but worse for cruising. The 27.5-degree sweep was the optimal trade-off.

## Technique 2: The Weight Budget

Sutter assigned weight budgets to every system. Every engineer knew exactly how much their component could weigh.

**How to do it:** Set a total weight target. Divide it among subsystems. Hold each subsystem accountable. If one system exceeds its budget, another system must compensate.

**Case: The 100-Pound Rule.** Sutter reportedly said: "If an engineer can save 100 pounds, I will buy him a steak dinner." Weight savings were celebrated. Weight increases were scrutinized.

## Technique 3: The Mock-Up

Boeing built a full-scale mock-up of the 747 before the first airplane was built. This allowed engineers to test systems, identify problems, and make changes before production.

**Case: The Cabin Mock-Up.** The mock-up revealed that the original cabin layout was too cramped. The aisles were too narrow. The overhead bins were too small. Changes were made based on mock-up testing.

**Case: The Cargo Door.** The 747's nose cargo door was tested on the mock-up. It worked. The airplane could be loaded from the front — a key requirement for cargo conversion.
