# Principles of Engineering Leadership

## I. Say Yes, Then Figure Out How

When Pan Am asked for an airplane twice the size of anything in existence, with a four-year deadline, Sutter said yes. Most engineers would have said no.

**Case: The Impossible Assignment.** Sutter was given the job of chief engineer for the 747 in 1965. The requirements: carry 350 passengers, fly 5,000 miles nonstop, cruise at Mach 0.85, and be certified by 1969. No one had built anything like it. Sutter said yes.

**Case: The Everett Plant.** Boeing built the largest building in the world to assemble the 747. The roof was too low — they had to raise it. The doors were too small — they had to enlarge them. The entire plant was a work in progress.

## II. Be Willing to Be Fired

Sutter's most important leadership moment: he told his boss the wing had to be redesigned, knowing the cost and schedule impact could end his career.

**Case: The Wing Meeting.** Sutter walked into his boss's office and said the wing was not good enough. He had data, analysis, and a proposed redesign. His boss listened, asked questions, and approved the change.

**Case: The Cultural Lesson.** Sutter's willingness to speak truth to power set the tone for the entire program. When the leader is honest about problems, the team is honest about problems.

## III. Build a Team That Believes

The "Incredibles" — 4,500 engineers who believed they were doing something historic.

**Case: The Name.** The engineers called themselves "the Incredibles." They wore buttons. They worked 12-hour days. They believed they were building the future of aviation.

**Case: The Collaboration.** Sutter describes the 747 program as a collaboration unlike any other. The engineers, the factory workers, the suppliers, the airlines — everyone was part of the effort.
