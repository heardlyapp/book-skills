# Building the Impossible — Core Framework

Joe Sutter's book is organized around a central paradox: the 747 was both the most successful airliner in history and a constant crisis from start to finish.

## The Framework: Three Crises That Defined the 747

**Crisis 1: The Engine.** The 747 needed a new kind of engine — the high-bypass turbofan — to achieve its range and payload. Pratt & Whitney's JT9D was the first of its kind. It did not work reliably during testing.

**Case: The Engine Delays.** The JT9D was late, overweight, and underpowered in its initial configuration. Boeing had to redesign parts of the 747 to accommodate the engine's problems. Sutter's team worked closely with Pratt & Whitney to solve the issues.

**Case: The First Flight With Non-Retracting Gear.** When the 747 first flew on February 9, 1969, the landing gear could not retract. The engines were not ready. The airplane flew with a temporary fix. Sutter describes the first flight as a qualified success — the airplane flew.

**Crisis 2: The Wing.** Midway through the program, Sutter realized the wing was not good enough.

**Case: The Redesign.** The original wing design did not provide enough lift at takeoff speeds. Sutter went to his boss and said the wing had to be redesigned. It would cost millions and delay the schedule. He was willing to be fired. His boss approved the redesign.

**Case: The Triple-Slotted Flap.** The redesigned wing featured a triple-slotted trailing edge flap — a complex mechanism that allowed the huge wing to generate enough lift at low speeds. It was an engineering marvel.

**Crisis 3: The Weight.** The 747 was getting heavier as the design matured. Every system added weight. The engines were heavier than expected. The structure had to be reinforced.

**Case: The Weight Reduction Program.** Sutter launched a systematic weight reduction effort. Every pound was scrutinized. The team saved thousands of pounds by redesigning components, using lighter materials, and eliminating unnecessary features.

**Case: The Empty Weight.** The final 747 weighed about 330,000 pounds empty. It could carry 735,000 pounds at takeoff. The ratio of payload to structure was extraordinary.
