# Core Framework — Spillover

## The Hendra Story (Chapters 1-2)

In September 1994, Brisbane, Australia. A mare named Drama Series died. Then 12 more horses. Then a trainer named Vic Rail. The cause: a virus from fruit bats (flying foxes) that "spilled over" into horses and then to humans. Case: "The virus now known as Hendra wasn't the first of the scary new bugs... its mortal impact was small — but it was representative."

## Chapter 3: Everything Comes from Somewhere

All zoonotic diseases have a source — usually a "reservoir host" that carries the virus without getting sick. Bats are a common reservoir: they carry Hendra, Nipah, SARS, Ebola, Marburg, and possibly COVID-19. Case: "A zoonosis is an animal infection transmissible to humans."

## Chapters 4-6: Nipah and Influenza

Nipah virus in Malaysia and Bangladesh. Spread from bats to pigs to humans. "Dinner at the Rat Farm" — ecological disruption pushes viruses into human populations. Case: Nipah kills 40-70% of infected humans. "A virus that kills more than half its victims should not be underestimated."

## Chapter 8: The Chimp and the River

HIV/AIDS — the most devastating spillover in human history. Crossed from chimpanzees to humans in central Africa, likely through butchering bushmeat. "More than 30 million people have died since 1981." Case: "HIV is a zoonosis that became a pandemic."

## Chapter 9: It Depends

Predicting the next pandemic is hard. But the risk factors are clear: ecological disruption, human-animal contact, population density, global travel. "The next big one could come from anywhere."

## The Threat

"We are the Pale Horse." Humans create the conditions for spillover through deforestation, wildlife trade, factory farming, and climate change. The question is not if — it's when and how bad. Case: "Humanity is a kind of animal, inextricably connected with other animals: in origin and in descent, in sickness and in health."
