# Key Principles — Spillover

## I. Zoonosis Is the Norm, Not the Exception

"A zoonosis is an animal infection transmissible to humans." Most emerging infectious diseases are zoonotic. AIDS, Ebola, SARS, influenza, rabies, plague — they all come from animals. Case: Hendra from bats to horses to humans. Nipah from bats to pigs to humans.

## II. Bats Are Special

"Bats are the most important reservoir of emerging viruses." They carry Hendra, Nipah, SARS, Ebola, Marburg, and likely COVID-19. They can fly long distances and their immune systems tolerate viruses without killing them. Case: "Flying foxes" carrying Hendra in Australia were the source of the 1994 outbreak.

## III. Ecological Disruption Drives Spillover

"Deforestation, habitat loss, and wildlife trade bring humans and animals into unnatural contact." When forests are cut down, bats and other animals move into human settlements. This is how spillover happens. Case: Nipah in Malaysia — bats displaced by deforestation fed in pig orchards, and pigs transmitted the virus to humans.

## IV. Prediction Is Difficult But Not Impossible

"The next big one could come from anywhere." But we know the risk factors: high population density, global travel, factory farming, wildlife markets, and ecological disruption. Case: SARS came from bats through civet cats in Chinese wet markets.

## V. Pandemic Preparedness Is Inadequate

"There were two cases, only one fatal — but it was representative." We ignore small outbreaks at our peril. Hendra was a warning. AIDS was a catastrophe. The next one could be worse. Case: "More than 30 million people have died since 1981" from AIDS — and it started as a single spillover event.

## VI. Humans Are Animals

"The old Darwinian truth — humanity is a kind of animal, connected with other animals." Recognizing this is essential to understanding why spillover happens. We are not separate from nature. Case: "Pondering them as a group reaffirms the old Darwinian truth."

## VII. Prevention Is Better Than Cure

"We can't stop every spillover. But we can reduce the risk." Surveillance, wildlife monitoring, regulating wildlife markets, reducing deforestation, improving farm biosecurity. Case: "Everything comes from somewhere" — knowing where viruses come from is the first step to preventing them.
