## 6 Anti-Patterns of the Uncurious Mind

### 1. "I Already Know That"

The moment you think you know, you stop learning. Feynman approached everything as a beginner — even topics he had mastered.

### 2. "The Expert Is Always Right"

Experts can be wrong — and often are. Feynman's Challenger demonstration proved that authority is not evidence.

### 3. "Science Is Boring"

Science is the process of finding out how the world works. If that is boring, you are doing it wrong.

> **Case: The Burning String** (1960): A young student wrote to Feynman about an experiment with a burning string. Feynman wrote back enthusiastically, explaining the physics. He took the time to answer a child's question with the same care he would give a colleague.
> **Key takeaway**: No question is too small for curiosity. A Nobel laureate answered a child's question about a burning string because he still found it interesting.

### 4. "Serious People Don't Play"

Feynman played bongo drums, cracked safes, and drew pictures. Play made him a better physicist. Seriousness is overrated.

### 5. "I Don't Have Time to Be Curious"

You have time for what you prioritize. Feynman made time for drums, drawing, and letters. Curiosity is not a time drain — it is a way of living.

### 6. "Certainty Is the Goal"

Certainty is the enemy of truth. Feynman was comfortable with uncertainty. "I would rather have questions that cannot be answered than answers that cannot be questioned."

> **Case: The Textbook Error** (1962): A textbook claimed that an atom's electrons move in fixed orbits. Feynman wrote a letter explaining that this was a simplified model — not the truth. The textbook was teaching a lie for the sake of simplicity.
> **Key takeaway**: Simplification is not the same as truth. Feynman could not tolerate teaching something that was wrong — even if it was easier.
