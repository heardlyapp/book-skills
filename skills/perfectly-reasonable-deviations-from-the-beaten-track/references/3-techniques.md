## Practical Techniques

### 1. The Feynman Technique for Learning

Take a concept. Write an explanation in simple language. If you cannot explain it simply, you do not understand it. Identify the gaps in your explanation — those are what you need to learn.

### 2. The Not-Fooling-Yourself Check

Whenever you find yourself believing something you want to be true, ask: "What evidence would prove me wrong?" Actively seek disconfirming evidence.

> **Case: The Student's Question** (1966): A student asked Feynman how to know if a scientific theory was correct. Feynman replied: "Try to prove it wrong. If you cannot, it might be right. If you find something wrong, you have learned something."
> **Key takeaway**: Truth is found through attempted falsification, not through confirmation. Try to prove yourself wrong.

### 3. The Curiosity Practice

Every day, spend 15 minutes learning about something you do not understand. Not because it is useful — because it is interesting. Curiosity is its own reward.

### 4. The Reality Check

When a project faces a problem, do not ask "what does the plan say?" Ask "what does reality say?" Reality always wins.

### 5. The Humility Practice

Whenever you hear an expert, remember: they could be wrong. Feynman respected expertise — but never surrendered his own judgment. Authority is not truth.

> **Case: The NASA Letter** (1986): During the Challenger investigation, NASA officials insisted that the O-rings were safe. Feynman demonstrated they were not — with a glass of ice water. His demonstration proved that authority is not evidence.
> **Key takeaway**: Authority is not truth. Evidence is truth. Test everything yourself if you can.
