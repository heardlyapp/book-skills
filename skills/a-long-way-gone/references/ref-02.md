# Reference: Life as a Child Soldier

## Overview
Beah was not captured by the rebels. He was taken in by the Sierra Leone government army, which was also desperate for soldiers. The line between victim and perpetrator blurs — and Beah does not let the reader forget that he was a child.

## Case: The Recruitment
After months alone, Beah and his friends encountered a government army unit. The soldiers offered food and protection, then gave them guns. Beah describes the moment as both rescue and capture: "I took the gun and held it for the first time. It was heavier than I expected." The boys were given drugs — a mix of cocaine, gunpowder, and a local sedative — to make them fearless and compliant. The drugs also made it impossible to sleep without nightmares.

## Case: The First Attack
Beah describes his first combat experience with brutal clarity. He was high on drugs, terrified, and driven by a survival instinct he did not know he had. He shot at shadows, watched other boys die, and emerged from the battle alive but fundamentally changed. The drugs helped him suppress the horror, but the effect faded. He began to realize that the line between government soldier and rebel was meaningless — both sides used children, both sides committed atrocities.

## Case: The Atrocities
The book does not flinch from describing what Beah was forced to do. He participated in attacks on villages, committed acts of violence, and saw things that no one should ever see. He describes the psychological mechanism that made it possible: the drugs, the rage, the belief that the people he was fighting were the ones who had killed his family, and the slow erosion of the distinction between enemy and human being. He also describes the aftermath — the shame, the nightmares, the feeling of being permanently broken.
