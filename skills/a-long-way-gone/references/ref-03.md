# Reference: Survival on the Run

## Overview
Between the destruction of his village and his forced recruitment, Beah spent months wandering through a war zone with no protection, no resources, and no adult to help him.

## Case: The River Crossing
At one point, Beah and his companions had to cross a river while rebels pursued them. Many of the boys could not swim. Those who could helped those who could not. Beah describes the terror of watching boys drown while gunfire erupted around them. The crossing was a turning point: some boys made it; some did not. Those who survived did not have time to mourn.

## Case: The Village Massacre
Beah and his group stumbled onto an RUF attack on a village. They hid in the bush and watched as rebels amputated the hands of civilians — a signature tactic of the RUF. Beah describes the sound: not just screams but the silence afterward, which was worse. He realized that the rebels wanted survivors, not just corpses. The amputated were meant to live — to carry the message of terror to other villages.

## Case: The Starvation Winter
Food became scarce. The boys ate leaves, bark, and rats. They fought over scraps. Some boys died of disease or starvation. Beah describes the terrible arithmetic of survival: the strength to keep walking, the judgment to know which fruits were safe, the luck to avoid rebel patrols, the resilience to watch friends die and keep moving. He also describes the guilt of survival — asking himself why he was still alive when others were not.
