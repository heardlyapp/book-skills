# Reference: Finding a Voice

## Overview
Beah's story did not end with rehabilitation. He went on to become an international voice for the cause of child soldiers.

## Case: The UN Speech
In 1998, Beah was chosen to speak at a United Nations conference on child soldiers. He was 17 years old. He stood before an audience of diplomats and told them what had happened to him. He described being forced to fight, the drugs, the violence, the rehabilitation. After his speech, several delegates were in tears. He realized that his voice had power — not despite his experience but because of it. He could speak for children who could not speak for themselves.

## Case: Meeting Laura Simms
Laura Simms, a professional storyteller, met Beah at the UN conference and became his mentor and guardian. She brought him to the United States, enrolled him in school, and supported him through the long process of writing his memoir. The relationship between Beah and Simms is one of the most important relationships in the book. It demonstrates that recovery does not happen in isolation — it requires someone to believe in you.

## Case: Writing the Memoir
Beah began writing A Long Way Gone while still in high school. The process took years and required him to relive the most traumatic experiences of his life. He describes writing as a form of exorcism — putting the memories on paper so they would stop haunting him. The book was published in 2007 and became an international bestseller. Beah donated a portion of his proceeds to UNICEF and continues to advocate for children affected by war.
