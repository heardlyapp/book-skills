# Reference: Rehabilitation

## Overview
The UNICEF rehabilitation center in Freetown was where Beah's recovery began. The process was slow, painful, and far from certain.

## Case: The First Day at the Center
Beah describes arriving at the rehabilitation center in a state of complete dissociation. He was still addicted to drugs, still in combat mode, and unable to trust anyone. The counselors did not force him to talk. They gave him clean clothes, a bed, and food. The first night, he slept for 16 hours straight — the first real sleep he had had in years. The counselors understood that safety had to come before healing.

## Case: Group Therapy and Storytelling
The center brought former child soldiers together to share their stories. At first, the boys competed over who had suffered the most. Gradually, the storytelling became less about competition and more about connection. Beah describes the moment when he realized that the other boys understood him in a way that no one else could. They had all done things they were ashamed of. They had all lost everyone. They were all trying to figure out how to be human again.

## Case: The Rap Performance
Toward the end of his rehabilitation, Beah performed a rap song for the other boys — the first time he had performed since the war began. The song was about peace, about hope, about returning to a normal life. The other boys cheered. Beah describes this as the moment he knew he could recover. Music had been his passion before the war. Reconnecting with it meant that the person he had been before was still inside him.
