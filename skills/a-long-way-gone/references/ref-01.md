# Reference: The Collapse of Innocence

## Overview
Ishmael Beah was 12 years old when the Sierra Leone civil war reached his village. His world collapsed in a single day, separating him from his family and launching him into a nightmare that would last years.

## Case: The Attack on Mattru Jong
Beah and his brother Junior were performing in a rap group when the RUF attacked their town. They fled into the forest with friends, assuming they would find their family and return home in a few days. They did not know that the attack had wiped out most of their village, including family members they would never see again. Beah describes the surreal transition from performing rap music to running for his life — a moment when childhood ended and survival began.

## Case: Wandering Alone
For months, Beah and a group of other boys wandered through the jungle, scavenging for food, avoiding rebel patrols, and searching for any sign of their families. They slept in trees, ate wild fruit, and watched entire villages burn. Beah describes the psychological toll of constant fear: the hypervigilance that never turned off, the hunger that became normal, the growing numbness to death. By the time he was "rescued" by the government army, he had already witnessed more violence than most people see in a lifetime.

## Case: The Meaning of Home
Before the war, Beah had a normal childhood — he loved rap music, played soccer with his friends, and dreamed of becoming a performer. His family was not wealthy but they were together. The war did not just take his family; it took the entire framework of meaning that had defined his life. He lost not only his parents and brothers but also his understanding of what the world was, what adults were supposed to do, and what the future could hold. This loss of meaning — the total destruction of the conceptual world a child relies on — was as traumatic as the violence itself.
