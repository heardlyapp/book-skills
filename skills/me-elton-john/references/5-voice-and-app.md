# 5 — Voice & App

> Source: *Me* by Elton John.

## Elton's Voice

Elton writes exactly as he speaks — honest, hilarious, profane, and unexpectedly tender. The book is narrated in his own voice: he tells stories about Studio 54 and his mother in the same breath, and both are treated with the same mixture of affection and brutal honesty. He does not sanitize his worst moments. He does not pretend to be a hero. He is the unreliable narrator of his own life — and he knows it.

> **Case: The Kaftan Story (Prologue):** Elton opens the book not with his greatest triumph but with a story about wearing a cheap kaftan and bells in a supper club in South Shields, looking like "a finalist in a competition to find Britain's least convincing flower child." Long John Baldry, his mentor, had ordered them all kaftans — expensive ones for himself, cheap ones for the band. "This, I felt, rather ran contrary to the kaftan's associations with peace and love and universal brotherhood." The story is self-deprecating, funny, and establishes the tone: Elton will not take himself too seriously.

> **Case: The Donald Duck Costume (Chapter 12):** Playing for 500,000 people in Central Park, Elton wore a Donald Duck costume that he could not walk or sit in. "Your Song" was ruined by giggles. "Once again, Bernie's tender ballad of blossoming young love was decimated by my choice of stage wear." This is Elton's voice — he knows the absurdity, he owns it, and he shares it.

## Five Scenarios

### 1. The Creative Person

You are an artist, writer, or musician looking for a creative partner. Elton and Bernie's model: separate domains, complete trust, no interference. Find someone whose skills complement yours — then stay out of their way.

### 2. The Person Struggling with Addiction

You know you have a problem. You have tried to stop. You cannot. Elton's lesson: you cannot do this alone. Rehab, AA, a partner who refuses to enable — you need help. Asking for it is not weakness. It is survival.

### 3. The Person Afraid to Live Authentically

You are hiding a part of yourself. You think people will reject you. Elton lived in fear for years. He came out. He survived. He thrived. The fear of rejection is almost always worse than the rejection itself.

### 4. The Successful Person Looking for Purpose

You have achieved what you set out to achieve. You feel empty. Elton's example: find a cause that personally affects you and use your resources to address it. The AIDS Foundation gave his fame meaning.

### 5. The Person Who Believes They Cannot Change

You have been the same person for decades. You think change is impossible. Elton went from cocaine-addicted and lonely to sober, married, and a father. If he can change, anyone can.
