# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *Me* by Elton John.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The Separation of Domains

Elton and Bernie's method: complete division of labor. One creates the lyrics. The other creates the music. No cross-interference. Apply this to your own creative partnership: define who owns what. Respect the boundary. Trust your partner's judgment in their domain.

> **Case: "Your Song" (Chapter 6):** Bernie handed Elton lyrics and went to make a phone call. By the time he returned, the song was finished. The separation of domains — Bernie's lyrics, Elton's music — meant there was nothing to negotiate. Pure creation, no friction.
> **Key takeaway**: When each partner owns their domain completely, there is no back-and-forth, no argument, no committee. Trust and autonomy produce faster, better work.

> **Case: The Central Park Duck Costume (Chapter 12):** For his 1980 Central Park concert — 500,000 people — Elton wore a Donald Duck costume. He couldn't walk in it, couldn't sit down, and spent the entire encore laughing uncontrollably. "Your Song" was ruined by giggles. The story illustrates Elton's philosophy: take your work seriously, but yourself not at all.
> **Key takeaway**: Showmanship is not about perfection. It is about presence. The duck costume was a disaster — and it was unforgettable. Dare to be ridiculous.

Elton hit bottom multiple times before he actually changed. The marker: "Can I not remember what I did last night?" If you cannot account for your actions, you have lost control. Honesty with yourself is the first step.

### 3. The Foundation Model

When you achieve success, identify one problem that personally affects you and use your resources to address it. Elton lost friends to AIDS, so he founded the AIDS Foundation. The cause was personal. The resources were significant. The impact is measurable.

### 4. The "Don't Sleep on the First Date" Principle

David Furnish refused to sleep with Elton on their first date. This single act signaled: "I am not here for your fame or your money. I am here for you." A partner who is not impressed by your success but loves who you are is a partner worth keeping.

### 5. The AA Method: One Day at a Time

Elton's recovery was built on the AA principle: one day at a time. Not "I will never do coke again" — but "I will not do coke today." The frame shift from a lifetime commitment to a daily choice made recovery possible.
