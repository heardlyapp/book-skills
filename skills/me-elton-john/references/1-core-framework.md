# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *Me* by Elton John — Prologue, Chapters 1-24.

## The Three Frameworks

### Framework A: The Songwriting Partnership (Chapters 2-6)

Elton's method with Bernie Taupin: Bernie sends lyrics on a piece of paper. Elton sets them to music in about 30 minutes. They rarely work in the same room. They never interfere in each other's domain — Bernie does not write music, Elton does not write lyrics. "Your Song" was written at the kitchen table of Bernie's parents' house while Elton waited for him to finish a magazine article. "Crocodile Rock" took 15 minutes.

> **Case: "Your Song" (Chapter 6):** Bernie brought lyrics to the kitchen table. Elton glanced at them, went to the piano, and wrote the melody in 30 minutes. Bernie said: "That's the first Elton John song." The phrase "I don't have much money, but boy if I did" was so perfectly matched to Elton's yearning delivery that it became their signature. The entire song was written in half an hour.

> **Case: The Separation Principle (Chapter 4):** Many people assume Elton and Bernie wrote together — Elton at the piano, Bernie in the room. They never did. Bernie mailed lyrics. Elton responded with music. This separation — a complete division of labor — is why their partnership lasted 50+ years. "We never interfered in each other's territory. I trusted him completely. He trusted me completely."

### Framework B: The Addiction and Recovery (Chapters 8-16)

Elton's cocaine addiction was catastrophic. He had violent tantrums — throwing objects, firing people mid-tour, smashing rooms. He was bulimic. He had sex with strangers in hotel rooms. He could not remember entire concert tours. Freddie Mercury's death from AIDS in 1991 was a profound shock — but even that did not stop Elton immediately. He hit bottom in 1990 after a particularly ugly tour. He checked into rehab in Chicago. He started AA. He met David Furnish in 1993, and David's love — combined with his refusal to enable — gave Elton a foundation for lasting recovery.

> **Case: The Freddie Mercury Loss (Chapter 16):** Elton was devastated when Freddie died. They had been close friends — wild, gay, brilliant men who understood each other. Freddie's death from AIDS could have been a cautionary tale. It was. But Elton was not ready. "You'd have thought that losing one of my closest friends to AIDS might have changed my behavior. It didn't. I still wasn't ready to admit I had a problem."

> **Case: The Rehab Turning Point (Chapter 17):** Elton finally entered Parkside Lutheran Hospital in Chicago in 1990. "I was a wreck. I was a coke addict, I was bulimic, I was having sex with strangers, I was in terrible shape." The turning point was admitting he could not stop by himself. He stayed in rehab, started going to AA, and slowly began to rebuild his life.

### Framework C: The David Furnish Love Story (Chapters 17-24)

Elton met David Furnish at a dinner party in 1993. David had no idea who Elton was — he was a Canadian advertising executive. He refused to sleep with Elton on the first date. He refused to be impressed by fame. He accepted Elton as he was — but also demanded he be better. They were together for 21 years before they could legally marry (UK law changed in 2014). They have two sons.

> **Case: The First Date (Chapter 18):** David Furnish came to Elton's house for dinner. Elton, expecting another one-night stand, was shocked when David said he would not sleep with him. "I think I fell in love with him that night. Here was someone who was not remotely impressed by my fame. He saw me — the real me — and he wasn't going to settle for being just another hookup."
