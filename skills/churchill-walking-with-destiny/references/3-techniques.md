# Wartime Leadership

> Source: Churchill, Part Two, Chapters 21-31

## How Churchill Led

### The Methods

| Tool | How He Used It |
|------|---------------|
| Rhetoric | Speeches as weapons: "Blood, Toil, Tears and Sweat," "We Shall Fight," "Finest Hour" |
| Energy | Worked all hours, visited bomb sites, traveled to meet allies |
| Delegation | Trusted his generals, but challenged them |
| Symbolism | The V-sign, the cigar, the siren suit |

> **Case: The Battle of Britain (Chapter 22)**
> In the summer of 1940, Britain stood alone. France had fallen. The Luftwaffe was bombing. Churchill's leadership was not just strategic — it was psychological. He visited bombed areas, talked to people, shared their danger. His radio speeches rallied the nation. He told the Cabinet: "If the British Empire and its Commonwealth last for a thousand years, men will still say: 'This was their finest hour.'" The RAF won the Battle of Britain. Churchill's words gave them the will to keep fighting.
> **Key takeaway:** Churchill understood that leadership in a crisis is not just about making decisions — it is about being seen. His presence in the bombed streets of London was worth more than any strategic order. The lesson: in a crisis, show up.

> **Case: The Grand Alliance (Chapters 24-26)**
> Churchill forged the alliance with Franklin Roosevelt and Joseph Stalin that won the war. His relationship with Roosevelt was warm and strategic: the two men developed a genuine friendship. His relationship with Stalin was cold and calculating: Churchill distrusted Stalin but needed him to fight Germany. The "percentages agreement" in 1944 divided Eastern Europe into spheres of influence. Churchill was realistic about the postwar order.
> **Key takeaway:** Churchill's relationship with Stalin shows his strategic realism. He hated communism but allied with Stalin because Hitler was the greater enemy. The lesson: in war, you ally with your enemy's enemy. Morality is for peacetime.
