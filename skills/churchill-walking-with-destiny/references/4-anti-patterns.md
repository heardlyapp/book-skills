# Anti-Patterns — Churchill's Flaws

> Source: Churchill, all chapters

## The Five Contradictions

### 1. Imperialism
**Error**: "The British Empire was a force for good."
**Reality**: Churchill was a committed imperialist. He resisted Indian independence. The Bengal famine of 1943 (3 million dead) happened under his watch. Roberts defends Churchill but the evidence is damning.

### 2. Gallipoli
**Error**: "A brilliant plan ruined by bad execution."
**Reality**: The plan was flawed. Churchill shared responsibility for one of the worst military disasters of WWI.

### 3. Racism
**Error**: "Churchill was a man of his time."
**Reality**: He held views that were racist even by the standards of his time. He opposed Indian self-rule and made deeply offensive statements.

### 4. Recklessness
**Error**: "Fortune favors the bold."
**Reality**: Churchill's boldness worked in WWII. It failed catastrophically at Gallipoli.

### 5. Resistance to Change
**Error**: "The postwar world should look like the prewar world."
**Reality**: Churchill was out of step with the postwar world. He lost the 1945 election. He resisted decolonization. The world moved on.

> **Case: The Bengal Famine (Chapter 28, notes)**
> In 1943, while Churchill was focused on the war, Bengal suffered a catastrophic famine. 3 million people died. Churchill's policies (prioritizing war supplies over food aid, refusing to divert shipping) contributed to the disaster. Roberts argues Churchill was not personally responsible — the war created the conditions. But the evidence suggests Churchill showed less concern for Indian lives than for British ones.
> **Key takeaway:** The Bengal famine is Churchill's darkest moment. It reveals the ugly side of his imperial worldview. The lesson: great leaders can have great blind spots. Churchill's greatness does not erase his failures.

> **Case: The 1945 Election Defeat (Chapter 31)**
> In July 1945, with WWII on the verge of victory, Churchill was rejected by the British people. Labour won in a landslide. Churchill was stunned. The man who had saved the country was voted out. The reason: the British people wanted a new direction — the welfare state, nationalization, decolonization. Churchill was a war leader. They wanted a peace leader.
> **Key takeaway:** Churchill's defeat in 1945 is the most important lesson in the book. The leader who is perfect for one moment can be wrong for the next. The lesson: the skills that make you successful in a crisis are not the same skills needed to build peace.
