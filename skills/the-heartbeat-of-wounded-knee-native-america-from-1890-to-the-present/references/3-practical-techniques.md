# Practical Techniques

## Overview

While *The Heartbeat of Wounded Knee* is a history rather than a how-to manual, it reveals techniques for survival, resistance, and cultural preservation that have been practiced by Native communities for generations. These are practical strategies employed by real people facing extraordinary challenges.

## The Sovereignty Strategy: Use the Master's Tools

Native activists and lawyers learned to use the American legal system to advance Native rights. This is not surrender to the system — it's strategic engagement.

**Case — Treaty lawyering:** Native nations have successfully sued to enforce treaties that the U.S. government violated. The Boldt Decision (1974) affirmed treaty fishing rights. The Cobell lawsuit (2009) resulted in a $3.4 billion settlement for the government's mismanagement of Indian trust funds.

**Case — Indian Child Welfare Act (1978):** Native advocates, including Treuer's mother, fought for this law, which gives tribal governments jurisdiction over adoption and foster care of Native children. The law was a direct response to the boarding school era's removal of Native children from their families.

## The Cultural Reclamation Protocol

The 1970s "Becoming Indian" movement was a conscious, structured process of cultural reclamation.

**Step 1: Learn the history.** Understand what was taken and why.
**Step 2: Learn the language.** Language carries worldview.
**Step 3: Learn the ceremonies.** Participate in community rituals.
**Step 4: Pass it on.** Teach others.

**Case — Language immersion schools:** The Cherokee Nation operates immersion schools where children learn Cherokee as their first language. This is a deliberate reversal of the boarding school policy of erasure.

**Case — Powwow revival:** The powwow tradition, suppressed for generations, was revived and transformed in the 1970s. Today, powwows are held year-round across the country, connecting Native communities and celebrating identity.

## The Urban Adaptation: Building Community Far from Home

Urban Natives who relocated in the 1950s created new institutions to maintain community.

**Technique — Urban Indian centers:** Natives in cities founded centers that provide health services, cultural programs, and social connection. These centers became the infrastructure of urban Native life.

**Case — Minneapolis American Indian Center:** Founded in 1975, this center provides health care, job training, and cultural programs. It was also the organizing ground for AIM. The center demonstrates how urban Natives rebuilt community from scratch.

## The Economic Sovereignty Toolkit

Tribes that have achieved economic success share common strategies.

**Technique — Diversification:** Successful tribes don't rely on a single revenue source. Casino revenue funds other enterprises — hotels, golf courses, convenience stores, construction companies.

**Case — Navajo Nation:* The Navajo Nation operates a diversified portfolio including coal mining, tourism, manufacturing, and renewable energy. This diversification provides stability when any single sector declines.

## The Digital Preservation Method

Native communities use technology to preserve culture for future generations.

**Technique — Digital archives:** Record elders speaking their language. Create searchable databases. Make them available online.

**Case — The Ojibwe People's Dictionary:** An online, searchable dictionary of the Ojibwe language with audio recordings from native speakers. Users can search in English or Ojibwe and hear correct pronunciations. This technology makes language preservation accessible to anyone with internet.

## The Storytelling Reclamation

Countering negative narratives requires telling your own stories in your own way.

**Technique — Indigenous media:** Native journalists, filmmakers, and writers create media from a Native perspective, countering stereotypes and telling authentic stories.

**Case — Treuer's own work:** As a writer and anthropologist, Treuer uses his craft to tell a different story about Native America. His novels, his memoir, and this history all serve the same purpose: "to communicate what it was that I loved" about his community.

**Case — Indigenous film:** The growth of Native filmmaking (Chris Eyre's *Smoke Signals*, Sterlin Harjo's *Reservation Dogs*) gives Natives control over their own representation. These works reach mainstream audiences with authentic Native voices.
