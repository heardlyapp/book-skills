# Core Framework: Seven Eras of Native Resilience

## Overview

Treuer organizes the book into seven parts, each covering a distinct era in post-1890 Native American history. The prologue establishes the Wounded Knee massacre as both the symbolic end of one era and the beginning of another. The epilogue brings the story to the present. Together, they form a complete counternarrative to the "vanishing Indian" myth.

## Part 1: Narrating the Apocalypse (10,000 BCE–1890)

The prologue establishes the context. The Wounded Knee massacre was not an isolated event but the culmination of centuries of colonization, war, and treaty-breaking. Frederick Jackson Turner's Frontier Thesis (1893) declared Native America finished. Dee Brown's *Bury My Heart at Wounded Knee* (1970), while influential, reinforced the narrative of Native death rather than Native life.

**Case — American Horse's testimony:** After the massacre, Chief American Horse described how women and children were chased down and killed, and how children who came out of hiding were "butchered." His testimony was recorded but did not change the narrative.

**Case — Lost Bird:** The infant found alive in her dead mother's arms. Her story became a symbol of survival — and of the continuing trauma imposed by the colonizers who "adopted" her.

## Part 2: Purgatory (1891–1934)

After Wounded Knee, reservation life became a kind of purgatory. The goal of the government was assimilation through three mechanisms: allotment (breaking up tribal lands into individual parcels), boarding schools, and the suppression of Native religions.

**Case — Allotment Act (Dawes Act, 1887):** The government divided reservation land into 160-acre parcels for individual Native families, declaring "surplus" land open to white settlement. Before allotment, Native nations controlled 138 million acres. After, they controlled 48 million — a loss of 90 million acres. The policy was sold as "civilizing" Natives.

**Case — Boarding schools:** The Carlisle Indian Industrial School and its imitators pursued "Kill the Indian, save the man." Children were forbidden to speak their languages, practice their religions, or wear their traditional clothing. Many died of disease and homesickness. Yet these schools also produced the first generation of Native professionals.

## Part 3: Fighting Life (1914–1945)

Native Americans served in WWI and WWII at higher rates than any other group. WWII was particularly transformative: 44,000 Native men and 800 Native women served. The war experience exposed Natives to the wider world, and returning veterans demanded their rights as citizens.

**Case — Navajo Code Talkers:** The Marines recruited Navajo men to create a code based on their language — the only military code never broken. The Navajo language had been suppressed in boarding schools. Now it was a strategic asset.

**Case — Returning veterans:** Native veterans returned from WWII to reservations without electricity, running water, or jobs. This disparity fueled the next wave of activism.

## Part 4: Termination and Relocation (1945–1970)

The 1950s brought two devastating policies: termination (ending federal recognition of tribes) and relocation (moving Natives off reservations to cities).

**Case — Menominee termination:** The Menominee of Wisconsin were one of the "successful" tribes targeted for termination. Their federal trust relationship was ended, their reservation became a county, and their tribal assets were distributed. Within a decade, the Menominee were destitute. The Menominee Restoration Act (1973) reversed the termination — the only tribe to successfully regain recognition.

**Case — Relocation to cities:** The Bureau of Indian Affairs moved Natives to Los Angeles, Chicago, Minneapolis, Denver, and San Francisco. Promised jobs and housing, many found slums and racism. But relocates formed urban Indian centers that became hubs of political organizing.

## Part 5: Becoming Indian (1970–1990)

The 1970s saw a cultural and political renaissance. The American Indian Movement (AIM) occupied Alcatraz, Wounded Knee, and the Bureau of Indian Affairs building in Washington.

**Case — Alcatraz occupation (1969-1971):** Activists claimed Alcatraz Island under the Treaty of Fort Laramie, which allowed Natives to occupy unused federal land. The 19-month occupation drew global attention and inspired a wave of Native activism.

**Case — Wounded Knee occupation (1973):** AIM members occupied the site of the 1890 massacre for 71 days, demanding treaty rights and an investigation of the Bureau of Indian Affairs. Two Natives were killed. The occupation ended in negotiations but forced the government to address Native grievances.

## Part 6: Boom City (1990–Present)

The Indian Gaming Regulatory Act (1988) transformed some tribal economies. Casinos became the most visible symbol of Native economic development.

**Case — Shakopee Mdewakanton Sioux:** This small Minnesota tribe operates Mystic Lake Casino, one of the most profitable in the country. Revenue has funded schools, healthcare, housing, and per capita payments. The tribe has moved from poverty to prosperity in one generation.

**Case — Uneven development:** Not all tribes have casinos, and not all casinos are profitable. Remote reservations lack the customer base for gaming. The "casino Indian" stereotype erases the many tribes still living in poverty.

## Part 7: Digital Indians (1990–2018)

The internet age has created new possibilities for Native identity and community. Social media, language apps, and online powwows connect the diaspora.

**Case — Language preservation:** YouTube and smartphone apps allow Native language teaching at a scale never before possible. Tribes are creating digital archives of elders speaking, interactive language courses, and online dictionaries.

**Case — Online community:** Native TikTok, Instagram, and Facebook groups create virtual spaces where urban Natives connect with their heritage. The digital reservation is borderless.
