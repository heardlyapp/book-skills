# Anti-Patterns: What Not to Do

## Overview

Treuer's book is, in large part, a critique of the anti-patterns that have shaped how America thinks about and treats Native peoples. These are not just intellectual errors — they have caused real harm for generations.

## Anti-Pattern 1: The "Vanishing Indian" Narrative

The belief that Natives were a dying race, destined to disappear before the march of civilization. This narrative served to justify land theft, assimilation policies, and the denial of Native rights.

**Why it fails:** It's factually wrong: there are more Natives today than at any point in the last 500 years. It also allows non-Natives to ignore contemporary Native issues by treating Natives as historical artifacts rather than living communities.

**Case — Frederick Jackson Turner's Frontier Thesis:** Turner argued that American history was a progression from "savagery" to "civilization," with Indians as a stage that had been passed. This became the dominant narrative for most of the 20th century, shaping textbooks, policy, and public opinion.

**Case — Dee Brown's *Bury My Heart at Wounded Knee*:** Despite being a sympathetic account, Brown's book reinforced the idea that Native history ended in 1890. The book's immense popularity (4 million copies sold) spread the "dead Indian" narrative more effectively than any political tract.

**Fix:** Always ask: "Where are the Native people today? What are they doing now?" If your mental image of Natives is historical, update it.

## Anti-Pattern 2: Reservations as "Basins of Suffering"

The dominant media image of reservations is one of poverty, alcoholism, violence, and hopelessness. While these problems are real, this framing is incomplete and dehumanizing.

**Why it fails:** It reduces complex communities to their worst problems. It denies the beauty, resilience, humor, and love that exist in these communities. Treuer writes: "I missed what I hadn't known was my Indian life, our collective Indian life."

**Case — Treuer's own journey:** When he left the reservation for Princeton, Treuer assumed he was leaving a place of suffering. Instead, he found himself homesick for the community he had taken for granted. The reservation was not just poverty — it was family, land, and belonging.

**Fix:** When learning about reservations, seek out multiple sources. Read Native authors. Visit tribal websites. Learn about tribal government, businesses, and cultural programs alongside the problems.

## Anti-Pattern 3: Treating All Natives as a Single Group

There are 574 federally recognized tribes in the United States, each with its own language, culture, history, and government. Treating "the Indian" as a monolith erases this diversity.

**Why it fails:** Policies that work for one tribe may fail for another. Stereotypes about Plains Indians (feathers, tipis, horses) don't apply to Woodland, Southwestern, or Northwest Coast tribes. Legal decisions affecting one tribe may not apply to others.

**Case — Casino stereotypes:** The "rich casino Indian" stereotype is applied to all tribes, but only about 240 of 574 tribes have gaming compacts, and only a handful have highly profitable casinos. Remote tribes with no customer base remain in poverty.

**Fix:** When discussing Native issues, specify which tribe or nation you're referring to. If you don't know, learn before speaking.

## Anti-Pattern 4: Ignoring Tribal Sovereignty

Treating tribes as simple minority groups rather than sovereign nations with government-to-government relationships.

**Why it fails:** Sovereignty is not just symbolic — it determines legal jurisdiction, land management, and the ability to make laws. Ignoring sovereignty means misunderstanding the entire legal and political framework of Native America.

**Case — Dakota Access Pipeline (2016):** The Standing Rock Sioux Tribe asserted its sovereignty and treaty rights to protest the pipeline's impact on its water supply and sacred sites. Non-Native supporters who treated the protest as a general environmental issue missed the core issue: tribal sovereignty.

**Fix:** Learn about the concept of tribal sovereignty. Understand that tribes are nations, not interest groups.

## Anti-Pattern 5: The "Authenticity" Trap

Judging Natives by how "authentic" they appear — expecting them to look, dress, and live like pre-contact ancestors.

**Why it fails:** This trap denies Natives the right to live in the modern world. A Native person who wears jeans, speaks English, and uses a smartphone is no less Native than an ancestor in buckskin.

**Case — Treuer's experience at Princeton:** A high school friend told him her parents wouldn't rent to Indians because "they're dirty and dangerous." When Treuer protested, she said "you're not really Indian." To be "really" Indian in her mind was to be the stereotype.

**Fix:** Native identity is not determined by appearance, dress, or adherence to stereotypes. It's determined by community recognition, tribal enrollment, and self-identification.

## Anti-Pattern 6: Historical Amnesia

Treating the colonization of North America as ancient history with no relevance to the present.

**Why it fails:** The most damaging policies — termination, relocation, boarding schools — ended within living memory. The effects of 500 years of colonization are still being felt. Treating these as "past" is a form of denial.

**Case — Boarding school survivors:** The last Native boarding school, run by the U.S. government, closed in the 1970s. Survivors are still alive. The trauma — family separation, abuse, cultural erasure — has been passed down through generations.

**Fix:** When discussing Native issues, acknowledge that the history is not distant. The policies that created current conditions — poverty on reservations, loss of language, distrust of government — are within living memory.
