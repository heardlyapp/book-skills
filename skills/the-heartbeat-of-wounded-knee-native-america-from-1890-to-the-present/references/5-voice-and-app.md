# Voice and Application

## Overview

*The Heartbeat of Wounded Knee* is a work of history, but its insights have practical applications for anyone interested in Native rights, cultural preservation, social justice, or understanding America's complex relationship with its Indigenous peoples. This file covers how to apply Treuer's framework across different domains.

## Finding Your Voice as an Advocate

Treuer writes as an insider — an Ojibwe from the Leech Lake Reservation who is also a PhD anthropologist and a novelist. His voice is personal, analytical, and unapologetically Native. The key: he doesn't write to explain Natives to non-Natives. He writes to tell his people's story on his own terms.

**Application for non-Natives:** Listen before speaking. Read Native authors. Follow Native journalists. The most important step non-Natives can take is to amplify Native voices rather than speaking for them.

**Case — Treuer's mother:** She returned to the reservation after law school to practice law. She didn't tell Natives what they needed — she used her skills to help them achieve what they already wanted.

## Application: Education

The book is an essential corrective to standard American history curricula, which largely ignore Native history after 1890.

**Prioritize:** The myth of the vanishing Indian, boarding schools, termination policy. These are the most neglected topics in standard education.

**Case — Curriculum reform:** Several states have passed laws requiring Native American history to be taught in public schools. *The Heartbeat of Wounded Knee* is being used as a textbook in some college courses.

**Case — Tribal colleges:** There are 38 tribal colleges and universities in the United States, serving primarily Native students. These institutions teach Native history from a Native perspective, preparing the next generation of Native leaders.

## Application: Activism and Organizing

The book provides historical context for current Native activism. Understanding the history of the movement informs strategy.

**Prioritize:** Sovereignty framework, treaty rights, cultural reclamation.

**Case — Standing Rock (2016):** The #NoDAPL movement drew on 130 years of activism strategy — occupation, media attention, legal challenge, coalition-building. Understanding the history of Alcatraz (1969) and Wounded Knee (1973) helps contextualize Standing Rock's tactics.

**Case — Land Back movement:** The contemporary movement for land return builds on centuries of treaty advocacy. The Biden administration's restoration of Bears Ears National Monument and the transfer of land to tribes continue this work.

## Application: Cultural Preservation Work

Native communities engaged in language preservation, cultural revival, and community building can use Treuer's historical framework to inform their work.

**Prioritize:** Language immersion, digital archives, intergenerational knowledge transfer.

**Case — Cherokee immersion schools:** The Cherokee Nation operates a K-8 immersion school where children learn Cherokee as their first language. This program is a direct response to the boarding school era's attempted erasure.

**Case — Digital archives:** The Ojibwe People's Dictionary and similar projects use technology to preserve languages for future generations. The approach is: record everything, make it accessible, teach it widely.

## Application: Legal and Policy Work

The book provides essential context for anyone working in federal Indian law, tribal governance, or Native policy.

**Prioritize:** Sovereignty, trust responsibility, treaty interpretation.

**Case — Brackeen v. Haaland (2022):** This Supreme Court case challenged the Indian Child Welfare Act, which gives tribes jurisdiction over Native child welfare. The Court upheld ICWA, but the battle demonstrated that sovereignty is never permanently settled.

**Case — Tribal tax compacts:** Growing numbers of tribes are negotiating tax compacts with states, asserting their sovereignty while creating shared revenue. This pragmatic approach builds on decades of legal precedent.

## Application: Journalism and Media

Journalists covering Native communities need to avoid stereotypes, respect sovereignty, and center Native voices.

**Prioritize:** Consult tribal sources, avoid the "vanishing Indian" framing, acknowledge sovereignty.

**Case — *Reservation Dogs* (FX):** This television series, created by Sterlin Harjo (Seminole/Muscogee), is the first show with all Native writers, directors, and regular cast. It presents reservation life with humor, complexity, and authenticity — a sharp contrast to the usual "suffering" narrative.

**Case — Native news organizations:** Indian Country Today, Native News Online, and tribal newspaper provide Native perspectives on current events. Non-Native journalists should read and cite these sources.

## The Broader Implications

Treuer's framework extends beyond Native America. His analysis of how dominant cultures write the history of colonized peoples applies globally. The techniques of cultural survival — adaptation, legal advocacy, storytelling, economic development — are used by Indigenous peoples worldwide.

The final message of the book: "We carry within us stories of our origins, and ideas about what our families, clans, and communities mean." The work of Native Americans since 1890 has been to protect those stories and ensure they continue. That work is not finished — and it never will be, as long as Native people exist.
