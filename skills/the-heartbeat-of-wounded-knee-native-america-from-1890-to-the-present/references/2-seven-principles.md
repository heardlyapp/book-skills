# Seven Principles in Depth

## Overview

Treuer's history reveals seven principles that have shaped Native American survival and resurgence since 1890. These are not abstract concepts but lived realities demonstrated through decades of policy, resistance, and adaptation.

## Principle 1: The Vanishing Indian Is a Myth

The most pervasive and damaging narrative is that Native Americans disappeared or are culturally extinct. Treuer shows this is demonstrably false.

**Case — Population recovery:** The Native population hit a low of about 237,000 in 1900. By 2010, the census counted 5.2 million Native Americans and Alaska Natives. Demographically, Natives are the fastest-growing ethnic group in the United States.

**Case — Language survival:** Of the approximately 300 Native languages spoken in 1492, about 175 are still spoken today. While many are endangered, tribes are actively reviving them through immersion schools, digital archives, and teaching programs.

## Principle 2: Reservations Are Not Prisons — They're Homelands

The popular image of reservations is one of poverty, alcoholism, and despair. This is not wrong — many reservations face severe challenges — but it is incomplete. Reservations are also places of cultural preservation, community solidarity, and political sovereignty.

**Case — Leech Lake Reservation:** Treuer's home reservation is described not as a "nowhere place" but as a community with deep roots, complex politics, and a vibrant if complicated social life. The poverty is real, but so is the love of place.

**Case — Tribal governance:** Many reservations have sophisticated governments with tribal courts, police forces, health systems, and schools. These institutions exercise genuine sovereignty, even within the constraints of federal law.

## Principle 3: Adaptation Is Not Assimilation

Natives have adopted Western tools — writing, English, law, business — without abandoning their core identities. This is often misunderstood as assimilation.

**Case — Native lawyers:** Treuer's mother became a lawyer and returned to the reservation to represent Native clients. She used the colonizer's legal system to defend Native rights. This is adaptation, not assimilation.

**Case — Code Talkers:** Navajo Marines used their suppressed language as a strategic weapon for the United States. They adapted a tool of war to their own purposes while maintaining their linguistic identity.

## Principle 4: Sovereignty Is the Core Issue

Everything — land, resources, culture, education, healthcare — depends on tribal sovereignty. The fight for sovereignty has been the unifying thread of Native politics for 130 years.

**Case — Indian Reorganization Act (1934):** This law reversed the allotment policy and allowed tribes to reorganize their governments. Over 170 tribes adopted constitutions under IRA.

**Case — Self-Determination Act (1975):** This law gave tribes the authority to administer federal programs themselves. For the first time, tribes could run their own schools, health clinics, and social services.

## Principle 5: Activism Works — But It Takes Generations

From the Ghost Dance to Alcatraz to the Dakota Access Pipeline protests, Native activism has a long history. Change comes slowly but it comes.

**Case — Fish-ins of the 1960s:** Tribes in the Pacific Northwest held "fish-ins" to protest state laws that violated treaty fishing rights. The protests led to the Boldt Decision (1974), which affirmed that treaties guarantee 50% of the salmon harvest to tribes.

**Case — NAGPRA (1990):** The Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act resulted from decades of activism demanding the return of ancestral remains and sacred objects from museums. Over 50,000 sets of remains have been repatriated.

## Principle 6: Economic Power Is Sovereignty

Without economic resources, legal sovereignty is hollow. The casino era showed that economic development can transform tribal communities.

**Case — Shakopee Mdewakanton:** This tribe used gaming revenue to build infrastructure, fund education, and provide healthcare. The casino made political sovereignty meaningful by giving the tribe financial independence.

**Case — Non-gaming tribes:** Tribes without casinos have found other paths — timber, mining, tourism, manufacturing. The key is controlling their own economic development rather than relying on federal funding.

## Principle 7: The Digital Future Is Native

The internet age offers unprecedented opportunities for Native communities to preserve culture, connect diaspora members, and tell their own stories.

**Case — Language apps:** The Duolingo Navajo course and apps from various tribes allow language learning at scale. Elders can record their knowledge for future generations.

**Case — Social media activism:** #NoDAPL (Standing Rock, 2016) showed how social media could mobilize global support for Native causes. The movement was the most visible Native activism since the 1970s.
