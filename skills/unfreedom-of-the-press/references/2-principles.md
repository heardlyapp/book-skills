# 2 — Media Bias and Agenda Setting

> Source: "Unfreedom of the Press" by Mark Levin

## Bias as Feature, Not Bug

Levin argues that media bias is not accidental — it's structural. News organizations hire like-minded people, and the result is a uniformly progressive worldview across major outlets.

## Agenda Setting

The media's most powerful function is agenda setting — deciding what stories to cover and how to frame them.

> **Case: The Russia Coverage** (Chapter on Modern Media): "The media coverage of the Trump-Russia story is a textbook case of agenda-driven journalism. For two years, the story dominated headlines based on unverified claims. When the Mueller report found no collusion, the coverage stopped without acknowledgment." Levin argues this was not reporting — it was activism.
> **Key takeaway**: When the media promotes a narrative for months or years and does not correct course when it's proven false, it is no longer reporting — it is advocating. The Russia coverage is a case study of agenda setting.

> **Case: The Access Problem** (Chapter on Government-Media Complex): "Journalists who cover Washington depend on access to government officials. This creates a symbiotic relationship: officials leak to friendly journalists, who then report favorably. The system is designed to produce coverage that serves the interests of both."
> **Key takeaway**: The independence of the press is compromised when journalists depend on government sources for their livelihood. The government-media complex ensures that the press serves power rather than checking it.
