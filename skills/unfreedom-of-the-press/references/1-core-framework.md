# 1 — The History of the American Press

> Source: "Unfreedom of the Press" by Mark Levin

## The Early Press

America's founding newspapers were openly partisan. The founders saw this as a feature, not a bug. A diversity of voices would prevent any single narrative from dominating.

## The Transformation

Journalism evolved through several stages: partisan press → penny press → objective journalism → interpretive journalism → activist journalism.

> **Case: The Partisan Press** (Chapter 2): "The early American press was fiercely partisan. Newspapers were founded by political parties and advocated for their causes openly. There was no pretense of objectivity." Levin argues that this is not necessarily bad — what matters is that many voices compete.
> **Key takeaway**: The founders did not expect or want a neutral press. They wanted a press that represented many viewpoints. The problem today is not that the press is partisan — it's that it's uniformly partisan on one side.

> **Case: The Loss of Objectivity** (Chapter on Modern Journalism): "For a brief period in the 20th century — roughly the 1950s through the 1970s — American journalism aspired to objectivity. That experiment is over. Modern journalism has abandoned objectivity for activism." Levin traces this transformation through key moments in media history.
> **Key takeaway**: Objective journalism was a historical anomaly. It lasted about 30 years. The media's current activist stance is a return to its partisan roots — but with a crucial difference: today's media is uniformly left-leaning, not diverse.
