# 3 — The First Amendment and Press Freedom

> Source: "Unfreedom of the Press" by Mark Levin

## Original Intent

The First Amendment prohibits Congress from abridging freedom of the press. The founders saw the press as a crucial check on government power.

## Modern Interpretation

Levin argues that the First Amendment has been misinterpreted to protect journalists while the press has abandoned its constitutional role.

> **Case: The Alien and Sedition Acts** (Chapter 3): "In 1798, Congress passed the Alien and Sedition Acts, which made it a crime to publish 'false, scandalous, and malicious' criticism of the government. This was an early test of press freedom. The public backlash was so severe that the Acts helped defeat John Adams in 1800."
> **Key takeaway**: The Sedition Acts were an early warning: government will always try to control the press. The founders' response — elect a new government — shows that the ultimate check on press abuse is political, not judicial.

> **Case: The Pentagon Papers** (Chapter 6): "The Pentagon Papers case (1971) established that prior restraint — government blocking publication — is almost always unconstitutional. This was a victory for press freedom. But the press has not always used this freedom responsibly." Levin argues that freedom without responsibility leads to abuse.
> **Key takeaway**: The press won the right to publish without prior restraint. But with that freedom comes the responsibility to be accurate and fair. Levin argues the modern press has failed in this responsibility.
