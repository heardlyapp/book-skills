# Resisting Evil — The Heroic Imagination

> Source: The Lucifer Effect, Chapter 16, Afterword

## The Heroic Imagination Project

Zimbardo's response to the Lucifer Effect was not despair — it was action. He founded the Heroic Imagination Project (HIP) to train people in situational awareness and heroic resistance.

### Ten Steps to Resist Unjust Systems

1. **Admit your own fallibility** — You are not immune to situational pressures.
2. **Be mindful of the power of the situation** — Recognize when you are in a situation that could corrupt you.
3. **Question authority** — Authority can be wrong. Learn to say no.
4. **Find allies** — Resistance is easier with support. Find others who see what you see.
5. **Keep your distance** — Physical and psychological distance from an unjust system helps you resist.
6. **Maintain personal accountability** — Do not let the system diffuse your responsibility.
7. **Resist the pressure to go along** — Conformity is powerful but not irresistible.
8. **Focus on the consequences of your actions** — Keep the human impact in mind.
9. **Cultivate a sense of personal responsibility** — "If not me, who?"
10. **Develop your heroic imagination** — Imagine yourself as the one who resists.

> **Case: The Milgram Disobeyers** (Chapter 15)
> In Stanley Milgram's obedience experiments at Yale, 65% of participants administered what they believed were lethal electric shocks to a stranger because an authority figure instructed them to. But 35% refused. What distinguished those who disobeyed? They did not accept the authority's definition of the situation. They challenged the experimenter. They questioned the purpose. They acted on their own moral judgment. Milgram's disobeyers are Zimbardo's evidence that resistance is possible — and teachable.
> **Key takeaway:** The 35% who disobeyed in Milgram's experiment are the most important people in the study. They prove that resistance is possible. What they shared: the ability to question authority, to recognize that the situation was wrong, and to act on their own judgment.

> **Case: The Good Guard** (Chapter 7)
> Zimbardo's "good guard" — an SPE guard who treated prisoners with respect — did not stop the abuse, but he did not participate. He was Zimbardo's first evidence that not everyone succumbs to situational pressure. Years later, Zimbardo asked him: why did you resist when others did not? The guard said: "I didn't see any reason to be cruel. It wasn't that I was braver than the others. I just didn't see the point." The good guard's resistance was not heroic — it was ordinary. That is the lesson: resistance does not require extraordinary courage. It just requires not joining in.
> **Key takeaway:** The good guard's resistance was not heroic — it was ordinary. That is the hopeful message: you do not need to be a hero to resist evil. You just need to refuse to participate.
