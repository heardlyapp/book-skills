# Author's Voice + Inspiration for the Next Generation

## Vashti Harrison's Vision

Vashti Harrison is an artist and filmmaker with a passion for storytelling. She earned her MFA in film/video from California Institute of the Arts, where she snuck into animation and illustration classes to learn from Disney and DreamWorks legends. This book grew out of a personal project: a drawing challenge to illustrate one African American woman from history every day for the month of February and post the finished image to social media with a brief summary of the woman's accomplishments.

> **Case: The Book's Origin** (Introduction + Acknowledgments): "I was surprised to be moved so deeply. As a black woman, I have studied the history of my people, but never had I felt this connected to the beauty and passion behind their boldness. Whether they were fighting for their families or for social justice or daring to become an artist or an astronaut, each one of these women broke barriers for those who came after her."
> **Key takeaway**: Vashti Harrison created this book because she wished she had known about these women when she was growing up. She imagined what kinds of dreams she might have had if she'd known that so many people who looked like her had done such incredible things.

## The Power of Representation

"I think about what kinds of dreams I might have had if I had known about all these women when I was growing up, if I'd known that so many people who looked like me had done such incredible things." This is the core message of the book: seeing yourself in someone else's story can be life changing. To know that a goal is achievable can be empowering.

> **Case: The Little Leaders Concept** (Introduction): Vashti Harrison writes: "I originally envisioned them as little girls serving as stand-ins for these famous women — that's why they're 'little' — but as I've started sending them out into the world, they've become bigger than I imagined. I designed them to be interchangeable because I want you, the reader, to see yourself in any one of them, and to feel their strength and possibility in you."
> **Key takeaway**: The "little leaders" are designed to be mirrors — readers are meant to see themselves in each illustration. The book is an invitation to imagine yourself as a leader, a pioneer, a changemaker.

## Creative Courage and Defying Expectations

"The book is for everyone — but it was created specifically for black girls who need to see that they belong in every field, in every story, in every history book." Vashti Harrison explicitly states that while the book is for readers of every background, she created it with black girls in mind.

> **Case: Octavia Butler's Defiance of Genre and Race** (Octavia Butler section): Octavia Butler began writing when she was ten years old. One day she was watching a bad science fiction movie on TV and was inspired to write something better. This was her first attempt at writing sci-fi — a genre that would become synonymous with her name. Despite struggling with dyslexia, she continued writing. In 1976, she published her first novel, Patternmaster — a science fiction novel with black female characters, which had never been seen before. Her masterpiece Kindred (1979) tells the story of a black woman who travels back to the time of slavery. She wanted to write "a story that would make others feel the history: the pain and fear that black people have had to live through in order to endure." Octavia created a space for black girls to like science fiction — a genre historically associated with white male writers and readers. In 1995, she was awarded the prestigious MacArthur Foundation Fellowship — the first science fiction writer to receive it.
> **Key takeaway**: Octavia Butler saw a gap in science fiction — there were no black women protagonists — and filled it herself. She didn't wait for permission. She wrote the stories she wanted to read. Her work proved that black women not only read science fiction, they write it brilliantly. She changed the industry's perception of who belongs in every genre.

> **Case: Maya Angelou's Five Years of Silence** (Maya Angelou section): After experiencing abuse at age seven, Maya Angelou never spoke again for five years. During those five years, she read. She devoured every book in the black school library and every book she could get her hands on from the white school library. It wasn't until a childhood mentor challenged her — "You don't really love poetry unless you speak it" — that Maya spoke again. She went on to write I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings. In 1993, she became the first female poet to write and recite a poem for a presidential inauguration.
> **Key takeaway**: Maya Angelou turned trauma into art. Her silence was not weakness — it was how she processed a pain too deep for words. When she found her voice, it became one of the most powerful voices in American literature. Her story shows that even the darkest periods of our lives can become the foundation for our greatest work.

## Key Scenarios

### Scenario 1: A Young Black Girl Feeling Invisible in History
Start with the Introduction. Then read about Octavia Butler, Mae Jemison, and Ruby Bridges. These women started as children who didn't see themselves in the stories around them — and ended up creating their own stories.

### Scenario 2: Someone Who Loves Arts but Is Told It's Not Practical
Read Zora Neale Hurston, Nina Simone, Gwendolyn Brooks, and Julie Dash. These women turned their creative passions into careers that changed culture. The arts are not a luxury — they are essential.

### Scenario 3: An Educator Looking for Diverse Curriculum Material
The book profiles 40 women across every field. Use it as a starting point for projects: assign each student a different woman to research further. The "Further Reading" section provides websites, books, films, and TV resources.

### Scenario 4: Someone Who Wants to Leave a Legacy
These women prove that legacy is not about fame or fortune. It's about the lives you touch, the doors you open, and the barriers you break. Read about Alice Ball (whose discovery saved lives but was credited to someone else), Mary Bowser (who helped win the Civil War but had to disappear), and Harriet Tubman (who gave away everything she had).

### Scenario 5: Parents and Mentors of Black Girls
This book is designed for you. Each two-page spread features one woman with a portrait and a concise biography. Read one profile per night. Talk about what "bold" means. Ask: "What would you be bold enough to try?"

> **Case: Oprah Winfrey's Journey** (Oprah Winfrey section): Oprah had a talent for speaking from an early age. After a childhood marked by abuse and neglect, she moved in with her father, who encouraged her to read and focus on her education. She landed a radio job in high school and eventually became a news anchor while still in college. She was demoted for getting too emotionally involved in her news stories, but soon found her home in talk shows. In 1986, she started her own production company, Harpo Productions, and became the first woman of color to own and produce her own television show. She became only the second African American woman billionaire. In 2013, President Barack Obama honored her with the Presidential Medal of Freedom.
> **Key takeaway**: Oprah Winfrey faced abuse, neglect, and career setbacks — and turned them into the engine of her success. She created her own production company to maintain creative control. She didn't just succeed within the system — she built her own system. Her story is a masterclass in turning obstacles into opportunities.
