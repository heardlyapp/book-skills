# Breaking Ground: Pioneers and Trailblazing Firsts

## The Central Theme

"Every woman in this book was a 'first' at something — the first African American woman to do what she did. These firsts were not just personal achievements; they were acts of resistance." By being first, each woman cracked the door open for everyone who came after her. The burden of being "the first" is heavy — you carry the weight of representation, the scrutiny of skeptics, and the isolation of being the only one.

> **Case: Rebecca Lee Crumpler, First Black Woman Physician** (Rebecca Lee Crumpler section): Rebecca attended a private school in Massachusetts and worked as a nurse for eight years. In 1860, she applied to the all-white New England Female Medical College — a bold and risky move. She was accepted and graduated in 1864, becoming the first African American woman physician in the United States. Out of more than fifty thousand physicians in the US at the time, only about three hundred were women, and Rebecca was the only black woman. In 1883, she published A Book of Medical Discourses, possibly the first medical article published by a black woman.
> **Key takeaway**: Rebecca Lee Crumpler was the only black woman physician in a country with over 50,000 doctors. She specialized in caring for women and children, working with the Freedmen's Bureau in the hostile post-Civil War South. Her solitude was not weakness — it was proof that she belonged.

## The Milestones of Being First

### First in Science and Medicine

"The women who broke into science and medicine faced the most rigid barriers — fields that were almost entirely white and male." Alice Ball developed the first effective treatment for leprosy at age 24 but died before receiving credit. Katherine Johnson calculated NASA's most critical trajectories. Mae Jemison became the first African American woman in space.

> **Case: Katherine Johnson's Mathematics at NASA** (Katherine Johnson section): Katherine skipped seven grades and graduated ahead of her older siblings. She became a math teacher but was encouraged to pursue research mathematics. In 1953, she got her chance at NASA's Langley Research Center, which had recently opened a lab that hired African American mathematicians. Her first assignment was to the flight research division during the space race — the 1950s and '60s competition between America and Russia for technological advancement in spaceflight. Her job was to calculate the flight path for the first mission in space. She continued computing at NASA until her retirement in 1986 and influenced every major space program up to that point. During the Friendship 7 mission in 1962, John Glenn — the first American to orbit the earth — refused to launch without verification that Katherine herself had double-checked the math.
> **Key takeaway**: Katherine Johnson's work was so trusted that an astronaut refused to fly without her personal verification. In 2015, President Barack Obama awarded her the Presidential Medal of Freedom, America's highest civilian honor. She was a "computer" before computers existed — and she was better than any machine.

### First in Politics and Law

"Black women had to fight for the right to vote, the right to practice law, and the right to hold political office — and they won every battle." Charlotte E. Ray became the first African American woman to graduate from law school in 1872. Shirley Chisholm became the first black woman elected to Congress in 1968 and the first woman to run for a major party's presidential nomination in 1972.

> **Case: Shirley Chisholm's Presidential Run** (Shirley Chisholm section): In 1964, Shirley Chisholm earned a seat in the New York State Assembly. In 1968, she became the first black woman elected to the US Congress. She made waves when she crossed party lines and stood up to the old leaders of her party. She was an independent thinker and never one to stay in her own lane. In 1972, she announced her run for president, making history as the first woman and first person of color to bid for a major party nomination. Her campaign slogan: "I am the candidate of the people of America." She ultimately lost, but she achieved her goal of making her party more aware of and responsive to the people.
> **Key takeaway**: Shirley Chisholm didn't run for president expecting to win. She ran to change the conversation. She said, "I am the candidate of the people," and she meant it — even though the political establishment wasn't ready for her. Her campaign paved the way for every woman and person of color who would run for president after her.

### First in Arts and Entertainment

"Black women were told their stories weren't worth telling. They told them anyway — and changed the culture." Gwendolyn Brooks became the first African American to win the Pulitzer Prize for poetry in 1950. Ella Fitzgerald became the first African American woman to win a Grammy in 1958. Nichelle Nichols became the first woman of color in a leading role on prime-time television as Lieutenant Uhura on Star Trek. Octavia E. Butler became the first science fiction writer to receive the MacArthur Foundation Fellowship in 1995.

> **Case: Nichelle Nichols and Dr. King** (Nichelle Nichols section): In 1966, Nichelle Nichols was cast in Star Trek as Lieutenant Uhura, becoming the first woman of color in history in a leading role on a prime-time television show. Uhura was smart, brave, dignified, and treated with respect. In 1968, she and one of her white cast mates shared the first-ever on-screen interracial kiss. The studio received letters of complaint and responded by cutting her lines and relegating her to the background. She considered leaving — until a chance encounter with Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. He urged her to stay, telling her that her representation mattered. Dr. Mae Jemison later named Nichelle as her inspiration to apply to NASA — and went on to become the first African American woman in space.
> **Key takeaway**: Nichelle Nichols's role on Star Trek did more than entertain — it inspired a generation. A fictional character on a television show helped produce America's first black female astronaut. That's the power of representation.

### First in Sports and Athletics

"Black women in sports faced segregation that literally kept them from competing." Althea Gibson became the first black tennis player at the US National Championships in 1950 and the first African American to win a Grand Slam in 1956. Wilma Rudolph overcame polio to become the first American woman to win three gold medals in a single Olympics in 1960. Dominique Dawes became the first African American woman on the US Olympic gymnastics team in 1988.

> **Case: Wilma Rudolph's Triumph Over Polio** (Wilma Rudolph section): When she was four years old, Wilma contracted polio, for which there was no cure at the time. The disease paralyzed her left leg, and she was forced to wear a metal leg brace. She was different and got teased. But with lots of hard work, determination, and physical therapy, she was walking without her brace by age twelve. A year later, she was running faster than all the boys and girls in her grade. By sixteen, she earned her first Olympic medal. In 1960 in Rome, she became the first American woman to win three gold medals in a single Olympics. When she returned home, a parade was organized in her honor — but it was planned to be segregated. Wilma refused to participate until they agreed to integrate it.
> **Key takeaway**: Wilma Rudolph went from a leg brace to Olympic gold in eight years. And then she used her platform to fight segregation. She is proof that physical limitations are no match for determination — and that success gives you power to create change.
