# Overcoming Barriers: Systemic Racism, Sexism, and Persistence

## The Central Challenge

"These women faced a double discrimination — racism because they were black, and sexism because they were women." The barriers were not personal failings but systemic: slavery, segregation, laws that denied them education, employment discrimination, and a history that simply erased their contributions. Yet each woman found a way through.

> **Case: Harriet Tubman's Brain Injury and Narcolepsy** (Harriet Tubman section): When Harriet was fifteen, she was accidentally hit in the head with an iron weight and fell into a coma for three days. Her brain injury resulted in narcolepsy — a permanent disorder that caused her to fall asleep at random times. Fearing her slave owner would discover her injury, she ran away. After escaping, she returned to the South thirteen times and led more than seventy men, women, and children to freedom via the Underground Railroad. She "never lost a passenger."
> **Key takeaway**: Harriet Tubman didn't just overcome racism and slavery — she overcame a debilitating physical condition. Her determination is a testament to the human spirit. When she could have stayed safe in the North, she chose to return again and again to danger.

## Key Barrier Patterns

### 1. The Double Discrimination of Racism and Sexism

"Black women faced discrimination not just from white society — they were often left out of the fight for equality itself." Sojourner Truth's famous "Ain't I a Woman?" speech was delivered because black women were being excluded from the women's rights movement. They faced racism from white suffragists and sexism from black leaders.

> **Case: Sojourner Truth's Fight for Her Son** (Sojourner Truth section): When Sojourner learned that her slave owner planned to keep her enslaved despite the Gradual Emancipation Act, she ran away with her infant daughter. But this came at a huge cost — she had to leave behind her five-year-old son, who was sold to a plantation a thousand miles away in Alabama. After her freedom was official, Sojourner filed a court case saying her son had been sold illegally. She was one of the first black women in America to file a court case. Against all odds, she won. She got her son back.
> **Key takeaway**: Sojourner Truth fought the system at every level — first as a slave, then as a black woman in a racist court system, then as a speaker challenging both racism and sexism. She couldn't read or write, but she made her voice heard.

### 2. Segregation and Legalized Discrimination

"No matter how accomplished these women were, segregation treated them as second-class citizens." Marian Anderson, one of the world's greatest opera singers, was barred from performing at Constitution Hall in 1939 because of her race. Althea Gibson, the first black tennis player to win a Grand Slam, still had to enter through the back door and couldn't eat with her fellow players.

> **Case: Rosa Parks's Calculated Defiance** (Rosa Parks section): In December 1955, after a long shift at work, Rosa Parks refused to give up her seat on a Montgomery, Alabama bus. She was arrested and jailed. But Rosa was not the first person to fight these laws — she had been arrested previously for defying bus rules. Her protest ignited a chain reaction. Martin Luther King Jr. called for a boycott of all public transportation in Montgomery. The Montgomery Bus Boycott lasted 381 days and ended with the integration of the bus system.
> **Key takeaway**: Rosa Parks's act is often portrayed as a simple act of tiredness. The reality: she was a trained activist and secretary of the Montgomery NAACP. Her defiance was strategic. She knew her arrest could spark a movement — and it did.

### 3. Invisibility in Historical Records

"Many of these women did extraordinary things and were then forgotten by history." Mary Bowser, who spied for the Union in Jefferson Davis's Confederate White House, was careful to conceal her identity after the war, using a variety of aliases. She eventually disappeared completely. Alice Ball's discovery was credited to the director of her program for decades. It wasn't until the 1970s that historians uncovered the truth.

> **Case: Ruby Bridges, Age Six** (Ruby Bridges section): In 1960, six-year-old Ruby Bridges became the first black student to attend an all-white school in New Orleans. Every step of the way was a challenge. Protesters surrounded the school. Ruby had to be escorted by her mother and US marshals to enter. White parents pulled their kids from classes. Most teachers refused to teach a black student. Only one teacher, Miss Henry, agreed — a young woman who had recently moved from Boston. Ruby thought the protests were a Mardi Gras celebration.
> **Key takeaway**: The youngest person in this book showed the greatest courage. Ruby didn't understand the full scope of what she was doing — but her bravery changed the course of American education. The vulnerability of a six-year-old child became a symbol of the fight for civil rights.

### 4. Financial Barriers

"Even after achieving extraordinary success, many of these women lived in poverty." Phillis Wheatley, despite being celebrated in Europe and corresponding with George Washington and Voltaire, struggled financially and lived in poverty for the rest of her life. Harriet Tubman gave away everything she had to help others. Augusta Savage struggled with finances and racism despite her artistic success. The barriers didn't end when they achieved their goals.

> **Case: Bessie Coleman Denied by Every School** (Bessie Coleman section): Bessie Coleman moved to Chicago after World War I. Her brothers told her about France, where women could fly planes. She applied to every aviation school in America she could find but was denied entry. No one thought a girl could fly — especially not a black girl. So in 1920, she moved to France, learned aviation, and graduated from the ten-month flight school in only seven months. In 1921, she became the first African American woman in the world to receive her pilot's license.
> **Key takeaway**: When every door in America was closed, Bessie Coleman didn't give up. She found a door in another country. Her determination to prove everyone wrong is a lesson in refusing to accept "no" as a final answer.
