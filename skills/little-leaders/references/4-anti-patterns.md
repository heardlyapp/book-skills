# Anti-Patterns: Correcting Common Misconceptions

## 1. Believing That History Has Been Fairly Told

**The mistake**: Assuming that the people in history books represent everyone who made significant contributions.

**Why it fails**: Alice Ball developed the first effective treatment for leprosy at the University of Hawaii in 1915. She was the first woman and first African American to earn a master's degree from the university. Less than two years after her discovery, Alice died. The director of her program took credit for her findings. Alice's method became known as the "Dean Method" — named after the man who stole her work. It wasn't until the 1970s that historians unearthed the truth and restored her name. On February 29, 2000, the University of Hawaii dedicated a plaque to her.

**Fix**: Question whose stories are being told and whose are being left out. The omissions in history books are not random — they reflect power structures.

> **Case: Mary Bowser, the Spy Who Disappeared** (Mary Bowser section): Mary Bowser was born into slavery in Richmond, Virginia, around 1840. She was purchased by the wealthy Van Lew family, who were secretly Northern spies and abolitionists involved in the Underground Railroad. Mary agreed to go undercover as a slave in Jefferson Davis's Confederate White House. She stole glances at confidential memos and eavesdropped on conversations between Confederate officials. She passed information about troop movements and army plans to Union officials. After the war, Mary educated freed slaves but was careful to conceal her true identity, using a variety of aliases. She eventually disappeared completely. In 1995, she was inducted into the Military Intelligence Hall of Fame.
> **Key takeaway**: Mary Bowser was one of the most effective spies of the Civil War. Her efforts directly helped the Union win the war. Yet her story was nearly lost to history because she had to hide her identity to survive. How many other Mary Bowsers are still hidden from history?

## 2. Thinking Segregation Was Only About the South

**The mistake**: Believing racism and discrimination were regional problems confined to the American South.

**Why it fails**: Raven Wilkinson, a ballerina, joined the Ballet Russe de Monte Carlo, which toured through the segregated South. Friends warned her she would never be accepted because she was black. She was fair-skinned, so she was encouraged to wear white makeup. She kept her race a secret but refused to lie when asked outright. Even in the North, opportunities were limited. She left dance for a while, exhausted and heartbroken, before being invited to join the Dutch National Ballet and moving to Holland.

**Fix**: Discrimination was — and remains — a national issue. The North had its own forms of segregation and exclusion. Black women faced barriers everywhere, not just below the Mason-Dixon line.

> **Case: Augusta Savage, Labeled a Troublemaker** (Augusta Savage section): Augusta Savage grew up in poverty in Florida with thirteen brothers and sisters. She had no toys but loved to sculpt with the natural red clay in her backyard. Her father did not approve of her creativity. In 1921, she moved to Harlem during the Harlem Renaissance. She thrived artistically, but this did not change the racism that was still prevalent. She openly fought racial prejudice in the art world and was labeled a troublemaker. She said that she was standing up not only for herself but for future students of color. Despite her artistic success — even creating a sculpture for the 1939 New York World's Fair — Augusta struggled with finances and racism until late in life.
> **Key takeaway**: Being talented was not enough. Augusta Savage was one of the most important artists of the Harlem Renaissance, yet she was labeled a troublemaker for fighting against racism in the art world. The system punished her for pushing back against injustice.

## 3. Equating Fame with Impact

**The mistake**: Measuring a woman's importance by how well-known she is today.

**Why it fails**: Some of the most impactful women in this book are the least known. Alice Ball's treatment for leprosy was used until the 1940s. Mary Bowser's intelligence work helped the Union win the Civil War. Charlotte E. Ray was only the third woman (of any race) to graduate law school in America. Phillis Wheatley was the first African American poet ever published — a monumental achievement given she was a slave.

**Fix**: Impact is not measured by fame. These women changed the course of history through their work, even if their names are not household ones.

> **Case: Phillis Wheatley's Unmatched Brilliance** (Phillis Wheatley section): Phillis Wheatley was taken from her home in Africa at around age eight, sold to a trader, transported across the Atlantic on a ship named the Phillis, and resold to John Wheatley as a personal servant for his wife. The Wheatleys recognized her intelligence and taught her theology, mythology, and literature — an education rare for a woman and nearly unheard of for a woman of color. She published her first poem at age fourteen. She published her book Poems on Various Subjects, Religious and Moral in London, becoming the first African American woman poet ever published. She corresponded with George Washington and Voltaire, who called her a master of English verse. Abolitionists used her work as an example of the intelligence and promise of black people. Yet she died in poverty, unable to find a publisher in America.
> **Key takeaway**: Phillis Wheatley's work was so powerful that it was used as political ammunition by the abolitionist movement. A slave girl who learned to read and write became one of the most important literary figures of the 18th century. But validation from white society was integral to her success — and it never came in her lifetime. Her natural talent and body of work live on as fundamental contributions to American literature.

## 4. Assuming That Once Doors Open, Everything Is Fine

**The mistake**: Believing that breaking a barrier means the struggle is over.

**Why it fails**: Althea Gibson became the first black tennis player to compete at the US National Championships in 1950 and the first African American to win a Grand Slam in 1956. Despite the barriers she broke in the public eye, segregation still kept her from entering through the front door, eating with the other players, and using the locker rooms. In 1958, she retired from tennis. Even though she was regarded as one of the best athletes in the world, it was difficult to make a living playing sports.

**Fix**: Breaking a barrier opens a door — but the room on the other side is not always welcoming. The struggle continues even after the firsts have been achieved. The work of inclusion is never finished.

> **Case: Angela Davis, Persecuted for Activism** (Angela Davis section): Angela Davis grew up in racially segregated Birmingham, Alabama. By her teens, she was organizing interracial study groups that were broken up by the police. She studied philosophy under Herbert Marcuse and became a prominent counterculture voice. She was affiliated with the Black Panther Party and the Communist Party USA. Both groups were considered dangerous, and she was put on the FBI's most wanted list. She was let go from her teaching job at UCLA for her activism. She was imprisoned on conspiracy charges, though she was acquitted sixteen months later. Despite all this, she maintained her devotion to teaching and remained a professor of philosophy into her seventies.
> **Key takeaway**: Angela Davis is living proof that activism comes at a personal cost. She was labeled a troublemaker, put on the FBI's most wanted list, imprisoned, and fired from her job. But she kept fighting. Her story shows that the struggle for justice is not a single battle — it's a lifelong commitment.
