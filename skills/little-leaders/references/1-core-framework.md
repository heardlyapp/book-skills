# Core Framework: The 40 Profiles

## The Central Story

"These 40 women were not exceptions — they were pioneers who refused to let racism, sexism, or segregation define what was possible." Vashti Harrison's book profiles 40 bold black women from the 1700s to the present day, spanning fields from science and politics to arts and athletics. Each woman broke barriers not just for herself, but for everyone who would come after her.

Each profile tells a story of boldness: a slave girl who became America's first published African American poet, a woman who "never lost a passenger" on the Underground Railroad, a mathematician who helped put an American on the moon, and a writer who redefined science fiction.

> **Case: Vashti Harrison's Purpose** (Introduction): "In a society where being black and female meant being an outsider or sometimes invisible, these women dared to go after what they wanted and demand what they deserved. Some of them were reluctant leaders, while others were not even conscious of their bravery, but their legacies live on to pave the way for more of us to follow."
> **Key takeaway**: The book was born from a personal drawing challenge during Black History Month. Harrison wanted black girls to see themselves in history and feel empowered to pursue their own bold dreams.

## The 40 Women and Their Fields

### Pioneers & Activists (1700s-1960s)
| Woman | Field | Key Accomplishment |
|-------|-------|-------------------|
| Phillis Wheatley | Poetry | First African American woman poet published (1773) |
| Sojourner Truth | Abolition, Women's Rights | "Ain't I a Woman?" speech; won lawsuit to free her son |
| Harriet Tubman | Abolition, Civil War | Conductor on the Underground Railroad; "never lost a passenger" |
| Mary Bowser | Espionage | Civil War spy in Jefferson Davis's Confederate White House |
| Ida B. Wells | Journalism, Activism | Anti-lynching campaign; refused to give up her train seat in 1884 |
| Rosa Parks | Civil Rights | Refused to give up bus seat, sparked Montgomery Bus Boycott |
| Mamie Phipps Clark | Psychology | Doll tests proved segregation harms black children; cited in Brown v. Board |
| Ruby Bridges | Civil Rights | First black student to attend an all-white school in New Orleans (age 6) |
| Angela Davis | Activism, Scholarship | Counterculture icon; fought for prison reform and against police brutality |
| Shirley Chisholm | Politics | First black woman in US Congress (1968); first woman to run for presidential nomination (1972) |

> **Case: The Scope Problem** (More Little Leaders — Page 82): The book acknowledges that it was extremely difficult to choose only 40 women. An additional section briefly profiles others: Mary Jane Patterson (first black woman college graduate), Madam C. J. Walker (first self-made female millionaire), Dorothy Height, Lorraine Hansberry, Alice Walker, Vernice Armour, Venus and Serena Williams, Misty Copeland, Gabby Douglas, Simone Manuel, and Carrie Mae Weems.
> **Key takeaway**: These 40 women represent a fraction of the bold black women who changed history. The book is designed as a starting point, not an encyclopedia.

> **Case: The Time Span** (Introduction): The women in this book span from the 1700s (Phillis Wheatley, born around 1753) to women still active today (Oprah Winfrey, born 1954; Lorna Simpson, born 1960; Dominique Dawes, born 1976). This span shows that black women have been achieving the extraordinary for over 250 years.
> **Key takeaway**: Black women's contributions to American history are not a recent phenomenon. They have always been there — they just haven't always been recorded.
