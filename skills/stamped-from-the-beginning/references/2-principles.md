# 2 — Key Historical Figures

> Source: "Stamped from the Beginning" by Ibram X. Kendi

## Cotton Mather (1663-1728)
Puritan minister. Promoted smallpox inoculation to Black people — but also promoted the idea that Black suffering was God's will. A segregationist who believed Black people could be "saved" but not equal.

## Thomas Jefferson (1743-1826)
The most important figure in the book. Author of the Declaration. Slaveholder. Wrote the most influential racist text of the 18th century. His ideas about Black inferiority shaped American thought for centuries.

> **Case: Jefferson's Notes** (Chapter on Jefferson): Jefferson wrote that Black people were "inferior in the endowments both of body and mind." He could not imagine a society where Black and white people lived together as equals. His "scientific" conclusions about race were adopted by generations of Americans.
> **Key takeaway**: Jefferson is the nation's original assimilationist — he believed some Black people could be "improved," but never equal. His views were not ignorance; they were deliberate ideology to protect slavery.

## William Lloyd Garrison (1805-1879)
Abolitionist. Started as colonizationist, became antiracist through engagement with Black abolitionists. Published "The Liberator."

## W.E.B. Du Bois (1868-1963)
The most complex figure. Started as assimilationist (talented tenth), evolved toward antiracism. Kendi shows Du Bois's internal contradictions honestly.

## Angela Davis (1944-present)
Prison abolitionist, feminist, antiracist. Represents the modern antiracist tradition.
