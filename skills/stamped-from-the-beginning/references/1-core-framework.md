# 1 — The Core Framework

> Source: "Stamped from the Beginning" by Ibram X. Kendi

## The Central Thesis

Kendi's revolutionary argument: racist ideas were not the cause of discrimination — they were created to justify it. As he puts it: "Racist policies produce racist ideas, not the other way around."

## The Three Camps

- **Segregationist**: Believes racial groups are permanently and fundamentally unequal. Advocates for separation or hierarchy.
- **Assimilationist**: Believes some members of a racial group can be "raised" to equality by adopting dominant culture. "Some are good."
- **Antiracist**: Believes all racial groups are equal. The problem is not the group — it's the racist policies targeting the group.

> **Case: The Founding Contradiction** (Jefferson Chapter): Thomas Jefferson wrote "all men are created equal" while owning over 600 human beings. He also wrote "Notes on the State of Virginia," arguing Black people were inferior. Kendi shows Jefferson wasn't a hypocrite — he was a segregationist who needed racist ideas to justify his slaveholding.
> **Key takeaway**: The most "enlightened" Americans have been the most effective promoters of racist ideas. Intelligence and education do not prevent racism — they can make it more sophisticated.

> **Case: The Antiracist Evolution** (Garrison Chapter): William Lloyd Garrison started as a colonizationist (assimilationist who wanted freed slaves sent "back" to Africa). Through engagement with Black abolitionists, he became a true antiracist — demanding immediate, unconditional abolition and equal rights. His evolution shows that change is possible.
> **Key takeaway**: People can change. Garrison went from assimilationist to antiracist. The key was listening to Black leaders rather than speaking for them.
