# Principles: Coalition Building and Time Management Across History

Mitchell traces how seven great powers across fifteen centuries used diplomacy to survive against overwhelming odds. This reference distills the strategic principles embedded in those historical cases.

## Principle 1: Buy Time at Any (Reasonable) Cost

Across every case in the book, time is the diplomatic trump card. Chrysaphius bought time for Byzantium by showering Attila with gifts. Venice bought time through complex financial negotiations. Metternich bought time for Austria through endless congresses. Nixon and Kissinger bought time for America through secret channels to Beijing.

> **Case: Venice vs. the Ottoman Empire** (Chapter 3): After the fall of Constantinople in 1453, Doge Francesco Foscari faced the expansionist Mehmed II on one side and the mainland rival Milan on the other. Venice's strategy was to buy time by negotiating with both, paying tribute when necessary, and building intelligence networks. Venice survived as a great power for centuries by never fighting a war it could postpone.
> **Key takeaway:** "Buying time" sounds weak but it is often the strongest strategic move. Time allows you to recruit allies, build resources, and wait for your enemy's position to weaken. The diplomatic question is never "Can I avoid this war?" but "When will I be in the best position to fight it?"

## Principle 2: Turn Enemies into Allies

The most dramatic diplomatic moves involve bringing former enemies to your side. Archidamus urged Sparta to ally with Persia. Richelieu allied Catholic France with Protestant German princes. Kaunitz orchestrated the "Diplomatic Revolution" of 1756, turning archrival France into an ally against Prussia. Nixon went to China after two decades of hostility.

> **Case: The Diplomatic Revolution of 1756** (Chapter 5): Count Kaunitz, Maria Theresa's minister, engineered one of history's great diplomatic reversals: an alliance between Habsburg Austria and Bourbon France, enemies for centuries. The goal was to isolate Frederick the Great's Prussia. France gained a reliable ally against Britain. The alliance was a masterpiece of long-term strategic thinking — Kaunitz spent years cultivating his French contacts before the deal was struck.
> **Key takeaway:** The most powerful diplomatic move is to convert an enemy into an ally. This requires patience, face-saving mechanisms, and an understanding that today's enemy may be tomorrow's partner if interests align.

## Principle 3: Keep Your Enemies Divided

The fear all great powers share is fighting on multiple fronts simultaneously. The most successful diplomatic strategies are those that keep enemies divided and prevent hostile coalitions from forming.

> **Case: Bismarck's Alliance System** (Chapter 6): After unifying Germany in 1871, Otto von Bismarck faced the nightmare scenario: a two-front war with France (seeking revenge for defeat) and Russia. He built an elaborate web of alliances — the Three Emperors' League, the Dual Alliance with Austria, the Reinsurance Treaty with Russia — designed to keep France isolated and prevent any anti-German coalition from forming. The system worked for two decades. When Bismarck was dismissed, his successors let it collapse. Germany was soon fighting on two fronts.
> **Key takeaway:** The goal of great-power diplomacy is not just to build your own alliances but to prevent hostile coalitions from forming. This is often the more important task. The worst outcome is fighting on two fronts — avoid it at almost any cost.

## Principle 4: Use Money as a Strategic Weapon

Mitchell traces the role of financial resources in diplomacy across all seven cases. Byzantine gold bought off barbarians. Venetian ducats hired mercenaries and bought time with the Ottomans. British subsidies bankrolled continental allies against Napoleon and Kaiser Wilhelm. American Marshall Plan aid built the postwar alliance system.

> **Case: Britain's Continental Strategy** (Chapter 7): Britain, a naval power, always needed continental allies to fight its land wars. From the Napoleonic Wars through both world wars, Britain used financial subsidies to maintain those allies. The British "golden bridge" kept coalitions together when military setbacks would otherwise have caused defection. It worked brilliantly against Napoleon and in WWI. In WWII, financial exhaustion meant Britain could no longer sustain this role, and the US had to take over.
> **Key takeaway:** Financial resources are as important to diplomacy as military forces. They buy allies, keep coalitions together, and provide leverage in negotiations. A state with deep pockets can sustain diplomatic strategies that a poorer state cannot.

## Principle 5: Bureaucracy Is Both Tool and Enemy

Diplomacy requires institutional memory — archives, trained professionals, and continuity. The Byzantines created a "bureau of barbarians" to track the dozens of tribes around their borders. Venice's intelligence network was the envy of Europe. But bureaucracy can also become a straitjacket. Kissinger famously sought to sideline the State Department bureaucracy to conduct secret diplomacy with China.

> **Case: Kissinger vs. the State Department** (Chapter 8): Nixon and Kissinger's opening to China required absolute secrecy. The State Department bureaucracy, with its pro-Taiwan biases and institutional caution, would have killed the initiative. Kissinger bypassed it entirely, using backchannels through Pakistan and Romania. The result was one of the most successful diplomatic initiatives of the 20th century — but it came at the cost of institutional legitimacy and alienated many in the foreign service.
> **Key takeaway:** Bureaucracy preserves institutional knowledge but stifles innovation. Great diplomats know when to use it and when to circumvent it. The key is having the judgment to know the difference.
