# Techniques: The Diplomat's Toolkit

Mitchell's book is organized around historical cases rather than explicit technique manuals, but the methods used by great power diplomats across the centuries form a consistent toolkit. This reference extracts those techniques.

## Technique 1: The Gift Economy

The oldest diplomatic technique: use material inducements to influence behavior. Byzantium gave Attila gold and gifts to "abate his anger." Venice used trade concessions. Britain offered subsidies. America used Marshall Plan aid.

> **Case: Chrysaphius and the Huns** (Chapter 2): The eunuch chamberlain Chrysaphius understood something the Byzantine generals did not: Attila could not be defeated, but he could be managed. The Huns were a nomadic confederation that needed plunder to hold together. By providing regular "gifts" (effectively tribute in exchange for peace), Byzantium gave Attila a reason to stay peaceful — and a convenient target in the Western Roman Empire when he needed plunder. The gifts were expensive, but far cheaper than war.
> **Key takeaway:** Know what your enemy values and use it as leverage. For the Huns, it was gold and plunder. For modern powers, it may be trade access, technology, or diplomatic recognition. The art is giving enough to influence behavior without giving so much that you appear weak.

## Technique 2: The Negotiated Delay

This is the core of the Archidamus strategy: use negotiations not to reach agreement but to gain time. Talk while preparing for war. Send emissaries with complex proposals that require lengthy study. Demand consultations with allies. Create procedural obstacles to immediate action.

> **Case: Venice's Delaying Tactics** (Chapter 3): When Mehmed II began threatening Venetian possessions in the eastern Mediterranean, Venice responded not with immediate military action but with a flurry of diplomatic missions. Each envoy carried elaborate proposals that required weeks of negotiation. Meanwhile, Venice fortified its outposts, recruited mercenaries, and sought allies. The delays meant Venice faced the Ottomans on its own timetable, not Mehmed's.
> **Key takeaway:** Every negotiation you can postpone is a battle you don't have to fight unprepared. The question is always: will I be in a better position to negotiate or fight tomorrow than I am today? If the answer is yes, delay.

## Technique 3: Secret Diplomacy

Some of the most important diplomatic moves in history were conducted outside official channels. Metternich maintained private correspondence with foreign leaders. Bismarck used secret treaties alongside public ones. Kissinger's secret trip to China was the culmination of months of clandestine communication through Pakistani intermediaries.

> **Case: Nixon's Secret Channel to Beijing** (Chapter 8): Nixon and Kissinger established a secret communications channel with China through Pakistan in 1970. Messages were hand-delivered by Pakistani diplomats, bypassing the State Department, the CIA, and normal diplomatic protocols. This secrecy was essential — any leak would have triggered a political firestorm from pro-Taiwan conservatives and likely killed the initiative before it could bear fruit.
> **Key takeaway:** Public diplomacy has its place, but the most important strategic moves often require secrecy. Secrecy allows you to explore options without commitment, protect yourself from domestic opposition, and catch adversaries by surprise.

## Technique 4: The Spoke-and-Hub System

Rather than direct negotiations between all parties, one power acts as the hub connecting multiple spokes. This gives the hub power over information and agenda-setting. Kissinger used this in his Middle East shuttle diplomacy. Metternich used it in the Congress of Vienna. The key is controlling the flow of information.

> **Case: Metternich's Congress System** (Chapter 5): After Napoleon's defeat, Metternich created a system where Austria acted as the hub of European diplomacy. Great powers would meet periodically — at Aix-la-Chapelle, Troppau, Laibach, Verona — with Austria setting the agenda, controlling information flows, and shaping outcomes. The system maintained peace for thirty years and gave Austria influence far beyond its military strength.
> **Key takeaway:** Controlling the diplomatic process is almost as important as controlling the outcome. The power to set agendas, choose venues, and manage information flows gives a diplomat enormous leverage.

## Technique 5: The Credible Threat

Diplomacy without military leverage is weakness. The most successful diplomatic strategies combine negotiation with the credible threat of force. Bismarck famously said that the great questions of the day would be decided by "iron and blood" — but his diplomacy was designed so that blood was never actually shed.

> **Case: Bismarck's "Iron and Blood"** (Chapter 6): Bismarck was both a diplomat and a military strategist. His alliances were backed by the most powerful army in Europe. But he was careful never to issue ultimatums he couldn't enforce or make threats he wasn't prepared to carry out. The credibility of German military power made his diplomacy effective — states took his proposals seriously because they knew the alternative was war with Prussia.
> **Key takeaway:** Your diplomatic proposals must be backed by the credible threat of force. But credibility means only making threats you are prepared to execute. Bluffing in diplomacy is dangerous — once discovered, your leverage is gone.

## Technique 6: The Door Left Open

Always leave your adversary a path to a negotiated settlement, even while preparing for war. This gives them an incentive to negotiate rather than fight to the finish. Archidamus urged Sparta to "remonstrate in a tone not too suggestive of war, nor again too suggestive of submission." The open door gives the enemy a face-saving way to avoid conflict.

> **Case: The Paris Peace Accords** (Chapter 8): Nixon and Kissinger combined intense military pressure on North Vietnam (bombing campaigns, mining of harbors) with a constant offer of negotiated settlement. The message was clear: we can hurt you, but we can also make peace. The combination of coercion and offer eventually produced the Paris Peace Accords. While the outcome was imperfect, the principle was sound.
> **Key takeaway:** Even while applying maximum pressure, always leave a path to negotiation. If you corner an adversary with no way out, they have no incentive not to fight to the death. The open door gives them a reason to compromise.
