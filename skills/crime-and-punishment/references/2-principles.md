# 2 — Key Principles

> Source: *Crime and Punishment* by Fyodor Dostoevsky — synthesized from the entire novel.

## The 7 Principles

### 1. The Conscience is Inescapable

You cannot outrun your own guilt. Raskolnikov was never caught by Porfiry Petrovich — he was caught by his own dreams, fevers, and the unbearable weight of silence. "The criminal lives in a state of terror of his own conscience."

### 2. Confession Begins the Journey Back

Raskolnikov's confession to Sonya in Part 4 is the turning point of the novel. Not because Sonya can forgive him, but because speaking the unspeakable aloud breaks the seal of isolation. The confession to Sonya precedes the legal confession by weeks — because emotional confession must come first.

### 3. Suffering Accepted Becomes Purification

Sonya's advice to Raskolnikov at the crossroads: "Go to the crossroads, bow down, kiss the earth, and say to everyone, 'I killed!' " This is not religious theater. It's the acceptance of public shame — the opposite of the proud, secret crime. In prison, the other convicts despise Raskolnikov for his pride. Sonya, who has suffered without pride, is loved by all.

### 4. The "What If?" Poison

Raskolnikov constantly revisits his crime: "What if I had done it differently? What if I'd been smarter?" This is the poison of unaccepted reality. The way out is to accept what was done and face the consequences — not to endlessly re-litigate the past.

### 5. Love is the Only Antidote to Isolation

Throughout the novel, everyone reaches toward Raskolnikov: Razumikhin, his mother, Dunya, Sonya, even Porfiry. He pushes them away. The restoration begins when he accepts Sonya's love in the Epilogue. "Love had resurrected them."

### 6. Dreams Speak the Unspoken Truth

The nightmare of the beaten mare (Part 1) reveals Raskolnikov's subconscious horror at violence — before his intellect has justified it. The pestilence dream (Epilogue) shows the logical endpoint of his theory: a world where everyone believes they're extraordinary descends into chaos and mutual destruction. Your dreams are your conscience's testimony.

### 7. The Ordinary is the Sacred

Raskolnikov despised ordinary life. He wanted to be extraordinary. In the end, what saves him is the most ordinary thing: human love, the riverbank, the sunrise. Sonya doesn't offer him a brilliant theory — she offers her presence.
