# 4 — Anti-Patterns

> Source: *Crime and Punishment* by Fyodor Dostoevsky — across all six parts.

## Common Errors Exposed by the Novel

### Anti-Pattern 1: The Utilitarian Rationalization

**The trap:** "The greatest good for the greatest number justifies this harm."

**Why it fails:** The "arithmetic" works on paper but doesn't account for the psychological cost on the perpetrator. The student in the tavern says "one death and a hundred lives in exchange is a matter of arithmetic!" But Raskolnikov discovers that the arithmetic was always a lie he told himself. He can't even bring himself to use the money.

> **Case: The Tavern Calculus** (Part 1, Chapter VI): "One death and a hundred lives in exchange — it's arithmetic!" But the officer sees through it: "If you won't dare to do it yourself, it's not a matter of justice."

### Anti-Pattern 2: The "Clean Theory, Dirty Hands" Split

**The trap:** You can carefully plan a transgression, execute it cleanly, and return to normal. The theory says it's clean; the hands don't know.

**Why it fails:** The psychic cost doesn't care about your theory. Raskolnikov planned the murder for a month but had a complete psychological breakdown from the moment he swung the axe. "It was the devil that killed the old woman, not me."

### Anti-Pattern 3: Secrecy as Strength

**The trap:** "I can carry this alone. I don't need anyone to know."

**Why it fails:** Raskolnikov's isolation is his punishment. The novel shows again and again: hiding the crime is worse than the crime. "I cannot live without you. I'll go with you to prison."

### Anti-Pattern 4: The "I'll Make It Right Later" Gambit

**The trap:** "I'll do this bad thing now and use the benefit to do good later."

**Why it fails:** The bad thing changes you. The benefit doesn't materialize (Raskolnikov never uses the money). And even if it did, the person who emerges from the crime is not the person who planned it.

### Anti-Pattern 5: Self-Deification

**The trap:** "I am extraordinary. The laws that apply to ordinary people don't apply to me."

**Why it fails:** The moment you believe you're above judgment, you've lost the capacity for self-correction. Raskolnikov says: "The man who dares much is right in their eyes." But he discovers he didn't dare enough — he merely destroyed himself.

> **Case: Raskolnikov's Self-Indictment** (Part 5, Chapter IV): "I didn't kill the old woman — I killed myself." The projected victim was always himself. The crime was suicide disguised as murder.

### Anti-Pattern 6: Rejecting Love as Weakness

**The trap:** "I don't need anyone. Love would only complicate my plan."

**Why it fails:** Raskolnikov pushes away Razumikhin, Dunya, his mother, Sonya — everyone who loves him. His isolation is both the cause of his crime and its punishment. The restoration only begins when he accepts Sonya's love in the Epilogue. "The heart of one contained infinite sources of life for the heart of the other."

> **Case: Razumikhin's Loyalty** (Throughout): Razumikhin suspects Raskolnikov's involvement but never abandons him. He takes care of Raskolnikov's mother and sister, keeps his secrets, and plans to follow him to Siberia. Love that persists despite knowledge of wrongdoing — that's the counterforce to the theory.
