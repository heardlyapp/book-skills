# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *Crime and Punishment* by Fyodor Dostoevsky (tr. Michael R. Katz) — distributed across all six parts and the Epilogue.

## The Three Interlocking Frameworks

### Framework A: The Raskolnikov Fallacy (Extraordinary Man Theory)

**The theory (articulated by Raskolnikov, overheard in the tavern):** "Ordinary people are meant to live in obedience and have no right to transgress the law. Extraordinary people have the right to commit any crime and transgress the law in any way, precisely because they are extraordinary."

**The flaw:** Who decides who is extraordinary? The man who calls himself extraordinary has already corrupted his own judgment. "If the question occurs to me: am I a Napoleon? — then clearly I am not."

**The consequence:** Raskolnikov doesn't discover he's extraordinary through success — he discovers he's ordinary through failure. The test proves him human, not exempt. "I didn't kill the old woman — I killed myself."

> **Case: The Tavern Conversation** (Part 1, Chapter VI): A student and an officer discuss the old woman. The student argues: "Murder her and take her money — thousands of good deeds would make up for one little crime." The officer asks: "Would you kill her yourself?" The student: "Of course not!" Theory is easy. Action reveals the truth.
> **Key takeaway**: The distance between intellectual justification and actual execution is where humanity lives.

### Framework B: The Punishment Before the Crime

The novel's structural genius: the psychological punishment begins before the murder. The "crime" occupies Part 1 (out of 6 parts + epilogue). The "punishment" fills everything else.

**The progression:**
1. **Isolation** — Raskolnikov stops seeing people, stops eating, stops living in the real world
2. **Fever** — The body reacts before the mind admits guilt
3. **Paranoia** — Every glance, every word from others is a threat
4. **Self-contradiction** — He tries to help (Marmeladovs) while hiding murder; the two impulses tear him apart
5. **Dream invasion** — The mare dream before the murder, the pestilence dream after

> **Case: The Fainting at the Police Station** (Part 2, Chapter I): Raskolnikov, having committed the murder, walks into the police station on trivial business and faints when the talk turns to the crime. His body betrays him before his mind decides to confess.
> **Key takeaway**: You cannot hide a crime from your own nervous system.

### Framework C: Svidrigaylov's Mirror

Svidrigaylov is Raskolnikov's double — the man who acts on every impulse without intellectual framework. If Raskolnikov believes the "extraordinary" are beyond good and evil, Svidrigaylov *lives* that belief.

**The mirror reveals:**
- Raskolnikov theorizes about violating others → Svidrigaylov actually does it (Dunya, young girls)
- Raskolnikov can't use the stolen money → Svidrigaylov gives his money away before suicide
- Raskolnikov talks about death → Svidrigaylov actually kills himself
- Raskolnikov is tortured by conscience → Svidrigaylov sees ghosts

> **Case: Svidrigaylov's Suicide** (Part 6, Chapter VI): After failing to win Dunya, Svidrigaylov wanders the streets, leaves money to Sonya for the orphans, and shoots himself. He's the dark path fully walked. Raskolnikov, who contemplates suicide, recoils — and chooses to turn himself in.
> **Key takeaway**: Your double is not your enemy — they're you without your resistance. They show where your philosophy leads when all brakes are removed.
