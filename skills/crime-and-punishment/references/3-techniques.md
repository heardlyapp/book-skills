# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *Crime and Punishment* by Fyodor Dostoevsky — practical methods extracted from the narrative.

## Practical Tools

### Technique 1: The Crossroads Confession

**When you need to confess something you've been hiding:**
1. **Find your Sonya** — one person who can hear the full truth without flinching but also without letting you off easy. Not a judge (Porfiry), not an admirer (Dunya), not a mirror (Svidrigaylov). Someone who will say both "I will go with you" and "You must accept suffering."
2. **Speak the words aloud** — Write them if you must, but read them aloud. The act of externalizing the secret breaks its power.
3. **Face the shame publicly** — Raskolnikov kissed the earth in the Haymarket. Find your equivalent: admit your wrongdoing to the person you harmed, or to a neutral third party.
4. **Accept the consequences** — Don't bargain. Raskolnikov didn't negotiate his sentence. He accepted it.

> **Case: The Confession to Sonya** (Part 4, Chapter IV / Part 5, Chapter IV): "I came to tell you who killed Lizaveta." The confession took two visits — the first announced, the second delivered. Sonya's response: "What have you done, what have you done to yourself?" Not "You're a monster" but "What have you done to yourself?"

### Technique 2: The Dream Audit

**When your subconscious is signaling trouble:**
1. **Record your dreams** — The mare dream preceded the murder. The pestilence dream followed the sentence. Dreams are the conscience speaking when the intellect sleeps.
2. **Ask what the dream reveals about your actual feelings** — The mare dream showed Raskolnikov's childhood horror at violence. His waking mind had justified it. The truth was in the dream.
3. **Look for bodily symptoms** — Fever, headaches, sleeplessness, fainting. Raskolnikov's body knew before his mind accepted guilt.

### Technique 3: The Double Check

**Identify your Svidrigaylov — the person in your life who represents your unexpressed darker impulses:**
1. Who makes you uncomfortable because they remind you of something in yourself?
2. What trait do they embody that you've been suppressing or rationalizing?
3. Projection check: If you're disgusted by them, what behavior of theirs mirrors something you've done or considered?

> **Case: Raskolnikov and Svidrigaylov** (Part 4, Chapter I / Part 6, Chapter III): Svidrigaylov says to Raskolnikov: "We're birds of a feather." Raskolnikov is horrified because he sees the logical conclusion of his own philosophy.

### Technique 4: The Porfiry Protocol (Recovery Under Investigation)

**When you're being investigated (interpersonally or professionally) and you're guilty:**
1. **Establish your baseline honesty** — Porfiry studied Raskolnikov's normal behavior before interrogating him.
2. **Don't overperform innocence** — Raskolnikov's elaborate games made Porfiry more suspicious. The more you protest, the more you signal.
3. **Let the investigator do the work** — Porfiry had all the psychology but almost no evidence. Raskolnikov kept convicting himself.
4. **Silence is safest** — Raskolnikov's longest winning streak with Porfiry came when he simply refused to engage.

> **Case: The Three Meetings with Porfiry** (Parts 3-6): In the first meeting, Raskolnikov is aggressive and ironic. In the second, he nearly collapses. Only Nikolai's false confession saves him. In the third, Porfiry openly tells Raskolnikov he knows — but advises him to confess anyway.
