# Reference: Neuroplasticity Foundations

## Concept

Neuroplasticity — from the Greek *plastikos*, meaning "mouldable" — is the brain's lifelong ability to reorganize itself by forming new neural connections in response to experience, learning, and injury. Nicole Vignola opens *Rewire* with a powerful image: holding a real human brain in her hands as a first-year neuroscience student and realizing she was touching an entire life — every memory, every relationship, every joy and sorrow.

The brain is not a fixed machine. It is malleable. And that is the single most hopeful fact in all of neuroscience.

## The Mechanism

The human brain contains approximately 100 billion neurons, each communicating at junctions called synapses. When two neurons fire together repeatedly, the synapse between them strengthens. As Nicole puts it: "Neurons that fire together, wire together." This is the fundamental principle of Hebbian learning — the cellular basis of memory, habit, and identity.

A helpful analogy: a small footpath turns into a dirt road, which becomes a gravel road, then a paved street, then a highway. Your repeated thoughts and behaviors literally carve neural highways into your brain.

The process works in both directions: you can strengthen pathways through repetition, AND you can weaken them over time through conscious non-use. All the behaviors and patterns you're trying to break can absolutely be dismantled.

## Case 1: The Author's Low-Power Mode Realization

As an exhausted undergraduate who had just moved to a new city, Nicole's iPhone ran low on battery and prompted her to switch to "low-power mode." She had a lightbulb moment: the brain is the **hardware**, and thoughts, habits, and behaviors are the **software**. When the hardware is running low (tired, stressed, malnourished), it cannot run software updates. You cannot rewire when your brain is in survival mode.

This insight reframes "laziness" or "lack of motivation" as a physiological limitation. If you have been struggling to concentrate at work, getting sick all the time, and feeling apathetic — you have probably reached your limits. Your brain is not going to prioritize homework or meetings. It is going to prioritize finding energy. Choosing delivery over cooking is not a character flaw; it is a sign of depletion.

## Case 2: Gene McGehee — Dementia and the Subconscious

Gene McGehee, an elderly man with dementia whose memory reset every 15 minutes, went viral on TikTok. Every day he came out of his home with his chair at a particular time — and never understood why. A school bus of children got off at the bus stop across from his house and ran over to say hello. For three years, these children visited him daily. And every day he thought he was meeting them for the first time.

Yet something in his subconscious — some deep pattern of ingrained behavior — compelled him to go outside at that exact time. This demonstrates that we don't always need conscious awareness to perform actions. The subconscious brain runs the show most of the time. The implication: you can reprogram yourself below the level of conscious thought.

## Critical Periods vs. Adult Plasticity

Children experience **developmental plasticity** — a state where the brain is rampant with brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF), a molecule that supercharges new synapse formation. Children absorb language, culture, and behavior without effort because their brains are optimized for absorption.

In adulthood, plasticity requires conscious attention and repetition. The reticular activating system (RAS) filters billions of sensory inputs down to what is relevant. If you want to learn French as an adult, you cannot just play recordings in the background — you must pay deliberate attention to the words. But it is still possible.

## The Piano Experiment (Pascual-Leone et al., 1995)

One of the most cited studies in the book: participants who learned a five-finger piano sequence showed cortical map expansion in the motor cortex. A separate group who **only mentally rehearsed** the sequence — without touching a piano — showed nearly identical cortical changes. While the physical practice group developed faster execution speed, the mental rehearsal group proved that thought alone can induce structural brain change.

This is the proof-point for visualization: your brain cannot tell the difference between a vividly imagined experience and a real one, at least not at the level of neural pathway formation.

## BDNF and Adult Plasticity

BDNF is the molecule that helped children form new synapses so easily. In adults, BDNF production is lower — but it can be increased through exercise, learning, and novel experiences. This is why new experiences are so powerful for rewiring: they tell the brain "this is important, pay attention."

## Implications for the Skill

When a user says "I can't change, I'm wired this way," this reference provides the counterargument: neuroplasticity is a biological fact, not a motivational slogan. The brain is designed to change. What feels like a permanent personality trait may just be a well-worn neural pathway. And pathways can be rerouted with attention, intention, and repetition.
