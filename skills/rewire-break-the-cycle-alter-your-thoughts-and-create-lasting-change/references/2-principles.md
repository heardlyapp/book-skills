# Principles: Negativity Bias and the NeuroToolkit

> Source: Rewire, Phase 1

## Negativity Bias

The brain evolved to scan for threats. Negative events stick like Velcro; positive ones slide off like Teflon. This bias makes it harder to form positive habits and easier to fall into negative thought loops. The fix is conscious attention and structured reframing.

> **Case: The Job Interview Scar Study (Kleck & Strenta, 1985):** Participants who believed they had a facial scar (actually removed without their knowledge) reported that interviewers treated them worse — even though interviewers behaved identically. Your belief about how others see you changes how you experience reality.

> **Case: The Milkshake Experiment (Crum et al., 2011):** Participants were given a 380-calorie milkshake but told it was either "indulgent" (620 cal) or "sensible" (140 cal). Those who believed it was indulgent showed a steeper ghrelin drop. Your beliefs directly affect your physiology.

## Affect Labeling

Simply naming an emotion ("I feel anxious") reduces amygdala activity (Lieberman et al., 2007). Verbalizing feelings calms the fear response. This is the first and most powerful tool in the NeuroToolkit.

## The NeuroToolkit (4 Steps)

1. **Acknowledge** — Bring subconscious patterns to conscious attention.
2. **Leverage cues & triggers** — Change environment cues to interrupt automatic responses.
3. **Analyze & reframe** — Write the thought, examine its validity, replace with evidence-based counter.
4. **Don't beat yourself up** — Self-criticism is a false attempt to regain control. It doesn't work.

## Creeping Normality

The gradual acceptance of deteriorating conditions. Small changes accumulate until you're living far below your standards without noticing. The fix: create an objective baseline and ask "If this had happened overnight, would I have accepted it?"
