# Reference: Stress, Negativity Bias & Breaking the Cycle

## Concept

Phase 1 of the Rewire Protocol is called "Ditch the Negative" — but this is not about toxic positivity. It is a neuroscientific dismantling of the brain's built-in negativity bias and the stress response that keeps us stuck. The core insight: not all stress is bad, and negativity is a biological default — not a character flaw.

## The Three Types of Stress

Nicole breaks stress into three categories, each requiring a different response:

### 1. Alert Stress (Healthy)
Adrenaline and cortisol at functional levels. Required for performance, focus, motivation, exercise, and meeting deadlines. Calling this "stress" is misleading — it is readiness.

### 2. Acute Adaptive Stress (Positive)
Short-term bursts that build resilience. The first exam, the first public talk, the first marathon. The body adjusts, learns coping mechanisms, and comes out stronger. This is why Nicole stopped panicking about MSc deadlines after surviving her BSc: the adaptive stress taught her she could handle it.

### 3. Maladaptive/Chronic Stress (Harmful)
When demands exceed capacity for too long. The brain enters "low-power mode." Key structural changes occur:

- **Frontal cortex** — shrinks, impairing decision-making and impulse control
- **Amygdala** — enlarges, causing hypervigilance and exaggerated emotional responses
- **Hippocampus** — degrades, damaging memory and the ability to shut down the stress response

Nicole's own experience: "I lived most of my twenties in a chronic state of emotional dysregulation and maladaptive stress. I was always getting sick, drawn to junk food, and a mess."

## Case: The Stress-Is-Functional Study (Jamieson et al., 2012)

Participants were told that their physiological stress response (racing heart, sweating) was functional and adaptive — it was preparing them to perform better. During a stressful task, these participants showed lower heart rate and blood pressure compared to the control group. The perception of stress as helpful literally changed the body's response to it.

This is not denial. It is reframing. The physiological arousal is real — but the meaning you assign to it determines whether it helps or harms you.

## The Negativity Bias (Velcro/Teflon)

The brain evolved to prioritize threats over rewards. Negative experiences stick like Velcro; positive ones slide off like Teflon. This kept our ancestors alive (better to mistake a stick for a snake than a snake for a stick), but it is maladaptive in modern life.

**Effect on relationships:** You have 10 positive interactions with your partner and 1 negative one. Which do you remember at 2 AM? The negative one. This bias must be actively counteracted.

## Affect Labeling — The Amygdala Hack

One of the most powerful tools in Phase 1: simply naming your emotion reduces amygdala activity. Lieberman et al. (2007) showed that "putting feelings into words" shifts activity from the emotional amygdala to the prefrontal cortex. This is not venting — it is precise labeling. "I feel anxious" is different from "I feel angry." Precision matters.

## Creeping Normality

The gradual acceptance of deteriorating conditions. Like the boiled frog: small changes accumulate until you're living below your standards without noticing.

**Method:** Identify one area where you've accepted less than you deserve. Ask: "If this had happened overnight, would I have accepted it?"

## Case: The Invisible Scar Study (Kleck & Strenta, 1985)

Participants believed they had a facial scar applied with makeup. Before the interview, the experimenter said "let me fix it" — but actually removed the scar entirely. The participant entered the interview believing they had a visible scar. Afterward, they reported that interviewers treated them worse, avoided eye contact, and seemed uncomfortable. In reality, interviewers behaved identically to the control group.

Your belief about how others see you changes how you experience reality. This is the power of confirmation bias: you see what you expect to see.

## Case: The Milkshake Experiment (Crum et al., 2011)

Participants received a 380-calorie milkshake. Half were told it was "indulgent" (620 calories); half were told it was "sensible" (140 calories). Despite all receiving the exact same shake, the "indulgent" group showed a significantly steeper drop in ghrelin (the hunger hormone). Their belief about what they consumed altered their hormonal response.

The implication: your beliefs directly affect your physiology, not just your mood.

## The NeuroToolkit (Phase 1)

Four action steps for ditching the negative:

1. **Acknowledge** — Bring subconscious patterns to conscious attention. A friend told Nicole she complained too much. It hurt — but it gave her awareness.
2. **Leverage cues & triggers** — Change environmental cues so the old pattern cannot activate.
3. **Analyze & reframe** — Write the negative thought. Examine its factual basis. Replace with evidence-based counter.
4. **Don't beat yourself up** — Self-criticism is an attempt to regain control through punishment. It does not work. Self-compassion is more effective than self-flagellation.

## Somatic Tools

- **Physiological sigh:** Double inhale through nose, long exhale through mouth. Resets the autonomic nervous system (Balban et al., 2023).
- **Nature exposure:** A one-hour walk in nature decreases amygdala activity (Sudimac et al., 2022).
- **EMDR-inspired eye movements:** Bilateral stimulation reduces the emotional charge of traumatic memories (de Voogd et al., 2018).

## When to Use This

Activate this reference when the user is in a negative spiral, feeling stuck, stressed, or ruminating. The goal is not to suppress negativity — it is to understand its biological origin and apply targeted tools to dismantle it.
