# Core Framework — The Heat Will Kill You First

## Prologue: The Goldilocks Zone

Extreme heat is "the engine of planetary chaos" — the first-order effect driving all other climate impacts. "You probably think of heat as a gradual, linear thing. But the kind of heat I'm talking about here is an active force, one that can bend railroad tracks and kill you before you even understand that your life is at risk."

**The Pacific Northwest heat dome (June 2021):** Temperatures in Portland jumped from 76°F to 114°F in 24 hours — the hottest in 147 years. Official death toll: 1,000+. Rosemary Anderson (67) found dead in her house at 99.5°F indoor temp. Jollene Brown (63) found in her La-Z-Boy. Lytton, BC reached 121°F, then burned to the ground. A billion sea creatures died. "When heat comes, it's invisible. The ground doesn't shake. It just surrounds you and works on you in ways you can't anticipate or control."

## Chapter 1: A Cautionary Tale — The Gerrish Family

Jonathan Gerrish (software engineer at Snapchat), Ellen Chung (yoga teacher/counselor), and their one-year-old daughter Miju moved to Mariposa, California to get closer to nature. On August 15, 2021, they set out on an 8-mile hike. Temperature at 11:56 AM: 107°F. They had 85 ounces of water for three people. Gerrish attempted a text: "can you help us... No water or ver heating with baby." All three died. So did their dog Oski.

Goodell's investigation: "If there is one idea in this book that might save your life, it is this: The human body, like all living things, is a heat machine." Exertional heatstroke hits the young and fit. Even ultramarathoners like Philip Kreycik (37) and Michael Popov died on runs in the heat. Hydration alone doesn't prevent heatstroke — "proper hydration cannot prevent heatstroke."

## Chapter 2: How Heat Shaped Us

Humans evolved to run. Persistence hunting: early humans ran down animals until they collapsed from heat exhaustion. "We are the only animal that can run a marathon on a hot day." But the heat we're creating now exceeds our evolutionary design parameters. "For the last 125,000 years, the Earth has never been hotter than it is today."

**Wet bulb temperature:** 95°F is the theoretical limit of human adaptability to humid heat. Beyond that, your body generates heat faster than it can dissipate it. Parts of Pakistan and India already exceed this threshold for weeks each year.

## Chapter 3: Heat Islands

Vivek Shandas drove around Portland during the heat dome: Lents (poor neighborhood, few trees, lots of concrete) = 124°F. Willamette Heights (million-dollar homes, tree-lined) = 99°F. "In a heat wave, wealth can afford twenty-five degrees of coolness."

**The legacy of redlining:** Formerly redlined neighborhoods (where banks refused mortgages to Black families) are still hotter today — 5-12°F hotter than whiter neighborhoods — because they were denied trees and parks.

## Chapter 4-5: Life on the Run & Anatomy of a Crime Scene

**The great migration:** Animals are moving to higher, cooler latitudes at 1 mile/year average. Malaria-carrying mosquitoes: 2.5 miles/year. "Robins can migrate more easily than elephants."

**Heat as a killer:** 489,000 deaths annually from extreme heat (2019) — more than guns (250K) or illegal drugs. Heat also amplifies smog (ozone), wildfire smoke (260K-600K deaths annually). Smoke from BC wildfires causes hospitalizations 3,000 miles away on the East Coast.

## Chapter 6-7: Magic Valley & The Blob

**Agriculture:** 21% loss in global agricultural production in 20 years. The Idaho potato industry is threatened. Dairy cows produce less milk in heat. "In a hotter world, your french fries cost more."

**The Blob:** A marine heat wave in the Pacific (2013-2016) killed 1 million seabirds, collapsed the cod fishery, and shifted tuna migration patterns. The ocean absorbs heat equivalent to 3 nuclear bombs every second.

## Chapter 8: The Sweat Economy

**Outdoor workers:** Farmworkers, construction workers, delivery drivers — they can't work from air-conditioned offices. In Florida, proposed laws would have banned heat safety requirements for employers. "There is no right to stop work in extreme heat." Heat exposure costs the global economy $16 trillion since 1990s.

## Chapter 9: Ice at the End of the World

Greenland loses 280 billion tons of ice per year. Antarctica: 150 billion tons. The meltwater drives sea level rise — but also slows ocean currents, disrupts weather patterns, and could trigger feedback loops.

## Chapter 10: The Mosquito Is My Vector

Heat expands the range of disease vectors. Malaria, dengue, Zika — all moving north. By 2080, half the world's population could be exposed to dengue fever.

## Chapter 11: Cheap Cold Air

AC is a lifesaver but an environmental disaster. It consumes enormous energy (mostly fossil-fuel generated) and leaks refrigerants that are thousands of times more potent than CO2 as greenhouse gases. "We're cooking ourselves to cool ourselves."

## Chapter 12-14: What to Do

**The gap:** Even if we transition quickly to clean energy, half the world's population will be exposed to life-threatening heat and humidity by 2100. "One to three billion people will be left outside the climate conditions that gave rise to civilization."

**Adaptation:** Shade trees. White roofs. Cooling centers. Behavioral changes. But Goodell argues these are insufficient. "If you have enough money, you can engineer your way out of anything. But eight billion people are not going to thrive by cranking up the AC."

**Geoengineering:** Solar radiation management (injecting sulfur into the stratosphere) is controversial but may be necessary. "We are in the terraforming business now."

**The White Bear:** The arctic is warming 4x faster than the global average. The last refuge is melting.
