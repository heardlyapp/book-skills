# Reference: Negotiation Basics

## Overview
Voss's approach starts with tactical empathy: understanding emotions without agreeing. Voice tone, mirroring, labeling, and silence are the foundational techniques.

## Case: The Late-Night FM DJ Voice
When negotiations get tense, most people's voice rises in pitch and speed. This signals fear and weakness. Voss practices a calm, slow, downward-inflected voice he calls the late-night FM DJ voice. It signals confidence and control. In one hostage situation, a calm voice prevented a suicide. The voice communicates: "I am in control of myself, and the situation is safe."

## Case: Mirroring in a Hostage Crisis
Voss describes a hostage negotiation where a bank robber said "I don't trust you." Voss mirrored: "You don't trust me?" The robber elaborated. Mirroring — repeating the last 1-3 words — is the simplest and most effective way to keep someone talking and gathering information.

## Case: The Power of Silence
After mirroring or labeling, silence is essential. The other person will fill the silence with information. Voss says the negotiator who speaks first after an offer loses. Silence is uncomfortable but productive.

## Case: Labeling Emotions
Voss teaches labeling as a way to diffuse negative emotions. In a hostage situation, he would say "It sounds like you're feeling frustrated." The label creates connection. The other person feels understood. The negative emotion loses its power. Labeling works because it activates the other person's prefrontal cortex, helping them regulate their emotions.
