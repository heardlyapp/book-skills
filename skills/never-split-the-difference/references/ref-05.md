# Reference: Deep Principles

## Overview
The deepest principles of Voss's approach challenge conventional negotiation wisdom and reveal why emotional intelligence beats logical argument.

## Case: The Three Types of Yes
Voss identifies three types of yes: counterfeit (said to get rid of you), confirmation (agreement on a point), and commitment (a binding agreement). Most yeses in negotiation are counterfeit. The skilled negotiator does not celebrate "yes" until it is a commitment yes.

## Case: Black Swans
The Black Swan is the hidden piece of information that can change everything. Voss tells the story of a hostage negotiation where the key was discovering that the hostage taker was a veteran and establishing rapport through shared military experience. The Black Swan was something neither side knew about the other until a specific question revealed it.

## Case: Leverage
Leverage in negotiation is not about power — it is about the other person's perception that they need you. Voss identifies three types: positive leverage (I can give you what you want), negative leverage (I can take away what you have), and normative leverage (you should do this because it is right/normal/consistent with your values). Normative leverage is often the most powerful.

## Case: The "That's Right" Breakthrough
Voss emphasizes that getting to "that's right" is the goal of the summary phase. After mirroring, labeling, and calibrating questions, summarize the other person's position. If your summary is accurate, they will say "That's right." That is the moment when real negotiation begins. From "that's right," you can move toward a solution.
