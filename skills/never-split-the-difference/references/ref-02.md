# Reference: Advanced Techniques

## Overview
Once the basics are mastered, calibrate questions, the accusation audit, and the "that's right" breakthrough are the next level.

## Case: The Calibrated Question
"How am I supposed to do that?" is the most powerful calibrated question. When a hostage taker demands $10 million, instead of saying no, ask "How am I supposed to do that?" The question shifts the burden to them. They must think about how to solve the problem. In business, when a client demands an impossible deadline, "How am I supposed to do that?" opens the conversation.

## Case: The Accusation Audit
Before the other person can accuse you, accuse yourself. Voss starts difficult conversations with "You're probably thinking I don't care about your position" or "You're probably going to feel like this is unfair." By labeling the negative ahead of time, you remove its power.

## Case: Getting to "That's Right"
The goal is not "yes" but "that's right." Voss describes a hostage situation where he summarized the captive taker's position so accurately that the person said "That's right" — and then surrendered. When a hostage taker says "that's right," they have accepted your understanding of their situation. Breakthrough occurs.

## Case: The Rules of "Yes" and "No"
Voss reframes "no" as the start of negotiation. He advises using "Is now a bad time to talk?" rather than "Do you have a few minutes?" The first invites "no" — and when someone says "no," they feel in control. The second invites "yes" — which is often a counterfeit yes. When you get a real "no," the conversation has truly started.
