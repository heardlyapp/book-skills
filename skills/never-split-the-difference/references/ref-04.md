# Reference: Difficult Situations

## Overview
Some negotiations are inherently adversarial. Voss's techniques for dealing with difficult people and high-stakes situations are tested in the most extreme circumstances.

## Case: Dealing with "F-U" People
Some people seem impossible to negotiate with. Voss calls them the "F-U" crowd. His technique: use the accusation audit to neutralize their aggression. "You probably think I'm wasting your time and that this is going nowhere." The accusation audit labels their likely emotions, which diffuses them.

## Case: The Bunker Door
Voss describes negotiating to get the FBI into a bunker with a bank robber who had taken hostages. The robber had a revolver and seemed determined. Voss used labeling to connect: "It sounds like you feel there's no way out." The robber said "That's right" — and then surrendered. The breakthrough came from understanding, not pressure.

## Case: The "How" Question
At the end of a negotiation, ask "How will we implement this?" The question reveals whether the other side is committed. If they cannot answer, they are not truly committed. "How" questions also identify potential problems before they arise.

## Case: The "Is Now a Bad Time" Opener
The best way to start any negotiation is with a "no" question. "Is now a bad time?" invites "no," which makes the other person feel in control. Once they say "no" and you accept it, they are more open to engaging with you. This technique works in sales, salary negotiations, and everyday conversations.

## Case: Pacing the Emotional High Ground
When the other person is angry or upset, do not match their emotional state. Use labeling: "It sounds like you're really upset about this." The label acknowledges their emotion without escalating it. Stay calm. Your calm will eventually bring their emotional level down.
