# Techniques — Outlaw Platoon

## How to Lead Under Pressure (Based on Sean Parnell)

### Technique 1: Arrival Moment
Parnell describes his "Arrival Moment" — the realization that leading is about presence, not bravado. "I realized that my job was not to be their friend, their father, or their hero. My job was to be their platoon leader. That meant being there, every time, no matter what."

### Technique 2: Trust Your NCOs
Parnell's Platoon Sergeant Sabatke, Staff Sergeants Greeson and Baldwin — these men had more combat experience than Parnell could imagine. He learned to listen to them. "When the bullets start flying, rank doesn't matter. What matters is who keeps their head."

### Technique 3: The Bloody Uniform Ritual
After the child died in his arms, Parnell burned his bloodstained uniform. The act was symbolic — shedding the old skin, putting on the new one. "I closed my eyes and forced the pain down into a deep and remote place." This is not suppression in the unhealthy sense — it's compartmentalization, a survival mechanism.

### Technique 4: The Darkness Is Coming — Prepare
Parnell realized on his first day that the sergeants were hardened because they had been through this before. The technique: you cannot avoid the darkness. You can only prepare yourself to carry it.

### Technique 5: Fight for Your Men's Humanity
The Outlaws could have devolved into brutality. Parnell fought to keep them human — treating detainees with dignity, protecting civilians, mourning the enemy dead. "The enemy is not subhuman. They are people fighting for what they believe."

### Technique 6: The Debrief
After every firefight, Parnell gathered his men to talk through what happened. Not a formal after-action review — just sharing, processing, making sure everyone was still present. "The talking helped. It didn't fix anything, but it helped."

### Technique 7: The St. Christopher Medal
Parnell's St. Christopher medal (patron saint of travelers) was his anchor. He touched it before every patrol. He asked Dave Taylor to return it to his mother if he died. The technique: carry something that connects you to home, to family, to the person you were before. It reminds you what you're fighting for.
