# Core Framework — Security Analysis: Sixth Edition

## The Great Illusion of the Stock Market (Introduction)

David Barams: "Investing looks easy, particularly in a world of inexpensive software and online trading." The "Great Illusion" is that anyone can get rich easily in the stock market. "All the people I know who've built wealth in the stock market have worked very hard at it." Case: The Internet bubble of 1999-2000, the mortgage market implosion — investors who chased fads got burned. The quote from Graham: "Many securities must be examined before one is found that has real possibilities for the analyst. Mainly by hard and systematic work."

## The Margin of Safety (Core Concept)

The single most important concept in value investing: buying a security at a significant discount to its intrinsic value. "The margin of safety is the difference between the price you pay and the intrinsic value you receive." Case: If a stock is worth $100 and you buy it at $70, your $30 margin of safety protects you against errors, bad luck, or market volatility.

## Intrinsic Value vs. Market Price

"Markets are not always efficient. Price and value diverge — sometimes wildly." The analyst's job is to calculate intrinsic value and buy when price is below that value. Case: The Barnsdall Oil warrants — "Graham and Dodd correctly conclude that these warrants were undervalued because the market priced them at their intrinsic value."

## Security Analysis: The Process

"Analysis will lead to a positive conclusion only in the exceptional case." Most securities are fairly priced or overpriced. The analyst spends most of their time eliminating candidates — and only occasionally finds a real opportunity. "Hard and systematic work" is the only path.

## Part VII: Additional Aspects

Covers stock-option warrants (Chapter 46), management compensation and stock options, shortcomings of relative value analysis, and the greed of investment bankers. "How markets work, how companies are run, and how people tend to act in certain situations never change." Case: The Japanese stock market bubble of the late 1980s — an example of extreme valuation divergence that value investors avoided.

## The Future of Value Investing

Barams is optimistic: "Rather than a glut of Graham and Dodd acolytes, money is ever more prone to sloshing around in giant waves, flowing from one fad to the next." The value approach remains viable because markets are perpetually prone to extremes. Case: "If anything, it seems that people controlling these mega-sums have become less intelligent and less sophisticated over time."
