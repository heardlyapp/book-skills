# Key Principles — Security Analysis: Sixth Edition

## I. Price Is What You Pay, Value Is What You Get

"The price of a security is not its value." The market's price is determined by supply and demand, emotion, and noise. Intrinsic value is what a careful analysis reveals. Case: "A stock trading at $50 might be worth $100 — or $20."

## II. The Margin of Safety Is Everything

"The margin of safety is the central concept of investment." Buy at a discount to intrinsic value. The bigger the discount, the safer the investment. Case: Warren Buffett has said he read the 1940 edition "at least four times" and built Berkshire Hathaway on this principle.

## III. Hard Work and Systematic Analysis

"Many securities must be examined before one is found that has real possibilities." The analyst's work is elimination, not discovery. Most securities are not bargains. Case: "All the people I know who've built wealth in the stock market have worked very hard at it."

## IV. Markets Are Emotional, Not Rational

"The Great Illusion of the Stock Market." Most market participants believe they can get rich quickly. This creates bubbles and crashes. The value investor profits from the emotional extremes of others. Case: "The Internet bubble was followed by utter collapse. Then the mortgage meltdown."

## V. Human Nature Doesn't Change

"How markets work, how companies are run, and how people tend to act never change." Greed and fear are constant. Investment fads come and go, but the principles of value remain. Case: "The Japanese stock market of the late 1980s reached ludicrously overvalued heights."

## VI. Management Incentives Matter

Chapter on stock options and management compensation: "When management issues options to itself, there is a potential decrease in common stock value." Align incentives. Case: Management overpaying themselves through option grants is a red flag.

## VII. Discipline Over Prediction

"Analysis will lead to a positive conclusion only in the exceptional case." The value investor doesn't predict the market — they calculate value and wait. "You don't need to predict the future. You need to know what something is worth today."
