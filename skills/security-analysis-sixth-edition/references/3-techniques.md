# Techniques — Security Analysis: Sixth Edition

## How to Analyze Securities Like Graham and Dodd

### Technique 1: The Margin of Safety Calculation
Determine intrinsic value through analysis of assets, earnings, dividends, and future prospects. Then compare to market price. "If the price is significantly below intrinsic value, you have a margin of safety." Case: "Buy at $70 what you believe is worth $100. The $30 difference is your protection."

### Technique 2: The Screening Method
"Many securities must be examined before one is found." Screen broadly (low P/E, low P/B, high dividend yield), then eliminate candidates one by one. Only act when you find a clear opportunity. Case: "Most of the analyst's work is elimination, not discovery."

### Technique 3: The Great Illusion Detector
When the market is euphoric or panicked, recognize the "Great Illusion." "Investing looks easy in a bull market. It's not." Stay disciplined, calculate value, don't chase fads. Case: Barams' observation that "money sloshes around in giant waves from one fad to the next."

### Technique 4: Management Incentive Analysis
When evaluating a company, investigate management compensation. "Excessive option grants dilute shareholder value." Red flags: management pays itself regardless of performance. Case: "The potential decrease in the value of common stock when options are issued to management."

### Technique 5: The Relative Value Check
Compare a security to similar securities in the same industry or market. "Is this stock cheap relative to its peers?" But beware: "Relative value analysis has shortcomings" — a stock can be cheap relative to peers but still overvalued absolutely.

### Technique 6: The Warrant/Option Framework
For derivatives, compound mispricing is key. "If the derivatives market undervalues derivatives on a mispriced security, the investor benefits from double leverage." Case: The Barnsdall Oil warrants were undervalued relative to the underlying stock.

### Technique 7: The Hard Work Commitment
Graham's method requires reading financial statements, calculating ratios, studying industries, and maintaining discipline. "You can get rich buying stocks, but it takes hard work and patience." Case: Warren Buffett — spent years studying Graham's methods before applying them.
