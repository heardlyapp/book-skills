# 4 — The North: Opportunity and Disillusionment

> Source: "The Warmth of Other Suns" by Isabel Wilkerson

## What Migrants Found

Better jobs, the right to vote, schools for their children, less overt segregation — but also discrimination, housing covenants, and the coldness of Northern cities.

## Housing Segregation

Northern cities used restrictive covenants to keep Black families out of white neighborhoods. Chicago was one of the most segregated cities in America — as segregated as any Southern city.

> **Case: Ida Mae's Chicago** (Chapter on Chicago): Ida Mae arrived in Chicago in 1937. She found a job as a domestic worker. She lived in a cramped apartment. The winters were brutal. "I nearly froze to death that first winter. But nobody called me a nigger. And my children went to school. In Mississippi, they couldn't go to school half the year because they had to pick cotton."
> **Key takeaway**: Ida Mae's calculus is the calculus of millions: freezing winters and cramped apartments were a fair trade for dignity, schooling, and the absence of terror. The North was not paradise — but it was not the South.

> **Case: George's Harlem** (Chapter on New York): George Starling arrived in Harlem in 1945 and could not find work in his field. He ended up working for the railroad as a porter. "I had a college education, but in New York, I was a porter. I was treated like a servant by white passengers. But at least I could vote." George's story shows the limits of Northern opportunity: education did not guarantee advancement.
> **Key takeaway**: The North offered more freedom but not equality. Many migrants, like George, found themselves in jobs far below their qualifications. The humiliation of Northern discrimination was different from the South — but it was still real.
