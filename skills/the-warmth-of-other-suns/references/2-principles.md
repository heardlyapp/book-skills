# 2 — The Jim Crow South

> Source: "The Warmth of Other Suns" by Isabel Wilkerson

## The World the Migrants Left

The South was a closed society. Black people could not vote, serve on juries, or testify against white people. They were subject to curfews, segregation in all public facilities, and the constant threat of racial violence.

## Sharecropping

After the Civil War, most Black farmers became sharecroppers — working land owned by white landlords in exchange for a share of the crop. The system was designed to keep them in permanent debt.

> **Case: The Lynching Economy** (Chapter 2): "If a Black farmer was too successful, if he tried to buy his own land, if he looked at a white woman the wrong way, he could be lynched — and his land would be taken over by white people." Wilkerson documents how violence was used as an economic weapon. The threat of lynching kept Black people in their place.
> **Key takeaway**: Lynching was not random violence — it was a systematic tool of economic and social control. The fear of being killed was a primary reason people left.

> **Case: Ida Mae's Decision** (Chapter on Ida Mae): Ida Mae Gladney lived in Mississippi with her husband and children. One day, her husband's cousin was beaten nearly to death by a white landowner for no reason. "The cousin survived but never walked again. My husband looked at me and said: 'We got to go.' They left within the month."
> **Key takeaway**: The decision to leave was often made in a single moment — a beating, a threat, an act of violence that made staying impossible. Ida Mae's family left Mississippi because staying meant accepting that they or their children could be killed with impunity.
