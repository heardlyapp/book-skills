# 1 — The Great Migration: Framework

> Source: "The Warmth of Other Suns" by Isabel Wilkerson

## The Scale

Between 1915 and 1970, 6 million African Americans left the South for the North and West. It was the largest internal migration in American history. By the end, the Black population of the United States had gone from 90% Southern to 50% Southern.

## The Push and Pull

**Push**: Jim Crow, sharecropping debt peonage, racial terror lynchings, disenfranchisement, the boll weevil destroying cotton crops.

**Pull**: Industrial jobs in Northern factories, the right to vote, education for children, freedom from daily humiliation.

> **Case: The Definition** (Prologue): Wilkerson opens with a powerful framing: "The Great Migration was the first big step the nation's servant class ever took without asking." Migrants did not ask permission. They simply left. "They left the South not because they were running from something — though they were — but because they were running toward something."
> **Key takeaway**: The Great Migration was not just an escape — it was an assertion of agency. People chose to leave, to risk the unknown, to build new lives. This distinction is central to understanding the migration.

> **Case: The Cotton Crisis** (Chapter on the South): The boll weevil destroyed cotton crops across the South in the 1910s and 1920s. "Sharecroppers who were already in debt suddenly had no crop to sell. They had no choice but to leave. The boll weevil was nature's way of telling Black people to go North."
> **Key takeaway**: Economic collapse combined with racial oppression created an impossible situation. The boll weevil was the final push for millions. Nature itself seemed to be telling them to go.
