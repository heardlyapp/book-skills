# 5 — Wilkerson's Voice + 5 Application Scenarios

> Source: "The Warmth of Other Suns" by Isabel Wilkerson

## Wilkerson's Voice

Isabel Wilkerson is a Pulitzer Prize-winning journalist and former New York Times bureau chief. Her voice: lyrical, deeply researched, humane. She spent 10 years researching the Great Migration, interviewing over 1,200 people and reading countless archival documents.

> **Case: The Research Journey** (Notes on Methodology): Wilkerson describes her research process: "I interviewed over 1,200 people and read through thousands of letters, diaries, and archival records. I traveled to the home towns of the three protagonists and retraced their journeys by train. I wanted to understand not just the statistics but the texture of their lives."
> **Key takeaway**: Wilkerson's credibility comes from the depth of her research. This is not a quick summary — it's a decade of work distilled into one book. The three protagonists are composites of thousands of stories.

> **Case: The Title** (Preface): The title comes from a passage by Richard Wright: "I was taking a part of the South to transplant in alien soil, to see if it could grow differently, if it could drink of new and cool rains, bend in strange winds, respond to the warmth of other suns." Wilkerson chose this as the epigraph because it captures the essence of the migration: people seeking warmth — physical, psychological, and spiritual — in a new land.
> **Key takeaway**: The title is a poem. It captures both the hope and the tragedy of the migration: the search for warmth, the transplanting into alien soil, the uncertainty of whether one could grow differently.

## Key Quote

"The Great Migration was the first big step the nation's servant class ever took without asking."

## 5 Application Scenarios

1. **Understanding systemic racism** → The Great Migration shows how policy and violence created and maintained racial hierarchy.
2. **Learning American history** → The migration is one of the most important events in American history, yet it is often overlooked.
3. **Understanding family history** → Many African American families have a migration story. Knowing the migration helps understand family.
4. **Understanding today's cities** → The demographic patterns of Northern cities today are a direct result of the Great Migration.
5. **Appreciating Black culture** → From blues to jazz to Motown to hip-hop — the music of the migration is the music of America.
