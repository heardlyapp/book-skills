# 3 — Three Journeys

> Source: "The Warmth of Other Suns" by Isabel Wilkerson

## Ida Mae Gladney

From Chip County, Mississippi to Chicago, 1937. Age 24. Married, two children. Her husband's cousin was beaten nearly to death. They left in secret, in the middle of the night. Ida Mae never returned to the South.

## George Starling

From Eustis, Florida to Harlem, 1945. Age 28. He organized citrus workers for better wages. The plantation owners threatened to lynch him. He fled on a train with his wife, leaving everything behind.

## Robert Joseph Pershing Foster

From Monroe, Louisiana to Los Angeles, 1953. Age 33. A surgeon who graduated from medical school at the top of his class but could not practice in the South. He drove alone from Louisiana to California in a Buick — three days, with no money, sleeping in his car.

> **Case: Robert's Drive** (Chapter on Robert Foster): Robert Foster was a brilliant surgeon who had trained at the top of his field. But in Louisiana, he could not practice in the white hospitals and could not get privileges at the Black hospitals because they were overcrowded. "I was a doctor. I had saved lives. But in Louisiana, I was a Negro first and a doctor second. I could not live like that." He packed his Buick and drove to California without knowing what he would find.
> **Key takeaway**: The Great Migration included professionals, not just laborers. Robert Foster was a highly trained surgeon who was not allowed to practice at his level in the South. The migration was a loss of talent for the South and a gain for the rest of America.

> **Case: George's Train Ride** (Chapter on George Starling): George Starling boarded the train from Florida to New York in 1945. "When the train crossed the Mason-Dixon line, the conductor came through and told the Black passengers they could now move to any seat they wanted. George did not move. He had been conditioned not to. But others did. There was a cheer." The small act of moving to a seat of one's choosing was a rite of passage for migrants.
> **Key takeaway**: The simplest act — choosing where to sit on a train — was revolutionary for people who had been denied choice their entire lives. The crossing of the Mason-Dixon line was a psychological as well as a geographical border.
