# Reference: The Global Workspace

## Overview
The global neuronal workspace (GNW) theory is Dehaene's most important contribution. It proposes that consciousness occurs when information is "ignited" in a distributed network of cortical neurons that broadcast the information to many brain regions simultaneously.

## Case: The Workspace Model
The brain has many specialized processors: visual, auditory, motor, memory, language. Each processor works locally and unconsciously. Consciousness arises when a processor's output is amplified and broadcast to the entire network through the global workspace. Workspace neurons — concentrated in prefrontal and parietal cortex — receive input from many processors and broadcast their output widely.

## Case: The Ignition Phenomenon
Ignition is the sudden, all-or-nothing transition from unconscious to conscious processing. In experiments, unconscious stimuli produce brief, localized activity that decays quickly. Conscious stimuli produce a sustained burst of activity that spreads across the brain. The ignition can be measured with EEG as the P3 wave. Ignition is the "moment of consciousness."

## Case: The All-or-Nothing Debate
A key debate: is consciousness graded or all-or-nothing? Dehaene argues that consciousness is all-or-nothing at the global level — information is either broadcast or it is not. But the content of consciousness can be graded: you can be more or less aware of details. The ignition threshold is discrete; the content that crosses it is continuous.
