# Techniques: Practical Money Tools

## Creating Your Spending Plan

1. Track all expenses for one month (use an app or spreadsheet)
2. Categorize: fixed costs, savings/investing, guilt-free spending
3. Set percentages: 50% needs, 30% wants (guilt-free), 20% savings/debt
4. Automate savings — transfer to savings on payday, not at month-end
5. Review and adjust monthly

## The Debt Payoff Strategy

1. List all debts with balances, interest rates, and minimum payments
2. Build your $1,000 F-you fund first (keep paying minimums)
3. Attack highest-interest debt first (avalanche method)
4. Put every extra dollar toward that debt
5. When it's paid off, roll that payment to the next debt

## The Salary Negotiation Script

1. Research market rate for your role (sites like Glassdoor, Levels.fyi)
2. Start the conversation: "I'd like to discuss my compensation"
3. State your case: "Based on my performance and market research, I believe my compensation should increase to X"
4. Pause — let them speak first
5. If they say no, ask: "What would I need to achieve to earn this?"
6. Get the next steps in writing

> **Case: The Underpaid Manager** (Chapter 6): A woman discovered she was paid $15K less than her male colleagues for the same role. She prepared her case with market data and a list of her achievements. She asked for the raise. Got it.
> **Key takeaway**: The research is your armor. When you have market data, the negotiation is about facts, not feelings.

> **Case: The Automated Investor** (Chapter 5): A woman was intimidated by investing and kept putting it off. She set up an automatic $50/month transfer to an S&P 500 index fund. One year later, she had $650 (including growth) — and the habit was automatic. She didn't have to think about it or be scared.
>
> **Key takeaway**: The hardest part of investing is starting. Automation removes the emotional barrier. You don't need courage to invest — you need a repeating calendar reminder.

## Starting to Invest

1. Open a retirement account (401k, IRA, or equivalent)
2. Choose a target-date fund or low-cost index fund (S&P 500)
3. Set up automatic monthly contributions
4. Increase the contribution by 1% every time you get a raise
5. Don't check it daily — check it yearly
