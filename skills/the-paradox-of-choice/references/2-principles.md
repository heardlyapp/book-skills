# Principles: Maximizing vs Satisficing and Happiness

## Principle 1: Know Whether You Are a Maximizer or a Satisficer

The first step is self-awareness. Most people do not realize they are maximizers. They think they are being thorough or responsible. But maximizing is not thoroughness. It is a decision style driven by the fear of missing out on the best option.

Maximizers share these traits: they read reviews long after they have enough information, they check multiple stores even when the price difference is negligible, they second-guess decisions after making them, and they keep looking for better options even after committing.

> **Case: The Computer Purchase** (Chapter 3): A maximizer shopping for a computer will read dozens of reviews, compare specifications across brands, visit multiple stores, and ask friends for recommendations. After buying, they will continue to check for better deals and newer models. A satisficer will read a few reviews, set a budget, pick a well-rated model within that budget, and stop looking once they buy. The satisficer is happier with their purchase despite doing less research.
> **Key takeaway:** More research does not lead to better outcomes or higher satisfaction. It leads to higher expectations and more opportunities for regret.

## Principle 2: Expectations Determine Satisfaction

Maximizers have high expectations. They expect the perfect outcome. When reality falls short — as it always does — they are disappointed. Satisficers have realistic expectations. They expect good enough. When reality meets or exceeds that, they are pleased.

The irony is that maximizers objectively get better outcomes. They find better deals, better products, better jobs. But they are less satisfied because their expectations are higher. The gap between expectation and reality determines satisfaction more than the objective quality of the outcome.

This is why the maximizer is trapped: their effort to find the best option raises their expectations, which then makes them less satisfied with what they find. The harder they work, the worse they feel.

## Principle 3: The Cost of Choice Is Invisible

Every choice has hidden costs. The time spent comparing options, the mental energy expended on deliberation, the emotional toll of regret, the opportunity cost of all the alternatives not chosen. These costs are invisible at the point of decision but accumulate over time.

Maximizers pay these costs without realizing it. They spend hours comparison shopping, feeling anxious about decisions, and regretting choices. This is not free. It is a tax on happiness that they pay every day. Satisficers avoid this tax by accepting good enough.

## Principle 4: Regret Is the Enemy of Satisfaction

Anticipated regret is a powerful force. We imagine how we would feel if we made the wrong choice, and this fear drives us to over-research and over-deliberate. But the strategy of avoiding regret through exhaustive comparison does not work. It creates more opportunities for regret by exposing us to more alternatives.

The solution is counterintuitive: make decisions that are harder to reverse. When you can return a purchase, you are less satisfied with it. When you know you cannot return it, you commit and find reasons to be happy. Commitment reduces regret by eliminating the possibility of undoing the decision.

> **Case: Reversible vs Irreversible Decisions** (Chapter 7 in context): Research shows that people are more satisfied with irreversible decisions than reversible ones. When you can exchange a purchase, you keep comparing. When you cannot, you focus on the positive aspects. The freedom to change your mind reduces satisfaction.
> **Key takeaway:** Commitment increases satisfaction. The easier it is to undo a decision, the less happy you will be with it.
