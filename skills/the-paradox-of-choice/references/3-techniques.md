# Techniques: What to Do About Choice

Schwartz dedicates the final part of the book to practical strategies for managing choice overload. These are specific, actionable steps anyone can take.

## Technique 1: Choose When to Choose

Not all decisions deserve the same level of attention. Reserve your maximizer energy for the few decisions that truly matter. For everything else, satisfice. The challenge is knowing which is which. A useful heuristic: if the decision will not matter in a year, satisfice. If it will, maximize selectively.

> **Case: The Pen** (Chapter 11): Schwartz describes a study where people were asked to choose a pen from a selection. Some were given the chance to change their choice later. Those who could not change were happier with their pens. Even with something as trivial as a pen, the freedom to change reduced satisfaction.
> **Key takeaway:** Apply the trivial decision satisficing rule. For most purchases under a certain threshold, pick the first good enough option and stop looking.

## Technique 2: Practice Gratitude

Gratitude is the antidote to social comparison and adaptation. When you actively appreciate what you have, you stop wanting what you do not have. Schwartz recommends keeping a gratitude journal: write down three things you are grateful for every day. This simple practice shifts attention from what could be better to what is already good.

The science is clear: gratitude practices increase happiness and reduce the negative effects of comparison. They work because they interrupt the adaptation cycle. Instead of taking good things for granted, you notice them.

## Technique 3: Set Constraints Before You Choose

Before you start shopping or deciding, set explicit constraints on the search. Decide how many options you will consider (three is often enough). Set a time limit for the decision. Define your criteria in advance and refuse to change them.

The power of constraints is that they reduce the burden of choice without reducing the quality of the outcome. Having three good options is usually as good as having thirty. The extra twenty-seven options add stress without adding value.

## Technique 4: Expect Adaptation and Adjust

Everything you acquire will eventually become normal. The new car, the promotion, the bigger house — they will all lose their luster. This is hedonic adaptation. Knowing this in advance changes how you decide.

Instead of asking "Will this make me happy?", ask "Will this make me happier than my current situation, even after I adapt to it?" This question is harder to answer honestly, but it leads to better decisions. It helps you avoid the trap of buying things that provide only a temporary boost.

## Technique 5: Make Decisions Non-Reversible

When you can return or exchange, you maintain the option to change your mind. This sounds good, but it reduces satisfaction. Make it harder to undo your decisions. Delete the return label. Throw away the receipt. Commit.

This technique works because it forces you to accept your choice and move on. The possibility of reversal keeps the comparison process alive indefinitely. Eliminate it, and you free yourself to enjoy what you have.

## Technique 6: Reduce Social Comparisons

Comparison is a primary source of dissatisfaction. The neighbor's new car, the colleague's promotion, the friend's vacation — these comparisons make your own choices seem inadequate.

The solution is to reduce exposure to comparison triggers. Unfollow social media accounts that make you feel inadequate. Limit time spent on shopping sites where you see what others have. Focus on your own values and goals. Your choices do not have to measure up to anyone else's standards.

> **Case: Social Comparison Study** (Chapter 9): Schwartz reviews research showing that people who compare themselves to others frequently are consistently less happy, even when they compare favorably. The act of comparing itself creates dissatisfaction, regardless of the outcome.
> **Key takeaway:** Social comparison is a zero-sum game. Even when you win, you lose, because the habit of comparing undermines your ability to be satisfied with what you have.
