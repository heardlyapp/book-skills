# Anti-Patterns: Why Choice Makes Us Suffer

Schwartz dedicates Part III of the book to the specific ways that choice causes suffering. These are the anti-patterns — the traps we fall into that make more choice feel worse.

## ❌ The Regret Trap

Anticipated regret is the fear that you will make the wrong choice. It leads to analysis paralysis and endless deliberation. After the decision, regret continues in the form of second-guessing and wondering about the road not taken.

The more options you have, the more opportunities for regret. Every unselected option becomes a what-if. Maximizers are especially susceptible because they believe there is a correct answer and they might have missed it.

## ❌ The Adaptation Trap

Hedonic adaptation is the tendency to return to a baseline level of happiness after any event. The new car feels amazing for a week, then becomes ordinary. The promotion feels great for a month, then becomes the new normal.

This adaptation makes consumers chase novelty. We buy new things to feel the initial thrill, knowing it will fade, but hoping this time will be different. It is never different. Adaptation is inevitable. The trap is believing that the next purchase will be the one that finally brings lasting happiness.

> **Case: Lottery Winners and Paraplegics** (Chapter 8): Classic research shows that lottery winners and people who become paraplegics return to their baseline happiness within a year. The winners are not as happy as expected. The paraplegics are not as unhappy as feared. Adaptation is powerful and universal.
> **Key takeaway:** No external event will change your happiness level permanently. Adaptation brings you back to your baseline. Knowing this helps you stop chasing the next purchase.

## ❌ The Comparison Trap

Social comparison is automatic and pervasive. We evaluate our choices by comparing them to others. The problem is that there is always someone with a better choice. Your neighbor has a nicer car. Your colleague got a better deal. Your friend went on a better vacation.

The abundance of options makes comparison easier and more damaging. With more people making different choices, there are more reference points for comparison. In a world of limited choices, comparison was limited. In a world of infinite choices, comparison is infinite.

## ❌ The Opportunity Cost Trap

Every choice has opportunity costs — the value of the alternatives not chosen. With two options, there is one opportunity cost. With ten options, there are nine. Each additional option adds more reasons to regret your choice.

Opportunity costs are invisible but powerful. They lurk beneath the surface of every decision, whispering that you could have done better. The more options you have, the more your chosen option's flaws stand out against the imagined perfection of the alternatives.

> **Case: The Car Purchase Study** (Chapter 6): Research shows that when people are given more options for a car, they are more likely to consider the features they gave up. The buyer who chose between two cars focuses on what they got. The buyer who chose between ten cars focuses on what they missed.
> **Key takeaway:** More options make you more aware of what you gave up. This awareness reduces satisfaction with what you chose.

## ❌ The Depression Connection

Schwartz presents evidence linking maximizing behavior to depression. Maximizers set unrealistically high standards. When reality fails to meet these standards, they blame themselves. This self-blame cycle can lead to or worsen depression.

The relationship is bidirectional: maximizers are more likely to become depressed, and depressed people are more likely to maximize. The solution is to break the cycle by lowering expectations and accepting good enough.
