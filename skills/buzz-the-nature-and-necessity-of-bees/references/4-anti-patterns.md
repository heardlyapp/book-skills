# Common Mistakes — Anti-Patterns

**Anti-Pattern 1: Only Thinking About Honeybees.** Conservation efforts often focus on honeybees, but honeybees are livestock, not wildlife. Native bees — bumblebees, mason bees, leafcutters — face different threats and need different support.

**Anti-Pattern 2: Ignoring Solitary Bees.** Most people think bees live in hives with a queen. In fact, most bee species are solitary. They do not live in colonies. They nest alone in the ground, in hollow stems, or in wood. Providing habitat for solitary bees is often more helpful than keeping honeybee hives.

**Anti-Pattern 3: Pesticide Denial.** Some gardeners believe that if they do not see dead bees, pesticides are not harming them. Sub-lethal effects — disorientation, reduced foraging, impaired learning — are invisible but devastating.

**Case: The Mason Bee.** A single mason bee can pollinate as much as 100 honeybees. Supporting solitary bees through bee houses and habitat is more effective than focusing only on honeybees.

**Case: The Lawn Problem.** A manicured lawn provides no food for bees. Letting clover and dandelions grow in your lawn provides essential early-season food for bees.

**Case: The Varroa Mite.** This parasitic mite is one of the greatest threats to honeybees. It feeds on bee blood and transmits viruses. Varroa is a major cause of colony collapse.

**Case: The Bee Garden. A diverse garden with native plants supports more bee species than a monoculture of exotic flowers.
