# Principles of Bee Biology

**Principle 1: Diversity Matters.** Honeybees get all the attention, but they are just one of 20,000+ species. Each species has a unique ecological role. Bumblebees are better at pollinating tomatoes. Mason bees are superior orchard pollinators. Leafcutter bees are used commercially for alfalfa pollination.

**Principle 2: Bees and Flowers Need Each Other.** About 75% of flowering plants rely on animal pollinators, mostly bees. Without bees, most wild plants could not reproduce. The relationship is not optional — it is essential.

**Principle 3: Bees Are Smarter Than We Think.** Research shows bees can count, recognize human faces, learn by watching others, and have individual personalities. The conventional view of bees as simple, instinct-driven insects is being replaced by a more complex picture.

**Case: The Almond Industry.** California's almond industry requires 1.6 million honeybee colonies each year. Beekeepers truck their hives across the country to pollinate almonds. Without bees, there would be no almonds — illustrating the economic value of pollination.

**Case: Native vs. Domesticated Bees.** Honeybees are domesticated livestock, managed by beekeepers. Native bees — bumblebees, mason bees, leafcutters — are wild animals. They are often more effective pollinators than honeybees but are threatened by habitat loss and pesticides.

**Case: The Colony Collapse.** In 2006, beekeepers began reporting massive losses of honeybee colonies. Worker bees would abandon the hive, leaving the queen and young behind. The cause was eventually linked to a combination of pesticides, parasites, and pathogens.
