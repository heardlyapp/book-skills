> Source: The Politics Industry, Chapters 1-8 — 7 principles of political innovation

## Principle 1: The System Is the Problem

The most important principle: the problem is not the people in politics — it is the system that produces them. Changing the people without changing the system will not fix the outcomes.

> **Case: The Freshman Congressperson** (Chapter 1): Gehl and Porter describe how idealistic new members of Congress quickly adapt to the system's incentives. They learn that independence is punished and party loyalty is rewarded. The system changes the person, not the other way around.
> **Key takeaway**: If you want different outcomes, you need different rules. Personality-based reform (electing "good people") cannot overcome structural incentives.

## Principle 2: Competition Creates Accountability

When politicians face real competition for all voters, they become more responsive. When they only face competition in party primaries (where voters are more extreme), they become more extreme. The solution: create a system where politicians must compete for all voters.

## Principle 3: The Duopoly Protects Itself

Both parties have an interest in maintaining the current system. They will not reform themselves. External pressure — from voters, from states, from structural reform — is required.

## Principle 4: Structural Reform Is Nonpartisan

Final-Five Voting, ranked-choice voting, and open primaries cannot be claimed by either party. These reforms do not favor Democrats or Republicans — they favor competition and voter choice.

## Principle 5: Reform Happens at the State Level

Federal gridlock means federal reform is unlikely. State-level reform — ballot initiatives, state legislation — is the most promising path. Alaska's Final-Four Voting was adopted through a ballot initiative.

> **Case: The Alaska Model** (Chapter 7): Alaska adopted Final-Four Voting through a ballot initiative in 2020. It survived a court challenge and was implemented in 2022. Alaska's experience provides a model for other states.

## Principle 6: The Primary System Is the Root Cause

Closed primaries produce extreme candidates because primary voters are more ideological than the general population. The fix: nonpartisan open primaries where all voters can participate regardless of party registration.

## Principle 7: Democracy Is Not a Product — It Is a Process

The goal is not a perfect system. The goal is a system that produces better outcomes through competition, accountability, and voter choice. Perfection is not required — improvement is.
