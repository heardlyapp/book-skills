> Source: The Politics Industry, Chapters 1-8 — 6 anti-patterns of political reform

## Anti-Pattern 1: Electing Better People

The most common reform approach — "if we just elect better people, things will improve." Evidence shows that good people are corrupted by a bad system. Structural reform must precede personal reform.

## Anti-Pattern 2: Third-Party Magic

The belief that a third party will break the duopoly. Third parties face overwhelming structural barriers (ballot access, fundraising, debate rules). Final-Five Voting is a better approach because it changes the rules rather than hoping for a savior.

> **Case: The Ross Perot Campaign** (Chapter 4): Ross Perot won 19% of the popular vote in 1992 — the best third-party performance in decades. He won zero electoral votes. The system absorbed the challenge without changing.

## Anti-Pattern 3: Term Limits

Term limits sound good but don't fix the underlying structural problems. They simply replace experienced politicians with inexperienced ones — both operating within the same broken system.

## Anti-Pattern 4: Campaign Finance Reform Alone

Money in politics is a symptom, not the cause. Even if campaign finance were perfectly reformed, the two-party duopoly would still suppress competition. Structural reform of the electoral process must accompany campaign finance reform.

## Anti-Pattern 5: Focusing on the Presidency

The President gets the most attention, but Congress is where the system is most broken. The legislative process — not the executive — is the primary source of gridlock.

## Anti-Pattern 6: Hoping for Bipartisanship

Bipartisanship is a symptom of a functional system, not a cure for a broken one. Calling for bipartisanship within a duopoly is like calling for "fair competition" in a market that has none.

> **Case: The Gridlock Congress** (Chapter 6): Despite repeated calls for bipartisanship, Congress has become increasingly polarized and gridlocked. The calls for bipartisanship have had no effect because the structural incentives reward gridlock.
