# Core Framework: The Three Great Untruths

## The Central Thesis

"Three bad ideas have spread from campus to the broader culture, weakening resilience, increasing anxiety, and polarizing society." The authors identify three "Great Untruths" that contradict ancient wisdom and modern psychology.

> **Case: The Microaggression Movement** (Chapter 2): Microaggressions — small, often unintentional slights — were originally a useful concept for understanding cumulative harm. But on campus, the response shifted from "teach people to be more aware" to "protect people from any discomfort." This applied the Untruth of Fragility: the belief that discomfort harms rather than strengthens.
> **Key takeaway**: The well-intentioned desire to protect people from harm can backfire. By treating students as fragile, universities made them fragile.

## The Three Great Untruths

| Untruth | Statement | CBT Distortion | Ancient Wisdom |
|---------|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| Fragility | What doesn't kill you makes you weaker | Catastrophizing | What doesn't kill you makes you stronger |
| Emotional Reasoning | Always trust your feelings | Emotional reasoning | Don't trust your feelings; examine them |
| Us vs Them | Life is a battle between good and evil people | Black-and-white thinking | The line between good and evil runs through every human heart |

> **Case: The CBT Connection** (Chapter 1): The authors realized that the Three Great Untruths map directly onto three classic Cognitive Behavioral Therapy distortions. The solution to campus fragility is not more protection — it's teaching students to recognize and challenge these distorted thoughts, just as CBT does.
> **Key takeaway**: CBT is not just therapy — it's wisdom for life. The same techniques that treat depression can inoculate against the Three Great Untruths.
