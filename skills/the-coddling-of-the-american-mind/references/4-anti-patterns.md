> Source: The Coddling of the American Mind, Chapters 2, 5, 8

# Anti-Patterns

## 1. Safetyism

**The mistake**: Believing that emotional safety is more important than exposure to challenge.

**Fix**: The goal is not to eliminate discomfort but to build the capacity to handle it.

> **Case: The Trigger Warning Debate** (Chapter 2): Trigger warnings were introduced to protect students from distressing material. The result: students became more anxious about encountering difficult content, not less. The warnings amplified the very fear they were meant to reduce.
> **Key takeaway**: The Preparedness Paradox: preparing people for worst cases makes them more fearful. The solution is not warnings but gradual exposure and coping skills.

## 2. Emotional Reasoning as Virtue

**The mistake**: Treating emotional reactions as infallible guides to truth.

**Fix**: Emotions provide information, not conclusions. Examine your feelings before acting on them.

## 3. Call-Out Culture

**The mistake**: Publicly shaming people for mistakes rather than giving them space to learn.

**Fix**: The authors advocate for "call-in" culture — private conversations that educate rather than public shaming that destroys.

> **Case: The Public Shaming Epidemic** (Chapter 7): Social media enables mass public shaming for minor transgressions. The shamed person is treated as a monster (Us vs Them thinking), given no opportunity to learn or apologize, and their life is often destroyed. The punishment far exceeds the crime.
> **Key takeaway**: Public shaming is not justice — it's mob violence enabled by technology. Call-in culture (private, educational) is more effective and more humane.

## 4. Assuming Bad Intent

**The mistake**: Assuming the worst possible motivation for others' actions.

**Fix**: Hanlon's Razor: "Never attribute to malice what can be adequately explained by ignorance, mistake, or fatigue."

## 5. Avoidance as Coping

**The mistake**: Avoiding difficult situations to reduce anxiety.

**Fix**: Exposure therapy — facing what you fear in manageable doses — is the most effective treatment for anxiety. Avoidance makes anxiety worse.
