# Core Framework — Walking with the Wind

## Prologue: The Windstorm (The Book's Central Metaphor)

As a four-year-old, Lewis and a dozen cousins huddled inside his aunt's wood-frame house during a tornado. The wind lifted one corner of the house. His aunt lined them up, clasping hands, and walked toward the rising corner — holding the house down with their weight. Then another corner lifted. They walked toward that one. Back and forth, all through the storm. "More than half a century has passed since that day... It has struck me that our society is not unlike the children in that house, rocked again and again by the winds of one storm or another. But the people of conscience never left the house. They clasped hands and moved toward the corner that was the weakest. And another corner would lift, and we would go there. And eventually, inevitably, the storm would settle, and the house would still stand."

## Part 1: Coming Up (Chapters 1-3)

### Childhood in Pike County, Alabama (1940s)
Born February 21, 1940, in a three-room shotgun house, delivered by his great-grandmother Bessie. The only white people he saw until age 6 were a mailman and the Rolling Store Man. His father Eddie bought 110 acres for $300 cash — the first land anyone in his family ever owned.

**The chickens:** At age 5, given charge of the family's 60 chickens. He named them, preached to them every night (reciting Bible verses), held funerals for dead chicks in lard-can coffins. Baptized baby chicks — on one occasion nearly drowned one. His family called him "Preacher." "I truly believe the kinship I felt with those chickens taught me patience, compassion, and nonviolence before I knew the words."

**The sharecropping trap:** His parents picked 400 pounds of cotton per day at 35¢ per hundred pounds — $1.40/day for a fifth of a ton. Lewis complained constantly: "Gambling. This is nothing but gambling." His mother: "Well, Robert, what else are we going to do?"

**First field trip:** To Tuskegee Institute at age 9. Walked through George Washington Carver's lab. Came home to a room piled with peanuts and thought of Carver's specimens in jars. Education was his way out.

### The Radio and the First Sermon (1948)
At age 8, Lewis heard a voice on the radio that changed his life: Martin Luther King Jr., preaching about the Montgomery Bus Boycott. "I felt something. I felt a connection. I could not articulate what it was, but I knew I wanted to do what he was doing."

**Leaving Alabama:** At 16, Lewis wrote to Dr. King asking how to help. King responded — sent him a bus ticket. Lewis arrived in Montgomery just as the boycott was ending. Met Rosa Parks, Ralph Abernathy, and Dr. King for the first time.

## Part 2 & 3: Nashville and the Freedom Rides

### The Jim Lawson Workshops (1959-1960)
At American Baptist Theological Seminary, Lewis met Rev. James Lawson, who was training students in Gandhian nonviolence. Tuesday night workshops: "We role-played being attacked. We learned to curl into a ball to protect our vital organs. We learned to look our attackers in the eye with love, not hatred." Lawson taught that nonviolence was not passive — it was "a way of life for courageous people."

**The sit-ins begin (February 1960):** After Greensboro, Nashville students moved. Lewis was among the first to sit in at Woolworth's. "We sat there in our best clothes — suits and ties for the men, dresses for the women — and we asked to be served. They refused. We stayed." The first day: 25 arrests. By spring: 3,000 students had been trained.

**The Krystal fumigation:** Lewis and Bevel were locked inside a Krystal restaurant while the manager turned on a fumigator. Thick white insecticide filled the room. "We're going to suffocate, I thought. We're going to die." Bevel began chanting the Book of Daniel — "Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego" — saved by the fire department breaking in.

### The Freedom Rides (May 1961)
Lewis was supposed to be on the Greyhound bus that was firebombed in Anniston. A last-minute trip to Philadelphia for a foreign service interview saved his life. "When I heard the news, I felt shock and guilt. That was my bus."

**The continuation:** While CORE called off the ride after Anniston and Birmingham, Diane Nash insisted Nashville students would continue. Lewis led a group of 10 to Birmingham. Bull Connor arrested them, then had them driven to the Tennessee state line at midnight and left in Klan territory. An elderly black couple took them in.

**Montgomery bus station attack (May 20, 1961):** Lewis and his group finally reached Montgomery on a bus. At the station, a mob of several hundred whites attacked. Lewis was beaten with a wooden crate. Jim Zwerg's face was smashed, teeth broken. John Seigenthaler (Kennedy's aide) was knocked unconscious. "I remember lying on the concrete floor, blood streaming into my eyes."

## Part 4 & 5: SNCC and Selma

### Chairman of SNCC (1963-1966)
At 23, elected chairman of SNCC. Moved to Atlanta — $10/week salary, $54/month rent. "I owned six shirts, three pairs of pants, two suits, two pairs of shoes."

**The March on Washington (August 28, 1963):** Lewis was scheduled to give a speech — the youngest speaker. His original draft was fiery: "Which side is the federal government on?" He criticized the Kennedy bill as "too little, too late." The Archbishop of Washington, other leaders demanded changes. Lewis ultimately toned down the speech — but kept the essence: "We must say, 'Wake up America!'"

**Bloody Sunday (March 7, 1965):** Lewis led 600 marchers across the Edmund Pettus Bridge in Selma. Alabama state troopers ordered them to disperse. "I remember turning and looking back at the crowd. Just for a moment. Then I heard the voice of a state trooper: 'Troopers, advance!'" Lewis was clubbed on the head. His skull was fractured. He suffered a concussion. The nation watched on television. Eight days later, President Johnson introduced the Voting Rights Act.

## Part 6 & 7: Going Down and Home

### After the Movement
Lewis was eventually "de-elected" from SNCC leadership as the organization grew more militant. He left SNCC in 1966 after Stokely Carmichael was elected chairman. "I was sad, but not bitter. The movement had changed, and I had not."

**Congress (1987-2020):** Lewis was elected to the House of Representatives in 1986, representing Georgia's 5th district. He served 17 terms. Known as "the conscience of the Congress." Continued the Beloved Community vision through legislation and moral leadership.

### The Beloved Community
Lewis' guiding philosophy: "The Beloved Community is a society based on simple justice that values all people. It is a society that respects the dignity and worth of every human being. This is the heart of the civil rights movement. This is the path I have been committed to since I turned from a boy to a man."
