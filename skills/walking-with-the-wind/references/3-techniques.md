# Techniques — Walking with the Wind

## How to Build a Movement (Based on John Lewis)

### Technique 1: Train Before You Fight
Lawson's Tuesday night workshops were the engine of Nashville's success. Students role-played scenarios for months before the first sit-in. They practiced being spat on, screamed at, physically attacked. They learned to curl into a ball, protect their faces, look at attackers with compassion. Result: when real violence came, nobody ran. Nobody retaliated. "The reason we were so effective in Nashville was the discipline we had developed in those workshops."

### Technique 2: Dress for the Revolution
Nashville students wore suits, ties, dresses. Lewis: "We dressed carefully because we wanted to show the world who we were — respectable, dignified human beings demanding respect." This was strategic: the contrast between well-dressed students and jeering white mobs was powerful on television.

### Technique 3: Use Drama — But Control It
Lewis: "I believed in drama. I believed in action." The sit-ins, Freedom Rides, and marches were performances designed to expose injustice. But they were disciplined performances — no name-calling, no retaliation, no chaos. The aim was to make segregation look ugly without making protestors look dangerous.

### Technique 4: Keep a Journal of the Movement
Lewis wrote letters from jail, kept his arrest envelopes, recorded his thoughts. The Brown envelope from the Tic-Toc arrest — labeled "Contents: Belt, Keys and Wallet. Money: none" — became a relic. His book exists because he documented everything.

### Technique 5: Never Back Down From a Challenge You've Been Called To
After Anniston, after CORE called off the Freedom Rides, Diane Nash refused. She called Farmer: she would send students down. Lewis: "There was no choice but to face it, to push on. To back down would effectively end the movement."

### Technique 6: Treat Your Opponent as a Human Being
Lawson taught: never dehumanize. Look the attacker in the eye. See their fear as well as their hate. This allowed protestors to absorb blows without escalating. It also confused attackers — the lack of resistance often made them stop. "When we did not react, things predictably turned more violent. Then eventually some of those people became our allies."

### Technique 7: Find Your "Chickens"
Lewis' first congregation was 60 chickens. He preached to them every night, held funerals for dead chicks, baptized baby birds. This absurd-seeming training ground taught him patience, discipline, compassion, and public speaking. The lesson: every great leader started with an audience that didn't seem to matter.
