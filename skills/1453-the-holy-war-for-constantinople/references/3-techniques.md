# Techniques of Siege Warfare

## Technique 1: The Siege Train

Mehmet did not bring one cannon to Constantinople. He brought a siege train — dozens of guns of various sizes, deployed systematically.

**How to do it:** Do not rely on a single solution. Build a system. The great bombard was for breaking the walls. Smaller guns were for the defenders on the parapets. Mortars were for firing over the walls into the city.

**Case: The Great Bombard.** The 27-foot bronze monster was the centerpiece. It was cast on site by Orban, who had designed it specifically for this task.

**Case: The Supporting Artillery.** Mehmet deployed dozens of smaller cannons to suppress defenders on the walls. The combination of large and small guns made repair work impossible.

## Technique 2: The Naval Blockade

Mehmet understood that Constantinople could not be taken by land alone. He had to control the sea.

**How to do it:** When one approach fails, find another. The Golden Horn chain prevented the Ottoman fleet from entering the harbor. Mehmet did not give up — he found a way around.

**Case: The Chain.** The defenders stretched a massive chain across the Golden Horn, supported by floats. No ship could break it. The Ottoman fleet was trapped outside.

**Case: The Portage.** Mehmet built a road of greased rollers over the hill behind Galata. Seventy ships were dragged overland. It took days and required hundreds of men and oxen.

## Technique 3: Mining

Mehmet employed miners to tunnel under the walls.

**How to do it:** Attack from every direction. While the defenders focused on the bombardment and the naval threat, miners were digging under the walls.

**Case: The Counter-Mining.** The defenders had their own miners, led by a Scotsman named Johannes Grant. They detected the Ottoman tunnels by placing bowls of water on the ground and watching for vibrations. When they found a tunnel, they dug a counter-mine and collapsed it.

**Case: The Grinding War.** The mining war was fought in darkness, with picks and shovels, sometimes hand to hand. It was slow, brutal, and invisible.

## Technique 4: The Constant Pressure

Mehmet never let the defenders rest. The bombardment continued day and night. Assaults came at irregular intervals.

**How to do it:** Exhaust your opponent. The defenders had to repair the walls every night. They had to man the walls every day. They could not sleep. Over 54 days, exhaustion was as deadly as the cannon.

**Case: The Night Repairs.** Every night, the defenders rushed to repair the damage from the day's bombardment. Women and children helped carry stones and fill gaps. The repairs were never quite good enough.

**Case: The Final Assault.** When Mehmet launched the final assault, he sent wave after wave of troops. The defenders beat back the first waves, but they were exhausted. The second wave was fresh. The third wave broke through.

## Technique 5: Integration of Forces

Mehmet coordinated land, sea, artillery, mining, and psychological warfare into a single plan.

**How to do it:** Do not think of battle as a single action. It is a system of coordinated actions. The land assault supports the naval threat. The bombardment supports the mining. The psychological warfare supports everything.

**Case: The Coordinated Assault.** On the final night, Mehmet attacked simultaneously from land and sea. The diversionary assaults forced the defenders to spread their forces thin. The main assault fell on the weakest section of the wall.

**Case: The Psychological Campaign.** Before the final assault, Mehmet had his army light bonfires, play music, and shout prayers. The psychological pressure on the defenders was immense.
