# Techniques — The Spy and the Traitor

## How to Run and Protect an Agent (Based on Ben Macintyre)

### Technique 1: The Dry-Cleaning Protocol
Gordievsky was trained at School 101 to detect and evade surveillance. "Proverka" was the constant process of checking for tails. The technique: never go directly to a meeting. Take multiple turns, go through shops, pause at windows. If you can't "dry clean" yourself, you don't have a meeting.

### Technique 2: The Signal Site
The Safeway bag + gray cap + KitKat + Harrods bag. No direct communication. No phone calls. No written messages. The technique: signals must be mundane — ordinary items that would not look suspicious. The more conspicuous the signal, the more dangerous it is.

### Technique 3: The Brush Contact
A brief physical encounter in a public space — passing a document, exchanging a word. The technique: never stop walking. Eye contact only at the last moment. The transfer takes less than a second. "Brush contact" is the highest-risk, highest-reward technique.

### Technique 4: The Dead Drop
Leave a message or item at a predetermined location for later pickup. The technique: the drop site must be accessible but not obvious. The drop must be executed when no one is watching. The pickup should occur at a different time. Never meet the same person twice.

### Technique 5: The Legend (Cover Story)
Gordievsky's "legend" was that he was a loyal KGB officer. The technique: a cover story must be simple, verifiable, and boring. "The best cover stories are the ones that no one questions because they're too ordinary to remember."

### Technique 6: The Mole Hunt Analysis
When Gordievsky found his apartment bugged, he had to determine whether the KGB knew everything or was just suspicious. The technique: a security breach does not necessarily mean total compromise. Sometimes it means the enemy is fishing.

### Technique 7: The Exfiltration Prep
Veronica Price prepared PIMLICO over months: reconnaissance, contingency planning, fallback plans. The technique: prepare for every eventuality, then assume something will go wrong. "The best plans are the ones that account for failure at every stage."
