# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *Born to Run* by Christopher McDougall — Chapters 1-20.

## The Three Frameworks

### Framework A: The Tarahumara Way (Chapters 1, 3, 4, 9)

The Tarahumara (Rarámuri) live in Mexico's Copper Canyons and run hundreds of miles weekly — well into old age — with virtually no injuries. They wear thin sandals made from old tire treads. They run for pleasure, not competition. Their races are multi-day celebrations with music, feasting, and community. Their secrets: simple diet (corn, beans, chia seeds), gentle running form, and the joy of running.

> **Case: The Tarahumara Catch a Deer** (Chapter 1): Explorers reported seeing a Tarahumara runner chase a deer until it dropped dead from exhaustion, "its hoofs falling off." The Tarahumara's persistence hunting technique: run the animal in the heat of the day until it overheats and collapses. The deer can sprint faster, but the human can keep going — hour after hour — because we sweat, and the deer can't.

> **Case: The Running Sandals** (Chapter 3): The Tarahumara make their own sandals from old tire treads and rawhide. They have no cushioning, no arch support, no motion control — everything modern shoe companies claim runners need. Yet the Tarahumara have almost no running injuries. Joe Vigil, America's greatest distance-running coach, studied them and concluded: "The shoe is the villain."

### Framework B: The Shoe Industry Fallacy (Chapters 4, 5, 7)

McDougall traces the history of the modern running shoe. Before the 1970s, most running shoes were thin-soled and flat. Then Nike introduced the "Waffle Trainer" with a cushioned heel. Overnight, running injuries skyrocketed. The cushioned heel encouraged runners to land on their heel — which sends impact forces of 2-3x body weight straight up the leg. Before cushioned shoes, runners naturally landed on their midfoot.

> **Case: Caballo Blanco's Ankle Injury** (Chapter 7): McDougall gave Caballo a pair of thick-soled Nike trail shoes as a gift. Caballo, who normally ran in thin sandals, wore the Nikes for a run and severely sprained his ankle. "I fucked my left ankle for the first time in many years! I'm not used to running with thick soles anymore." The injury happened because the thick soles changed his form — he landed differently, and his foot couldn't feel the ground.

### Framework C: Human Evolution — Born to Run (Chapters 10, 11)

McDougall presents the evidence that humans are the best distance runners on the planet. We have: a nuchal ligament that keeps our heads steady while running, large glutes that stabilize our trunks, long Achilles tendons that store and release elastic energy, an extraordinary ability to cool via sweating, and the biomechanics for persistence hunting. In a marathon, a human can outrun a horse.

> **Case: The Leadville 100** (Chapters 9, 10): The Leadville Trail 100 is a 100-mile race through the Colorado Rockies at altitudes above 10,000 feet. Ken Chlouber created it to put his dying mining town back on the map. "Maybe killing a few folks will get us back on the map." The race became the proving ground that showed the Tarahumara's dominance — and the site of an epic battle between Ann Trason (the greatest female ultrarunner of her era) and Victoriano, a 23-year-old Tarahumara who barely spoke Spanish.
