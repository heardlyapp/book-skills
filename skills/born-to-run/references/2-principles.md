# 2 — Key Principles

> Source: *Born to Run* by Christopher McDougall.

## The 7 Principles

### 1. Humans Are Built to Run — Not Fast, But Far

Our anatomy is designed for distance running: nuchal ligament for head stability, glutes for trunk stabilization, Achilles for elastic energy storage, and sweat glands for cooling. We can outrun horses over marathon distances.

> **Case: Persistence Hunting (Chapter 10):** Before bows and arrows, humans hunted by running. They would chase an animal in the midday heat, at a steady trot. The animal could sprint, but it would overheat and have to stop. The human could keep going because of sweating. Eventually, the animal collapsed. This is why we are hairless, sweaty, and upright — all adaptations for running.

### 2. The Shoe Is the Villain

Modern cushioned running shoes change the way your foot lands. They encourage heel-striking, which sends impact forces up through your knees and hips. The Tarahumara, running in thin sandals on rocky trails, have almost no running injuries.

### 3. Pain Is a Teacher, Not an Enemy

"Make friends with pain, and you will never be alone." The modern approach: avoid pain. The Tarahumara approach: understand it, work with it, use it. Pain tells you something. Listen to it — just don't obey it immediately.

### 4. Running Is Play

The Tarahumara do not "train." They run to visit friends, to check on crops, to get to a celebration. Their races are parties. The joy is the point. When you run for joy, you run forever. When you run for a number on your watch, you burn out.

### 5. Form Matters More Than Shoes

A shorter stride, higher cadence, and midfoot landing under your center of gravity prevents the impact forces that cause injury. The shoe is irrelevant if your form is wrong.

### 6. The Body Knows How to Run Naturally

> **Case: Barefoot on Grass (Chapter 12):** When you take off your shoes and run barefoot on grass, you naturally land on your midfoot or forefoot with a shorter stride. Your body knows how to protect itself. The shoes trick you into landing wrong. The Tarahumara don't need coaches to teach them how to run. They never forgot.

### 7. Community Makes You Faster and Happier

The Tarahumara run in groups. They help each other. They laugh and sing while running. The social connection is not a distraction from performance — it is the foundation of performance. Running alone is harder. Running together is freedom.
