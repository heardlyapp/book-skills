# 4 — Anti-Patterns

> Source: *Born to Run* by Christopher McDougall.

## Common Errors

### 1. Heel-Striking in Cushioned Shoes

**The trap:** "My expensive running shoes have thick, padded heels. That must be better for my knees."

**Why it fails:** Cushioned heels encourage heel-striking. When you land on your heel, the impact force (2-3x body weight) goes straight up through your ankle, knee, hip, and spine. The padding does not absorb this force — it just delays the onset of pain. Before cushioned shoes existed, no one ran this way. The Tarahumara land on their midfoot. Their knees last a lifetime.

> **Case: The Shoe Industry's Dirty Secret (Chapters 4, 5):** McDougall documents that after the running boom of the 1970s — when cushioned shoes became the norm — running injuries actually increased. The technology intended to prevent injury was causing it. The National Institutes of Health found that 70-80% of runners get injured every year. The Tarahumara injury rate: near zero. The only variable that changed: the shoes.
> **Key takeaway**: If the solution makes the problem worse, question the solution. Before 1970, runners ran in thin flats and rarely got injured. After cushioned shoes, the injury crisis began.

> **Case: Ann Trason's $40,000 Medical Bill (Chapter 10):** Ann Trason, one of the greatest ultrarunners in history, had multiple surgeries and medical treatments related to running. She was the anti-Tarahumara: modern gear, modern medicine, and constant injuries. The Tarahumara Victoriano, who ran the same race and beat her, had zero medical support, zero gear budget — and zero injuries.
> **Key takeaway**: More technology does not equal better outcomes. Sometimes less is more.

**The trap:** "I need to take long strides to run fast."

**Why it fails:** Long strides mean you're landing with your foot far ahead of your center of gravity. This acts as a brake with every step. It increases impact forces dramatically. The fix: higher cadence (180 steps/minute), which automatically shortens your stride.

### 3. Treating Running as Punishment

**The trap:** "No pain, no gain. I have to push through."

**Why it fails:** The Tarahumara don't push through pain. They listen to it. They adjust. They run for joy. The "tough it out" approach leads to injury, burnout, and (most commonly) quitting. Running should feel good. If it doesn't, something is wrong.

### 4. Buying Expensive Gear Before Fixing Form

**The trap:** "I'll buy better shoes, compression socks, a good watch, and then I'll be ready."

**Why it fails:** None of these things fix your running form. The Tarahumara run in tire-tread sandals and have no watches. The gear is not the problem or the solution. The form is.

### 5. Running on Autopilot Without Awareness

**The trap:** "I just run. I don't think about it."

**Why it fails:** Most of the bad habits — heel-striking, over-striding, tensing up — happen because you're not paying attention. The first step to better running is awareness. Feel the ground. Listen to your body. The Tarahumara run in a state of relaxed awareness — not distraction, not strain. Presence is the practice.
