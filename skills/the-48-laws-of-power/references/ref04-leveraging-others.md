# Laws 7, 11, 13, 26, and 33: Leveraging Others and Hidden Hands

This reference covers the laws of indirect control: how to get others to do your work, how to create dependence, how to appeal to self-interest, how to keep your hands clean, and how to discover and exploit weaknesses. These laws are about power that operates through others—the most durable kind of power because it doesn't require you to appear powerful.

## Law 7: Get Others to Do the Work, But Always Take the Credit

**The Core Idea**: Use the wisdom, knowledge, and legwork of other people to further your own cause. Not only will such assistance save you valuable time and energy, it will give you a godlike aura of efficiency and speed. In the end, your helpers will be forgotten and you will be remembered.

**The Classic Case: Talleyrand**

Charles-Maurice de Talleyrand-Périgord, Napoleon's foreign minister, was a master of this law. Talleyrand was physically unimpressive—he walked with a limp from a childhood injury—but he had an extraordinary ability to make others serve his purposes without realizing it. He would listen carefully to proposals from subordinates, let them develop the ideas, and then present them to Napoleon as if they were his own. His subordinates felt valued (he listened to them), but he got the credit.

Talleyrand's genius was not in his own ideas but in recognizing good ideas in others and making them his own. He survived the French Revolution, Napoleon's fall, and the Bourbon Restoration because he never appeared to be the driving force behind anything.

**Modern Application: Management the Talleyrand Way**

The best managers follow this law without being manipulative: they hire talented people, give them autonomy, and then present their team's work as the team's—while making sure their own role in enabling the work is visible to senior leadership. The manager who claims "my team did it all themselves" is admirable but may be overlooked. The manager who says "I put together a team that accomplished X" gets the credit while the team feels ownership.

## Law 11: Learn to Keep People Dependent on You

**The Core Idea**: To maintain your independence, you must always be needed and wanted. The more you are relied upon, the more freedom you have. Make people depend on you for their happiness and prosperity, and you have nothing to fear. Never teach them enough so they can do without you.

**The Classic Case: The Court Wizards of Ancient China**

Throughout Chinese imperial history, scholars and officials secured their power by making themselves indispensable. They cultivated exclusive knowledge—rituals, calendar-keeping, classical texts—that only they could interpret. The emperor could not function without them because the legitimacy of his rule depended on performing the correct rituals at the correct times.

This is the purest form of this law: create a monopoly on a critical skill or knowledge set. The person who is the only one who knows how the system works cannot be fired.

**Modern Application: The Indispensable Engineer**

A software engineer who is the only person who understands the company's core system is in a position of immense power. But this is fragile: if the system is rewritten or the engineer is replaced, the power vanishes. A smarter application: become indispensable for your judgment and decision-making, not just your technical knowledge. The person whose advice is sought on key decisions is more secure than the person who wrote the code.

## Law 13: Appeal to Self-Interest, Never to Mercy or Gratitude

**The Core Idea**: When you need help, never appeal to someone's past gratitude, friendship, or sense of mercy. These are unreliable. Instead, show them what they have to gain. Frame your request in terms of their self-interest.

**The Classic Case: Henry Kissinger's Negotiations**

Henry Kissinger's approach to international diplomacy was a masterclass in Law 13. When negotiating with Soviet leaders, he didn't appeal to shared humanity or past cooperation. He laid out the concrete benefits of cooperation: trade deals, grain sales, arms reduction. Every negotiation was framed as a transaction where both sides gained. By making his requests align with the other party's self-interest, Kissinger achieved outcomes that appeals to gratitude never could.

**Modern Application: Asking for a Raise**

When negotiating a raise, don't list your years of service or loyalty. Instead, demonstrate the value you've created and how much more you can contribute. The employer's interest is in retaining someone who generates profit, not in rewarding loyalty. Frame your request accordingly: "Here is the revenue I generated last year. I believe I can do even more. Here's what it would take to keep me focused on this."

## Law 26: Keep Your Hands Clean

**The Core Idea**: You must appear to be a paragon of civility and efficiency. Use others as scapegoats and cat's-paws to disguise your involvement in dirty work.

**The Classic Case: Queen Elizabeth I and Mary, Queen of Scots**

Elizabeth I spent years being urged to execute her cousin Mary, Queen of Scots, who was implicated in plots against Elizabeth's throne. Elizabeth resisted publicly, expressing reluctance to execute a fellow monarch. But she created the conditions for Mary's execution: she allowed the courts to try her, let Parliament pressure her, and finally signed the death warrant. The execution was carried out by others. Elizabeth's hands appeared clean; history holds Mary's trial and the court officials responsible.

**Modern Application: Organizational Restructuring**

When a company needs to fire people, the CEO rarely does it personally. HR delivers the news. The CEO issues a carefully worded statement. The "hatchet man" takes the blame, and the CEO remains the benign figure at the top. This is not cowardice—it's strategic. The leader who personally fires people becomes the target of resentment and loses their ability to lead the remaining team.
