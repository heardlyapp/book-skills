# Laws 15, 22, 23, 28, 29, and 47: Offensive and Defensive Strategy

This reference covers the strategic core of the 48 Laws—the laws about when to attack, when to retreat, and how to position yourself for maximum power. These are the most "military" of the laws, drawing heavily on Sun Tzu, Napoleon, and Clausewitz.

## Law 15: Crush Your Enemy Totally

**The Core Idea**: More is lost by stopping halfway than by total annihilation. A wounded enemy will recover and seek revenge. If you must fight, fight to the finish. Leave nothing to chance.

**The Classic Case: Cesare Borgia**

When Cesare Borgia conquered the province of Romagna, he needed to establish order. He appointed the cruel Ramiro de Lorqua to govern in his absence. Lorqua was effective: he restored order through brutal means, suppressing rebellion and enforcing Borgia's rule. The people hated Lorqua but feared Borgia.

Once order was established, Borgia faced a problem: Lorqua had become too powerful and the people associated Borgia with Lorqua's cruelty. On the night of December 26, 1502, Borgia had Lorqua arrested. The next morning, Lorqua's body was found in the public square of Cesena, cut in half, with a bloody knife and a wooden block nearby. The spectacle was terrifying and brilliant: Borgia disassociated himself from Lorqua's cruelty while demonstrating that even his most powerful deputy was expendable. The people were grateful for peace and terrified of the man who could kill his own henchman.

**Modern Application: Organizational Restructuring**

When a company needs to eliminate a toxic department or underperforming executive, half-measures are worse than nothing. Moving the person sideways, demoting them, or reducing their responsibilities without removing them creates resentment, confusion, and organizational drag. The effective approach: a clean, swift removal with a clear rationale. The organization heals faster.

## Law 22: Use the Surrender Tactic

**The Core Idea**: When you are weaker, never fight for honor's sake. Surrender first. Surrender gives you time to recover, time to irritate your conqueror, time to wait for their power to wane. Make surrender a tool.

**The Classic Case: The Chinese Art of Submission**

Throughout Chinese history, the art of strategic surrender was refined into a high art. When a weaker state faced a stronger invader, the wise ruler would submit—paying tribute, sending hostages, performing elaborate ceremonies of submission. But submission was never genuine: it bought time. The weaker ruler would rebuild, fortify, and wait for the stronger power's inevitable decline. The Barbarians eventually tired of governing China; the Chinese were patient.

**Modern Application: Career Setbacks**

When you're fired, demoted, or publicly embarrassed at work, the instinct is to argue, explain, and defend yourself. This is usually the wrong move. A graceful surrender—accepting responsibility, thanking the employer for the opportunity, leaving with dignity—preserves your reputation (Law 5) and keeps doors open. The person who fights their firing creates enemies; the person who accepts it gracefully is invited back.

## Law 23: Concentrate Your Forces

**The Core Idea**: Intensity defeats extensity every time. Find one rich vein and mine it deep. Scattered efforts produce shallow results.

**The Classic Case: Rothschild Banking**

The Rothschild banking family built the largest fortune in 19th-century Europe by concentrating their forces. Instead of diversifying across industries, they focused exclusively on sovereign debt and government bonds. They became the indispensable bankers of Europe—financing wars, treaties, and empires—because they did one thing better than anyone else.

**Modern Application: Deep Work and Specialization**

In a world of generalists, the specialist with deep expertise has disproportionate power. A software engineer who is "okay at everything" is replaceable. A software engineer who is the world's best at optimizing database queries is indispensable. The concentration of forces principle means: invest in becoming exceptional at one thing before branching out.

## Law 28: Enter Action with Boldness

**The Core Idea**: Timidity is dangerous. Any mistakes committed through audacity can be corrected with more audacity. The bold are admired; the timid are ignored.

**The Classic Case: Alexander the Great's Gordian Knot**

When Alexander the Great arrived in Gordium, he was shown the legendary Gordian Knot—a complex tangle of ropes tied by King Gordius. Prophecy said whoever untied the knot would rule Asia. Alexander examined the knot, drew his sword, and cut through it in one stroke. Whether the story is historically accurate doesn't matter—it perfectly illustrates the principle. While others deliberated, Alexander acted. Boldness is its own form of intelligence.

**Modern Application: Startup Fundraising**

The founder who approaches investors with hesitation and excessive caution signals that they don't believe in their own venture. The founder who asks for $5 million with absolute conviction may be wrong, but they'll get further than the timid founder who asks for $500,000 while qualifying it with "but I'm not sure we need it."
