# Core Framework — Outgrowing God

## Part 1: Goodbye God

**Chapter 1: So Many Gods!** Dawkins opens with the "so many gods" argument. "I don't believe in Jupiter or Poseidon or Thor. I don't believe in ancient Egyptian gods, Babylonian gods, Celtic gods, Hindu gods." Then: "And I don't believe in Yahweh, the god of the Jews." The implication: you're already an atheist about thousands of gods. You just haven't taken the final step.

**Chapter 2: But Is It True?** "Evidence is what matters." Faith is belief without evidence — or despite evidence. Dawkins contrasts how we know things in science (evidence, testability, peer review) with how religion claims to know (revelation, scripture, tradition).

**Chapter 4: The Good Book?** The Bible is not a reliable source of truth. Contradictions, historical errors, the story of Noah (impossible), the treatment of women, endorsement of slavery. Case: "If you were on a jury, would you convict someone based on the Bible as evidence?"

## Part 2: Evolution and Beyond

**Chapter 7: Surely There Must Be a Designer?** The argument from design is the most powerful argument for God — and Darwin refuted it. "Living things look designed. But evolution by natural selection explains how they came to look that way without needing a designer."

**Chapter 8: Steps Towards Improbability** "Climbing Mount Improbable" — evolution works in tiny steps. The eye evolved independently 40+ times. "Each small step was an improvement over the previous one. No step needed to be a large jump."

**Chapter 9: Crystals and Jigsaw Puzzles** Explaining how complex order emerges naturally: crystals (simple), jigsaw puzzles (complex but designed), living things (complex and emerges naturally through evolution). Case: The bombardier beetle's chemical defense system — often cited by creationists as "irreducibly complex" — actually evolved step by step.

**Chapter 11: Did We Evolve to Be Religious?** Religion is a byproduct of other evolved traits: the tendency to see agency where there is none (a twig is a snake), the tendency to obey authority, the tendency to seek causes. Religion hijacks these useful traits.

**Chapter 12: Taking Courage from Science** "Science gives us a different kind of courage — the courage to face reality as it is, not as we wish it were."
