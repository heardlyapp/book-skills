# Reference 04: The Rule of Law and FBI Independence

## Overview

McCabe's *The Threat* is fundamentally a book about the rule of law — what it means, why it matters, and what happens when it is attacked from within. McCabe defines the rule of law not as a abstract concept but as a practical operating system for democratic governance: laws apply equally to everyone, no one is above the law, and the institutions that enforce the law must be independent from political influence.

## Key Principles

1. **The Rule of Law Is Not Self-Executing** — McCabe emphasizes that the rule of law doesn't exist because it's written down in the Constitution. It exists because people in law enforcement and the justice system *choose* to uphold it, every day, often at great personal cost. When the President attacks the FBI, he is not attacking a building or a bureaucracy — he is attacking the people who have sworn to uphold the law against all enemies, foreign and domestic.

2. **FBI Independence Is a Counterintelligence Imperative** — McCabe explains that FBI independence from political pressure is not just a nice ideal — it is a practical necessity for the Bureau's counterintelligence mission. The FBI cannot investigate a sitting president for potential coordination with a foreign adversary if the president can simply fire those who lead the investigation. The independence of the FBI is a feature, not a bug, designed precisely for moments when the investigation reaches the highest levels of power.

3. **The Special Counsel Regulation Was Designed for Exactly This Scenario** — McCabe describes the special counsel regulation (28 CFR Part 600) as a carefully crafted mechanism for protecting politically sensitive investigations. When the Attorney General is recused (as Jeff Sessions was on Russia matters) and the Deputy Attorney General determines that an investigation requires independence from the normal chain of command, a Special Counsel can be appointed with specific protections — most importantly, the Special Counsel can only be fired for cause. McCabe's behind-the-scenes account of the Mueller appointment reveals just how deliberately this mechanism was used to protect the Russia investigation from presidential interference.

4. **Loyalty to the Constitution vs. Loyalty to a Person** — McCabe draws a sharp distinction between loyalty to the Constitution (which every FBI agent swears to uphold) and personal loyalty to a political leader. The former is the foundation of the rule of law; the latter is the foundation of authoritarianism. When Trump demanded personal loyalty from FBI leaders, he was, in McCabe's view, asking them to abandon their constitutional oath.

## Case Study 1: The Rosenstein Memo — How a Legal Document Was Weaponized

Deputy Attorney General Rod Rosenstein wrote an internal memorandum critiquing Comey's handling of the Hillary Clinton email investigation — specifically, Comey's decision to publicly announce the investigation's conclusion without charging Clinton, which Rosenstein believed exceeded FBI protocols. The memo was never intended as a justification for firing Comey. But when Trump decided to fire Comey, the White House seized on Rosenstein's memo as cover, publicly attributing the firing to Rosenstein's recommendation. McCabe describes Rosenstein's reaction when he learned the memo would be used this way: a mixture of anger and resignation. Rosenstein knew that defending himself would look like disloyalty to the President. The incident illustrates McCabe's broader point: the rule of law can be subverted not just by breaking rules but by using legal documents for purposes they were never intended to serve.

## Case Study 2: McCabe's Own Firing — Justice Delayed and Denied

McCabe was fired from the FBI on March 16, 2018 — just 26 hours before he was eligible to retire with his full pension. The firing was based on an internal investigation by the Justice Department's Office of the Inspector General, which found that McCabe had authorized the disclosure of information to a reporter and then misled investigators about it. McCabe disputes the findings and argues that the timing was deliberately punitive. He points out that the Inspector General process is usually lengthy and that the final decision rushed to completion just before his retirement eligibility — a pattern he calls "justice weaponized." McCabe writes about the experience with bitterness but also with a sense of institutional duty. He accepted his firing knowing that fighting it would draw attention away from the more important story: what was happening to the FBI as an institution. His willingness to absorb the punishment rather than escalate the political conflict is, in his telling, the opposite of what a mob boss would do — and evidence of the very institutional integrity Trump was attacking.

## Practical Application

When users ask about the rule of law, explain that it's not an abstraction — it's a practical system that depends on the independence of law enforcement institutions. The FBI's independence is protected by design, including special counsel regulations that allow investigations to continue even against political pressure. McCabe's key insight is that the real threat to the rule of law is not external attack but internal subversion — using legal tools for illegal or unethical purposes, demanding personal loyalty over constitutional loyalty, and attacking institutions until they bend to political will.

> 💡 **Heardly Tip:** McCabe's distinction between loyalty to the Constitution and loyalty to a person is a powerful frame for understanding the central conflict of the Trump-FBI relationship. It's also a useful lens for evaluating any institution facing political pressure.
