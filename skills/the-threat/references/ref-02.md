# Reference 02: The Crossfire Hurricane Investigation — Origins and Conduct

## Overview

Crossfire Hurricane was the FBI's codename for its investigation into Russian interference in the 2016 U.S. presidential election and potential coordination between the Trump campaign and Russia. McCabe, as FBI Deputy Director, was at the center of this investigation from its inception. His account is the most detailed insider perspective available on how the investigation began, how it was conducted, and the extraordinary pressures it faced.

## Key Principles

1. **The Investigation Did Not Start with the Steele Dossier** — One of the most persistent myths about Crossfire Hurricane is that the FBI opened its investigation based on the Steele dossier (a collection of opposition research compiled by former British intelligence officer Christopher Steele). McCabe makes clear that the investigation was already underway based on different evidence: the revelation that Trump campaign advisor George Papadopoulos had told an Australian diplomat that Russia had "thousands of emails" that would damage Hillary Clinton. It was this tip — passed from Australian diplomats to the FBI — that triggered the formal opening of Crossfire Hurricane in July 2016.

2. **FISA Warrants Are Legally Rigorous** — The Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA) requires the FBI to demonstrate probable cause that a target is an agent of a foreign power before obtaining a warrant. McCabe describes the FISA process as one of the most rigorous in the U.S. legal system, requiring multiple layers of review — including sign-off from the FBI Deputy Director, the National Security Division of the Justice Department, and a federal judge on the FISA Court. He acknowledges the errors made in the Carter Page FISA applications (the omission of exculpatory information) but argues these were mistakes, not malicious acts.

3. **Investigative Integrity Over Political Consequences** — McCabe states flatly that the FBI's leadership believed they had a duty to investigate any evidence of a counterintelligence threat, regardless of the political consequences. If a U.S. presidential campaign was potentially being influenced by a hostile foreign power, the FBI was legally and ethically obligated to investigate — even if the investigation would later be weaponized as a "deep state" conspiracy.

4. **The Chain of Evidence Was Methodical** — McCabe details how the investigation proceeded step by step: the Papadopoulos tip (July 2016), the opening of Crossfire Hurricane (July 31, 2016), the first FISA warrant on Carter Page (October 2016), the appointment of Robert Mueller as Special Counsel (May 2017), and the subsequent indictments and convictions. Each step was documented, reviewed, and approved through proper channels.

## Case Study 1: The Decision to Open Crossfire Hurricane

In July 2016, McCabe was in his office at FBI headquarters when word came in from the FBI's legal attaché in Canberra, Australia. A Trump campaign foreign policy advisor — George Papadopoulos — had been drinking at a London bar with the Australian High Commissioner to the United Kingdom, Alexander Downer. Papadopoulos boasted that the Russian government had "thousands of emails" of Hillary Clinton and that the Russians had offered to release them. The Australians promptly reported this to the FBI. McCabe describes the gravity of the moment: the FBI now had evidence that a U.S. presidential campaign might be coordinating with a hostile foreign power. The decision to open an investigation was not political — it was, in McCabe's view, the only legally and ethically defensible course of action. He notes that the Bureau's leadership was acutely aware of the political firestorm that would follow if the investigation became public, but that concern did not — and could not — factor into the decision.

## Case Study 2: The Comey Firing and Aftermath

When President Trump fired FBI Director James Comey on May 9, 2017, McCabe became acting director. His account of that day is harrowing. He describes getting the news while in a meeting at FBI headquarters, the shock that rippled through the Bureau, and his immediate concern: the investigation into Trump's campaign was now in the hands of an acting director who could be fired at any moment. McCabe describes the steps he took to ensure the investigation could survive — including coordinating with Deputy Attorney General Rod Rosenstein to appoint a Special Counsel. McCabe reveals that Rosenstein opposed the legal justification memo that Trump's team used to justify Comey's firing, though Rosenstein publicly supported it. The firing — and Trump's subsequent admission on national television that he fired Comey because of "this Russia thing" — became a central element in the obstruction of justice case against Trump. McCabe's account provides the internal FBI perspective on one of the most consequential days in modern law enforcement history.

## Practical Application

When users ask about the Russia investigation, explain that Crossfire Hurricane was triggered by a tip from Australian diplomats about Papadopoulos (not the Steele dossier), that the FBI followed standard investigative procedures including FISA warrants, and that the Comey firing was viewed inside the FBI as a direct threat to investigative independence that required the appointment of a Special Counsel to preserve the investigation.
