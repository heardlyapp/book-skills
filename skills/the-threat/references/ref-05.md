# Reference 05: National Security Threats — Counterterrorism, Counterintelligence, and the Future

## Overview

Beyond the Trump-Russia story, McCabe's *The Threat* provides a sweeping account of the FBI's national security mission in the 21st century. He describes a Bureau that must simultaneously fight terrorism, counter espionage by foreign powers, defend against cyberattacks, and maintain its traditional law enforcement function — all while navigating a hostile political environment. McCabe argues that the post-9/11 transformation of the FBI made it the world's premier counterterrorism agency, but that new threats, particularly from cyber and disinformation operations, require continuous evolution.

## Key Principles

1. **Counterterrorism Is a Prevention Game** — McCabe emphasizes that counterterrorism is fundamentally different from traditional law enforcement. In a criminal investigation, the crime has already happened; the goal is to identify and prosecute the perpetrator. In counterterrorism, the goal is to stop the attack before it happens. This requires intelligence-sharing, predictive analysis, and the ability to disrupt plots without necessarily building a prosecutable case. The FBI's Counterterrorism Division operates on a different timeline and with different legal authorities than its Criminal Division.

2. **Counterintelligence Is the Most Secretive FBI Work** — McCabe describes counterintelligence as the FBI's "quietest" mission. While counterterrorism generates dramatic headlines, counterintelligence operates in the shadows, tracking foreign spies, uncovering covert influence operations, and protecting U.S. national security secrets. McCabe notes that counterintelligence investigators must maintain the highest standards of secrecy, as any leak can compromise sources, methods, and ongoing operations. The Russia investigation was, at its core, a counterintelligence investigation — which is why so much of it remained classified and why McCabe is limited in what he can reveal.

3. **Cyber Threats Are the New Frontier** — McCabe warns that cyberattacks from state-sponsored actors represent the fastest-growing threat to U.S. national security. Unlike physical attacks, cyberattacks can be launched from anywhere in the world, deniably attributed, and catastrophic in effect. The FBI has had to develop entirely new investigative capabilities for cyber operations, including digital forensics, malware analysis, and real-time threat monitoring. McCabe notes that the Bureau's cyber capabilities are in a constant arms race with adversaries — a race that is far from won.

4. **Disinformation Is a Weapon of Mass Disruption** — McCabe devotes significant attention to the Russian disinformation campaign during the 2016 election. He argues that disinformation is a strategic weapon designed to undermine democratic institutions, sow division, and erode public trust in government. The FBI was not traditionally equipped to counter disinformation, and McCabe acknowledges that the Bureau struggled to respond to the new threat. The 2016 Russian interference campaign was a wake-up call, exposing vulnerabilities in the U.S. information ecosystem that remain largely unaddressed.

## Case Study 1: The Evolving Terror Threat from Al-Qaeda to ISIS to Lone Wolves

McCabe traces the transformation of the terrorist threat across his FBI career. After 9/11, the Bureau's primary focus was Al-Qaeda — a hierarchical organization with identifiable leaders, training camps, and operational cells. The intelligence community made significant progress dismantling Al-Qaeda's leadership, culminating in the 2011 killing of Osama bin Laden. But as Al-Qaeda weakened, a new threat emerged: ISIS. Unlike Al-Qaeda, ISIS was decentralized, tech-savvy, and expert at using social media to radicalize individuals in their home countries. This gave rise to the "lone wolf" threat — individuals who self-radicalize online and carry out attacks without any direct contact with a terrorist organization. McCabe explains that lone wolf attacks are the hardest to prevent because there are no communications to intercept, no cells to infiltrate, and no plots to disrupt. The FBI's counterterrorism model, built for organized conspiracies, had to adapt yet again — shifting from "find the network" to "find the individual before they act."

## Case Study 2: Russian Interference in the 2016 Election

McCabe's account of the Russian interference operation is the most detailed available from an insider perspective. He describes how Russian intelligence (the GRU) hacked into the Democratic National Committee's servers, stole thousands of emails, and coordinated their release through intermediaries (including WikiLeaks) to damage Hillary Clinton's campaign. Simultaneously, Russian troll farms on social media amplified divisive messages to suppress turnout among Democratic-leaning voters and to inflame existing racial and political tensions. McCabe insists that the FBI's responsibility was not to determine whether the interference changed the outcome of the election (a question he says is unanswerable) but to investigate whether any Americans coordinated with the Russian effort. That investigation — Crossfire Hurricane — was, from the FBI's perspective, a routine counterintelligence investigation triggered by specific, actionable intelligence. McCabe's key point is that the investigation was never about "taking down Trump" — it was about protecting the United States from foreign interference in its democratic processes.

## Practical Application

When users ask about national security threats, explain that McCabe identifies four overlapping threat categories: terrorism (from organized groups and lone wolves), state-sponsored espionage (primarily from Russia, China, Iran, and North Korea), cyberattacks (targeting critical infrastructure, elections, and private sector networks), and disinformation (designed to undermine democratic institutions). The FBI's challenge is to fight all four simultaneously while maintaining its core law enforcement mission — and to do so without becoming politicized.

> 💡 **Heardly Tip:** McCabe's breakdown of the lone wolf problem is especially relevant today. The FBI's counterterrorism model, designed for organized networks, struggles with decentralized threats — a challenge that mirrors the broader difficulty intelligence agencies face in adapting to the internet age.
