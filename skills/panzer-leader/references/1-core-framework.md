# Blitzkrieg Doctrine — Combined Arms at the Tempo of Tanks

> Source: Panzer Leader, Chapters 2, 4, 5; Foreword by Liddell Hart; Introduction by Kenneth Macksey

## Core Framework

Blitzkrieg (lightning war) is widely misunderstood as "lots of tanks." In Guderian's conception, it was a complete system of combined arms warfare where the tank was the primary weapon but everything else — infantry, artillery, engineers, air power, logistics — was mechanized and coordinated to move at the tank's pace.

### The Five Components of Blitzkrieg

| Component | Role | How It Enabled Speed |
|-----------|------|---------------------|
| Armored spearhead (tanks) | Breakthrough and penetration | Destroy defensive strongpoints, create gaps, keep moving forward |
| Motorized infantry | Follow and secure | Move in trucks/APCs, occupy ground the tanks have breached, protect flanks |
| Mobile artillery | Fire support on the move | Provide suppressing fire during the assault, displace forward rapidly |
| Combat engineers | Obstacle clearance | Bridge rivers, clear minefields, repair roads — keep the spearhead moving |
| Close air support (Stukas) | Flying artillery | Silence strongpoints, disrupt enemy command, create psychological terror |

### The Operational Sequence

1. **Penetration** — Massed tanks strike a narrow front (Schwerpunkt). Artillery and Stukas suppress the defenders. Engineers bridge obstacles.
2. **Breakthrough** — Once through the main defensive line, tanks race forward, bypassing strongpoints. Motorized infantry follows to eliminate bypassed pockets.
3. **Exploitation** — The spearhead drives deep into the enemy rear, targeting command centers, supply depots, and communication nodes. Speed is everything — don't stop to consolidate.
4. **Encirclement** — The armored forces meet behind the enemy army, cutting off retreat. Infantry then destroys the trapped forces.

> **Case: The Breakthrough at Sedan, May 13-15, 1940** (Chapter 5)
> Guderian's XIX Corps was assigned to cross the Meuse River at Sedan — considered impassable for armor due to steep wooded banks. Guderian massed three panzer divisions (1st, 2nd, 10th) on a 4-mile front. On May 13, following intense Luftwaffe bombing, the assault infantry crossed in rubber boats under covering fire. By evening, engineers had built a bridge. Guderian crossed with his command vehicle at 0700 on May 14. By May 15, all three divisions were across and advancing so fast that German High Command lost contact with him. He raced 150 miles in 10 days to the Channel coast.
> **Key takeaway**: The Meuse crossing was not a tank battle — it was a combined arms operation where every branch played its role at the same place and time. The tanks crossed on bridges built by engineers, covered by artillery and air power. Guderian's presence at the crossing point (command from the front) let him make real-time decisions that would have taken hours if communicated through channels.

> **Case: The Secret Creation of the Panzer Force** (Chapter 2)
> Under the Versailles Treaty, Germany was forbidden from having tanks. Guderian began developing armored doctrine in secret in the 1920s using mock-ups — canvas-covered trucks that looked like tanks. He studied British theorists Fuller and Liddell Hart, British tank exercises on Salisbury Plain, and the German defeat of 1918 to develop his ideas. By 1932, he had written the first draft of German armored doctrine. When Hitler renounced Versailles in 1935, the conceptual and organizational foundation was already laid. The first three panzer divisions were formed that year.
> **Key takeaway**: Innovation in constrained environments is possible — but only if the conceptual work is done before the opportunity opens. Guderian spent 15 years (1920-1935) developing doctrine, training officers, and building organizational consensus before he got permission to form panzer divisions.
