# Operational Techniques — Campaigns and Battles

> Source: Panzer Leader, Chapters 4, 5, 6, 8, 9

## The Polish Campaign (1939)

Guderian commanded XIX Corps (3rd Panzer, 2nd and 20th Motorized Divisions) in the attack from Pomerania toward Warsaw. The campaign showed how even early-model panzers (Panzer I and II, light tanks with machine guns only) could achieve rapid breakthroughs against a determined but less mobile enemy. The encirclement of Polish forces near Brest-Litovsk became a model for future operations.

## The Russian Campaign (1941) — The Great What-If

| Phase | Dates | Achievement |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| Border battles | June 22-July 9 | Panzer Group 2 advanced 300 miles in 18 days, encircled Minsk |
| Smolensk pocket | July 10-August 5 | Captured 300,000 prisoners, but delay before Moscow |
| Kiev encirclement | August 25-September 26 | Hitler diverted from Moscow to Kiev. 600,000 prisoners. But lost 6 weeks |
| Moscow advance | October 2-December 5 | Mud (October) then snow (November). Stopped 20 miles from Moscow. |

> **Case: The Moscow-Kiev Debate, August 1941** (Chapter 6)
> In August 1941, Guderian was summoned to Hitler's headquarters. The decision: should Army Group Center advance on Moscow or turn south to Kiev? Guderian argued for Moscow — the political and psychological center of the Soviet Union. Field Marshal Brauchitsch supported him. Field Marshal Bock also wanted Moscow. But Hitler insisted on Kiev: "My generals know nothing about the economic aspects of war." Guderian was ordered to turn south. The Kiev operation was a tactical masterpiece (600,000 prisoners) but a strategic disaster — the attack on Moscow was delayed until October, by which time autumn rains had turned roads to mud and winter was approaching. Guderian's panzers reached the outskirts of Moscow in December but could go no further.
> **Key takeaway**: The Kiev debate is the classic case of tactical brilliance serving strategic error. The panzers won a stunning victory at Kiev — but lost the war by not taking Moscow in September while the weather held. Military strategy must prioritize the decisive point, not the largest available target.

> **Case: The First Dismissal, December 1941** (Chapter 6)
> By early December 1941, Guderian's forces were frozen 20 miles from Moscow in -30°F conditions. Tanks wouldn't start. Troops lacked winter clothing. The Soviet counteroffensive began on December 5. Guderian ordered a tactical withdrawal — the only sensible move. Hitler ordered no withdrawals. Guderian flew to Hitler's headquarters on December 20 to argue his case. The meeting was tense. Guderian pointed out that the troops were suffering, that the equipment was failing in the cold, that the position was untenable. Hitler refused. On December 26, Guderian was dismissed from command. He spent the next 13 months on inactive service.
> **Key takeaway**: There is a limit to how far professional military judgment can prevail against political leadership. Guderian chose to give his honest assessment, knowing it would cost him his command. The question "when do you resign?" is the hardest ethical question a military professional faces.
