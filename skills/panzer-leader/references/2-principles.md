# Command Philosophy — Auftragstaktik and Fronthand Leadership

> Source: Panzer Leader, Chapters 2, 5, 6, 14; Introduction by Kenneth Macksey

## The German Command Tradition

Auftragstaktik (mission-type orders) was the Prussian-German command philosophy long before Guderian. His contribution was applying it to fast-moving armored warfare, where the tempo of operations made centralized control impossible.

### Mission-Type Orders vs. Detailed Orders

| Aspect | Mission-Type Orders (Auftragstaktik) | Detailed Orders (Befehlstaktik) |
|--------|--------------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| Commander gives | Intent and objective | Specific instructions |
| Subordinate decides | Method of execution | Nothing — just follow orders |
| Communication | Brief — what to achieve | Lengthy — what, when, where, how |
| Response to change | Immediate — subordinate adapts | Delayed — must request new orders |
| Required trust | High — subordinate's judgment is trusted | Low — superior doesn't trust subordinate's judgment |

### Principles of Fronthand Leadership

1. **The commander's place is where the critical decision will be made.** — For Guderian, this was near the spearhead, not the rear.
2. **Radio is the command weapon.** — Guderian's command vehicle had radios to all division commanders. He could talk to them individually or broadcast to all simultaneously.
3. **See the terrain yourself.** — No map or report replaces a commander's eyes. Guderian personally reconnoitered the Meuse crossing sites.
4. **Make decisions at the speed of battle.** — A decision made in 5 minutes at the front is worth more than a perfect decision made in 5 hours at headquarters.
5. **Lead by example, not by order.** — Guderian crossed the Meuse with his assault troops, not after the bridge was secure.

> **Case: Guderian's Command Vehicle in the French Campaign** (Chapter 5)
> Throughout the 1940 campaign, Guderian commanded XIX Corps from a radio-equipped armored command vehicle (a modified Panzer III). He drove at the head of his divisions, not at the rear. When his panzers crossed the Meuse, he was one of the first across. When they raced to the Channel, he was with the leading units. When his superiors issued cautionary orders, he often did not receive them because he was ahead of the signal units. This was not accidental — Guderian deliberately stayed ahead of the communications chain to preserve his freedom of action.
> **Key takeaway**: Physical position is a command decision. Being at the front gives you better information, faster decision cycles, and more freedom from higher headquarters. If you want to lead fast, you must be at the leading edge — not just metaphorically but physically.

> **Case: The Conflict with von Rundstedt and Kluge** (Chapters 5-6)
> Guderian's command style repeatedly brought him into conflict with his superiors. During the French campaign, his army commander von Rundstedt ordered him to halt and consolidate. Guderian interpreted "halt" as "slow down" and continued advancing while sending misleading reports. Later, under von Kluge in Russia, the conflict became open — von Kluge ordered Guderian to stop northward movements, Guderian refused, and the resulting feud led to Guderian's first dismissal in December 1941.
> **Key takeaway**: Fronthand leadership creates tension with rear-echelon commanders who don't have the same information or sense of tempo. Guderian accepted this tension as the price of speed. The question is not whether to follow orders but whether your superior has the same picture of the battlefield that you do. If not, your duty is to inform them — but also to act.
