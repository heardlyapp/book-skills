## 6 Anti-Patterns of the Dramatic Worldview

### 1. The Binary Trap

Judging the world as "rich vs poor" or "developed vs developing." In reality, 75% of humanity lives on middle income levels. The binary is misleading.

### 2. News as Reality

Believing that what you see on the news is representative of the world. The news covers the unusual, not the typical. It is systematically biased toward negative, dramatic events.

> **Case: The Plane Crash Coverage** (Chapter 4): Every plane crash is covered extensively. Every safe flight is not. If you judged aviation safety from the news, you would never fly. Yet flying is safer than driving. The news is not reality — it is a selection of the most dramatic events from reality.

### 3. Straight Line Thinking

Assuming current trends will continue indefinitely. Most trends — population growth, poverty reduction, disease eradication — follow S-curves, not straight lines.

### 4. Blaming Individuals Instead of Systems

Looking for someone to blame is a satisfying instinct. But it prevents us from understanding the systemic causes of problems. Blame is a shortcut; systems thinking is the path.

> **Case: The Blame Game in Politics** (Chapter 9): Rosling observed that politicians around the world blame immigrants, foreign powers, or the previous administration for problems. But most problems are systemic — immigration is driven by economic disparities, not malice. Blaming does not solve anything.
> **Key takeaway**: Blame is emotionally satisfying but intellectually lazy. The question is not "who did this?" — it is "what system produced this outcome?"

### 5. The Urgency Trap

Every problem is presented as urgent. But urgency creates panic, and panic creates bad decisions. The antidote: ask for data before deciding.

### 6. The Certainty Illusion

Believing you already understand the world. The most dangerous people are those who never doubt their own knowledge. Rosling's first lesson: "You don't know what you don't know."
