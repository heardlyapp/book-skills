## The Factfulness Framework: The Dramatic Worldview vs. The Fact-Based Worldview

Rosling's core insight: humans have evolved to notice dramatic, negative, and immediate information — but most global trends are gradual, positive, and slow-moving. The mismatch between our instincts and reality creates a "dramatic worldview" that is systematically wrong.

The solution: 10 thinking tools (rules of thumb) that counteract the 10 dramatic instincts. Each instinct has a corresponding tool.

> **Case: The Chimpanzee Test** (Introduction): Rosling asked thousands of people — including Nobel laureates, medical doctors, and top executives — simple questions like "What percentage of the world's 1-year-olds are vaccinated?" and "How many girls finish primary school?" On most questions, people scored worse than random guessing (chimpanzees choosing randomly would beat them). Even experts in global health scored poorly. The problem is not ignorance — it is a systematically wrong worldview.
> **Key takeaway**: Being wrong about the world is not a failure of intelligence. It is a failure of information. The media, our instincts, and our education all conspire to give us a dramatic worldview that is not just inaccurate — it is dangerous.

> **Case: The Machete Mob** (Dedication): Early in his career as a doctor in Mozambique, Rosling was confronted by an angry mob with machetes. He survived because a barefoot woman argued rationally with the mob, using facts. This experience convinced him that the most powerful force in the world is not weapons or money — it is a fact-based argument made by a credible person.
> **Key takeaway**: Facts are not weak. Under the right conditions, a rational argument can defuse even a mob. The problem is not that facts don't matter — it is that most people don't have access to the right facts.
