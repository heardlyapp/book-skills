## 7 Principles for a Fact-Based Worldview

### 1. Most People Are in the Middle

The gap instinct makes us see binary divisions: rich vs poor, developed vs developing. In reality, most of humanity lives on Levels 2 and 3 — not extreme poverty, not wealth. The world is a continuum, not a divided place.

### 2. Expect Bad News

The negativity instinct causes the media to systematically overreport negative events and underreport positive ones. The solution: when you hear bad news, ask "Is this the exception or the rule?"

> **Case: The Nepal Earthquake** (Chapter 2): The 2015 Nepal earthquake was a terrible disaster that killed 9,000 people. But it was heavily covered by news. Meanwhile, the fact that Nepal reduced child mortality by 75% between 1990 and 2015 received almost no coverage. The negative event was exceptional; the positive trend was typical.
> **Key takeaway**: The absence of positive news does not mean the absence of positive progress. Good news is not dramatic enough to be news.

### 3. Not All Lines Are Straight

The straight line instinct assumes current trends will continue. Population growth, for example, looks like it will explode — but the UN projects stabilization by 2100. Curves can bend.

### 4. Risk = Danger × Exposure

The fear instinct makes us overestimate rare, dramatic risks. The formula: risk = hazard × exposure. Before fearing something, check both the actual danger AND how likely you are to encounter it.

> **Case: Plane Crashes vs. Traffic Accidents** (Chapter 4): After 9/11, many people stopped flying and drove instead. The fear of terrorism caused an estimated 1,500 additional traffic deaths in the US in the following year. The fear instinct made a rare risk seem large, and the actual response — driving — was far more dangerous.
> **Key takeaway**: What we fear most is rarely what kills us. The most dangerous things are the boring ones.

### 5. Always Get Things in Proportion

The size instinct makes a single number seem large. Always ask: "compared to what?" and "per person?" A big number by itself is meaningless without context.

### 6. Question Categories

The generalization instinct creates stereotypes. Every category hides variation. Look for the differences within groups, not just between groups.

### 7. Look for Systems, Not Villains

The blame instinct finds someone to blame. But most problems are caused by systems, not individuals. Ask: "what is the system that produced this outcome?"
