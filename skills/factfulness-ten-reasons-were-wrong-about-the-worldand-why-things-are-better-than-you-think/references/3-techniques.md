## Practical Techniques

### 1. The Chimpanzee Check

Before forming a strong opinion based on news headlines, ask: "Would a chimpanzee (random guesser) do better than me on this topic?" If the answer is yes, you need data.

### 2. The Proportion Reflex

Whenever you see a large number, immediately ask: "Compared to what?" and "Per person?" A big number is meaningless without context.

> **Case: The CO2 Bar Chart** (Chapter 5): Rosling shows a bar chart of CO2 emissions by country. China's bar is enormous, dwarfing everyone else. But per person, the US emits nearly twice as much. The size instinct makes you blame China. The proportion instinct shows the real picture.
> **Key takeaway**: A number without a comparison is not information — it is a weapon.

### 3. The System Question

When something goes wrong, resist the urge to blame. Instead ask: "What is the system that made this outcome likely?" Finding the system is more useful than finding the villain.

### 4. The Urgency Pause

When someone says "We must act now!" stop. Take a breath. Demand data. Most urgent problems are not as urgent as they appear. Panic decisions are bad decisions.

> **Case: The Ebola Panic** (Chapter 10): During the 2014 Ebola outbreak, media coverage created global panic. Rosling showed that the number of people who died from Ebola in West Africa was statistically insignificant compared to deaths from malaria, HIV, and traffic accidents in the same region. But the urgency instinct made Ebola the focus of global attention.
> **Key takeaway**: The urgency instinct hijacks our decision-making. The rule: take small steps, demand data, resist the pressure to act now.
