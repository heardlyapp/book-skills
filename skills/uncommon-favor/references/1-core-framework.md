# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *Uncommon Favor* by Dawn Staley — Preface, Lessons 1-3.

## The Three Frameworks

### Framework A: Discipline as Foundation (Lesson 1)

Staley opens her book with Lesson Number One: "The disciplined person can do anything." Her mother, Estelle Staley, taught this through example — raising five children in the Raymond Rosen projects, keeping a flower garden, maintaining a home, never missing church. Discipline was not a punishment. It was the structure that made everything else possible. Basketball became the expression of that discipline.

> **Case: Estelle Staley's Example (Lesson 1):** "My mother taught me everything that ended up mattering in the long run of my life, even if I didn't recognize those lessons at the time. Estelle Staley was loving, funny, compassionate. She sacrificed her needs to provide us, her children, our wants. She was also the epitome of discipline in every way." From her mother, Staley learned that discipline in small things — how you keep your house, how you tend your garden, how you show up — translates to discipline in everything.

> **Case: The Neighborhood Watch (Lesson 1):** Raymond Rosen projects had a block captain (Miss Jones) who ran the fourth block like a drill sergeant. "Every adult believed they had jurisdiction to parent any young person. If you were acting out, there were multiple sets of eyes on you." This communal discipline created a safe environment where Staley could develop. Discipline was not just individual — it was collective.

### Framework B: The Fish Out of Water (Lesson 3)

Staley arrived at UVA as the top high school player in the country — and felt completely lost. The culture shock of moving from North Philly to Charlottesville nearly broke her. She was isolated, homesick, unable to connect. Her survival mechanism: retreat into basketball. She watched game tape on loop, avoided social situations, and focused obsessively on her craft.

> **Case: The UVA Grounds (Lesson 3):** "When I think back on my eighteen-year-old self, leaving North Philly and venturing to the great unknown of the University of Virginia, I recall a young woman filled with angst, unprepared, out of her element." Staley describes the campus as "pumpkin spice latte pool" — a world so foreign it felt like another planet. Her solution: "I fixated on my game. I didn't allow myself a social life." She was miserable — but she never transferred, never quit, never stopped playing.

### Framework C: The Ladder of Hope (Lesson 2, Epilogue)

Carolyn Peck, the first Black woman to win an NCAA basketball championship, gave Staley a piece of her championship net. Staley kept it in her wallet for years. When South Carolina won the title in 2017, she cut up the net and sent pieces to every Black female Division I coach. This is the core of Staley's philosophy: success is not complete until you have used it to lift others.

> **Case: The Championship Net (Lesson 2):** "Carolyn told me she wanted me to hold on to that piece of nylon until we earned our own national title. She suspected that I was close to winning it. I kept that piece of net in my wallet for years." After winning, Carolyn told her: "Now go find the next coach you believe deserves a piece of the net." Staley decided: "I'm going to give pieces of it to every Black female Division I coach. Because they're looking at me and imagining their own futures."
