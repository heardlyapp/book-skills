# 5 — Voice & App

> Source: *Uncommon Favor* by Dawn Staley.

## Staley's Voice

Staley writes as a competitor reflecting on her journey. The voice is direct, humble, and fiercely proud. She does not posture or pretend to be someone she is not. She admits her struggles — the isolation at UVA, the loneliness, the difficulty of navigating a world that was not built for her. But she never frames herself as a victim. Every difficulty is presented as something she overcame. The book is structured as life lessons — each chapter begins with a numbered lesson that she returns to throughout.

> **Case: The Preface's Declaration (Preface):** "I was made for basketball. I knew from the moment I was old enough to run. Maybe earlier. I can't recall a time when I didn't know it." The certainty of this opening sets the tone for the entire book. Staley knew who she was and what she was for. Everything else — the obstacles, the isolation, the odds — was just something to get through on the way to her purpose.

> **Case: The Mural Unveiling (Lesson 2):** When Staley's mother saw the 100-foot mural of her daughter at 8th and Market in Philadelphia, she burst into tears. "I don't think my mom really knew how big an impact her daughter had made on the culture until that moment. It's one thing to see me hoop. Quite another to see me memorialized downtown like a civil rights leader or a pop star." The pride of a mother — who had raised five children in the projects, who never drove a car, who took the bus everywhere — seeing her daughter immortalized.

## Five Scenarios

### 1. The First-Generation College Student

You are the first in your family to attend college. You feel out of place. You are considering transferring to a school where you will be more comfortable. Staley's experience at UVA: she stayed, she struggled, she grew. Do not leave unless you must. Give yourself time to find your anchor.

### 2. The Person from a Low-Income Background Entering an Elite Space

You grew up with nothing and now you find yourself in a room full of people who had everything. You feel like an imposter. Staley's response: your background is not a weakness. It is a source of perspective, toughness, and wisdom that no one else in the room has. Use it.

### 3. The Mentor or Coach

You lead a team or organization. Staley's model: recruit character, build trust, hold high standards, serve your people. The best leaders are not the stars — they are the ones who make everyone else better.

### 4. The Person Who Has Achieved Success and Wonders What Is Next

Staley's answer: pay it forward. Find the next person coming up behind you. Give them a piece of your net. Your success is not complete until it has been used to lift others.

### 5. The Shy/Introverted Leader

You are not the loudest person in the room. You wonder if you can lead. Staley: "I'm socially inept." She is also one of the most successful coaches in NCAA history. Leadership is not about volume. It is about presence, consistency, and service.
