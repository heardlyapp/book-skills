# 4 — Anti-Patterns

> Source: *Uncommon Favor* by Dawn Staley.

## Common Errors

### 1. Believing Where You Start Determines Where You Finish

**The trap:** "I grew up in poverty / in a bad neighborhood / with no connections. I will never get ahead."

**Why it fails:** Staley grew up in the Raymond Rosen projects in North Philly. The odds were overwhelmingly against her. She became a Hall of Fame player and NCAA championship coach. The starting point does not determine the ending point.

> **Case: The Odds Beater (Lesson 2):** Staley: "When all is said and done, I'm most proud of being an odds beater. Growing up where I did, people looking in believed I wasn't supposed to amount to anything. The odds were stacked against me. And I demolished them."
> **Key takeaway**: Your starting point is a fact. It is not a destiny. The odds can be beaten — but only if you refuse to believe they are final.

> **Case: The Fish Out of Water (Lesson 3):** Staley struggled to adjust at UVA because she thought she had to become someone else. "I was a fish out of water, slowly suffocating." She spent her first year hiding, avoiding social contact, retreating to game tape. Only later did she realize that her difference was not a deficit — it was her unique perspective.
> **Key takeaway**: When you try to become someone else to fit in, you lose what makes you valuable. The goal is not to assimilate. The goal is to find where your authenticity succeeds.

**The trap:** "I need to leave my old neighborhood / background behind if I want to succeed."

**Why it fails:** Staley never left North Philly behind. She carried it with her. The toughness, the community, the discipline — all of it came from Raymond Rosen. "I eventually learned North Philly would never be in the rearview. My hometown was as integral to me as my spine."

### 3. Assuming You Have to Change Who You Are to Fit In

**The trap:** "I'm the only one who looks like me / talks like me / comes from where I come from. I need to act like them to belong."

**Why it fails:** Staley was miserable at UVA because she thought she had to become someone else. Eventually she learned that her difference was not a weakness — it was her perspective. She did not need to become like her classmates. UVA needed to learn who she was.

### 4. Isolating Completely When Discomfort Hits

**The trap:** "I don't fit in. I'll just retreat and focus entirely on my work."

**Why it fails:** Staley did this — and admits it was a mistake. She hid in her dorm, avoided social situations, and did not extend herself to recruits. "I would kill one of my players if they were as rude or absent as I was at my age." Finding your anchor is good. Complete isolation is not. You still need connection.

### 5. Keeping Success to Yourself

**The trap:** "I earned this. I made it on my own. I don't owe anyone anything."

**Why it fails:** Staley believes the opposite: "I was given a chance. And so, in return, I wake up every morning with the intention to give others a chance." The championship net ritual embodies this: success that is not shared is incomplete.
