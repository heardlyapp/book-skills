# Reference: Civic Virtue & Conscience

> Route when the user asks about the moral foundations of self-government, religion in American public life, John Adams's views on virtue, or Washington's Farewell Address.

## Core Teaching

The Founders believed that republican self-government could not survive without a virtuous citizenry. This is not a marginal theme in their thinking — it is expressed repeatedly by Washington, Adams, Jefferson, Franklin, and Madison. The book captures this through the Founders' reflections on what they observe in modern America: a people who have lost the habits of self-discipline and civic responsibility.

**John Adams on Morality.** Adams was the most explicit: "Our Constitution was made only for a moral and religious people. It is wholly inadequate to the government of any other." In the speech, Adams delivers this line with characteristic bluntness. He argues that liberty without virtue degenerates into license, and that a people who cannot govern themselves will be governed by tyrants.

**Washington's Farewell Address.** Washington's most famous speech as President is actually a written farewell address published in 1796. In the book, Washington (through his actual words) warns against the "baneful effects of the spirit of party," the danger of foreign entanglements, and the importance of religion and morality as "indispensable supports" of political prosperity. The speech is read aloud in the Founders' gathering, and its warnings are applied to modern political divisions.

**Religion and the Public Square.** The Founders consistently affirmed the importance of religion for public morality. This does not mean they wanted a theocracy — they were clear about protecting religious liberty and prohibiting an established church. But they believed that a people without religious restraint would not long remain free.

## Case Study: Adams's Defense of the Constitutions

John Adams wrote "A Defense of the Constitutions of Government of the United States" (1787) while serving as ambassador to Britain. It was the most systematic defense of balanced government written by any Founder. The book traces how republics throughout history had failed due to lack of virtue and imbalance of power. The Founders in the speech reference Adams's historical analysis when they warn about what happens when a people forgets its founding principles.

## Case Study: Franklin's Call for Prayer at the Convention

During the Constitutional Convention, when the debates had become hopelessly deadlocked, the elderly Benjamin Franklin stood and proposed that the Convention begin each day with prayer. "The longer I live," Franklin said, "the more convincing proofs I see of this truth — that God governs in the affairs of men." His proposal was not adopted (the Convention had no funds to pay a chaplain), but the moment illustrates the Founders' belief that divine providence was connected to the success of the American experiment. The Founders in the speech recall this moment as a model of humility before providence.

## Case Study: Jefferson on the "Laws and Institutions" of Free Government

Jefferson's 1801 First Inaugural Address contains one of the most concise statements of the Founders' civic philosophy. He calls the American people to "the preservation of the General Government in its whole constitutional vigor, as the sheet anchor of our peace at home and safety abroad," and urges them to remember that "a wise and frugal Government, which shall restrain men from injuring one another, shall leave them otherwise free to regulate their own pursuits of industry and improvement." The Founders in the speech return to Jefferson's inaugural as a model of how to state the principles of free government without party rancor.
