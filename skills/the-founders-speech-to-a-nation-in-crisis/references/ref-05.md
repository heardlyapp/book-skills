# Reference: Property, Law & The Call

> Route when the user asks about economic freedom, property rights, the rule of law, Hamilton's economic vision, or what the Founders would urge modern America to do.

## Core Teaching

The final chapters of the Founders' speech turn to what must be done. The Founders have diagnosed the crisis — a people who have forgotten their principles, a government that has exceeded its limits, and institutions that have lost their way. Now they issue a call to action.

**Property Rights as a Foundation of Liberty.** The Founders believed that the right to acquire, possess, and transfer property was essential to liberty. Jefferson included property in his original draft of the Declaration (the Continental Congress replaced it with "the pursuit of happiness"), and Madison argued that "the preservation of property and the distribution of property are the two most fundamental objects of government." In the speech, the Founders argue that economic freedom is not separable from political freedom — the same government that controls your property also controls your life.

**The Rule of Law.** The Constitution created a government of laws, not of men. This means that no one is above the law — not the President, not Congress, not the military. The Founders in the speech emphasize this principle because they see modern Americans treating the law as a political weapon rather than a neutral framework for justice. A government of laws cannot survive if the law is applied selectively.

**Hamilton's Economic Vision.** Alexander Hamilton, as the first Secretary of the Treasury, created the financial system that made American prosperity possible: a national bank, federal assumption of state debts, tariffs to protect American industry, and a public credit system. In the speech, Hamilton defends his economic program as essential to national strength and argues that economic liberty and constitutional government are mutually reinforcing.

## Case Study: Hamilton's Report on Manufactures (1791)

Hamilton's "Report on Manufactures" was his most comprehensive statement on economic policy. He argued that a nation should not rely on agriculture alone but should develop manufacturing, which would create jobs, increase productivity, and reduce dependence on foreign goods. Hamilton's vision was contested by Jefferson, who favored an agrarian republic. The Founders in the speech present this debate not as a dispute between pro-business and pro-farming factions but as a fundamental argument about what kind of nation America would become — a theme that continues in modern debates about trade, industry, and economic policy.

## Case Study: The Call — What the Founders Would Say Today

The final chapter of the book is the Founders' direct address to modern Americans. They call for:
1. A return to first principles — read the Declaration, the Constitution, and The Federalist. Understand what the founding actually said.
2. Civic participation — vote, serve on juries, attend town halls, run for office. Self-government is not a spectator sport.
3. Education — teach the next generation the principles of liberty, not as propaganda but as honest history.
4. Defense of the Constitution — resist the temptation to bypass constitutional procedures for short-term political advantage.
5. Unity in essentials — "In matters of principle, stand like a rock; in matters of taste, swim with the current" (Jefferson).

The Founders end with a warning and a hope: the American experiment is not guaranteed to succeed. It requires active maintenance by each generation. But the framework they built is strong enough to endure — if the people will defend it.

## Case Study: The Rising Sun Chair

A recurring symbol in the book is the Rising Sun chair, from which George Washington presided over the Constitutional Convention. As the Convention ended, Franklin gestured toward the chair and observed that painters had struggled to decide whether the sun carved on the back was rising or setting. "Now, at length," Franklin said, "I have the happiness to know that it is a rising and not a setting sun." The Founders in the speech return to this image in their closing remarks — the sun of American liberty rose once, but whether it will continue to rise depends on the generation holding it now.
