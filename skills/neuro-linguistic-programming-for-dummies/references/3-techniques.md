## Practical Techniques

### 1. The Rapport Builder

Match the person's body posture, voice tone, breathing rate, and key words. Do not mimic — subtly match. Rapport is the foundation.

### 2. The Anchor Setup

Recall a powerful positive memory. As the feeling peaks, create a physical anchor (thumb to finger). Repeat 3-5 times. Test: trigger the anchor and feel the state return.

> **Case: The Speaker's Anchor** (Part III): A public speaker anchored confidence before every speech. The anchor allowed him to access his best state on demand.
> **Key takeaway**: Anchors give you control over your state. You do not have to wait for confidence to come — you can call it.

### 3. The Submodality Shift

To reduce the emotional charge of a memory, make it smaller, darker, farther away, and black-and-white. To increase positive feelings, make the image bigger, brighter, closer, and colorful.

### 4. The Meta Model Question

When someone speaks vaguely, ask: "Specifically what/who/where/when?" Recover the deleted information. "They are against me" → "Specifically who is against you?"

### 5. The Six-Step Reframe

1. Identify the unwanted behavior. 2. Communicate with the part. 3. Find positive intention. 4. Generate new choices. 5. Test. 6. Check ecology.

### 6. The Perceptual Positions Shift

When stuck in a conflict: (1) Express your view from first position. (2) Step into the other person's shoes (second position). (3) Step back as an observer (third position). Insights often come from the shift.

> **Case: The Business Negotiation** (Part II): A negotiator used perceptual positions to understand the other side's constraints. The deal closed when she proposed a solution that met both sides' needs.
> **Key takeaway**: Understanding the other person's position is not weakness — it is strategic. Second position reveals what first position cannot see.
