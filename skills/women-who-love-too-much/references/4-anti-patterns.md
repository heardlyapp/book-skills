> Source: Women Who Love Too Much, Chapters 1-11 — 6 anti-patterns of codependent love

## Anti-Pattern 1: Mistaking Intensity for Intimacy

The most common anti-pattern: believing that the intensity of your feelings proves the depth of your love. The more obsessed you are, the more you believe you love him. In reality, intensity is a sign of anxiety, not love. Healthy love is calm.

> **Case: The Drama Addiction** (Chapter 3): A woman described how her relationship was a rollercoaster. When things were good, they were amazing. When things were bad, she was devastated. She believed the highs and lows meant the relationship was passionate. Norwood's diagnosis: the relationship was not passionate — it was addictive. The drama was not love — it was adrenaline.
> **Key takeaway**: If the relationship is a rollercoaster, it won't stabilize. The intensity you call passion is actually anxiety. Real love is not a drama.

## Anti-Pattern 2: The Rescuer Fantasy

"I can save him." "If I love him enough, he'll change." "He's not bad — he's just wounded." The rescuer fantasy is the belief that your love has healing powers. It doesn't. You cannot rescue someone from themselves.

> **Case: The Wounded Bird** (Chapter 2): A woman fell in love with a man who had been through a terrible divorce, had a drinking problem, and had lost his job. She believed that if she just loved him enough, he would quit drinking, find a job, and be happy. She poured her savings into his debts. She sacrificed her friendships. He drank more. The rescue failed — not because she didn't love enough, but because rescue is not possible.
> **Key takeaway**: Rescuing is not love. It is control disguised as compassion. You cannot save someone who does not want to be saved.

## Anti-Pattern 3: Tolerating the Intolerable

"I know he shouldn't treat me this way, but..." The woman who loves too much has an extraordinary tolerance for bad behavior. She makes excuses, minimizes the damage, and convinces herself it's not that bad.

> **Case: The Excuse Machine** (Chapter 1): A woman whose boyfriend called her names, broke things during arguments, and once pushed her against a wall told Norwood: "He's not abusive. He just has a temper. He had a hard childhood." Norwood: "Every abuser had a hard childhood. That does not make the abuse acceptable."
> **Key takeaway**: There is no excuse for abuse. A hard childhood, a difficult job, a bad day — these are not licenses to mistreat you. What you tolerate, you accept.

## Anti-Pattern 4: Confusing Need with Love

"I need him." "I can't live without him." This is not love — it is dependency. Needing someone is not the same as loving them. Love wants what is best for the other person. Need wants what is best for itself.

> **Case: The Impossible Choice** (Chapter 6): A woman was in a relationship with a man who refused to get help for his depression. She couldn't leave because she "needed" him. She was afraid of being alone. She was afraid he would hurt himself. She was afraid of starting over. These fears kept her trapped. Norwood: "If you stay because you can't bear to leave, you are not choosing love. You are choosing fear."
> **Key takeaway**: If you can't leave, you can't choose. And if you can't choose, you're not in love — you're in a trap.

## Anti-Pattern 5: Sacrificing Yourself for the Relationship

"I'll give up my friends, my hobbies, my career — for him." The woman who loves too much believes that love requires total sacrifice. She loses herself in the relationship. But a relationship where one person has disappeared is not a relationship — it's a possession.

> **Case: The Vanished Woman** (Chapter 7): A woman who had been a successful artist gave up her studio, her gallery contacts, and her painting practice when she moved in with her boyfriend. She told herself he needed her more. Two years later, she didn't know who she was anymore. Her identity had been absorbed by the relationship. Rebuilding her self took longer than the relationship lasted.
> **Key takeaway**: The relationship that requires you to shrink is not worth having. Love should expand you, not diminish you.

## Anti-Pattern 6: The Next Time Illusion

"Next time will be different." "He promised he would change." "This time, I know he means it." The woman who loves too much lives in the future — in the fantasy of who he could become, not the reality of who he is.

> **Case: The Broken Promise** (Chapter 4): A woman's boyfriend promised to stop drinking after a DUI. He stopped for three weeks. She believed the promise was real. When he started drinking again, she believed his next promise. The pattern repeated for years. Norwood: "A promise is not a change. A promise is a hope. Change is demonstrated over time, not declared once."
> **Key takeaway**: Believe behavior, not promises. If he has a pattern of breaking promises, the next promise is not different — it's the same pattern continuing. Trust is demonstrated, not promised.
