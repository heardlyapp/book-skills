> Source: Women Who Love Too Much, Chapters 1-11 — 7 principles for understanding and breaking the cycle

## Principle 1: The Intensity of Obsession Is Not the Depth of Love

The most deceptive principle: the more obsessed you are with a man, the more you believe you love him. But obsession is not love. It is anxiety disguised as passion. Real love is calm, consistent, and reciprocal. If you're in constant emotional turmoil, you're not in love — you're in addiction.

> **Case: The Adrenaline Trap** (Chapter 3): Norwood describes how women who love too much become addicted to the adrenaline cycle of a dysfunctional relationship. The high of reconciliation after a fight is so powerful that it becomes addictive. The woman begins to crave the crisis because the resolution feels so good. She becomes addicted to the drama, not the man.
> **Key takeaway**: If you feel more alive during the fights than during the peaceful times, you are addicted to adrenaline, not to love. True intimacy is peaceful, not dramatic.

## Principle 2: You Cannot Change Him — He Must Want to Change

The single most important principle for recovery. You cannot fix, save, or heal a man through love. If he drinks too much, works too much, is emotionally closed, or abuses you — your love will not change him. He will only change if he acknowledges his problem and seeks help for himself.

> **Case: The Waiting Game** (Chapter 1): A woman spent seven years waiting for her boyfriend to stop drinking. She went to Al-Anon. She prayed. She begged. She threatened to leave. Nothing worked. He finally stopped drinking — two years after she left him. The change came not from her love but from his own rock bottom.
> **Key takeaway**: Waiting for a man to change is the most expensive investment you can make. The return on investment is almost always zero. He changes when he decides to, not when you decide he should.

## Principle 3: Your Relationship Pattern Is Not Random — It's Blueprinted

The partner you choose is not a coincidence. You are drawn to the type of man who recreates the emotional dynamics of your childhood. You are trying to master the old wound by reenacting it with a new person. The key to breaking the pattern is understanding the original wound.

> **Case: The Father Connection** (Chapter 4): A woman whose father was cold and critical grew up believing she was unlovable. As an adult, she chose men who were cold and critical — because they felt familiar. She spent every relationship trying to earn their love, just as she had tried to earn her father's. The pattern broke when she recognized it.
> **Key takeaway**: You are not choosing the wrong men by accident. You are choosing men who feel like home — even if home was painful. Awareness of the pattern is the first step to changing it.

## Principle 4: Detaching Is Loving — for Both of You

Staying in a dysfunctional relationship is not loving. It enables his dysfunction and destroys yours. Detaching — stepping back, letting him face the consequences of his actions — is the most loving thing you can do. It gives him the chance to change and gives you the chance to heal.

> **Case: The Enabling Trap** (Chapter 7): A woman covered for her husband's alcoholism for years — calling his boss when he was too hungover to work, making excuses to friends, cleaning up his messes. She thought she was helping. She was enabling. When she finally stopped, he hit bottom quickly — and eventually sought treatment. Her detachment was the catalyst for his recovery.
> **Key takeaway**: Protecting someone from the consequences of their behavior is not kindness — it is enabling. Letting them face the consequences is love.

## Principle 5: Being Alone Is Better Than Being in the Wrong Relationship

Many women stay in bad relationships because they fear being alone. But being alone is not the worst thing. The worst thing is being in a relationship that destroys your self-worth. Learning to be alone — and to be happy alone — is the foundation of recovery.

> **Case: The Year Alone** (Chapter 10): Norwood recommends that women in recovery spend at least one year alone — no dating, no relationships, no casual hookups. One woman who followed this advice said: "The first three months were agony. I felt like I would die without a man. But by the ninth month, I had built a life I loved. I had friends, hobbies, a career I was proud of. When I finally started dating again, I was a different woman. I didn't need him. I wanted him."
> **Key takeaway**: The year alone is not punishment. It is the foundation. Without it, you will repeat the pattern. With it, you have a chance at healthy love.

## Principle 6: Codependency Is Not Love — It's Addiction

When your happiness depends on someone else's behavior, you are not in love — you are addicted. True love is interdependent: two whole people choose each other. Codependency is one person propping up another.

> **Case: The High and the Crash** (Chapter 8): Norwood compares loving too much to drug addiction. The woman gets a "high" when the relationship is going well and a "crash" when it's not. She needs more — more intensity, more drama, more proof of love — to maintain the high. Like any addiction, the dose must increase over time. What once felt like love eventually feels like survival.
> **Key takeaway**: If your relationship feels like a drug — with highs and crashes, cravings and withdrawals — it is not love. It is addiction. Treat it like one.

## Principle 7: Healthy Love Is Reciprocal

The simplest but hardest principle: in a healthy relationship, both partners give and receive. If you are always giving and he is always taking, the relationship is not balanced. No matter how much you love him, a one-sided relationship cannot survive.

> **Case: The Reciprocity Test** (Chapter 11): Norwood suggests a simple test: ask yourself, "If I stopped giving for one month, would this relationship survive?" If the answer is no, you are doing all the work. A healthy relationship has a natural give-and-take. Both partners show up. Both partners sacrifice. Both partners feel loved.
> **Key takeaway**: Love is not how much you give. Love is how much both give. If you're the only one giving, you're not in a relationship. You're in a caretaking arrangement.
