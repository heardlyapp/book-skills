## The 10-Step Recovery Program

### Step 1: Detach from the Chaos

**The technique**: Stop obsessing over his problems, his moods, his addictions. You are not responsible for him. You are responsible for yourself. Detachment does not mean you don't care — it means you care enough about both of you to let him face his own life.

**How to do it**: When you feel the urge to "fix" him, stop. Go for a walk. Call a friend. Attend a meeting. Remind yourself: "I am not responsible for his choices. I am only responsible for mine."

> **Case: The Detachment Practice** (Chapter 10): One woman described how she used to wait up for her alcoholic husband every night, checking the clock, calling his phone, imagining car crashes. When she learned to detach, she went to bed at 10 PM. If he came home late, she didn't wait up. In the morning, she didn't ask where he'd been. The detachment was terrifying at first — then liberating.

### Step 2: Focus on Yourself

**The technique**: Shift the attention from him to you. What do you need? What do you feel? What do you want? If you've spent years focused on someone else, you may not know the answers.

**How to do it**: Start a journal. Write down your feelings every day. Ask: "What do I need right now?" The first few times, you may have no answer. Keep asking. The answers will come.

### Step 3: Learn to Be Alone

**The technique**: Spend time alone without a man. No dating. No looking. No hoping. Learn to enjoy your own company. This is the hardest step for most women.

**How to do it**: Schedule one evening per week alone. No calls, no dates, no company. Cook yourself a nice dinner. Watch a movie. Read a book. Learn to be comfortable with yourself.

> **Case: The Solitude Practice** (Chapter 10): Norwood recommends one full year without a relationship. A woman who did this said: "I used to think being alone meant being lonely. Now I know the difference. Lonely is being in a relationship where you're not seen. Alone is being with yourself fully."

### Step 4: Understand Your Childhood

**The technique**: Identify the patterns from your family of origin. What did you learn about love? What role did you play in your family? How did you try to earn love?

**How to do it**: Write your family history. Describe each parent. What was their relationship like? How did they show love? What did you learn about what love requires?

> **Case: The Family Pattern** (Chapter 4): A woman realized that her mother had been the caretaker of her alcoholic father. She learned that love required suffering and sacrifice. She repeated the pattern with her own partners. Identifying the pattern was painful — and liberating.

### Step 5: Develop Healthy Friendships

**The technique**: Build relationships with women that are reciprocal, supportive, and honest. Healthy friendships are the practice ground for healthy romantic relationships.

**How to do it**: Join a support group. Call one friend per day. Practice being vulnerable about your real feelings — not caretaking theirs.

### Step 6: Set Boundaries

**The technique**: Learn to say no. Identify what you will and won't tolerate. Boundaries are not walls — they are guidelines that protect your well-being.

**How to do it**: Start with small boundaries. "I can't talk after 10 PM." "I won't accept being yelled at." "I need 24 hours notice for plans." Each boundary you set strengthens your sense of self.

> **Case: The Boundary Practice** (Chapter 7): A woman who had never set a boundary started with: "I will not answer my phone after 11 PM." The first time she let a call go to voicemail, she felt guilty. By the end of the week, she felt empowered.

### Step 7: Stop Caretaking

**The technique**: Stop trying to fix, rescue, or save other people. Your job is not to be his therapist, mother, or savior.

**How to do it**: When you feel the urge to take care of him, ask: "Would I do this for a friend?" If the answer is no, it's caretaking, not love.

### Step 8: Deal with Your Own Addictions

**The technique**: Women who love too much often have their own addictions — food, shopping, work, prescription drugs — that they use to numb the pain. Recovery requires addressing these.

### Step 9: Find a Support Group

**The technique**: Al-Anon, CODA (Codependents Anonymous), or a similar group. Isolation keeps the pattern alive. Support groups break the isolation.

### Step 10: Practice Intimacy in Safe Relationships

**The technique**: Once you have completed the first nine steps, practice being in a relationship without losing yourself. Choose a man who is emotionally available, consistent, and respectful. The relationship will feel boring at first — that's a good sign.
