# 3. Techniques — Memoir Craft

> Source: "Unbearable Lightness" by Portia de Rossi (2010). Techniques drawn from her narrative approach.

## The Present-Tense Confession

Portia writes most of the memoir in present tense. This creates immediacy — the reader experiences the disorder as it happens, not as a past event being remembered. The effect is claustrophobic and urgent.

> **Case: The Prologue** (Prologue): "He doesn't wait until I'm awake. He comes into my unconscious to find me, to pull me out." The present tense immediately establishes the ongoing nature of the disorder. It is not over. It is happening now. The reader cannot distance themselves from the experience.
> **Key takeaway**: Present tense creates urgency and intimacy. The reader is inside the experience, not looking back at it.

## The Voice as Character

Portia personifies her eating disorder as "the voice" — a distinct character with its own personality, demands, and deceptions. This technique allows her to externalize the disease.

> **Case: The Drill Sergeant Metaphor** (Prologue): "A drill sergeant of a voice that is pushing me forward, marching ahead, keeping time." By giving the voice a recognizable form (a drill sergeant), Portia makes it concrete and knowable. The voice is not her — it is an invader.
> **Key takeaway**: Personifying the disorder makes it easier to fight. You cannot fight an abstraction. You can fight a drill sergeant.

## The Number Pattern

Portia uses numbers throughout the memoir — calories, pounds, miles run, hours exercised — to show how the disorder reduces life to metrics. The abundance of numbers creates a sense of obsessive counting that mirrors her mental state.

> **Case: The Calorie Tracking** (Part One): Portia describes counting every calorie, every gram of fat, every minute of exercise. The numbers dominate the narrative as they dominated her life. The reader feels the exhaustion of the counting.
> **Key takeaway**: Repetition of patterns (numbers, rituals) in memoir can create a visceral sense of what the disorder felt like.
