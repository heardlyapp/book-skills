# 1 — The "Love Everybody, Always" Framework

> Source: "Everybody, Always" by Bob Goff

## The Core Framework: Love Is Someone We Become

Goff's central thesis is deceptively simple: **love everybody, always**. No exceptions, no fine print, no circumstances where love takes a break. The book is 24 stories that explore what this looks like in practice — with creepy people, difficult family members, strangers, and enemies.

### Three Pillars of the Framework

**1. Love Is a Verb, Not a Noun**
You don't fall into love — you become it. Goff writes: "Love isn't something we fall into; love is someone we become." Thoughts and feelings about love don't count. Only action does.

**2. Extravagant Grace Costs Something**
"If it doesn't cost you, it's not love." Real love requires sacrifice — of time, comfort, pride, money, or safety. The measure of your love is what it costs you.

**3. Love the People Who Creep You Out**
"The truth is, we probably creep them out as much as they do us." Goff challenges readers to identify who they've been avoiding — and to start with that person.

> **Case: The Stolen Manuscript** (Prologue): Goff's van was broken into in San Francisco. All windows shattered. Luggage, wallets, phones, laptop — all gone. The first draft of this book was on that laptop, unbacked up. He had to write the entire book again. His response? Not anger at the thieves. "I was disappointed everything was stolen, but I figured it would all work out." The rewritten book became "Everybody, Always" — a book about loving the people who steal from you.
> **Key takeaway**: Even when someone takes something precious from you, you can choose love over anger. The thieves weren't enemies — they were people God loves too.

> **Case: The Creepy People** (Chapter 1): Goff admits: "I've spent my whole life avoiding the people Jesus spent His whole life engaging." People who are different, who make us uncomfortable, who we don't understand. His conclusion: "God's endgame has always been the same. He wants us to love the people near us and love the people we've kept far away."
> **Key takeaway**: Your discomfort with someone is not a sign to avoid them — it's a sign to engage them. The people who creep you out are the people you most need to love.
