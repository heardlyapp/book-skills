# 3 — Techniques for Loving in Action

> Source: "Everybody, Always" by Bob Goff

## Technique 1: Learn Their Name

Goff's signature technique: learn someone's name. Names matter. A name is the beginning of relationship.

> **Case: The Witch Doctors** (Chapter 20): Goff traveled to Uganda and spent time with witch doctors — people most missionaries would avoid. He didn't preach at them. He learned their names. He asked about their families. He listened to their stories. "Find a way to love difficult people more, and you'll be living the life Jesus talked about. Go find someone you've been avoiding and give away extravagant love to them."
> **Key takeaway**: Love starts with a name. Before you can love someone, you have to know who they are. Not who you think they are — who they actually are.

## Technique 2: Show Up

Showing up is 90% of love. Not when it's convenient — when it's hard. Goff's life is marked by showing up in places most people avoid: prisons, conflict zones, difficult family situations.

> **Case: Karl's Dive** (Chapter 13): Goff went to visit a friend in a difficult situation. The details aren't glamorous — it involved a dive bar, a hard conversation, and uncomfortable silence. But Goff showed up. That was the love. No sermon, no solution, no fix. Just presence.
> **Key takeaway**: Most of the time, people don't need your advice. They need you there. Presence is the most underrated form of love.

## Technique 3: Use What You Have

You don't need more resources to love people. You need to use what you already have — your time, your home, your skills, your table.

## Technique 4: Don't Wait for Perfect Conditions

Goff's life is full of imperfect love: a crumbling foreclosure house, a van with no windows, stolen luggage, a book rewritten from scratch. He doesn't wait for the perfect moment to love. He loves with what he has, where he is.

> **Case: The Yellow Truck** (Chapter 4): Goff bought a yellow truck that barely ran. It was ugly, unreliable, and cost more in repairs than it was worth. But he used it to haul things for people who needed help. The truck became a symbol of imperfect love: not fancy, not impressive, but useful. Love doesn't have to look good — it just has to be there.
> **Key takeaway**: Your love doesn't have to be beautiful. It just has to be present and useful. A beat-up yellow truck can carry more love than a shiny new one that never leaves the garage.

## Technique 5: Ask One Question

When you don't know how to love someone, ask: "How can I help?" Or better: "Tell me your story." Goff says most people just want to be heard.
