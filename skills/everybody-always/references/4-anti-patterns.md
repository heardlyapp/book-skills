# 4 — Anti-Patterns: What Blocks Love

> Source: "Everybody, Always" by Bob Goff

## Anti-Pattern 1: Conditional Love

**The mistake:** Loving people only when they are easy to love. Only when they agree with you. Only when they're nice to you.

> **Case: The Wrong People** (Chapter 1): Goff admits: "It's easy to love kind, lovely, humble people. I mean, who wouldn't? These are the ones I've spent much of my life loving. What I've come to realize, though, is that I was avoiding the people I didn't understand." Conditional love is not love — it's a transaction. You love people who make you feel good about yourself.
> **Key takeaway**: If you only love people who are easy to love, you haven't learned to love. You've learned to trade affection for comfort.

## Anti-Pattern 2: Fear-Based Inaction

**The mistake:** Letting fear prevent you from loving. Fear of rejection. Fear of awkwardness. Fear of getting involved. Fear of being hurt.

> **Case: The Barriers We Build** (Chapter 1): Goff writes: "Oftentimes, when I encounter someone who makes me feel afraid, I instantly put up barriers. I put them up with my big words and opinions. I construct them to protect myself. Barriers make me feel right, and that makes me feel safe." The problem: barriers also block love.
> **Key takeaway**: Every time you feel afraid to love someone, that's exactly when you should. Fear is not a stop sign — it's a go sign.

## Anti-Pattern 3: Score-Keeping

**The mistake:** Loving with an invisible ledger. Tracking who gave more, who hurt more, who owes whom. Love that keeps score is not love — it's accounting.

> **Case: The Auction House Debacle** (Chapter 2): Goff bought a house at probate auction by accidentally scratching his nose and ear — the auctioneer interpreted his fidgeting as bids. He ended up with a house he didn't intend to buy. His wife could have kept score. Instead, she loved him through his mistake.
> **Key takeaway**: Love doesn't keep a record of wrongs. If you're tracking who owes whom, you've left the territory of love and entered the territory of contract law.

## Anti-Pattern 4: Waiting for Reciprocation

**The mistake:** Only loving people who love you back. This is not love — it's a mirror. Real love loves without guarantee of return.

## Anti-Pattern 5: Prioritizing Rightness Over Kindness

**The mistake:** Winning arguments instead of winning hearts. Being correct at the expense of being loving.

> **Case: The Arsonist** (Chapter 1): "Burning down others' opinions doesn't make us right. It makes us arsonists." Goff says we can be "correct and not right." When we make our opinions more important than the difficult people God made, "we turn the wine back into water."
> **Key takeaway**: Being right is not the same as being loving. You can win every argument and lose every relationship. Kindness is more important than correctness.
