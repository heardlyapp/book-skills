> Source: The Day of the Jackal, Frederick Forsyth (1971).

# Key Principles — Attention to Detail, Clean Methods, and the Unremarkable Enemy

## Core Principles

> **Case: The Forged Passport** (Early): The Jackal's forger is the best. He creates documents that are indistinguishable from real ones. But he warns the Jackal: the documents will pass a casual inspection. They will not pass a detailed examination. The Jackal must avoid any situation where his documents are examined closely.
> **Key takeaway**: The best disguise is not invisibility. It is not drawing attention. The Jackal does not try to be invisible. He tries to be unremarkable.

> **Case: The Custom Rifle** (Mid-novel): The Jackal does not buy a rifle. He has one built. Each part is manufactured separately, by different people, none of whom know what they are making. He assembles it himself. The rifle cannot be traced. It is a ghost weapon.
> **Key takeaway**: If you need something critical, do not rely on off-the-shelf solutions. Build exactly what you need. Control the entire supply chain.

> **Case: The Escape Routes** (Throughout): The Jackal plans his escape before he enters the country. He has multiple exit strategies. He knows how he will leave if he succeeds and how he will leave if he fails. Every operation includes the escape.
> **Key takeaway**: Never begin an operation without knowing how you will end it. The exit strategy is as important as the entry. Plan your escape before you start.

> **Key takeaway**: Traceability is a double-edged sword. The Jackal's untraceable methods protected him. But they also meant no one could help him. Clean methods come with isolation.
