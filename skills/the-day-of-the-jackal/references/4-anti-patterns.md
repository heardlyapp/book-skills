# Anti-Patterns — Overconfidence, Loose Talk, and Leaving a Trail

## The Central Fallacy

The most dangerous mistake: thinking you are smarter than the system. The Jackal is brilliant. He almost succeeds. He fails because a single detail — an overlooked connection — leads Lebel to him. No plan survives contact with reality. The best preparation reduces the chance of failure but does not eliminate it.

> **Case: The OAS Bunglers** (Early): The OAS leaders who hire the Jackal are amateurs. They talk too much. They trust the wrong people. They are infiltrated by the police. The Jackal, a professional, is disgusted by their amateurism.
> **Key takeaway**: Amateurs talk. Professionals work. The OAS talked about their plans. That is how the police found out about the Jackal in the first place.

> **Case: The Betrayed Contact** (Mid-novel): The Jackal's contact Kowalski is arrested. He gives up information. The Jackal kills him before he can give up too much. But the damage is done. A single point of exposure is all the police need.
> **Key takeaway**: Every person who knows your plan is a point of vulnerability. Trust no one with the complete picture. Compartmentalize.

> **Case: The Near Miss** (Climax): The Jackal almost succeeds. He has de Gaulle in his sights. A split-second difference in timing saves de Gaulle's life. The Jackal's plan was perfect — except for timing.
> **Key takeaway**: You can do everything right and still fail. The system has its own randomness. Accept this. Do not let the fear of randomness stop you from doing everything you can control.
