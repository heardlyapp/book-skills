# Core Framework — God Is Not Great

## Chapter 1: Putting It Mildly — The Four Irreducible Objections

Hitchens opens with his childhood epiphany. A teacher named Mrs. Watts told him that God made all vegetation green because it's restful to our eyes. "I simply knew, almost as if I had privileged access to a higher authority, that my teacher had managed to get everything wrong in just two sentences. The eyes were adjusted to nature, and not the other way about."

**The Four Irreducible Objections to Religious Faith:**
1. "It wholly misrepresents the origins of man and the cosmos" — contradicts science.
2. "Because of this original error it manages to combine the maximum of servility with the maximum of solipsism" — demands submission while claiming you're the center of creation.
3. "It is both the result and the cause of dangerous sexual repression."
4. "It is ultimately grounded on wish-thinking."

**The alternative:** "We do not rely solely upon science and reason, but we distrust anything that contradicts science or outrages reason. We respect free inquiry, openmindedness, and the pursuit of ideas for their own sake."

## Chapter 2: Religion Kills

A catalog of religious violence: the Crusades, Inquisition, Thirty Years' War, witch hunts, sectarian conflict in Northern Ireland, the Balkans, India, the Middle East. "Religion has caused innumerable people not just to conduct themselves no better than others, but to award themselves permission to behave in ways that would make a brothel-keeper or an ethnic cleanser raise an eyebrow."

## Chapter 5: The Metaphysical Claims of Religion Are False

"Religion spoke its last intelligible or noble or inspiring words a long time ago." The central claims of religion — creation, resurrection, revelation, miracles — are not supported by evidence. "How much contortion is required to receive every new insight of science and manipulate it so as to 'fit' with the revealed words of ancient man-made deities?"

## Chapter 7-9: The Scriptures Examined

**Old Testament:** Hitchens details the genocides, infanticides, misogyny, and absurdities — the flood, Sodom and Gomorrah, Joshua's massacres, the treatment of women as property. "The nightmare of the Old Testament."

**New Testament:** Jesus' moral teachings contain "evils that exceed those of the Old." The doctrine of eternal hell (torture without end), the demand to hate one's family, the exclusion of the unbaptized from salvation. "Paul of Tarsus, the hysterical hysteric."

**Koran:** "Borrowed from both Jewish and Christian myths." Hitchens notes the scientific inaccuracies (semen from the backbone, mountains as pegs), the endorsement of slavery, the misogyny, and the doctrine of jihad.

## Chapter 13: Does Religion Make People Behave Better?

Hitchens argues the evidence shows it does not. "No statistic will ever find that without these blandishments and threats we commit more crimes of greed or violence than the faithful." He points to prison populations, crime statistics, and the behavior of religious versus secular societies.

## Chapter 16: Is Religion Child Abuse?

Hitchens' most controversial chapter. He argues that labeling children with a religion before they can consent is a form of abuse. "The first thing to inflict on the child is the sin of Onan (masturbation) or the sin of being ungrateful for the sacrifice of one's parents." He criticizes circumcision, religious schooling, and the theology of original sin.

## Chapter 19: The Need for a New Enlightenment

Hitchens concludes with a call for reason, science, and free inquiry. "We believe with certainty that an ethical life can be lived without religion." The New Enlightenment would be based on the separation of church and state, the primacy of reason, and the recognition that "we are reconciled to living only once."
