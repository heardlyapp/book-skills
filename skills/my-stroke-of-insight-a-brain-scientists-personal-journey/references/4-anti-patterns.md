# Anti-Patterns: Mistakes in Brain Injury Recovery

> Source: My Stroke of Insight, Chapters 13-14

## Testing Instead of Supporting

The most common mistake caregivers make: testing the patient's memory or abilities. "Do you remember me?" "Can you say this word?" This creates fight-or-flight stress that inhibits healing.

> **Case: The Well-Meaning Visitor** (Chapter 13): Visitors who asked "remember this?" or "do you know who I am?" actually hurt Taylor's recovery. Each failed attempt reinforced failure. The brain cannot heal under stress. Taylor recommends: just be present. Don't test.

> **Key takeaway**: When visiting someone with brain injury, don't test them. Just love them.

## Rushing the Timeline

The brain takes the time it takes. Eight years for Taylor to fully recover. Pushing faster creates pressure, which triggers cortisol, which inhibits neuroplasticity.

> **Case: The Frustration Trap** (Chapter 14): When Taylor couldn't walk one day, she felt like she was backsliding. Her mother reminded her: "You walked yesterday and you'll walk again tomorrow." The brain's recovery is not linear.

## Assuming the Left Brain is the Only Reality

Before her stroke, Taylor identified fully with her left hemisphere's narrative: "I am a Harvard neuroscientist." After the stroke, she realized that consciousness is bigger than language.

> **Key takeaway**: The left brain's story about who you are is a useful map — but it's not the territory. You are also the right brain's experience of nowness and connection.
