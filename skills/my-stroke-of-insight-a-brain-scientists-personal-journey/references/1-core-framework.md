# Core Framework: The Stroke and the Brain

> Source: My Stroke of Insight, Chapters 1-4

## The Stroke

December 10, 1996. Taylor woke up with a pounding headache behind her left eye. She felt her cognitive functions shutting down one by one — language first, then proprioception, then consciousness itself. As a neuroanatomist, she recognized what was happening and dialed 911. Within four hours, she could not walk, talk, read, write, or recall any of her life.

> **Case: The Left Hemispheric Hemorrhage** (Chapter 4): A congenital malformation of blood vessels in her left hemisphere ruptured. Blood pooled, compressing the left brain's language and cognitive centers. Taylor described it as "watching my mind completely deteriorate." She felt euphoria as the left hemisphere's constant narrative chatter fell silent — she was experiencing pure right-hemisphere nowness.

> **Case: The 911 Call** (Chapter 5): Despite losing the ability to speak clearly, Taylor managed to dial 911. She could still recognize stroke symptoms because she studied the brain. She knew the mantra: time is brain. Every minute of untreated stroke kills approximately 2 million neurons.

## Left vs Right Hemispheres

The left hemisphere processes time, language, logic, and creates a story about who "you" are. It's the "I am" center. The right hemisphere experiences the present moment, big picture, energy, and connection to the universe. Taylor's stroke forced her into pure right-hemisphere consciousness.

> **Key takeaway**: The left hemisphere's narrative is useful for navigating the world — but it's not the only reality. The right hemisphere's experience of nowness and universal connection is equally valid and vastly more peaceful.
