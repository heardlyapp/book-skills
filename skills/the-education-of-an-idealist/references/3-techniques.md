# The Upstander's Toolkit — Practical Techniques

Power's memoir is not just a story — it is a manual for how to act effectively in the face of overwhelming odds. These are the specific techniques she used to navigate impossible situations.

## Technique 1: The "X Test" for Decision-Making

**When to use:** When you are paralyzed by the fear of failure and cannot decide whether to take a high-risk opportunity.

**How it works:** Ask yourself — "If I end up not achieving my primary goal, will something else I learn in the process make it worth trying?" Frame it as: "In trying for Y, the most I accomplish is X."

**Case: Moving to Croatia.** Power was 23 years old, working a desk job at the Carnegie Endowment. She wanted to become a war correspondent in Bosnia. Everyone told her it was impossible — the strings were taken, she had no experience, she would get killed. She used the X Test: "If the most I achieved by moving to the former Yugoslavia was to learn the history and language of the region, it will have been worth it (provided I did not die)." She made the move. She failed as a journalist. But she learned the history and language, made contacts, and eventually wrote "A Problem from Hell" — which won the Pulitzer and changed her life.

**Case: Joining the Obama Campaign.** When a young state senator from Illinois asked Power to advise him on foreign policy, she faced another X Test moment. She had just finished her book on genocide. She had a secure academic position. Joining a long-shot presidential campaign seemed like a career risk. But she asked herself: if Obama loses, will the experience still be valuable? Yes — she would learn how politics works, meet people who would become lifelong allies, and contribute to the first serious conversation about human rights in a presidential campaign. She said yes.

## Technique 2: "Don't Overshoot. Don't Undershoot." — The Negotiation Dial

**When to use:** When you are in a high-stakes negotiation and need to balance principled demands with the need to get a deal done.

**How it works:** Obama gave Power this instruction during the Syria chemical weapons negotiations with Russia. Push too hard and you force your counterpart to walk away (overshoot). Push too little and you leave value on the table (undershoot). The skill is finding the runway — the zone where a deal is possible — and landing there.

**Case: The Russia Chemical Weapons Resolution.** Power was negotiating the UN Security Council resolution that would authorize the removal of Syria's chemical weapons. Her Russian counterpart had instructions to give away as little as possible. Power had to decide how much to demand. She pushed for unfettered UN access, a tight timeline, and a clear verification mechanism. She accepted that punishing Assad or referring him to the International Criminal Court was off the table — the Russians would never agree. She landed the deal in what she calls "the zone of possible agreement." Was it perfect? No. Was it the best available outcome under the constraints? Yes.

**Case: The Runway Problem.** The honest truth Power discovered: "Where the hell is the runway?" Often, you don't know where the landing zone is until you're in the approach. Obama's instruction was useful, but it assumed the runway was known. In reality, most high-stakes negotiations involve discovering the runway as you go. The skill is not just staying between the lines — it's finding out where the lines actually are.

## Technique 3: The Empathy Loop

**When to use:** When you need to connect with someone whose experience is radically different from yours, or when you need to persuade someone who is skeptical of you.

**How it works:** Power's approach to empathy has three steps: (1) Listen with full attention, as her mother did — "cock her head to the side and peer earnestly at the other person, digging for details and drawing connections across time and space." (2) Find the shared humanity — not by pretending you're the same, but by recognizing that everyone has dignity. (3) Use stories, not arguments, to bridge difference.

**Case: The Bosnian Funeral.** During her time reporting in Bosnia, Power attended a funeral for a young man killed by snipers. His mother wailed over his body. Power didn't speak the language well. She had no notebook. She just stood there, present. Her presence was not reported in any article. But she writes about it as one of the most important moments of her time in Bosnia — because it taught her that journalism is not just about extracting stories. It's about bearing witness.

**Case: Diplomatic Storytelling at the UN.** As UN Ambassador, when formal arguments failed to move her colleagues, Power would share firsthand stories about the people affected by UN decisions. A story about a Syrian mother who lost her children to a barrel bomb was worth more than ten legal briefs. She writes: "When foreign officials refused to budge in negotiations, I would try to shake up stale debates by sharing authentic, firsthand stories."

## Technique 4: The System Bypass

**When to use:** When a bureaucratic or institutional barrier prevents you from doing something important, and following the rules would mean failing at your mission.

**How it works:** Power forged a letter from the editor of Foreign Policy to get a UN press pass for Bosnia. She knew it was wrong. She knew it was a fireable offense. She did it anyway, because the alternative was not being able to report on a genocide.

**Case: The Forged Letter.** "Hands shaking, I began typing a letter impersonating the unwitting Maynes. I was committing a fireable offense, but to me it felt like a felony. All these years later, I still feel terrible for having violated the trust of a program that was giving me so much. But determined to get to Bosnia, I went ahead and wrote to the head of the UN Press Office." This is not a recommendation to break rules — it is an honest account of a moment when the rule was a barrier to something more important. The lesson: understand the rules, respect them when you can, but know when they are unjust or irrelevant and act accordingly.

**Case: Process Fouls at the NSC.** Power was repeatedly chastised for committing "process fouls" — sending material directly to senior officials without getting clearance from all the relevant mid-level colleagues first. While she learned to respect the process over time, she also recognized that the clearance system was designed to preserve the status quo. Every new idea had to survive a gauntlet of vetos. Her instinct to bypass the system was not insubordination — it was the only way to get new ideas in front of the people who could act on them.

## Technique 5: The Grief Protocol

**When to use:** When you have lost someone or something important and need to process it without letting it destroy your capacity to function.

**How it works:** Power's father died alone in her childhood bed, an alcoholic who drank himself to death at 41. She was 14. She did not attend his funeral. She did not properly grieve. She writes: "By returning to school the day after I learned that my dad had died, I did not honor the pain that was tearing at me. By not flying back to Ireland, I took on another cause for regret."

**Case: The Aftermath of Her Father's Death.** Power's teenage brain created a causal sequence: "When I left Ireland, I left my dad; I didn't visit my dad; and thus, he died." This is irrational, but it is how unprocessed grief works. She carried this burden for years before she was able to name it. The technique she eventually developed: acknowledge the grief directly, don't try to rationalize it away, and recognize that guilt and loss do not need to be "fair" to be real.

**Case: The Letter to Bam Bam.** Power cultivated a deep correspondence with her paternal grandfather, Bam Bam, who lived to 101. She shared the ups and downs of her life with him in letters that spanned 11 years. This was not just a relationship — it was a way of staying connected to her father through the man who raised him. Grief, she learned, can be carried alongside life. You don't "get over" it. You integrate it.
