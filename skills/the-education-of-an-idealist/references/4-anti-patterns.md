# The Cost of Inaction — Anti-Patterns

Power's memoir is a cautionary tale about how good people fail to act in the face of mass atrocities. These are the recurring anti-patterns she identifies — from her research on past genocides to her own experience in the Obama administration.

## Anti-Pattern 1: The Semantic Dodge

**The pattern:** When leaders avoid calling a crisis what it is because naming it would create an obligation to act.

**Case: Rwanda and the "G" Word.** During the 1994 Rwandan genocide, the Clinton administration refused to use the word "genocide" to describe the killing of 800,000 Tutsis because the Genocide Convention obligated signatories to "prevent and punish" genocide. Instead, officials used phrases like "acts of genocide" and "a humanitarian catastrophe." In "A Problem from Hell," Power documented this semantic evasion devastatingly. Then, years later, she watched her own administration do the same thing with Syria. When Ben Rhodes was asked if Assad had crossed Obama's red line, he said: "We are continuing to do further work to establish a definitive judgment as to whether or not the red line has been crossed." Power recognized the pattern immediately: "This White House language recalled the Clinton administration's hesitation."

**Case: Rwanda's "Tutsi" Problem.** One of the most damning details Power uncovered: the State Department instructed its officials not to use the word "Tutsi" when describing the victims, because doing so would "create pressure" for action. By making the victims nameless, it was easier to look away. Power saw the same dynamic in Syria — the victims were abstracted into statistics until the numbers became too large to process.

## Anti-Pattern 2: The Complexity Trap

**The pattern:** When leaders use the complexity of a situation as a rationale for inaction — "we don't have a good option" becomes "therefore we should do nothing."

**Case: Syria's "No Good Options."** Power writes extensively about the legitimate constraints facing Obama: the opposition was fragmented and contained extremists, Russia would veto any Security Council action, air strikes risked hitting civilians, and a ground war was politically impossible. Each of these constraints was real. But together, they became a cage that prevented any action at all. Power's critique: the "no good options" frame ignores that "doing nothing" is itself an option with consequences. When you choose inaction, you are choosing the deaths that result from it.

**Case: Libya's Complication.** The messy aftermath of the 2011 Libya intervention made Obama deeply skeptical about military action in Syria. The fall of Qaddafi was a victory, but the chaos that followed — the political vacuum, the violence between militias, the rise of extremists — haunted the administration. Obama would often cite Libya when arguing against action in Syria. Power acknowledges the Libya experience was traumatizing. But she also notes that Libya's population was one-third of Syria's, and the circumstances were fundamentally different. The lesson: using one complex case to justify inaction in another is a form of intellectual laziness disguised as prudence.

## Anti-Pattern 3: The "More Information" Stall

**The pattern:** When the response to a clear atrocity is to demand more information, more investigation, more certainty — even when the evidence is already overwhelming.

**Case: The UN Investigators in Syria.** After Assad's August 21, 2013 chemical weapons attack, the UN had a team of inspectors in Damascus — but their mandate was only to determine if chemicals had been used, not who used them. The answer was obvious: sarin had been used on civilians. Yet the Secretary-General refused to withdraw the team because he wanted the definitive report. Power argued that every day the inspectors stayed was another day for Assad to destroy evidence and move weapons into hiding. She told Ban Ki-moon: "This is a moot mission. The UN is being manipulated." But Ban was unyielding: "We cannot not proceed."

**Case: The Four-Month Gap.** The US intelligence community confirmed in April 2013 that Assad had used chemical weapons "on a small scale." The White House sent a letter to Congress. And then... nothing. The administration debated, asked for more analysis, considered options, and waited for the next attack. Power writes: "Had he ordered limited strikes then, I wondered whether Assad's forces would have dared to stage such a large subsequent attack." The delay was not caused by lack of information. It was caused by lack of will.

## Anti-Pattern 4: The "One More Meeting" Reflex

**The pattern:** When the solution to a crisis is always another meeting, another working group, another interagency process — because meetings create the illusion of action without the risk of actual consequences.

**Case: The Situation Room Vortex.** Power describes how the NSC system was designed for deliberation, not action. Every decision had to survive the clearance process — a gauntlet of stakeholders, each with veto power. When a crisis demanded speed, the bureaucracy demanded process. "The flow of paper — and the so-called clearance process — from one official to the next was the lifeblood of government policymaking." But it was also the death of initiative.

**Case: The "One More Sanction."** The US had already imposed significant sanctions on Syria by 2013. When Assad used chemical weapons, the administration's first instinct was to expand sanctions. Power's response: "In apartheid South Africa and Milošević's Serbia, two places where sanctions played an important role in changing government behavior, they did so over a period of years, not months." The sanctions reflex was a way of taking an action that looked like action but was unlikely to change Assad's calculus. It was motion disguised as progress.

## Anti-Pattern 5: The Personal Guilt Trap

**The pattern:** When an individual takes on responsibility for systemic failures they could not have prevented, allowing guilt to distort future judgment.

**Case: Power's Guilt About Her Father.** Power's teenage brain created a devastating narrative: if she hadn't left Ireland, her father wouldn't have died. "When I left Ireland, I left my dad; I didn't visit my dad; and thus, he died." This is not true — her father was an alcoholic who refused help. But it felt true. This guilt shaped her for years, making her feel responsible for things beyond her control.

**Case: The Syria Guilt.** Power carried the guilt of Syria with her after leaving government. She had been UN Ambassador during the worst of the crisis. She had argued for action. She had lost. Watching Assad continue to kill his own people — including using chemical weapons again in 2017 and 2018 — was a wound that did not heal. The anti-pattern: taking personal responsibility for a systemic failure. The corrective: acknowledge the pain, name what was lost, but recognize that some forces are beyond any individual's control.

**The antidote to all anti-patterns:** Name the pattern, recognize it in real-time, and actively resist it. Power's mother taught her this: when you see something wrong, you don't look away. You find a way to act — even if the action is imperfect.
