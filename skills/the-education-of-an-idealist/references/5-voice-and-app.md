Power's memoir is a masterclass in how to tell stories that move people to action. This reference covers her narrative techniques and how they can be applied through the Heardly App.

## Power's Narrative Voice

Power writes in a distinctive voice that combines three elements: (1) The journalist's eye for detail — she notices the specific, the concrete, the human-scale. (2) The activist's moral clarity — she does not hide her judgments. (3) The diplomat's hard-won humility — she knows that every story has a counter-story.

**Case: The Opening Scene.** The book begins with Power in a diner in Manhattan, taking a call from President Obama. Her four-year-old and one-year-old are next to her. She takes "two long sips of water." She walks outside to the corner of 50th and Lexington. In three paragraphs, she establishes: she is a mother, she is a diplomat, she is an immigrant, she is in the middle of the most consequential negotiation of her career, and she has no idea what she is doing. "Where the hell is the runway?" — the question she asks herself is the question that defines the book. It is intimate, honest, self-deprecating, and universally relatable.

**Case: The Hartigan's Basement.** Power describes reading Nancy Drew mysteries in the basement of her father's Dublin pub, surrounded by the smell of "urine, chlorine disinfectant, and the swirl of barley, malt, and hops." It should be a sad image — a child spending her afternoons next to a cigarette machine. But Power does not write it as sad. She writes it as love: "My father brought me with him. I was his sidekick." This is the voice's most distinctive quality: it refuses to flatten experience into a simple judgment. She loved her father. Her father was an alcoholic whose drinking destroyed him. Both are true.

## How Power Uses Stories to Persuade

**At the Negotiating Table.** As UN Ambassador, when formal arguments failed to move skeptical counterparts, Power would share stories. A story about a Syrian mother watching her children die from barrel bombs. A story about a Bosnian refugee whose leg was amputated by Serbian forces. She writes: "When foreign officials refused to budge in negotiations, I would try to shake up stale debates by sharing authentic, firsthand stories about the many people who were being affected (for good and bad) by our decisions."

**Case: The South African Ambassador.** During the negotiations over UN sanctions on North Korea, the South African ambassador was skeptical. She had legal concerns, foreign policy interests, and procedural objections. Power couldn't overcome these with logic — the ambassador's position was rational. Instead, she told the ambassador about the human impact of North Korea's human rights abuses. The story didn't change the legal analysis. But it changed the frame. The ambassador began asking different questions — not "what does this mean for our trade relations" but "what does this mean for the people in the camps?"

**In Personal Relationships.** Power's relationship with her mother is mediated through stories. Her mother refused to express love through words — "which to her would have sounded maudlin." Instead, she conveyed love through "intense, affectionate focus" — watching Wimbledon, playing squash, driving to Cork singing "Molly Malone." Power's memoir is, in part, an attempt to translate her mother's language of love — action and presence — into a form the reader can understand.
