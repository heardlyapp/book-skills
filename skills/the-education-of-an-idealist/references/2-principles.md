# The Dignity Principle — Core Values

Dignity is the thread that runs through Power's entire life. Not as a concept in an academic paper — but as the organizing principle of how she navigated every major decision. This reference covers the key principles derived from her experience.

## I. Dignity Is Non-Negotiable

From her mother's custody battle to the UN Security Council, Power learned that the fundamental question in any situation is: are human beings being treated with dignity?

**Case: Vera Delaney's Custody Battle.** Power's mother, a brilliant Irish doctor, stood before a judge who questioned her right to be "so educated." The judge was hostile to a working mother who wanted to move her children to America. Vera won, but only under three conditions: raise the kids Catholic, teach them Irish, and send them back for summers with their father. The judge's condescension was a violation of dignity — but Vera didn't let it defeat her. She argued her case, accepted the conditions that protected her children's relationships with their father, and moved forward. Dignity in action means knowing what to fight for and what to absorb.

**Case: The Atlanta School Integration.** When Power arrived at Lakeside High School, the school was in the middle of a racial integration battle. Hundreds of African-American students were bused into the formerly white school. Power watched her black classmates arrive after a two-hour commute, stay for sports practice, then wait for an "activity bus" that left at 7 PM — getting home too late to start homework before 9 PM. "We may have been equal before God," she writes, "but I had three more hours of sleep, vastly more time to prepare, and many more resources at my disposal." The lesson: dignity requires not just equal opportunity, but equal support. Systems that claim to be fair often reproduce the very inequalities they claim to solve.

## II. Empathy Is Strategic

Power's mother had a gift: she gave people "a quality of attention" that made them feel seen. This wasn't sentimentality — it was a skill that created trust, connection, and influence.

**Case: The Bosnian Refugee at the Embassy.** When Power first arrived in Croatia, she went to the Bosnian embassy to get a visa. Outside, she saw dozens of Bosnian refugees — Muslims whom Serbs had tortured and marked with crosses etched into their faces. One man had his leg amputated above the knee. A proper journalist would have asked him for his story. Power couldn't. She writes: "Forcing them to rehash what had happened seemed cruelly voyeuristic." Instead, she simply sat with them. Sometimes the most empathetic thing is not to ask for more — it's to just be present.

**Case: The NPR Spot.** When Power nervously called NPR with her first story idea, they asked for a 45-second "spot." She had no idea what that meant. She turned to Laura Pitter, the veteran war correspondent, who calmly explained how to structure it: get attention, deliver the news, conclude efficiently. "You can totally do this," Laura said, writing her number on a cardboard coaster. That coaster became Power's bookmark — a reminder of what happens when someone extends empathy to a stranger. Years later, she would try to offer the same to young diplomats and journalists entering her orbit.

## III. The Cost of Inaction Must Be Named

Power's Pulitzer Prize-winning book "A Problem from Hell" was built on a simple premise: Americans and their leaders failed to stop genocide not because they didn't know, but because they chose not to know. Naming this failure is the first step to preventing it.

**Case: The "Red Line" That Wasn't Enforced.** President Obama warned Assad that using chemical weapons would cross "a red line." When Assad killed 1,400 people with sarin gas, the US government debated for weeks, then backed down from military action. Power writes about the moment the White House admitted the red line had been crossed but proposed a UN investigation instead: "This White House language recalled the Clinton administration's hesitation in calling the slaughter of 800,000 Tutsis in Rwanda 'genocide.' In both cases, an American administration had resisted making a clear determination for fear that doing so would oblige the President to undertake actions that he hoped to avoid." The pattern is predictable and devastating.

**Case: The "A Problem from Hell" Framework.** In her book, Power identified the recurring pattern of American non-response to genocide: First comes information, then denial, then acknowledgment without action, then excuses about complexity, then retrospective regret. She saw this pattern repeated in Syria in real-time while serving in the very government that was failing to act. The lesson: understanding the pattern is not enough. You must actively resist it.

## IV. "Being Useful" Is the Highest Praise

When Power's mentor Mort Abramowitz praised someone as "useful," it was the highest compliment he could give. It meant the person didn't just have good ideas — they knew how to make things happen.

**Case: Fred Cuny's Water System.** Fred Cuny was a humanitarian engineer who designed a system to deliver clean water to besieged Sarajevo. He and his team landed C-130 planes in the middle of a war zone, unloaded water purification modules in seven minutes flat, and turned on the pipes for the first time. "We got our time down to seven minutes!" he boasted. Then he was doused in 500 gallons of water alongside his Bosnian colleagues — a moment of pure joy in the middle of a genocide. That is what "being useful" looks like: not a speech, not a resolution, but 500 gallons of clean water for people who had none.

**Case: Power's Own Definition.** After the Syria chemical weapons negotiations, Power had to accept an imperfect outcome: instead of punishing Assad, she helped dismantle his chemical weapons through negotiation. She knew it was not enough. But she also knew that removing 1,300 tons of sarin and VX from a war zone prevented future mass casualties. She chose to be useful rather than to be morally pure. "What would the old Samantha Power say to the current Samantha Power?" reporters asked. Her answer was simple: "They talk all the time. And they agree." But the full answer, she admits, was more complicated.
