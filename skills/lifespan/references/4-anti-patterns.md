# 4. Anti-Patterns — Critiques of Lifespan

> Source: "Lifespan" by David Sinclair (2019). Critiques of the book and Sinclair's claims.

## Critique 1: The Claims Are Overhyped

**What it looks like**: Sinclair promises radical life extension based on animal studies that may not translate to humans.

**Why it matters**: The science is promising but not proven. Many interventions that work in mice fail in humans. Sinclair's optimism may lead people to spend money on unproven supplements.

> **Case: The Resveratrol Backlash** (Part I): Early resveratrol studies showed dramatic effects in mice. Follow-up studies were less impressive. Some researchers could not replicate the results. Resveratrol became a cautionary tale about hype in longevity research.
> **Key takeaway**: Early results in animals are promising — but they are not proof of human efficacy.

## Critique 2: Sinclair Has Financial Conflicts

**What it looks like**: Sinclair is a founder, advisor, or investor in several longevity companies. His financial interests overlap with his scientific claims.

**Why it matters**: This does not mean he is wrong, but it means his claims should be evaluated with appropriate skepticism.

## Critique 3: The Regulatory Critique

**What it looks like**: Sinclair blames the FDA for blocking longevity research.

**Why it's complicated**: The FDA's position (aging is not a disease) is outdated. But Sinclair's solution (regulate aging as a disease) could create its own problems, including pathologizing normal aging.

## Critique 4: The Supplement Industry Problem

**What it looks like**: Sinclair's recommendations (NMN, resveratrol) are sold by companies he is associated with.

**Why it matters**: The supplement industry is poorly regulated. The products Sinclair recommends may not contain what their labels claim, and their long-term safety in humans is unknown.

> **Case: The NMN Human Trials** (Part II): At the time of publication, NMN had not completed large-scale human trials. Sinclair took NMN himself based on animal data. His personal use was a vote of confidence, not scientific proof.
> **Key takeaway**: Personal experimentation is not clinical evidence. The fact that a scientist takes a supplement does not mean it has been proven safe and effective in humans.
