# Anti-Patterns — Self-Sabotage, Repression, Grandiosity, and the Entitlement Curse

## The Central Fallacy

**The most dangerous assumption: that you are the exception to these laws.** Everyone believes they are more rational, less narcissistic, less envious, and less susceptible to groupthink than the average person. Greene calls this the "above-average effect" — and it is the very bias that makes you most vulnerable to the laws you think you are immune to.

> **Case: The Constricted vs Expansive Attitude** (Law 8): Greene contrasts two fundamental life attitudes. The constricted attitude sees obstacles, risks, and reasons to say no. The expansive attitude sees opportunities, possibilities, and reasons to try. He tells the story of Ivan Boesly, the Wall Street trader who became fabulously wealthy and then destroyed himself with insider trading. After his arrest, Boesly said: "I got caught up in something." Greene reframes this: "He did not 'get caught up.' He chose to operate from the constricted attitude — the belief that more was never enough, that he needed to cut corners, that the rules did not apply to him." The constricted attitude does not look negative. It looks reasonable. That is what makes it dangerous.
> **Key takeaway**: Your attitude is not a personality trait. It is a choice you make in every moment. When you find yourself focusing on why something cannot work, ask: "What would it look like if I believed it could work?"

> **Case: The Shadow and Contradictory Behavior** (Law 9): Greene draws on Jung's concept of the shadow — the parts of yourself you repress because they clash with your self-image. "Every quality you repress becomes stronger." He tells the story of the French revolutionary Maximilien Robespierre, who was known as "the Incorruptible" — a man of absolute moral purity who could not tolerate any flaw in himself or others. He repressed his own capacity for violence so completely that he projected it onto everyone else. In the end, Robespierre sent thousands to the guillotine, believing he was purifying the revolution. Greene's analysis: "The shadow is the hole in the soul that you try to fill with accusations against others. What you condemn most fiercely in others is what you cannot accept in yourself."
> **Key takeaway**: When you find yourself morally outraged by someone else's behavior, pause. Ask: "Is there a part of me that does the same thing in a different form?" The intensity of your reaction is proportional to the degree of repression.

> **Case: The Success Delusion** (Law 11): Greene warns specifically about the effect of success on judgment. "Success inflates your sense of your own abilities and makes you lose touch with reality." He tells the story of J. P. Morgan, the legendary financier, who after years of success believed he could not fail. In 1907, when a financial panic threatened, Morgan stepped in to save the banking system — and succeeded. But the success convinced him that he was infallible. He began making riskier and riskier bets. When he died, his fortune was significantly diminished from its peak. Greene's point: "Success is the most dangerous drug. It tells you that you are special, that the rules do not apply to you. But the rules always reassert themselves."
> **Key takeaway**: After a success, do not assume you are now smarter or more capable. The market, the circumstances, luck — these played a role. Success is a moment, not an identity.

> **Case: The Entitlement Curse** (Law 15): Greene describes a paradox of leadership: "Those who most want to lead are often the least suited to do so." He calls this the entitlement curse — the belief that because you have earned the position, you deserve loyalty, respect, and obedience. But authority is not inherited with the title. It must be earned from the people who follow you. Greene contrasts two leaders: the entitled leader who demands respect and the authentic leader who earns it through competence and care for their people. "People will forgive a lack of charisma. They will not forgive a lack of genuine care."
> **Key takeaway**: Your title gives you power over people's behavior. It does not give you authority over their hearts. Authority is earned through competence, consistency, and genuine concern for those you lead.
