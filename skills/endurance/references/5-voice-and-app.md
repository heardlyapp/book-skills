## 5 Common Scenarios

**1. "How do I lead a team through a crisis?"**
Attack: Be calm. Communicate constantly. Know your people. Maintain routine. Celebrate small wins. Never ask your team to do what you will not do yourself. And never, ever give up. Shackleton demonstrated that leadership is not about never failing — it is about what you do after failure.

> **Case: The James Caird Journey** (Part III, Chapters 1-4): Shackleton and five men sailed a 22-foot lifeboat 800 miles across the Southern Ocean to get help. They navigated by sextant in hurricane conditions, nearly capsized multiple times, and faced impossible odds. They slept among rocks in the bottom of the boat. They chipped ice off the rigging with an axe while perched on a pitching deck. When they finally reached South Georgia after 16 days, they still had to cross an unmapped mountain range to reach the whaling station. They did all of this because one man refused to accept failure. Worsley's navigation was so precise — using a single surviving chronometer and a sextant in mountainous seas — that they hit South Georgia exactly where he aimed. It is considered one of the greatest navigational feats in history.
> **Key takeaway:** The difference between success and failure in extreme circumstances is often just one person who refuses to quit. Be that person. Expertise and stubbornness combined are unstoppable.

**2. "My team is losing hope. What do I do?"**
Attack: Hope is not a feeling — it is a practice. Give them a plan. Give them work. Give them each other. Shackleton's method: daily briefings, shared meals, visible sacrifice from the leader, and a refusal to show doubt. Create small wins. Celebrate anything worth celebrating. Remind them why the goal matters.

**3. "How do I make decisions with incomplete information?"**
Attack: Gather the best information you can. Consider the options. Make the call. Commit fully. If it does not work, adapt. The worst decision in a crisis is no decision. Indecision destroys confidence faster than a wrong decision. Shackleton's decision to take the James Caird to South Georgia rather than the closer Falkland Islands was controversial — but he made the call and committed fully.

> **Case: The South Georgia Decision** (Part III, Chapter 1): Shackleton faced three possible destinations for the boat journey: Cape Horn (500 miles), the Falkland Islands (550 miles), or South Georgia (800 miles). The closer options required sailing into the prevailing wind and current — a nearly suicidal prospect in a 22-foot boat. South Georgia was farther but had following winds. Shackleton chose the harder, longer route because the physics demanded it. The decision was counterintuitive but correct.
> **Key takeaway:** In a crisis, choose the option supported by the best available evidence, even if it is counterintuitive. The easy-looking path is often the deadliest.

**4. "How do I prepare for a crisis that might never come?"**
Attack: Build trust with your team before the crisis. Know their families, their strengths, their fears. Develop the habits of communication and routine. Shackleton spent months on the Endurance before the crisis hit — building relationships, establishing routines, learning each man's character. When the crisis came, he did not have to build these foundations from scratch. They were already in place. Build them now.

**5. "I feel like I am failing as a leader."**
Attack: Leadership is not about never failing. It is about what you do after failure. Shackleton's expedition "failed" in its original goal — they never crossed Antarctica. But he saved every man, making it one of the greatest leadership stories in history. Redefine success. Sometimes getting your team through the crisis is the victory that matters more than the original mission.
