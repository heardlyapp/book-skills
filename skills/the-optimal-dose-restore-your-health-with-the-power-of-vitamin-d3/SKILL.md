## Philosophy (4 Rules to Remember)

1. Standard vitamin D recommendations (RDA) are far too low for optimal health. They were designed to prevent deficiency diseases (rickets), not to promote optimal function.
2. Vitamin D3 is not a vitamin — it's a pro-hormone that affects nearly every cell in your body.
3. There is no "one size fits all" dose. Your optimal dose depends on your weight, skin color, sun exposure, and health status.
4. Vitamin D works in synergy with other nutrients — especially magnesium, vitamin K2, and zinc.

## Core Framework Quick Reference

- **Vitamin D3 (Cholecalciferol)** — The form of vitamin D produced by the body when skin is exposed to sunlight. The form used for supplementation.
- **25-Hydroxyvitamin D** — The blood test that measures vitamin D status. Optimal range: 50-80 ng/mL. Deficiency: under 30 ng/mL.
- **RDA (Recommended Dietary Allowance)** — 600-800 IU/day. Enough to prevent rickets. Far below optimal for most people.
- **Optimal Dosing** — Somerville's protocol: 30-50 IU per pound of body weight daily. A 200 lb person would take 6,000-10,000 IU/day.
- **Co-factors** — Magnesium, vitamin K2, zinc, and boron are needed for vitamin D metabolism.

---

## Key Principles

1. **Vitamin D3 affects every cell** — Vitamin D receptors exist in nearly every cell in the body. Deficiency affects immunity, mood, sleep, bone health, and metabolism.
2. **RDA is for deficiency prevention, not optimal health** — The standard 600-800 IU/day prevents rickets but doesn't optimize immune function or sleep.
3. **Your optimal dose is personal** — Body weight, skin color (melanin reduces D production), geography, and health status all affect your needs.
4. **Co-factors matter** — Vitamin D requires magnesium for activation. Without adequate magnesium, you can't properly utilize vitamin D.
5. **Blood testing is essential** — You can't know your optimal dose without measuring your 25-hydroxyvitamin D level.
6. **Optimal dosing is safe** — Somerville argues that doses up to 30,000-50,000 IU/day are safe when monitored. Vitamin D toxicity (hypercalcemia) is rare and easily avoided.
7. **Sunshine is still optimal** — The body regulates D production from sunlight perfectly. Supplementation is a substitute, not a replacement.

---

## Anti-Pattern Summary

The most common mistake: **taking the RDA dose and assuming it's enough.** The RDA of 600-800 IU/day was designed to prevent rickets — it's not an optimal dose. The second mistake: taking vitamin D without magnesium or K2. Vitamin D increases calcium absorption, and without K2 to direct calcium to bones instead of soft tissues, you can experience side effects. The third mistake: not testing. Without a blood test, you're guessing.

## Self-Check: Recall Test

1. "How much vitamin D should I take?" — The RDA (600-800 IU) prevents rickets but doesn't optimize health. Somerville recommends 30-50 IU per pound of body weight. Test your blood to dial in the dose.
2. "What is the optimal blood level?" — 50-80 ng/mL (125-200 nmol/L). Deficiency is under 30 ng/mL.
3. "Can I get enough from the sun?" — Yes, if you have sufficient sun exposure. But most people don't. Supplementation is a reliable alternative.
4. "What are the benefits of optimal D?" — Improved immune function, better sleep, more energy, weight loss support, reduced inflammation.
5. "Do I need to take magnesium with D?" — Yes. Vitamin D requires magnesium for activation. Without it, you may not absorb D properly.
6. "Is high-dose vitamin D safe?" — Yes, when monitored. Toxicity (hypercalcemia) is rare. Somerville has given patients 30,000-50,000 IU/day without issues.
7. "Can vitamin D help with weight loss?" — Vitamin D supports metabolic function and may help regulate appetite. It's not a weight loss drug, but deficiency can hinder weight loss.
8. "Does vitamin D help with COVID?" — Optimizing vitamin D levels supports immune function. Studies show that D deficiency is associated with worse COVID outcomes.
9. "How do I test my vitamin D level?" — Ask your doctor for a 25-hydroxyvitamin D blood test. Optimal range is 50-80 ng/mL.
10. "What if I overdose on vitamin D?" — Symptoms of hypercalcemia: nausea, vomiting, constipation, weakness, confusion. Stop supplementation and contact your doctor. Toxicity is rare and reversible.
