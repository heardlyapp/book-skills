# 2 — Political Humor: The Absurdity of D.C.

> Source: "How to Test Negative for Stupid" by John Kennedy

## Why Humor Matters

Kennedy uses humor because the truth is too depressing to tell straight. Laughter is the only appropriate response to some of the things that happen in Washington.

## The Art of the Senate One-Liner

Senators who can land a good line have more influence than senators who can write a good bill. It's not right, but it's true.

> **Case: Constituent Letters** (Chapter 10): Kennedy read actual constituent letters on the Senate floor. One woman wrote to complain about "the socialists taking over" and then demanded her Social Security check. Another asked the senator to "fix the weather." Kennedy's response: "Ma'am, if I could fix the weather, I wouldn't be in the Senate. I'd be rich and living somewhere with better weather."
> **Key takeaway**: Constituents often expect senators to have powers they don't have. But the letters are a reminder: people are watching, and they care. You just have to answer them honestly.

> **Case: The Hearing Exchange** (Chapter 8): In a Senate hearing, Kennedy asked a witness a simple question. The witness answered for 7 minutes without saying anything. Kennedy: "I asked a yes-or-no question. That was a 7-minute answer that was neither yes nor no. I'm going to ask again."
> **Key takeaway**: Washington's superpower is talking without answering. Kennedy's approach: ask the question again, and again, until you get an answer.
