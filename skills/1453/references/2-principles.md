# Principles of the Siege

## I. Technology Overcomes Tradition

The Theodosian Walls had held for a thousand years. They were defeated not by superior courage or numbers but by superior technology.

**Case: Orban's Bombard.** Orban offered his services to Constantine XI first. The Byzantine emperor could not afford his price. Mehmet paid four times what Orban asked. The decision was purely economic — but it had world-historical consequences.

**Case: The Cumulative Effect.** The bombard did not breach the walls quickly. It worked slowly, day after day, cracking and weakening the masonry. The defenders could repair the damage at night, but eventually the wall was so compromised that a final assault could succeed.

## II. Leadership Is the Difference

Mehmet II was 21 years old — but he was one of the most capable commanders in history. Constantine XI was brave but hamstrung by lack of resources.

**Case: Mehmet's Vision.** Mehmet had been planning the capture of Constantinople since childhood. He read about the city in history books. He studied the walls, the currents, the winds. He was not a young man with a dream — he was a strategist executing a long-prepared plan.

**Case: Constantine's Dilemma.** Constantine had almost no army, no money, and no allies. The Western Christian powers promised help but never delivered. He fought bravely but could not overcome the material disadvantage.

## III. Psychology Matters as Much as Tactics

Both sides understood that morale was crucial. Omens, prophecies, and superstitions shaped the siege as much as cannonballs.

**Case: The Four Ships.** When four Christian ships broke through the Ottoman blockade, the defenders saw it as divine intervention. The wind had died, then returned from the south. It was a miracle. The Ottomans saw it as a bad omen.

**Case: The Final Night.** On the night of May 28, the Ottomans lit fires, played music, and shouted prayers. The defenders watched and listened, knowing the final assault was coming. The psychological pressure was part of Mehmet's strategy.

## IV. Fortune Favors the Bold

Mehmet took risks that a cautious commander would never have taken. The ships-overland maneuver was audacious to the point of absurdity. It succeeded because no one believed it was possible.

**Case: The Hill Transport.** The hill behind Galata was steep and wooded. The ships were heavy. The operation required hundreds of men and oxen. It took days. But Mehmet did not hesitate.

**Case: The Night Assaults.** Mehmet launched multiple assaults at night, in bad weather, when the defenders were exhausted. He did not follow the conventional rules of siege warfare.
