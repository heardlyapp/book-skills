# Reference: ADHD Reframed

## Overview
Archer reframes ADHD from a disorder to a trait set. The reframing is not denial of difficulties — it is recognition that the same traits that create challenges in some contexts create advantages in others.

## Case: The Hunter vs Farmer Theory
ADHD traits may have been adaptive in our evolutionary past. In hunter-gatherer societies, the ability to rapidly shift attention, take risks, and respond to novelty was valuable. The modern world demands "farmer" traits: sustained attention, planning, patience. Archer argues that many people with ADHD are "hunters in a farmer's world." The mismatch creates difficulties, but the hunter traits are still valuable.

## Case: Diagnosis as Identity
Archer is careful about diagnosis. A label should be a tool for understanding, not a limiting identity. The diagnosis explains why you struggle in certain areas, but it does not define what you can achieve. Many successful entrepreneurs, artists, and innovators have ADHD traits. The key is finding environments where those traits are advantages.

## Case: The Medication Decision
Archer neither promotes nor opposes medication. He presents it as a personal choice that should be informed by evidence and self-awareness. Medication can improve focus and impulse control for many people. It does not change core personality or creativity. The decision to use medication should be based on how it affects your quality of life, not on stigma or ideology.

## Case: The Label's Power
A diagnosis of ADHD can be liberating: the struggles you have faced have a name and an explanation. You are not lazy, stupid, or broken. Your brain is wired differently. Understanding this is the first step to working with your brain rather than against it.
