## 7 Principles of Leadership Under Extremis

### 1. Character Is Revealed in Crisis

When everything goes wrong, the facade falls away. TR's stubborn courage, Rondon's quiet competence, the murderer's violence — the crisis revealed who everyone really was.

### 2. Complementary Leadership Is Stronger

Roosevelt and Rondon had opposite styles — but together, they worked. Roosevelt's energy and vision needed Rondon's methodical patience. Rondon's caution needed Roosevelt's willingness to take risks.

> **Case: Rondon's Approach to Indigenous People** (Chapter 7): When the expedition encountered the Cinta Larga, Rondon insisted on respectful engagement. He forbade weapons. He left gifts. Roosevelt, who held more typical early 20th century views, initially saw the tribe as a threat. But he deferred to Rondon — whose policy of engagement averted disaster.
> **Key takeaway**: The best leaders know when to defer to someone with more expertise. Roosevelt's willingness to follow Rondon's lead saved the expedition.

### 3. A Leader's Mood Infects the Team

When Roosevelt was sick and wanted to give up, the entire expedition's morale collapsed. When he recovered and took charge again, morale returned. A leader's emotional state is contagious.

### 4. Sometimes There Is No Right Choice

The murder cover-up was morally wrong. But prosecuting the killer in the jungle might have triggered more violence. Leadership sometimes means choosing between bad options.

### 5. The Team Must Carry the Leader

When TR could not walk, the men carried him. A leader is only as strong as the team that supports them. The leader may be the face of the expedition — but the team's willingness to bear the burden is what matters.

> **Case: Kermit Carrying His Father** (Chapter 15): Kermit Roosevelt physically carried his father through the jungle. He did not complain. He did not hesitate. His love for his father was the expedition's hidden strength.
> **Key takeaway**: The bonds between team members — not just the leader's vision — are what sustain an expedition through the worst moments.

### 6. Knowledge Is Survival

Rondon knew the jungle. He knew which plants were edible, how to navigate, how to read the river. Without his knowledge, the expedition would have died.

### 7. Survival Is Not Heroic — It Is Cumulative

The expedition survived because of a thousand small decisions: what to eat, when to rest, who to trust, which rapid to run. Survival is not one heroic act — it is the accumulation of wise choices.
