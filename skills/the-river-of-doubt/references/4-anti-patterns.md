> Source: The River of Doubt, Chapters 1-18

## 6 Anti-Patterns of Mismanaged Crisis

### 1. "I Can Do It Alone"

Roosevelt could not have survived the Amazon alone. He needed Rondon's knowledge, Kermit's strength, Cherrie's hunting, the men's labor. No leader survives a true crisis alone.

### 2. "My Status Will Protect Me"

The Amazon did not care that Roosevelt was a former president. The piranhas did not care. The mosquitoes did not care. Status is useless in nature.

> **Case: The Malaria Return** (Chapter 5): Roosevelt had recovered from malaria years earlier. The Amazon environment triggered a relapse. His status as a former president did not protect him from the parasite.
> **Key takeaway**: Nature does not recognize hierarchy. In the jungle, you are just another animal with the same biological vulnerabilities.

### 3. "Refusing Help Shows Strength"

Roosevelt wanted to be left behind. That was brave but wrong. True strength is accepting help when you need it.

### 4. "Justice Must Be Done Immediately"

The expedition chose not to pursue justice for Julio's murder. This was a morally compromising choice. But it was the right call for survival. Not every wrong must be righted immediately.

### 5. "The Plan Will Work"

The expedition's plan was destroyed in the first week. Canoes were lost. Food was lost. The plan was fiction. Adaptability, not the plan, was what saved them.

> **Case: The Lost Canoes** (Chapter 9): The expedition lost two canoes in rapids, along with most of their supplies. They had to split the remaining food. The original plan became irrelevant. Every day was a new improvisation.
> **Key takeaway**: The plan is the first casualty of reality. The ability to improvise is more valuable than any plan.

### 6. "Strong Leaders Never Show Weakness"

Roosevelt showed weakness — pain, doubt, exhaustion, the desire to give up. Showing weakness did not weaken his leadership. It made him more human. The team responded with more loyalty, not less.
