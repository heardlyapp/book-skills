# Reference: Claude Frollo

## Overview
Claude Frollo is one of literature's great villains. He is complex: learned, devout, and destroyed by his own desires.

## Case: The Archdeacon
Frollo is the Archdeacon of Notre Dame. He is a scholar of alchemy and theology. He raised Quasimodo from infancy. He is respected and feared.

## Case: The Obsession
Frollo sees Esmeralda dancing and is consumed by lust. His religious faith makes him hate his own desire. He tries to resist — and fails. He pursues Esmeralda with destructive obsession.

## Case: The Villain
Frollo's obsession leads him to commit terrible acts: he stalks Esmeralda, frames her for murder, lets Quasimodo take the blame, and watches her hang. He represents the corruption of religious and intellectual authority.

## Case: The Tragedy
Frollo is not simple evil. He is a man of faith and learning destroyed by his own desires. He could have been a great man. His tragedy is that he could not control what consumed him.
Frollo's death is fitting. Quasimodo hurls him from the tower of Notre Dame. The archdeacon who could not resist his own demons is destroyed by the monster he created.
Frollo is the novel's warning: intellect and faith without compassion are dangerous. He could have been a saint. He became a monster.
He is the embodiment of ananke — fate. Frollo could not escape his own destiny any more than Esmeralda or Quasimodo could escape theirs.
Frollo's final scream as he falls from the tower echoes across Paris — the sound of a soul damned by its own choices.
