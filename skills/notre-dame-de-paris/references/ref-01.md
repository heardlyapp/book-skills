# Reference: Quasimodo

## Overview
Quasimodo is one of literature's most memorable characters. Born deformed, deafened by the bells, isolated from humanity, he represents the innocence of the outcast.

## Case: The Deformed Child
Quasimodo was abandoned at Notre Dame as a baby, left on the foundling bed. Claude Frollo adopted him, raising him as a son. Quasimodo grew up in the cathedral, ringing the bells that eventually deafened him.

## Case: The Bell-Ringer
Quasimodo's life is the bells. He loves them. They are his family, his music, his language. The largest bell, Emmanuel, is his greatest joy and his curse. It deafened him, but he does not resent it.

## Case: The Pure Love
Quasimodo loves Esmeralda with absolute devotion. He cannot have her — he knows he is a monster. But he protects her, serves her, and ultimately dies loving her.

## Case: The Outsider
Quasimodo is feared and reviled by society. He is crowned King of Fools for his ugliness, then beaten when he asks for water. Esmeralda gives him water, and he falls in love.

## Case: The Tragic End
After Esmeralda's execution, Quasimodo disappears. Years later, his skeleton is found embracing Esmeralda's remains in the crypt of Montfaucon.
