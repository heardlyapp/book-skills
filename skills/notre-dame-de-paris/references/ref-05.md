# Reference: Themes and Symbols

## Overview
The novel explores love, fate, architecture, and the cruelty of society. Its symbols are rich and powerful.

## Case: Love
Love takes many forms. Quasimodo's love is pure devotion. Frollo's love is destructive obsession. Esmeralda's love is innocent and misplaced. Phoebus loves himself. Gringoire loves philosophy. None of the loves in the novel find fulfillment.

## Case: Fate (Ananke)
Ananke — the Greek word for fate — is scratched into the cathedral wall. The novel's characters are trapped by forces beyond their control. Their tragic ends are inevitable.

## Case: The Cathedral
Notre Dame is the novel's true protagonist. It witnesses everything. It endures. It is the permanent monument against which human lives are fleeting.

## Case: Architecture vs. Printing
Hugo's famous argument: in the Middle Ages, architecture was the great book of humanity. The cathedral told the story of civilization. The printing press would kill architecture — the book would replace the building.

## Case: Justice and Injustice
The novel is a critique of the justice system. Esmeralda is tortured and executed for a crime she did not commit. Frollo, the real criminal, is protected by his position. Society punishes the innocent and protects the guilty.
Hugo wrote the novel in part to save Notre Dame Cathedral from neglect. It worked. The novel's popularity sparked a movement to restore the cathedral.
The 2019 fire that destroyed much of Notre Dame made Hugo's novel more relevant than ever. The cathedral he fought to save nearly burned.
