# Rex Walls — The Glass Castle Dream

> Source: The Glass Castle, Parts II, IV

## The Father

Rex Walls was one of the most memorable characters in modern memoir: brilliant, charming, and utterly destroyed by alcohol.

### The Contradictions

| The Gift | The Cost |
|----------|----------|
| Taught Jeannette astronomy, geology, physics | Too drunk to keep a job |
| Drew plans for the Glass Castle | Never built it |
| Told stories that made his children believe in magic | Lied constantly |
| Supported Jeannette's dreams | Sold her piggy bank for whiskey |

> **Case: The Glass Castle Dream (Part II)**
> Rex drew detailed plans for a glass house in the desert — a 4,000-square-foot, solar-powered, self-sustaining castle made entirely of glass. He showed his children the blueprints. He promised it would be built. Jeannette believed him. The Glass Castle became the family's symbol of hope: one day, everything would be perfect. It was never built. But the dream gave the children something to hold onto through years of poverty and chaos.
> **Key takeaway:** The Glass Castle was never built — but it was not a lie. It was a dream, and the dream was real even if the structure was not. Rex gave his children the gift of imagination and possibility. The tragedy is that he could not give them the stability to match the dream.

> **Case: Rex Teaches Jeannette to Swim (Part II)**
> In one of the book's most beautiful scenes, Rex teaches Jeannette to swim by having her jump into a hot spring, promising he will catch her. She jumps. He lets her sink. She panics. Then she realizes she can swim. He says: "You didn't think I'd let you drown, did you?" The lesson: trust yourself, not me. Rex's parenting was paradoxical: he taught independence through abandonment. He forced his children to learn that they could only rely on themselves.
> **Key takeaway:** The swimming scene is Walls's metaphor for her entire childhood. Her father pushed her into deep water. He did not save her. But in the process, he taught her that she could save herself. The lesson was cruel — but it was also real.
