# Practical Techniques: Leadership Lessons from Accidental Presidents

This reference extracts practical leadership lessons from the eight accidental presidents' experiences.

---

## Lesson 1: The Predecessor's Shadow

Every accidental president had to navigate the shadow of a beloved predecessor. Those who succeeded acknowledged the loss, honored the legacy, then established their own authority.

**Techniques for stepping out of a predecessor's shadow**:
- Acknowledge the loss genuinely (Coolidge: "I do not know any way to show my appreciation of the confidence of the people except to try to do the best I can")
- Don't promise to continue everything — you won't and shouldn't
- Make your own cabinet appointments quickly (Fillmore fired the entire cabinet on day one)
- Establish one clear, signature accomplishment that is uniquely yours

**Book case — Truman vs. Johnson**: Truman honored FDR but immediately established his own style — plainspoken, direct, decisive. Johnson kept Kennedy's cabinet and staff, creating an impossible situation where he was constantly compared to the fallen president.

## Lesson 2: Character Reveals Under Pressure

The eight accidental presidents had similar circumstances, different character. Character determined outcomes.

**Techniques for character assessment** (for yourself and others):
- Watch how someone handles unexpected bad news
- Observe their treatment of people who can't help them
- Notice whether they take responsibility or deflect blame
- See if their principles hold when principles are costly

**Book case — Andrew Johnson's character failure**: Johnson's racial animus was well-known before Lincoln's death. When given power, he didn't moderate — he amplified. His character was revealed, not changed, by the presidency.

**Book case — Chester Arthur's character surprise**: Arthur was corrupt as a machine politician but reformed as president. Why? Some historians argue he was always a decent man trapped in a corrupt system. Others say the weight of the office transformed him. Either way, character revealed under pressure was better than expected.

## Lesson 3: Speed of Decision-Making Matters

Accidental presidents had to make rapid decisions with incomplete information. Those who deliberated too long lost momentum.

**Techniques for rapid decision-making**:
- Make the first decision as soon as possible — it establishes momentum
- Delegate what you can't decide immediately
- Build a trusted circle of advisors before you need them (most accidental presidents didn't)
- Accept that you'll get some decisions wrong

**Book case — Truman's atomic bomb decision**: Truman had 4 months from taking office to the Trinity test. He had to decide: use the bomb, demonstrate it, or end the war by invasion. He made the call, accepted responsibility, and never second-guessed himself publicly. Speed was essential.

**Book case — Tyler's assertion of authority**: Tyler made the most consequential decision of his presidency on Day 1: assert full presidential authority. If he had hesitated or accepted "acting president" status, every future succession would have been contested.

## Lesson 4: Surprising Your Critics Can Be Strategic

Several accidental presidents succeeded because they defied low expectations.

**Techniques**:
- Don't tell people what you're going to do — show them
- Use low expectations as cover (they're not watching you carefully)
- Build trust through small, consistent actions before making big moves

**Book case — Chester Arthur**: Everyone expected Arthur to continue the spoils system. Instead, he signed the Pendleton Act. The surprise was strategic — his opponents were caught off guard.

**Book case — Coolidge's quiet competence**: Coolidge was dismissed as boring and inactive. Then he quietly fired the Harding holdovers, prosecuted the corrupt officials, and restored the presidency's reputation. By the time anyone noticed, it was done.

## Lesson 5: The Cabinet Problem

Every accidental president faced a cabinet loyal to the predecessor. Those who replaced it quickly succeeded; those who kept it struggled.

**Techniques**:
- Ask for resignations immediately (Fillmore)
- Keep the transition team small (Truman)
- Don't let predecessor loyalists undermine your authority
- Replace key positions (State, Defense, Treasury) first

**Book case — Fillmore's decisive move**: Fillmore demanded the entire cabinet resign on his first full day. This shocked everyone — no one expected it from the mild-mannered VP. But it established immediately that he was in charge.

**Book case — Johnson's cabinet problem**: Johnson kept Lincoln's cabinet, creating a nightmare of divided loyalties. The cabinet members reported to their departments and to the ghost of Lincoln, not to Johnson.

## Lesson 6: The Vice-Presidential Selection Matters

The book's most important lesson: the choice of vice president is not trivial. It can determine the fate of the nation.

**Techniques for evaluating VP choices** (applies to any succession planning):
- Imagine the person as president for one year. Are you comfortable?
- Look beyond ticket-balancing. The VP might become president.
- Consider character and judgment, not just geography and demographics.
- Brief the VP on everything. They might need to know tomorrow.

**Book case — Lincoln's terrible choice**: Lincoln chose Johnson as a wartime unity gesture. He barely knew him. The result was a presidential catastrophe.

**Book case — FDR's lucky choice**: FDR chose Truman almost by accident — the convention dumped Henry Wallace at the last minute, and Truman was the compromise. FDR barely briefed Truman on anything. But Truman was a man of extraordinary character and judgment. FDR got lucky.
