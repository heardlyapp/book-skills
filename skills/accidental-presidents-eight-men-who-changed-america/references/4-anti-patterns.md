# Anti-Patterns: Lessons from Presidential Failure

This reference examines what went wrong with the accidental presidents who failed or underperformed.

---

## Anti-Pattern 1: Keeping the Predecessor's Team

**The problem**: Every accidental president inherited a cabinet and staff loyal to the predecessor. Those who kept them in place struggled with divided loyalties, sabotage, and constant second-guessing.

**Why it's a trap**: The predecessor's team has no reason to be loyal to you. They will compare every decision to what the predecessor "would have done." They will leak to the press. They will undermine your authority.

**What to do instead**: Ask for resignations immediately. You can rehire some, but they must understand loyalty is now to you.

**Book case — Andrew Johnson**: Johnson kept Lincoln's entire cabinet. The results were disastrous. Secretary of War Edwin Stanton actively conspired against Johnson and was one of the driving forces behind his impeachment.

**Book case — Millard Fillmore**: Fillmore did the opposite — he demanded all cabinet resignations on his first full day. This shocked the establishment but established immediate control.

---

## Anti-Pattern 2: Trying to Be the Predecessor

**The problem**: Some accidental presidents tried to govern exactly as their predecessor would have. This never works — you can't be someone else.

**Why it's a trap**: The predecessor had a mandate from voters and a unique relationship with the public. You have neither. Imitation is always inferior.

**What to do instead**: Be yourself. Acknowledge that you're different. Emphasize your own strengths.

**Book case — Lyndon Johnson**: Johnson tried to govern as Kennedy's continuation. He kept the Kennedy cabinet, the Kennedy rhetoric, the Kennedy staff. But he was not Kennedy. The comparison was always unfavorable. His forceful, dominating style ("the Johnson treatment") was a betrayal of Kennedy's cool, intellectual approach. The result: he was resented by those who loved Kennedy and didn't satisfy those who wanted something new.

**Book case — Harry Truman**: Truman did the opposite. He never pretended to be FDR. He was folksy, blunt, and decisive where FDR was patrician, indirect, and Machiavellian. The country appreciated the honesty.

## Anti-Pattern 3: Impeachment-Providing Behavior

**The problem**: Three accidental presidents faced serious impeachment threats — Tyler (first president threatened), Johnson (first president impeached), and Arthur (suspected in Garfield's assassination).

**Common triggers**: Defying Congress, vetoing popular legislation, perceived corruption, and acting against party interests.

**What to do instead**: Build congressional relationships before you need them. Consult with party leadership. Pick your fights strategically — not everything is worth the fight.

**Book case — Andrew Johnson's impeachment**: Johnson violated the Tenure of Office Act by firing Secretary of War Stanton without Senate approval. He did this knowing it would provoke Congress. It was both principled and foolish. He was saved from conviction by one vote.

**Book case — John Tyler's brushes with impeachment**: Tyler vetoed so many Whig bills that party members threatened impeachment. The resolution failed, but Tyler was kicked out of his own party and functionally powerless for most of his term.

## Anti-Pattern 4: Letting the Vice Presidency Be an Afterthought

**The problem**: Most of the accidental presidents were chosen for ticket-balancing — geography, faction, or demographics — not for readiness to govern.

**Why it's a trap**: The VP may become president. The choice is arguably the most important personnel decision a president can make.

**What to do instead**: Choose a VP you would trust with the presidency. Brief them on everything. Include them in key decisions.

**Book case — Lincoln and Johnson**: Lincoln barely knew Johnson. He chose Johnson for the 1864 election as a "Union" gesture. He never consulted Johnson about Reconstruction plans. The result: Johnson pursued policies directly opposite to what Lincoln intended.

**Book case — FDR and Truman**: FDR was too ill and distracted to properly choose a running mate in 1944. The convention dumped Henry Wallace at the last minute, and Truman was a compromise. FDR never briefed Truman on the atomic bomb, the Yalta agreements, or postwar planning. Truman learned about the Manhattan Project after FDR's death.

---

## Anti-Pattern 5: Over-Promising Continuity

**The problem**: To reassure the nation during a crisis, accidental presidents often promise to "continue the policies of my beloved predecessor." Then they can't deliver.

**Why it's a trap**: You've set an impossible standard. Every deviation from the predecessor's policies will be seen as betrayal. You've also minimized your own agency — implying that you're just a caretaker.

**What to do instead**: Promise to honor the predecessor's memory and serve the country's interests. Don't promise specific policy continuity.

**Book case — Theodore Roosevelt**: TR promised to "continue absolutely unbroken" McKinley's policies. Within months, he was aggressively pursuing trust-busting, which McKinley never would have countenanced. His promise was a lie, and everyone knew it.

**Book case — Lyndon Johnson**: Johnson's "Let us continue" speech after Kennedy's assassination was moving, but it set impossible expectations. When Johnson's style inevitably differed from Kennedy's, the comparison was always to his disadvantage.

---

## Anti-Pattern 6: Failing to Establish Independent Authority

**The problem**: Some accidental presidents governed as though they were still vice presidents — deferential to Congress, reluctant to assert executive power.

**Why it's a trap**: Congress will walk all over a president who seems unsure of their authority. The bureaucracy will ignore their directives.

**What to do instead**: Assert authority early. Make one clear, visible decision that establishes you as the decision-maker. Appoint a cabinet. Use the veto. Make the office yours.

**Book case — John Tyler**: Tyler faced the ultimate authority test: was he president or acting president? His refusal to accept "acting" status was a courageous assertion that set precedent for every subsequent succession.

**Book case — Andrew Johnson**: Johnson had the constitutional authority but squandered it through poor judgment and political isolation. Authority without wisdom is dangerous.
