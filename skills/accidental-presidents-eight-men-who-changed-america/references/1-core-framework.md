# Core Framework: Accidental Presidents — 8 Case Studies

## The Framework

Cohen's book examines eight accidental presidents through a consistent lens:
1. How they were chosen as VP (typically as a ticket-balancing afterthought)
2. How they received the news of the president's death
3. How they navigated the transition of power
4. How they dealt with their predecessor's cabinet and party
5. What they achieved (or failed to achieve) in office
6. How history judges them

## The Eight Presidents Compared

| President | Predecessor | VP Tenure | Key Achievement | Verdict |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|----------------|---------|
| John Tyler | W.H. Harrison | None (day 1) | Set succession precedent, annexed Texas | Stubborn, precedent-setting |
| Millard Fillmore | Zachary Taylor | 16 months | Compromise of 1850, delayed Civil War | More capable than remembered |
| Andrew Johnson | Abraham Lincoln | 42 days | Nothing positive | Worst of the eight |
| Chester Arthur | James Garfield | 199 days | Pendleton Civil Service Reform | Surprising redemption |
| Theodore Roosevelt | William McKinley | 194 days | Trust-busting, Panama Canal, modern presidency | The only one who would have been elected anyway |
| Calvin Coolidge | Warren Harding | 2 years, 151 days | Cleaned up Harding scandals, restored trust | Limited but perfect for the moment |
| Harry Truman | Franklin Roosevelt | 82 days | Ended WWII, Marshall Plan, NATO, desegregated military | Miracle of the eight |
| Lyndon Johnson | John F. Kennedy | 2 years, 314 days | Civil Rights Act, Voting Rights Act, Great Society | Towering domestic, catastrophic foreign |

## Book Cases

**Case 1: John Tyler's power grab**: Tyler's most significant contribution was his assertion of full presidential authority. The cabinet (and many in Congress) believed he was merely "acting president." He insisted on being sworn in, moved into the White House, and governed as president. This set a precedent that governed succession for 122 years until the 25th Amendment codified it. Without Tyler's bold assertion, every future succession would have been contested.

**Case 2: Andrew Johnson's failure**: Johnson is the cautionary tale. Lincoln chose him as a wartime unity gesture — a Southern Democrat who remained loyal to the Union. After Lincoln's assassination, Johnson's racial animus and stubbornness led him to oppose every element of Reconstruction. He vetoed the Civil Rights Act of 1866 (Congress overrode him). He vetoed the Reconstruction Acts. He was the first president to be impeached (saved by one vote). Cohen argues that Johnson's failure was the single greatest catastrophe in presidential history, setting back racial equality by a century.

**Case 3: Chester Arthur's redemption**: Arthur was the embodiment of the spoils system. His appointment as VP was a corrupt backroom deal at the 1880 Republican Convention. When Garfield was assassinated by a deranged office-seeker (Guiteau, who believed he deserved a government job), Arthur was suspected of involvement. Instead of continuing the spoils system, Arthur championed the Pendleton Civil Service Reform Act — ending the very system that created him. It is one of the most dramatic political transformations in American history.

**Case 4: Truman's miracle**: Truman had been VP for only 82 days and was kept entirely out of the loop. He didn't know about the Manhattan Project. He wasn't briefed on postwar planning. On the day he became president, he told reporters: "I felt like the moon, the stars, and all the planets had fallen on me." Despite this, he made the decision to use the atomic bomb, ended the war with Japan, rebuilt Europe through the Marshall Plan, recognized Israel, desegregated the military, established NATO, and began the Cold War containment strategy. Cohen's verdict: Truman had the biggest shoes to fill and filled them better than anyone could have predicted.
