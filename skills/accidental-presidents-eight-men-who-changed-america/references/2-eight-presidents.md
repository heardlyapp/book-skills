# The Eight Presidents in Depth

This reference provides deeper analysis of each accidental president, drawn from Cohen's narrative.

---

## 1. John Tyler: The Precedent Setter

**Background**: Former Democratic senator from Virginia who joined the Whigs. Chosen as Harrison's running mate to balance the ticket geographically.

**The succession**: Harrison died of pneumonia 30 days into his term. Tyler was at his Virginia estate in his nightclothes when messengers arrived. The cabinet addressed its letter to "John Tyler, Vice-president" — implying he was merely acting president. Tyler ignored this and asserted full authority.

**Key actions**: Annexed Texas (setting up the Mexican-American War); vetoed Whig legislation (a national bank), leading to his expulsion from the party; became the first president threatened with impeachment.

**Legacy**: Set the precedent that the VP becomes president, not acting president. This precedent governed succession for over a century.

## 2. Millard Fillmore: The Compromiser

**Background**: Former New York congressman and comptroller. Chosen to balance Taylor's Southern slaveholding background.

**The succession**: Taylor died of gastroenteritis after 16 months. Fillmore shocked the cabinet by demanding their resignations on his first full day.

**Key actions**: Supported the Compromise of 1850, which admitted California as a free state but strengthened the Fugitive Slave Act. This delayed Southern secession by a decade.

**Legacy**: Fillmore is remembered poorly — partly for the Fugitive Slave Act, partly for his general mediocrity. But Cohen argues he was more capable than history suggests. The Compromise of 1850 was a political masterstroke that bought time.

## 3. Andrew Johnson: The Catastrophe

**Background**: Southern Democrat from Tennessee who remained loyal to the Union. Chosen by Lincoln for the 1864 election as a gesture of national unity.

**The succession**: Lincoln was assassinated at Ford's Theatre on April 14, 1865. Johnson was in his hotel room. The assassin (Booth) had also planned to kill Johnson but the conspirator lost his nerve.

**Key actions**: Opposed every element of Reconstruction; vetoed the Civil Rights Act of 1866 and the Reconstruction Acts; was impeached by the House (saved by one vote in the Senate); pardoned thousands of former Confederates; allowed "Black Codes" to be enacted in the South.

**Legacy**: Johnson's presidency was a catastrophe. His racism and stubbornness destroyed the possibility of a just Reconstruction. Cohen calls him "the biggest catastrophe of the eight."

## 4. Chester Arthur: The Redemption

**Background**: New York political machine boss. Chosen as VP at the 1880 convention as a compromise to placate the Stalwart (pro-spoils system) faction.

**The succession**: Garfield was shot by Charles Guiteau, a deranged office-seeker who believed he deserved a consulship. Guiteau famously shouted "I am a Stalwart and Arthur will be president!" Arthur was suspected of involvement.

**Key actions**: Surprised everyone by championing the Pendleton Civil Service Reform Act of 1883, which ended the spoils system and established the modern federal civil service on a merit basis.

**Legacy**: One of the most dramatic political transformations in history. The man who symbolized the spoils system ended it. Cohen calls it a redemption story.

## 5. Theodore Roosevelt: The Giant

**Background**: Former New York governor and Spanish-American War hero. Forced onto McKinley's 1900 ticket to sideline him from New York politics.

**The succession**: McKinley was shot at the Pan-American Exposition in Buffalo by anarchist Leon Czolgosz. Roosevelt rushed to Buffalo but McKinley initially seemed to recover. Then McKinley died eight days later.

**Key actions**: Broke up the trusts (Northern Securities, Standard Oil); built the Panama Canal; expanded the Navy (Great White Fleet); won the Nobel Peace Prize (Russo-Japanese War mediation); established the modern presidency as the center of American political life.

**Legacy**: The only accidental president who would have been elected in his own right anyway. Cohen's verdict: "the best of the lot."

## 6. Calvin Coolidge: The Clean-Up Man

**Background**: Former Massachusetts governor. Chosen as Harding's running mate in 1920.

**The succession**: Harding died suddenly in San Francisco — possibly of a heart attack, possibly overwhelmed by the emerging Teapot Dome scandal. Coolidge was at his family farm in Vermont. His father, a notary public, administered the oath by lamplight.

**Key actions**: Investigated and prosecuted the Harding scandals without fanfare; restored trust in the presidency; signed the Indian Citizenship Act (1924); won election in his own right in 1924.

**Legacy**: Coolidge is often dismissed as a do-nothing president who slept 11 hours a night. But Cohen argues he was exactly what the country needed after Harding's corruption — quiet, honest, and scandal-free.

## 7. Harry Truman: The Miracle

**Background**: Former Missouri senator. Chosen as FDR's running mate at the 1944 convention to replace Henry Wallace, who was seen as too radical.

**The succession**: FDR died of a cerebral hemorrhage in Warm Springs, Georgia. Truman had been VP for only 82 days and was kept out of the loop on the atomic bomb, war strategy, and postwar planning.

**Key actions**: Authorized the atomic bomb; ended WWII; rebuilt Europe (Marshall Plan); established NATO; recognized Israel; desegregated the military; contained Soviet expansion (Truman Doctrine, Berlin Airlift); fired General MacArthur.

**Legacy**: Truman is the miracle. He had the biggest shoes to fill and filled them better than anyone could have predicted. Cohen: "the best of the lot other than the great Theodore Roosevelt."

## 8. Lyndon B. Johnson: The Tragic Giant

**Background**: Texas senator and Senate Majority Leader. Chosen by Kennedy in 1960 to win Texas and the South.

**The succession**: Kennedy was assassinated in Dallas, November 22, 1963. Johnson was two cars behind in the motorcade. He was sworn in on Air Force One with Jackie Kennedy at his side.

**Key actions**: Passed the Civil Rights Act (1964); Voting Rights Act (1965); Medicare; Medicaid; Head Start; the Great Society programs. But escalated the Vietnam War from 16,000 advisors to 500,000 troops, destroying his presidency.

**Legacy**: Johnson's domestic achievements are among the greatest of any president. But Vietnam overshadows everything. Cohen calls him a tragic figure — a man who achieved so much at home while failing catastrophically abroad.
