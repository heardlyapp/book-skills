# Voice, Application, and Adaptation

This reference covers how the lessons from accidental presidents apply to modern leadership.

---

## Applying Presidential Succession Lessons to Business

The eight accidental presidents faced challenges that mirror business succession scenarios: sudden leadership change, predecessor's shadow, team loyalty, rapid decision-making under pressure.

### CEO Succession (Sudden Departure)

**The problem**: A founder/CEO dies, resigns, or is removed. The successor — often a COO, board member, or #2 — must step up immediately.

**Apply the lessons**:
- Assert authority immediately (like Tyler). If you act like a caretaker, everyone will treat you like one.
- Ask for the predecessor's team's resignations (like Fillmore). You can rehire, but loyalty must now be to you.
- Don't try to be the predecessor (like Johnson). Be yourself.
- Make one clear, signature decision quickly (like Truman).

**Book case — Tyler's precedent**: Tyler's greatest contribution was not a policy but a precedent — that the successor is the full president, not an acting placeholder. In business, if you're the new CEO, act like the CEO from day one.

### Vice President / #2 Selection

**The problem**: Leaders often choose their second-in-command based on complementary skills or political expediency — not readiness to lead.

**Apply the lesson**: Imagine this person as the leader for one year. Are you comfortable? If not, choose differently or invest in their development.

**Book case — Lincoln's mistake**: Lincoln chose Johnson for political reasons during wartime. The result was a presidential catastrophe. The choice of VP is the most important personnel decision a president (or CEO) can make.

### Crisis Leadership

**The problem**: Sudden leadership transitions inevitably involve crisis. The new leader inherits problems they didn't create.

**Apply the lessons**:
- Communicate honestly (like Truman). The public can handle bad news if you tell them the truth.
- Make decisions quickly (like Truman). Indecision is worse than wrong decisions.
- Build your own team (like Fillmore). Don't inherit someone else's loyalties.

**Book case — Truman's honesty**: Truman told reporters on his first day: "I felt like the moon, the stars, and all the planets had fallen on me." The honesty was disarming. The country trusted him because he didn't pretend.

## Key Leadership Principles from the Eight

### Principle 1: Character Is Destiny

The book's central lesson: **character determines performance during crisis**. The eight presidents had similar circumstances but vastly different outcomes because of who they were.

**How to assess character**:
- What does the person do when no one is watching?
- How do they treat people who can't help them?
- Do they take responsibility or deflect blame?
- Do their principles hold when principles are costly?

**Book case — Arthur vs. Johnson**: Both were viewed negatively before becoming president. Arthur surprised everyone by rising to the occasion. Johnson confirmed everyone's worst fears. The difference was character.

### Principle 2: You Can't Be Someone Else

Every accidental president who tried to be their predecessor failed. Every one who embraced their own identity succeeded.

**How to apply**: Don't study your predecessor's moves and try to replicate them. Study your own strengths and lead with them. Your unique capabilities are your advantage.

**Book case — TR vs. McKinley**: McKinley was a protectionist, pro-business conservative. TR was a reformer, trust-buster, progressive. They were opposites. TR succeeded because he was authentically himself, not because he continued McKinley's policies.

### Principle 3: Speed Creates Momentum

The presidents who moved fast — Tyler (asserting authority), Fillmore (cleaning cabinet), Truman (atomic bomb decision) — created momentum that carried them through difficult transitions.

**How to apply**: In any transition, make your first visible decision early. It doesn't have to be the biggest decision — just one that signals you're in charge.

**Book case — Fillmore's cabinet purge**: Fillmore fired the entire cabinet on his first full day. This was not a subtle move. But it signaled clearly: I am the president now.

### Principle 4: Low Expectations Can Be an Advantage

Several accidental presidents (Arthur, Coolidge, Truman) succeeded in part because expectations were so low. They used this as cover.

**How to apply**: When you're underestimated, don't correct people. Let them underestimate you. Then deliver results while they're not watching.

**Book case — Coolidge**: Everyone dismissed Coolidge as boring and lazy. He quietly fired Harding's corrupt appointees, prosecuted the scandals, and restored the presidency's reputation. By the time anyone noticed, it was done.

### Principle 5: The Nation Comes First

Every successful accidental president put the country above party, faction, or personal interest. Those who put party or self first failed.

**How to apply**: When making a difficult decision, ask: "What serves the mission?" not "What serves my career?"

**Book case — Arthur's Pendleton Act**: Arthur signed a law that ended the spoils system that created him. It was against his party, his faction, and his personal interest. He did it because it was right.

## Modern Relevance

The book's lessons are particularly relevant today:
- The 25th Amendment (ratified 1967) finally codified succession procedures, but it was only created after JFK's assassination shocked the nation into action
- Presidential succession remains a critical vulnerability — what if the president and VP were both incapacitated?
- The lesson of Johnson and Lincoln is eternally relevant: the VP choice matters enormously

**Book case — The 25th Amendment**: It took 122 years and eight deaths in office (and many close calls, which Cohen covers in Chapter 10) for the Constitution to be amended to clearly define succession. The founders didn't think of everything — and their omissions required centuries to correct.

**Book case — Close calls**: Cohen's Chapter 10 covers presidential close calls — attempts and near-misses that could have produced more accidental presidents: Reagan's shooting, two attempts on Ford, FDR's near-assassination in 1933 (the mayor standing next to him was killed), and more.
