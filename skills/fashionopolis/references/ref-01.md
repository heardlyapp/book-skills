# Reference: The Fast Fashion Machine

## Overview
Fast fashion transformed the clothing industry in the late 20th century. Before the 1990s, most clothing was produced in two seasons per year. Today, Zara produces 24 collections per year — and some ultra-fast-fashion brands like Shein introduce thousands of new items daily.

## Case: The Zara Revolution
Zara's founder Amancio Ortega built a business model that defied every convention of the fashion industry. Instead of outsourcing production to Asia (cheaper but slower), Zara kept production close to home in Spain and Portugal. Instead of producing in bulk, Zara produced small batches and replenished based on real-time sales data. A garment could go from design to store in two weeks. This speed created artificial scarcity: if you saw something you liked, you bought it immediately, because it would not be there next week. The Zara model was not just faster — it was more profitable, because it sold at full price rather than at markdown.

## Case: The H&M Race to the Bottom
H&M took the opposite approach: no in-house production, extreme outsourcing to the cheapest factories. H&M's prices were lower than Zara's, but the quality was lower too. The H&M model depended on volume — selling enormous quantities of cheap, low-quality garments that would be worn a few times and discarded. This is the business model that created the 70 pounds of textile waste per American per year.

## Case: The Shein Disruption
Shein, the Chinese ultra-fast-fashion giant, took the model to its logical extreme. Using AI-powered trend prediction and an agile supply chain in Guangzhou, Shein can produce thousands of new items per day at prices that undercut even H&M. Shein exemplifies everything wrong with fast fashion: rock-bottom prices, disposable quality, no transparency about supply chains, and massive environmental damage. Its success demonstrates that the fast fashion industry has not learned any lessons — it has simply gotten faster and cheaper.
