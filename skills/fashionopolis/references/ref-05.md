# Reference: The Consumer's Role

## Overview
Individual consumers cannot fix the fashion industry alone, but individual choices matter. The most powerful action a consumer can take is to buy less and buy better.

## Case: The Capsule Wardrobe
The capsule wardrobe movement — owning 30-40 versatile, high-quality pieces — is a direct response to fast fashion. The philosophy: if you own fewer clothes that you love and that fit well, you dress better with less. Thomas highlights how the capsule wardrobe reduces consumption without reducing style. The challenge is cultural: we are trained to believe that new clothes = happiness. The capsule wardrobe challenges that belief.

## Case: The 30-Wear Challenge
Patron of the Livia Firth launched the 30-Wear Challenge: before buying any new garment, ask yourself whether you will wear it at least 30 times. If the answer is no, do not buy it. This simple rule changes the economics of fashion: a $200 dress worn 30 times costs $6.67 per wear. A $30 dress worn twice costs $15 per wear. The expensive dress is actually cheaper per wear.

## Case: The Power of Transparency Apps
Apps like Good On You rate fashion brands on their environmental, labor, and animal welfare practices. The ratings are based on publicly available information and third-party certifications. By checking before you buy, you can make informed choices. Thomas argues that consumer pressure — informed by transparency tools — can move the industry. When brands see that customers care about sustainability, they respond.
