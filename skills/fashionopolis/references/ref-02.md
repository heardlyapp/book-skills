# Reference: The Human Cost

## Overview
The fashion industry employs approximately 60 million people worldwide, most of them women in developing countries. The conditions in which they work range from difficult to deadly.

## Case: Rana Plaza
On April 24, 2013, the Rana Plaza factory complex in Dhaka, Bangladesh, collapsed. The building housed five garment factories producing for major Western brands. The collapse killed 1,134 workers and injured more than 2,500. The building had been constructed on a swamp, had visible cracks the day before, and had been ordered evacuated — but workers were forced to return. The disaster was not an accident: it was the predictable result of a system that prioritizes speed and low cost over human life.

## Case: The Wages
Garment workers in Bangladesh earn approximately $100 per month — among the lowest wages in the world. This is the foundation of the fast fashion business model: cheap labor equals cheap clothes. Brands argue that they cannot pay more because consumers will not pay more. Thomas argues that this is a choice, not an inevitability. Brands choose to pay low wages and consumers choose to buy cheap clothes. The system is designed to make both choices easy.

## Case: The Aftermath of Rana Plaza
After Rana Plaza, brands signed the Accord on Fire and Building Safety in Bangladesh. But enforcement has been inconsistent, and brands have found new sourcing destinations (Ethiopia, Myanmar) where wages are even lower and safety regulations even weaker. The Rana Plaza disaster did not solve the problem of garment worker exploitation. It exposed it, but the system did not fundamentally change.
