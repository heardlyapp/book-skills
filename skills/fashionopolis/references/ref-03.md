# Reference: The Environmental Toll

## Overview
Fashion's environmental impact is staggering: it is the second most polluting industry in the world, producing 10% of global carbon emissions, consuming enough water for 110 million people, and filling landfills with 92 million tons of waste per year.

## Case: Water and Dyeing
Cotton is one of the most water-intensive crops in the world. A single cotton t-shirt requires 2,700 liters of water — enough for one person to drink for 2.5 years. The dyeing process is another environmental disaster: textile dyeing is the second largest polluter of clean water globally. In countries like Bangladesh and India, untreated dye waste is dumped directly into rivers, turning them toxic. The Aral Sea has been largely destroyed by cotton irrigation.

## Case: Microplastics
Synthetic fabrics (polyester, nylon, acrylic) are made from petroleum. They do not biodegrade. When washed, they shed tiny plastic fibers called microplastics that pass through water treatment plants and enter the ocean. A single load of laundry can release 700,000 microplastic fibers. These fibers enter the food chain and have been found in human bloodstreams and placentas.

## Case: Textile Waste
The average American throws away 70 pounds of clothing per year. Globally, 92 million tons of textile waste end up in landfills annually. The problem is that many synthetic fabrics do not biodegrade — a polyester shirt will sit in a landfill for 200+ years. Even donated clothing often ends up in landfills or is shipped to developing countries, where it destroys local textile industries.
