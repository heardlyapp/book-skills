# The Regimes Compared — Principles

Snyder's book is a work of history, not a philosophical treatise. But its analysis yields principles for understanding political mass murder.

## I. Ideology Is Real — But It Is Not the Only Explanation

Hitler was an anti-Semite. Stalin was a class warrior. Their ideologies were real and murderous. But ideology alone cannot explain the timing, targeting, or methods of mass killing.

**Case: The Choice of Victims.** Stalin's Great Terror targeted "enemies of the people" — initially defined by class, then by nationality. In 1937, Stalin ordered the execution of "anti-Soviet elements" by national category: Poles, Germans, Latvians, Koreans, Finns, Estonians, and others. The targeting was ideological — but it was also practical: Stalin saw these groups as potential fifth columns in an approaching war.

**Case: The Timing of the Holocaust.** Hitler had been an anti-Semite since the 1920s. But the systematic murder of all European Jews began only in 1941, after the invasion of the Soviet Union. The decision to kill all Jews was not a single moment but a process that developed over months. Ideology set the direction; the war provided the opportunity.

## II. Mass Killing Requires Organization, Not Just Hatred

The 14 million victims were not killed by mobs or spontaneous violence. They were killed by organized state policies implemented by millions of ordinary people following orders.

**Case: The Einsatzgruppen.** The mobile killing units that shot Jews in Soviet territories were composed of police, SS, and local auxiliaries. They shot women, children, and the elderly at close range. The killers were not specially selected sadists — they were ordinary men who were trained to follow orders and given alcohol to numb their consciences.

**Case: The NKVD's Death Quotas.** During the Great Terror, Moscow sent death quotas to regional NKVD offices. The quotas specified how many "anti-Soviet elements" were to be shot. Local officials often asked for the quotas to be increased — not because they were more enthusiastic killers, but because they wanted to prove their loyalty to Stalin.

## III. War Accelerates Killing

Both regimes killed before the war — Stalin far more than Hitler. But war allowed each regime to kill with less restraint and on a much larger scale.

**Case: Before the War.** In the 1930s, before the Second World War, the Soviet Union was the only state in Europe carrying out policies of mass killing. Stalin had already starved 3 million and shot 700,000. Hitler, by contrast, had killed no more than about 10,000 people in the first six and a half years of his rule.

**Case: During the War.** Once the war began, the pace of killing accelerated dramatically. The Nazi regime killed 5.7 million Jews between 1941 and 1945. The Soviet regime killed hundreds of thousands in occupied Poland and the Baltic States. War made mass murder easier by creating chaos, loosening moral restraints, and providing cover for extreme policies.

## IV. The Victims Must Be Named

Snyder insists on naming the victims — not just as numbers, but as individuals. He quotes Anna Akhmatova: "I'd like to call you all by name, but the list has been removed."

**Case: The Jewish Girl in Belarus.** In 1942, a 12-year-old Jewish girl wrote to her father: "I am saying good-bye to you before I die. I am so afraid of this death because they throw small children into the mass graves alive." Snyder includes her voice. The 14 million are not a statistic.

**Case: The Polish Officer's Diary.** A Polish officer being transported to the Katyn forest wrote his last words: "They asked for my wedding ring, which I..." The diary breaks off. The officer was shot by the NKVD. Snyder includes the fragment not as evidence of Soviet guilt — that is well established — but as a record of a human being's last moment of consciousness.

## V. The Moral Obligation Is to Remember

Snyder's final principle: the dead cannot be brought back, but they can be remembered. And remembering is a political act.

**Case: The Architect of the Bloodlands.** Both regimes tried to control the historical record. The Nazis destroyed evidence of their crimes. The Soviets suppressed the truth about the famines and the Great Terror. Snyder's work is part of a generation of scholarship that has used opened archives to recover what was hidden.

**Case: The Writer's Vocation.** Snyder draws on writers who bore witness — Vasily Grossman, Anna Akhmatova, Arthur Koestler, George Orwell. These writers understood that the truth about mass murder is the beginning of resistance to it. "Without a history built and defended upon an entirely different foundation, we will find that Hitler and Stalin continue to define their own works for us."
