# The Mistakes We Make — Anti-Patterns

Snyder identifies recurring patterns of error in how we think about Nazi and Soviet mass murder.

## Anti-Pattern 1: Thinking the Camps Tell the Whole Story

The most pervasive misconception: when we think of Nazi atrocities, we think of concentration camps — Belsen, Buchenwald, Dachau. When we think of Soviet atrocities, we think of the Gulag. But these camps are not where most of the victims died.

**Case: The American and British Experience.** American and British forces liberated German concentration camps in the west. They saw the piles of corpses, the emaciated survivors. Those images shaped the Western understanding of Nazi evil. But American and British forces never reached the bloodlands. They never saw Treblinka, Sobibor, Belzec, or the death pits of Ukraine and Belarus.

**Case: Auschwitz as Exception.** Auschwitz was two things at once — a labor camp and a death facility. Non-Jewish prisoners at Auschwitz had a chance of survival. But for Jews selected for the gas chambers on arrival, there was no chance. Most of the Jews killed at Auschwitz were gassed within hours. They never saw a camp.

## Anti-Pattern 2: Equating Nazi and Soviet Crimes

Hitler and Stalin both killed millions. But they killed in different ways, at different times, for different reasons. Equating the two regimes obscures important differences.

**Case: The Peace/War Distinction.** Before the Second World War, the Soviet Union was the only state in Europe carrying out policies of mass killing. Hitler killed relatively few people before 1939. The Soviet Union's mass murder was a peacetime phenomenon; Nazi Germany's was a wartime one.

**Case: The Citizen/Alien Distinction.** Stalin killed mainly his own citizens. His victims were Soviet people whom he saw as internal enemies. Hitler killed mainly citizens of other countries — Poles, Ukrainians, Belarusians, Jews from across Europe. This difference has implications for how we understand each regime's relationship to its own society.

## Anti-Pattern 3: Forgetting That Hitler and Stalin Were Allies

The Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact of August 1939 made Nazi Germany and the Soviet Union allies. They jointly invaded Poland. They divided Eastern Europe between them. This fact is often forgotten or minimized.

**Case: The Joint Occupation of Poland.** For nearly two years (1939-1941), Germany and the Soviet Union occupied Poland together. The NKVD and the SS cooperated in suppressing Polish resistance. Both regimes shot Polish elites. The Katyn massacre was a product of this alliance.

**Case: The Fuel for the Holocaust.** The Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact gave Hitler the strategic security to invade Poland without fighting a two-front war. Without the pact, there might have been no war in the East. Without the war in the East, the Holocaust could not have happened.

## Anti-Pattern 4: Focusing Only on the Holocaust

The Holocaust was the largest single component of the bloodlands — 5.7 million Jews murdered. But it was not the only mass killing. Of the 14 million victims, 3.3 million died in Soviet famines, 700,000 were shot in the Great Terror, more than 3 million Soviet prisoners of war were deliberately starved by the Germans, and millions more were shot in reprisals or died in ethnic cleansing.

**Case: The Non-Jewish Starvation Victims.** The Soviet famine of 1933 killed mainly Ukrainians. The Nazi Hunger Plan killed Russians, Ukrainians, and Belarusians. The siege of Leningrad killed Russians. These deaths are often omitted from accounts that focus exclusively on the Holocaust.

**Case: The Postwar Ethnic Cleansings.** The killing did not stop in 1945. Poles were expelled from Ukraine. Germans were driven from Czechoslovakia and Poland. The Soviet regime continued to persecute Jews in the late 1940s and early 1950s. The bloodlands did not stop being bloody when the war ended.

## Anti-Pattern 5: Treating the Victims as a Statistic

The number 14 million can blunt our sense of individual suffering. Snyder warns against this. "The sheer numbers of the victims can blunt our sense of the individuality of each one."

**Case: Anna Akhmatova's Requiem.** Snyder quotes the Russian poet: "I'd like to call you all by name, but the list has been removed and there is nowhere else to look." The quote is a reminder that every number was a person, and every person had a name.

**Case: The Trope of Impersonal Evil.** It is easier to say "the Nazis killed 6 million Jews" than to imagine the specific deaths of a specific family in a specific village on a specific day. Snyder's book works against this abstraction by including diary entries, personal testimony, and the words of victims. He insists that the reader confront the specific, not just the statistical.
