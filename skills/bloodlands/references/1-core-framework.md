# The Killing Fields — Core Framework

Snyder's central argument is that the 14 million victims of Nazi and Soviet mass murder in Eastern Europe must be understood together — not because the two regimes were equivalent, but because their killing policies were so intertwined.

## The Three Layers of Understanding

**Layer 1: The Geography of Murder.** The bloodlands were not a single country or a single political territory. They were the lands between Berlin and Moscow — Poland, Ukraine, Belarus, the Baltic States, and western Russia. This was where most of Europe's Jews lived, where Hitler and Stalin's imperial ambitions overlapped, where the Wehrmacht and Red Army fought, and where the NKVD and the SS concentrated their forces.

**Case: Why Eastern Europe?** Jews were fewer than 1% of Germany's population in 1933, but they were 10% of Poland's population and a significant minority in the Baltic States and Soviet Ukraine. Hitler could not realize his vision of eliminating Jews from Europe without invading the places where Jews lived. Similarly, Stalin's class terror targeted peasants and national minorities — and most of the Soviet Union's peasants lived in Ukraine, not Russia.

**Case: The Killing Sites.** Auschwitz was liberated by the Red Army; Treblinka, Sobibor, Belzec, and Chelmno were all in lands subsequently occupied by the Soviets. American and British forces never reached the bloodlands. This is why the iconic images of liberated camps — Belsen, Dachau — show concentration camps, not death factories. The visual record of the Holocaust is skewed toward the western camps.

**Layer 2: The Methods of Murder.** Starvation was the primary weapon. Of the 14 million killed, more than half died because they were denied food.

**Case: The Soviet Famine of 1933.** Stalin requisitioned grain from Ukrainian peasants, knowing it would cause mass starvation. More than 3 million died. The little boy in the preface who said "Now we will live!" — he died because the grain had been taken. Snyder writes: "Stalin knew what would happen when he seized food from the starving peasants of Ukraine in 1933."

**Case: The Nazi Hunger Plan.** Hitler planned to starve 30 million Soviet civilians in the winter of 1941-1942. The plan was partially realized: more than 3 million Soviet prisoners of war were deliberately starved, and more than 1 million Leningraders died in the siege.

**Layer 3: The Interaction of Regimes.** The killing policies of Hitler and Stalin were not separate — they were connected in time, space, and causation.

**Case: The Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact.** In August 1939, Hitler and Stalin agreed to divide Eastern Europe between them. They jointly invaded Poland in September. The pact gave Hitler the eastern front he needed to start the war, and it gave Stalin control over the Baltic States and eastern Poland, where he carried out mass deportations and executions.

**Case: The Katyn Massacre.** In 1940, the Soviet NKVD shot more than 20,000 Polish officers and intellectuals in the Katyn forest. It was part of the joint German-Soviet occupation of Poland — a crime enabled by the alliance between Hitler and Stalin.
