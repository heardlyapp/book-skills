# Analytical Tools — Techniques

Snyder's book provides a set of analytical tools for understanding mass political violence.

## Technique 1: Comparative History Without False Equivalence

Snyder compares Nazi and Soviet mass murder not to say they were the same, but to understand how they interacted. Comparison is not equivalence — it is a method of understanding.

**How to do it:** When comparing two systems or events, start by noting the differences before the similarities. Do not collapse distinct categories. Snyder does not say "Hitler and Stalin were both murderers." He says: Stalin killed in times of peace; Hitler killed in times of war. Stalin killed his own citizens; Hitler killed other people's citizens. These differences are as important as the similarities.

**Case: Before vs. During the War.** Before 1939, the Soviet Union killed millions. Nazi Germany killed thousands. After 1941, Nazi Germany killed millions. The comparison reveals that war changed the balance of killing — and that the Holocaust was enabled by the war that Hitler started.

**Case: The Camps vs. The Killing Sites.** Auschwitz is the most famous killing site, but it is not representative. Most victims never saw a camp. The technique of distinguishing between concentration camps (where people were worked to death or died of disease) and death facilities (where people were killed within hours of arrival) is essential for accurate understanding.

## Technique 2: Following the Victims, Not the Borders

Snyder organizes his history not by the borders of states but by the lives of victims. This changes what we see.

**How to do it:** When studying mass violence, do not organize your analysis by the perpetrator's administrative categories. Ask: who was killed? Where did they live? What policies affected them? This leads to different conclusions than asking: what did the Nazi or Soviet regime do?

**Case: Jews, Poles, Ukrainians, Belarusians.** Snyder follows the experience of each group across time and across the border changes of 1939-1945. The same person might be a Soviet citizen in 1939, a German subject in 1941, and a displaced person in 1945. Following the victim reveals the interaction of the two regimes.

**Case: The Polish Officers.** Polish officers were arrested by the Soviets in 1939, held in camps, and shot at Katyn in 1940. They were victims of Stalin. But if Snyder had organized the book by regime, they would appear only in one chapter. By following the victims, he shows how the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact — the alliance between Hitler and Stalin — made their deaths possible.

## Technique 3: Distinguishing Intent from Outcome

Not every death was intended. But the policies that caused mass death were intentional, and their consequences were foreseeable.

**How to do it:** Distinguish between policies designed to kill (gas chambers, executions) and policies that caused mass death as a foreseeable consequence (the Soviet grain requisitions, the Nazi Hunger Plan). The distinction matters for moral judgment and historical understanding.

**Case: The Soviet Famine.** Stalin did not say "I will starve 3.3 million Ukrainians." He said "I will collect the grain quota regardless of the human cost." But he knew that taking the grain would cause mass starvation. The foreseeable consequence was death. Snyder classifies the famine as a policy of mass murder — not because Stalin explicitly ordered it, but because he knowingly caused it.

**Case: The Hunger Plan.** Nazi planners calculated that 30 million Soviet civilians would die of starvation if the German army seized Ukrainian grain for its own use. They called this the "Hunger Plan." The deaths were not a side effect — they were the plan. The technique of reading economic plans as killing directives reveals the genocidal logic of the Nazi regime.

## Technique 4: Listening to the Voices of the Victims

Snyder insists on including the voices of the victims — their diaries, letters, songs, and last words. This is not sentimentality. It is a method.

**How to do it:** Whenever possible, let the victims speak in their own words. Their perspective is not always historically accurate — they may not know what is happening on a larger scale. But their perspective is morally essential.

**Case: Tania's Diary.** The 11-year-old Russian girl in Leningrad wrote: "Only Tania is left." Her short diary entry — recording the deaths of her family members one by one during the siege — is more powerful than any statistical analysis of the 1 million Leningrad deaths.

**Case: The Jewish Woman at Babi Yar.** The young Jewish woman who survived the Babi Yar massacre by pretending to be dead, then dug herself out of the mass grave. Her testimony is included because it preserves the specific horror of being shot at close range into a pit filled with bodies of people you knew.

## Technique 5: Tracing the "If Not" Path

Snyder constantly asks: what if things had been different? This counterfactual thinking is not speculation — it is a technique for understanding causation.

**How to do it:** When analyzing a historical outcome, ask: what choices could have led to a different result? This reveals where human agency — not impersonal forces — determined the course of events.

**Case: If the Soviets Had Not Signed the Pact.** If Stalin had not signed the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact with Hitler, Germany would not have been able to attack Poland in 1939 without risking a two-front war. The Holocaust required the war in the East. The pact enabled the war.

**Case: If the West Had Responded.** Snyder notes that the world knew about the Holocaust in progress. The Allies knew about the gas chambers. They knew about the mass shootings. They chose not to bomb the railways leading to Auschwitz. The counterfactual is not about whether bombing would have saved lives — it is about the moral significance of knowing and not acting.
