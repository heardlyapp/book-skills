# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *On Becoming a Person* by Carl R. Rogers — Chapters 2, 3, 6, 17.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The Reflection of Feeling

Rogers' signature technique: reflect the feeling behind the content, not the content itself. When a client says "I just can't seem to get anything right," don't say "You're wrong" or "Let me help you." Say "It sounds like you're feeling discouraged and maybe a little hopeless about it."

> **Case: The "Flashes of Sanity" Reflection** (Chapter 6): Mrs. Oak said she had "flashes of sanity." Rogers didn't say "That's good!" or "What do you mean?" He reflected the implicit fear: "Almost as if there was no place for such a person to exist." This reflection allowed Mrs. Oak to continue exploring without being evaluated.
> **Key takeaway**: Reflect the feeling that is implied but not yet said. That's where the growth is.

### 2. The Genuineness Check

Before any important interaction, ask yourself: "What am I actually feeling right now?" If you're frustrated, bored, anxious — acknowledge it. Rogers found that hidden feelings always leak. Better to express them cleanly than let them contaminate the relationship subtly.

### 3. The Evaluation Pause

"When I am trying to help someone, I catch myself wanting to evaluate — 'that's good,' 'that's bad,' 'you should...' I stop. I remind myself: evaluation is always threatening. I ask instead: 'What is this person experiencing right now?'"

### 4. The Unconditional Regard Reset

When you're struggling to accept someone: separate the person from the behavior. You can dislike what they did without withdrawing your regard for who they are.

### 5. The Process Check

"Is this relationship helping the other person grow? Or is it making them dependent on me?" The test of a helping relationship is not whether the person feels good — it's whether they are more free to become themselves.
