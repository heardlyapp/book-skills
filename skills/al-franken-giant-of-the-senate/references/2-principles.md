# 2 — The 2008 Recount and Democracy

> Source: "Al Franken, Giant of the Senate" by Al Franken

## Every Vote Counts

The 2008 Minnesota Senate recount is one of the most dramatic elections in American history. Franken won by 312 votes out of nearly 3 million cast. The recount took 8 months.

> **Case: The Long Count** (Chapter on the Recount): "I won by 312 votes. That's not a lot. It's the number of people at a medium-sized wedding. Every single one of those 312 people changed the course of history. Every person who knocked on a door, made a phone call, or showed up to vote — they all mattered." Franken uses the recount to argue that individual participation matters.
> **Key takeaway**: The recount proves that every vote truly counts. If 313 people had voted differently, Franken would not have been a Senator. The health care debate, Supreme Court appointments, and countless other issues would have been different. Democracy is decided by margins.

> **Case: The Legal Battle** (Chapter on the Recount): "The recount went to the Minnesota Supreme Court. Both sides had lawyers, and both sides fought hard. But in the end, the system worked. The courts reviewed the ballots, made their decisions, and Norm Coleman conceded. Democracy can be messy, but it can work." Franken describes the legal process without bitterness.
> **Key takeaway**: The recount showed that the system, while imperfect, can resolve disputes peacefully. The loser conceded. Courts made decisions. The transition was peaceful. In a world where election disputes are increasingly contentious, Franken's recount is a case study in democracy functioning as designed.
