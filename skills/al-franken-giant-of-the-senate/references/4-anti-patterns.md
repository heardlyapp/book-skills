# 4 — Policy and Political Dysfunction

> Source: "Al Franken, Giant of the Senate" by Al Franken

## Anti-Pattern: The Filibuster as a Weapon

Franken is critical of how the filibuster evolved from a rare tool to a routine weapon of obstruction. He argues it has fundamentally changed how the Senate functions.

> **Case: The Obamacare Fight** (Chapter on Health Care): "The fight for the Affordable Care Act was the most intense legislative battle of my career. The opposition was unified and relentless. They used every procedural tool available — including the filibuster — to try to stop it. The bill passed, but the process revealed how broken the Senate had become." Franken provides an insider account of the ACA battle.
> **Key takeaway**: The ACA passed, but the experience showed Franken how difficult it had become to pass major legislation. The filibuster, once used sparingly, had become a routine tool of obstruction. This has made the Senate less functional and more partisan.

## Anti-Pattern: Money in Politics

Franken is critical of the influence of money in the political system, calling it a corrosive force that distorts priorities.

> **Case: The Fundraising Reality** (Chapter on Money): "Senators spend an enormous amount of time raising money. It's not a conspiracy — it's a practical reality. If you're not raising money, you're not scaring off challengers. And every hour spent on the phone asking for money is an hour not spent on policy." Franken is honest about the fundraising demands.
> **Key takeaway**: The fundraising imperative distorts the Senate's priorities. Time that should be spent on legislation is spent on phone calls with donors. Franken is critical of this system but honest that he participated in it. The solution requires systemic change, not individual virtue.
