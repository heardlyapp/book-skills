# Core Framework — I've Been Thinking...

## Introduction: The Journey

Maria Shriver grew up in the Kennedy family — "where people did really big things. They ran for President. They started programs that changed people's lives." She had to figure out who she, Maria, was — separate from her famous family. This book comes from her weekly newsletter column, "The Sunday Paper."

"I believe that every single one of us is here on this earth for a reason. I believe it's our life's work to figure out who we are, what we think, what our gifts are, and how we can make a difference in this world."

## I Am Who I Choose to Become

Drawing on Carl Jung's quote: "I am not what happened to me. I am what I choose to become." Shriver reflects that she used to live in the past or the future, but has learned that "living in either the past or the future keeps me up in my head, out of reality, robbing me of the present." The key: start where you are, not where you wish you were.

## Seeing the Jewel Inside

The central metaphor: we are jewels wrapped in wrinkled gift wrap. "No matter who you are, the challenge is the same: How do we turn our focus away from the glittering gift wrap we think is us and focus instead on the jewel inside?" Shriver admits she has given away her power, letting others decide her worth. Her resolution: "I'm going to do what I did for my parents: love myself no less as I age."

## Working on "Intestinal Fortitude"

"Faith is at the root of fortitude. Strong unwavering faith is built out of small acts along the path of your life." Shriver writes about staying calm in the storm, especially during the Trump-era culture of rage and screaming. "People with intestinal fortitude are calm in the storm. They exude a steady, strong, stable energy." The key: focus on strengthening your own faith so you can respond from strength, not anger.

## Life Is Yours to Create and Re-Create

After her divorce from Arnold Schwarzenegger, Shriver had to re-create her life. "I had to step into the unknown and sit there. It was terrifying. I can't tell you I enjoyed it, but I can say I learned that I could re-imagine, re-construct, and re-build my life."

## What I Learned About Power from a Community of Nuns

Shriver visited Sister Joan Chittister's Benedictine community in Erie, Pennsylvania. The nuns impressed her: "Each one of them is smiling, loving, laughing, smart as all get-out... These are women who work in our inner cities, teaching, helping, serving. They don't make any money doing it." Her insight: real power comes from values, beliefs, purpose — not from external trappings.

## The Power of Gratitude

Shriver's daily practice: "Every morning when I open my eyes, and before my feet touch the ground, I thank God for the gift of my life." Scientific research backs this up — daily gratitude practice makes you "a happier, more hopeful person."

## Sixty Life Lessons for My Birthday

A numbered list of life advice drawn from her experience. Includes: "Don't call me an empty nester" (reframe loss as opportunity), "It's okay — in fact, it's crucial — to grieve," "Go back to move forward," "We're all in this together," "One person can make a huge impact on you."
