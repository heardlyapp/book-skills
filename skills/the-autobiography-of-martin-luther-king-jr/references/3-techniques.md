# Techniques — The Autobiography of Martin Luther King, Jr.

## How to Lead a Movement (Based on MLK)

### Technique 1: The Nonviolent Direct Action Plan
King's four-step process: (1) Collection of the facts to determine whether injustices exist. (2) Negotiation. (3) Self-purification (training in nonviolence). (4) Direct action. Case: Birmingham — after negotiations failed, King led sit-ins, marches, and boycotts. "Nonviolent direct action seeks to create such a crisis and foster such a tension that a community which has constantly refused to negotiate is forced to confront the issue."

### Technique 2: The Jail as a Stage
King used imprisonment strategically. "I was arrested 30 times." Each arrest created moral drama. Case: Letter from Birmingham Jail was written on newspaper margins and toilet paper. It was smuggled out and published. The arrest of a Nobel laureate created national pressure.

### Technique 3: The Improvised Speech
The "I Have a Dream" speech was not in King's prepared text. Mahalia Jackson shouted "Tell them about the dream, Martin!" He put down his notes and preached. The technique: prepare thoroughly, then be ready to abandon your preparation when the moment demands it.

### Technique 4: The Coalition of Conscience
King built coalitions across racial and religious lines. "The goal of America is freedom." He brought together black churches, white liberals, labor unions, and Jewish organizations. Case: The March on Washington was a "coalition of conscience" — 250,000 people, black and white, religious and secular.

### Technique 5: The Mountaintop Sermon
King's final speech, April 3, 1968, in Memphis. He didn't know he would die the next day — but he knew he might. "I've been to the mountaintop. I've seen the Promised Land. I may not get there with you." The technique: speak as if it might be your last. Your audience will feel it.

### Technique 6: The Triple Evils Framework
King identified three interconnected evils: racism, poverty, and militarism. "These are the triple evils that are interrelated." The technique: don't fight one injustice alone — connect the dots. Case: The Poor People's Campaign linked racial justice to economic justice.

### Technique 7: The "Somebodyness" Affirmation
King often said: "I refuse to accept the idea that the 'isness' of man's present nature makes him morally incapable of reaching up for the eternal 'oughtness' that forever confronts him." The technique: never let people forget what they can become. "I have a dream" is a statement about future possibility, not current reality.
