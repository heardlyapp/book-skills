# 4 — Anti-Patterns

> Source: *The Road to Unfreedom* by Timothy Snyder — Chapters 1, 5, 6.

## Common Errors

### 1. The "Both Sides" Fallacy

**The trap:** "There are two sides to every story, and the truth is somewhere in the middle."

**Why it fails:** This assumes good faith on both sides. When one side is actively destroying the concept of truth, meeting them "in the middle" is surrendering.

### 2. The "Everyone Does It" Normalization

**The trap:** "All countries do propaganda. What's so special about Russia?"

**Why it fails:** The goal is to normalize the behavior so that no one responds. Yes, all countries have propaganda. Not all countries make it their central strategic doctrine to destroy factuality.

### 3. Responding to Every Lie

**The trap:** "If I can just debunk this one claim, people will see the truth."

**Why it fails:** The goal of the disinformation campaign is to exhaust you. You cannot debunk every lie. Choose your battles.

> **Case: The Izborsk Club Manifesto** (Chapter 5): "Facts are information technologies from the West, and to destroy factuality is to destroy the West." This was stated openly. The goal is not to win arguments — it's to eliminate the grounds on which arguments can be settled.
> **Key takeaway**: When the opponent openly declares war on factuality, don't respond with more facts. Restrategize.

> **Case: The MH17 Seven Explanations** (Chapter 5): After shooting down MH17, Russia produced seven different explanations in rapid succession — a Ukrainian fighter jet, a ground-to-air missile, a false flag — each was false, each was replaced by another. The point was not to make any one explanation stick. The point was to create "creative ignorance" — a state where no explanation can be trusted and all are equally possible.

### 4. The "Inevitable Progress" Assumption

**The trap:** "Democracy will win because it's the natural direction of history."

**Why it fails:** This is the "politics of inevitability." It makes people passive. History is not a story of progress — it's a story of choices.

### 5. The "They Just Don't Know the Facts" Trap

Disinformation is not a knowledge problem. It's a trust problem. People who believe disinformation are not lacking facts — they are lacking trusted sources.
