# Key Principles

## 1. Get Out of the Building

"Your office contains no facts. The only facts are outside, with real customers." This is the most important principle. Every assumption must be tested with real people in the real world.

## 2. A Startup Is Not a Small Company

"A startup is a temporary organization designed to search for a repeatable and scalable business model." Most startups fail because they act like small versions of big companies — executing instead of learning.

## 3. Pivoting Is Progress

"A pivot is not a failure — it's a change in strategy based on what you've learned." Blank emphasizes that successful startups often pivot multiple times before finding the right model.

> **Case: The PayPal Pivot** (Chapter 4): PayPal started as a cryptography company, then shifted to money transfer for Palm Pilots, then became the online payment system we know. Each pivot was not a failure — it was learning about what customers actually needed.
> **Key takeaway**: The business model you start with is almost never the one you end with. The faster you can test and pivot, the faster you find the right model.

## 4. Sales Validate, Everything Else Is Opinion

"The only real validation is a customer paying money. Everything else — surveys, opinions, focus groups — is noise." Real validation means someone writing a check.

## 5. Customer Discovery Never Ends

"Even after product launch, you must continue to discover what customers need." The process is not linear — it's a continuous cycle of learning and adapting.

> **Case: The Feature Creep Trap** (Chapter 5): A startup added features based on what they thought customers wanted, without validating. The product became bloated and nobody used the new features. Meanwhile, competitors focused on the core need and won.
> **Key takeaway**: Every feature you build without customer validation is a gamble. Build only what customers have explicitly asked for — and validated they will use.
