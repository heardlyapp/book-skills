# Principles from Space

## I. Aim to Be a Zero

The book's most famous principle. In any team, do not try to be a hero. Aim to be the person who does not create problems.

**Case: The NASA Culture.** At NASA, the worst thing you can do is create a problem that distracts the team. A "zero" is someone who does their job without needing attention, without causing delays.

**Case: The Ego-Free Astronaut.** Hadfield learned that astronauts who think they are special are dangerous. Space does not care about your ego.

## II. Sweat the Small Stuff

In space, small mistakes kill. Hadfield learned to check everything.

**Case: The Checklist Obsession.** Astronauts use checklists for everything — even brushing their teeth. The checklist is not about being incapable. It is about being sure.

**Case: The Rag Near the Vent.** A janitor who leaves a rag near a vent could cause a fire that kills the crew. Hadfield learned to respect every detail.

## III. What's the Next Thing That Could Kill Me?

Hadfield's question for every situation. Not from fear — from preparedness.

**Case: The Soyuz Re-Entry.** Hadfield and his crew simulated every possible Soyuz re-entry failure. They knew what to do if the capsule depressurized, if the parachute failed, if the retrorockets misfired.

**Case: Fire on Mir.** In 1997, a fire broke out on the Mir space station. The crew had trained for it. They knew what to do. They survived.

## IV. Competence Breeds Confidence

Hadfield did not become confident by believing in himself. He became confident by being prepared.

**Case: The First Spaceflight.** Hadfield was terrified before his first launch. But he had trained for every scenario. He trusted the training. The confidence came from preparation, not from personality.

**Case: The Unflappable Commander.** As commander of the ISS, Hadfield was responsible for a crew of six. The confidence of the crew came from knowing that their commander was prepared.
