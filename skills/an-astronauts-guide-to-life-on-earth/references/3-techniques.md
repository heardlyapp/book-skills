# Practical Techniques

## Technique 1: The Pre-Mortem

Before any mission, NASA runs a "pre-mortem": imagine the mission has failed. What went wrong? Then prepare for those scenarios.

**How to do it:** Before starting any project, ask: "If this fails, what will have caused it?" Then address those causes proactively.

**Case: The Soyuz Simulation.** Hadfield's crew simulated every failure: depressurization, fire, parachute failure, landing in water. They were ready for everything because they had rehearsed the worst.

**Case: The Everyday Pre-Mortem.** Hadfield applies this to daily life: before a trip, ask "What could go wrong?" Then pack accordingly.

## Technique 2: The Checklist

Astronauts use checklists for everything. Hadfield adopted this habit.

**How to do it:** For any critical task, write a checklist. Do not rely on memory. Check each step. The checklist is not a sign of weakness.

**Case: The Memory Failure.** Hadfield describes a time when he forgot a critical step because he was distracted. After that, he used checklists religiously.

## Technique 3: The Elevator Speech

Hadfield learned to explain complex ideas in simple terms.

**How to do it:** Practice explaining your work to someone who knows nothing about it. If you cannot explain it simply, you do not understand it well enough.

**Case: The Media Interviews.** Hadfield became one of the most effective communicators in space because he practiced explaining complex concepts simply.
