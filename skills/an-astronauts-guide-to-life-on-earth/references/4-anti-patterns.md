# Mistakes and Lessons — Anti-Patterns

## Anti-Pattern 1: Thinking You Are Special

The most dangerous attitude in space. Astronauts who think they are special make mistakes.

**Case: The Ego Problem.** Hadfield describes astronauts who complained about assignments, who thought they deserved better. They were removed from crews.

**Case: The Humble Best.** The best astronauts are the most humble. They know that space is bigger than they are.

## Anti-Pattern 2: Not Asking for Help

In NASA culture, asking for help is a sign of strength, not weakness.

**Case: The Reluctant Student.** Hadfield initially did not want to ask his Russian instructors for help with language. He learned that asking was better than failing.

**Case: The Solo Hero.** The astronaut who tries to do everything alone is a danger to the crew. Teamwork means asking for help.

## Anti-Pattern 3: Ignoring Small Problems

In space, small problems become big problems if ignored.

**Case: The Leaking Valve.** A small leak in a valve could lead to a catastrophic failure. Hadfield learned to address problems immediately.

**Case: The Cracked Window.** When a tiny crack appeared in a window on the ISS, the crew reported it immediately. It was fixed.

## Anti-Pattern 4: Forgetting Why You Started

Hadfield returned to Earth a different person. The danger: forgetting the perspective that space gave him.

**Case: The Post-Flight Letdown.** Many astronauts struggle after returning. The intense focus of a mission is gone. Hadfield worked to maintain the perspective he gained in space.
