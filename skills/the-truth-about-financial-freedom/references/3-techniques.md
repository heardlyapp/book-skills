# Techniques: Practical Money Moves

## Technique 1: The 24-Hour Rule

For any non-essential purchase over $50, wait 24 hours before buying. Put it in a shopping cart and walk away. Most impulse purchases feel unnecessary after a night's sleep. For purchases over $500, wait a week.

Chilton explains that this works because the dopamine spike of "I want this" peaks immediately and fades fast. By inserting a delay, you let System Two (rational brain) catch up with System One (impulse brain). The 24-hour gap is enough for most impulses to die natural deaths.

> **Case: The 24-Hour Test** ("Plastic Surgery"): Chilton describes a reader experiment where participants committed to waiting 24 hours before any non-essential purchase. After one month, 70% reported spending less without feeling deprived. The key finding: they didn't want most things after the wait — the desire was temporary, but the debt would have been permanent.
> **Key takeaway:** Inserting a delay between impulse and action eliminates most unnecessary purchases.

## Technique 2: Cash Envelope System

Withdraw your budgeted amount for discretionary categories (eating out, entertainment, clothing) in cash at the start of the month. Put each category in a labeled envelope. When the envelope is empty, you're done spending in that category. No exceptions.

The mechanism isn't about budgeting — it's about making the pain of paying real again. Counting cash, watching the stack shrink, and physically running out creates a natural spending governor that no app can replicate.

## Technique 3: The Raise Rule

Whenever you get a raise, bonus, or tax refund, immediately divert 60-70% to savings and investments before you adjust your lifestyle. The remaining 30-40% is your reward. This prevents the most insidious form of wealth destruction: lifestyle inflation.

Chilton's math: if you get a 5% raise and spend 4% of it, your spending grows almost as fast as your income. Over a 10-year career, your lifestyle inflates to consume nearly all of your income growth. By banking 60-70% of every increase, your savings grow exponentially while your lifestyle grows modestly.

## Technique 4: Automate Everything

Set up automatic transfers on payday: a fixed amount to savings, a fixed amount to investments, automatic bill payments. The key insight is that every manual step is a point of failure. If you have to remember to transfer money, you'll find excuses. If you have to log in to pay a bill, you'll be late. If you have to decide to invest, you'll procrastinate.

Chilton's observation: the people with the most money aren't the ones who made the best investment decisions. They're the ones who automated saving and then forgot about it for 20 years.

## Technique 5: The Spending Journal

For one month, write down every single thing you spend money on. No judgment, no changes — just observation. Every coffee, every subscription, every impulse buy. Most people have no idea where their money actually goes. The journal reveals the gap between "I think I spend" and "I actually spend."

> **Case: The Hidden Subscription** ("A Worthwhile Hassle"): Chilton recounts a reader who discovered $340/month in automatic subscriptions she'd forgotten about — a gym membership she didn't use, a streaming service she'd cancelled (or thought she had), a magazine subscription from a promotional offer. She didn't need to budget — she just needed to cancel things she was already paying for.
> **Key takeaway:** Before optimizing your budget, cancel everything you're already paying for but not using. This is the easiest "raise" you'll ever get.

## Technique 6: Dollar-Cost Averaging

Invest a fixed amount at regular intervals regardless of market conditions. When prices are low, you buy more shares. When prices are high, you buy fewer. Over time, this smooths out volatility and removes the impossible task of predicting market movements.

Chilton's key point: dollar-cost averaging isn't about maximizing returns — it's about **removing emotion from investing**. You don't need to decide when to buy. The system decides for you. And over decades, systems beat decisions every time.
