# Anti-Patterns: What NOT to Do

## ❌ Trying to Time the Market

"I'll get in when it drops." "I'll sell before the crash." "This time is different."

Chilton is blunt: nobody can consistently predict short-term market movements — not hedge fund managers, not algorithms, not financial TV pundits. Every study of market timing shows that timers underperform buy-and-hold investors. The ones who got lucky once try again and give it back. The evidence is overwhelming.

> **Case: The Dot-Com Timer** ("When Average Isn't"): Chilton profiles an investor who sold all his holdings in 1996 because the market "felt too high." He waited on the sidelines through 1997 (+33%), 1998 (+28%), and 1999 (+21%), watching the market triple. Finally bought back in early 2000 — right before the crash. He lost more waiting than he would have lost in the crash.
> **Key takeaway:** Time in the market beats timing the market. Every study, every time, every decade.

## ❌ Chasing Hot Investment Tips

"Did you hear about this stock?" "My cousin's friend made a killing on crypto." "This newsletter has a 90% success rate."

Chilton's response: the people selling tips are making money selling tips, not from the investments themselves. If the tip were real, they wouldn't be selling it to you for $29/month. By the time a "hot tip" reaches you, the professional investors have already priced it in.

## ❌ Using Consumer Debt for Consumption

Lines of credit, credit cards, and personal loans used for spending are wealth destroyers. Chilton is emphatic: "The banks market them as flexibility. They're really anchors."

> **Case: The Line of Credit Spiral** ("All Too Willing to Lend a Hand"): Chilton describes a couple who used their home equity line of credit to "consolidate" credit card debt. They felt relieved for about three months. Then they ran up the credit cards again. Now they had credit card debt *and* a larger line of credit balance. The line of credit didn't solve their problem — it doubled it.
> **Key takeaway:** Consolidation without behavior change is just rearranging deck chairs on the Titanic.

## ❌ Keeping Up with the Joneses

You don't know their finances. That new car might be leased. That vacation might be on a credit card. Their retirement might be empty. Their "wealth" might be 100% borrowed. Chilton's rule: compare your bank account to your goals, not your neighbor's lifestyle.

## ❌ Waiting for the "Right Time"

"I'll invest when I have more money." "I'll start saving when I get a raise." "I'll make a budget next month."

Procrastination is the most expensive financial mistake. A dollar saved at 25 is worth roughly 10x more than a dollar saved at 45 (at 7% compound growth). Starting late doesn't just cost you the money — it costs you the growth of that money over the years you wasted.

## ❌ Making Decisions Based on Fear or Greed

The market drops → you panic-sell → you lock in losses. The market soars → you FOMO-buy → you buy at the peak. Chilton's observation: emotional investors consistently underperform the very funds they invest in, because they buy high and sell low. The best investment strategy is boring.

> **Case: The Panic Seller** ("Up and Down and All Around"): Chilton recounts the 2008 financial crisis. The market dropped 38%. Investors who sold in October 2008 locked in catastrophic losses. Those who held (and kept buying) recovered everything by 2010 and were significantly ahead by 2015. The difference wasn't investment skill — it was the ability to sit still.
> **Key takeaway:** Your stomach for volatility matters more than your investment strategy. If you can't hold through a 30% drop, you shouldn't own stocks at all.

## ❌ Overcomplicating Your Finances

You don't need 12 different accounts, 8 funds, and a hedge fund allocation. You need: a high-interest savings account (emergency fund), one or two broad-market index funds in a tax-advantaged account (retirement), low-cost term insurance, and a will. Complexity is costly — in fees, in time, and in cognitive load.

## ❌ Ignoring the Small Stuff

"A coffee a day won't matter." Actually, $5/day invested at 7% for 30 years = ~$190,000. The unused gym membership that's $50/month = $57,000 over 30 years. Small leaks sink big ships. The small stuff compounds — on both sides of the ledger.
