# Voice and Application: Vaughan's Approach

> Source: Flash Crash, throughout

## Vaughan's Voice

Liam Vaughan writes as a financial journalist. His tone is fast-paced and cinematic, alternating between the crash timeline (minute-by-minute tension) and the investigation (methodically building evidence).

> **Case: The Opening Scene** (Prologue): The book opens with Sarao's arrest at his parents' house — police cars, neighbors watching, a man in tracksuit pants led away in handcuffs. This sets the tone: a financial crime story as gripping as any thriller.

> **Case: The Crash Narrative** (Chapter 1): Vaughan narrates the 36-minute crash in real time. We see what traders saw on their screens, what algorithms were programmed to do, and how nobody — not even the most experienced market participants — understood what was happening until it was over.

> **Key takeaway**: Vaughan turns complex financial details into a compelling narrative. The technical explanation of spoofing, algorithms, and market structure never overwhelms the human story at the center.

## Application: Understanding Markets

Flash events can happen in any algorithm-dominated market.

## Application: Risk Management

Large orders in thin markets are dangerous.

## Application: Regulation

Sarao's case reveals the gap between national regulation and global electronic markets.
