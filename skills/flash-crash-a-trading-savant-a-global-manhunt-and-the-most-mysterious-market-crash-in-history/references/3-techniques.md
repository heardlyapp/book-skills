# Techniques: The Investigation and Extradition

> Source: Flash Crash, Chapters 6-9

## The Hunt Begins

After the Flash Crash, the CFTC and FBI launched parallel investigations. For years, the true source of the spoofing orders remained hidden behind multiple brokerage accounts and international borders.

> **Case: The IP Address Breakthrough** (Chapter 6): Investigators traced suspicious trading to a single IP address. It led to 31 Hounslow Avenue, London — Sarao's parents' house. After a global manhunt, the most wanted man in finance was found in a suburban bedroom.

> **Case: The CME's Early Warning** (Chapter 5): The CME had flagged Sarao years before the crash. They fined him and limited his activity, but their authority was limited — they could ban him from their exchange but couldn't prosecute or publicize his identity.

> **Key takeaway**: In the digital age, every trade leaves a trail. The challenge is assembling the trail across fragmented jurisdictions and regulatory bodies.

## The Arrest and Extradition

Sarao was arrested in 2015. The extradition fight lasted years.

> **Case: The Extradition Battle** (Chapter 8): Sarao's lawyers argued he should be tried in the UK. The US claimed jurisdiction because the CME (where the E-mini traded) was in Chicago. This legal gray area — where does a digital crime occur? — was fought for years before the US won.

> **Case: The UK Prison Experience** (Chapter 8): Sarao spent months in Wandsworth prison, a notoriously harsh facility. The experience broke him mentally and physically, contributing to his decision to plead guilty.

> **Key takeaway**: Financial crime crosses borders instantly. Legal systems don't. Sarao fell into the gap between jurisdictions, and the gap nearly crushed him.
