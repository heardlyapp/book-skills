# Principles: Spoofing and Market Manipulation

> Source: Flash Crash, Chapters 3-5

## How Spoofing Works

Sarao placed large sell orders he never intended to execute. These created the appearance of massive supply, driving prices down. He then bought at the lower price and canceled the fake orders.

> **Case: The SEFC Algorithm** (Chapter 3): Sarao named his software SEFC — Spoofing Exchange Feed Cancelling. It automated everything: place fake orders, wait for the market to move, cancel them, trade against the move. He could sleep while it ran.

> **Key takeaway**: Spoofing exploits a basic feature of electronic markets: visible order depth — the "order book" — creates expectations about price direction.

## Why Spoofing Worked

The E-mini market had thin liquidity relative to the order sizes Sarao used. A few hundred fake contracts could move the entire S&P 500 futures market.

> **Case: The Multiple Accounts** (Chapter 4): Sarao used accounts at multiple brokers. When one flagged his activity, he moved to another. The fragmented regulatory system — CFTC, SEC, CME, UK FCA — couldn't connect the dots.

> **Case: The Chicago Investigation** (Chapter 5): The CME (Chicago Mercantile Exchange) investigated Sarao but its authority was limited. They could kick him off the exchange but couldn't prosecute. This regulatory gap allowed him to continue for years.

> **Key takeaway**: Spoofing works because markets trust displayed liquidity — even when it's fake. Fragmented regulation makes catching spoofers extremely difficult.

## The Money

Sarao made $40-70 million. Most sat untouched in his accounts.
