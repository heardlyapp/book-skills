# Anti-Patterns: Mindfulness Traps

> Source: Search Inside Yourself, throughout

## Using Mindfulness to Avoid Feelings

Mindfulness is not about suppressing emotions. It's about being more present with them — including the difficult ones.

> **Case: The Spiritual Bypass Problem** (Chapter 5): Meng warns against using meditation to detach from pain. The goal is not to feel nothing but to feel everything without being overwhelmed. True equanimity includes experiencing difficult emotions, not escaping them.

> **Case: The Suppressed Manager** (Chapter 6): A manager who used meditation to stay "calm" — actually she was suppressing anger. When she learned to feel the anger mindfully instead of suppressing it, her leadership improved.

> **Key takeaway**: If you're meditating to avoid feelings, you're doing the opposite of the practice. Mindfulness is about presence with all experience.

## Expecting Immediate Results

The brain changes slowly. Consistency matters more than intensity.

> **Case: The Weekend Retreat Trap** (Chapter 1): Meng argues 10 minutes daily beats 8 hours once a month. Small, frequent doses create lasting change.

## Believing You Can't Do It

"I can't meditate — my mind is too busy." This objection misses the point entirely.

> **Case: The Busy Engineer** (Chapter 1): "I can't go to the gym — I'm too weak." The busy mind is the reason to practice.

> **Key takeaway**: The only requirement for mindfulness is the willingness to begin. One minute. Start there.
