# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *Democracy in America* — practical methods.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The Dissent Agenda

**When you suspect groupthink:** Before a decision, explicitly put the minority position on the agenda. "I'd like to hear from anyone who disagrees with the consensus." Tocqueville noted that the legal profession's practice of hearing both sides protects against majority tyranny.

### 2. The Association Launch

**When you face a problem too big for individual action:** Find three people who share the concern. Call a meeting (anywhere). Make a plan. Start a committee. Send a letter. Create a group. "When individuals in a democracy feel a common need, they must learn to associate."

### 3. The Local Reframe

**When power is too centralized:** Ask: "Can this decision be made closer to the people it affects?" The township system taught Americans self-government. Push authority down.

### 4. The Religion Audit

**When materialism has taken over:** Ask: "What do we value beyond money and comfort?" What aspiration would survive if everyone had enough? If nothing remains, the society has lost its moral compass.

### 5. The Soft Despotism Checklist

**When the state (or employer) is providing everything:**
1. Are you being cared for or controlled?
2. Can you easily leave or are you trapped by comfort?
3. Do you still practice the habits of self-governance or has the state taken over?
4. Are there associations that check power or is the state the only power?

### 6. The Self-Interest Properly Understood Exercise

**When you need people to cooperate:** Show them how serving the common interest serves their own. Not through altruism but through enlightened self-interest. "The American doctrine is to follow the principle of interest properly understood."
