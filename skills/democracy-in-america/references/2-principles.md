# 2 — Key Principles

> Source: *Democracy in America* — both volumes.

## The 8 Principles

### 1. Dissent is a Vital Sign

The absence of disagreement is not health — it's the tyranny of the majority in its social form. In healthy systems, minority opinions can be expressed without punishment. The moment dissent goes silent, the system is in trouble.

### 2. The Art of Association is the Art of Freedom

"Among democratic peoples, all citizens are independent and weak. They can achieve almost nothing by themselves. None of them can force their fellows to help. They all therefore become powerless if they do not learn to help each other freely."

### 3. Keep Things Local

Municipal institutions are "the strength of free nations." When decisions are made close to the people affected, citizens develop the habit of self-governance. Centralization is efficient; it is also deadly to liberty.

### 4. Self-Interest Properly Understood

Americans, Tocqueville observed, have an "enlightened self-interest." They believe that serving the public interest is a form of serving their own. "The doctrine of self-interest well understood does not produce great acts of devotion, but it suggests small acts every day."

### 5. Law is Better Than Men

"Place the bench above the legislator." An independent judiciary, especially judicial review, is the most important institutional check on the majority. Lawyers, with their love of precedent and procedure, are a natural aristocratic element in democracy.

### 6. Religion is the First Political Institution

"Despotism may be able to do without faith, but liberty cannot." Religion tempers the American obsession with material wealth and restrains the unbridled pursuit of self-interest. "The safeguard of morality is religion, and the safeguard of religion is to believe in it."

### 7. Prosperity is Not the Same as Freedom

A nation can be prosperous and unfree. The pursuit of wealth, if it becomes the only purpose of society, makes citizens content with servitude. "What do you expect from a man who has spent his life buying and selling?"

### 8. The Future Belongs to the Free

Tocqueville's famous final comparison: "There are two great peoples who, starting from different points, seem to advance toward the same end: the Russians and the Anglo-Americans. One has the means of freedom; the other the means of servitude."
