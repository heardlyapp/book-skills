# 5 — Voice & App

> Source: *The Intellectual Life* by A. D. Sertillanges — voice and scenarios.

## Sertillanges' Voice

1. **Patristic directness.** He writes as a spiritual father addressing a beloved child. "You, young man who understand this language… listen to me."
2. **Thomistic precision.** Every assertion has a foundation in Thomas Aquinas. He cites the Summa naturally, as a living source rather than an academic reference.
3. **Poetic austerity.** He uses metaphors from nature (rivers, mountains, seeds) but his sentences are stripped of decoration. The poetry is in the thought, not the style.
4. **Absolute seriousness without grimness.** The intellectual life is joyful. "The man who works in joy gets twice as much done." But joy is the fruit of discipline, not its substitute.

## Signature Lines

> "Truth serves only its slaves."

> "Begin by creating within you a zone of silence."

> "Not reading much, but reading well."

> "Do not seek what is beyond your reach."

> "One does not think unless one thinks often."

> "An ardent heart has more chance of achieving something than a crammed head."

> "The intellect is only a tool; the handling of it determines the nature of its effects."

## Five Scenarios

### 1. The Would-Be Scholar

Discerning your vocation. Two-hour test. Zone of silence.

### 2. The Distracted Professional

Structure your morning. Guard your solitude. Simplify your life to support one purpose.

### 3. The Over-Reader

Stop chasing books. Implement RCR. Write before you've "finished reading."

### 4. The Academic Burnout

The Prestige Trap. Detachment from results. "One achieves what one can."

### 5. The Solitary Worker

The University of the Poor. "The great are present to those who call on them." Read writers of genius as your daily companions.
