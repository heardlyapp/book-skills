# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *The Intellectual Life: Its Spirit, Conditions, Methods* by A. D. Sertillanges, O.P. — Chapters 4, 7, 8.

## Practical Methods

### 1. The RCR Method (Read → Copy → Reflect)

Sertillanges' three-step for assimilative reading:
1. **Read** a passage attentively
2. **Copy** key points by hand (not typed)
3. **Reflect** — add your own thoughts, connections, criticisms

This is not note-taking — it's digestion. The physical act of writing embeds the thought in the body as well as the mind.

> **Case: The Monk's Copying Practice** (Chapter 7): Sertillanges points to the monastic tradition of copying texts by hand. The physical act does more for retention than any machine. "Writing is a second reading, and a profound one." The RCR method is a secular adaptation of this ancient practice — slow, deliberate, transformative.
> **Key takeaway**: Speed is the enemy of assimilation. If you want to remember, write by hand.

### 2. The Four Kinds of Reading (Chapter 7)

Know which mode you're in:
1. **Exploratory** — browsing to survey a field; fast, loose, no notes
2. **Assimilative** — RCR method for core texts
3. **Creative consultation** — targeted reading for a specific output problem
4. **Reading for pleasure** — "innocent relaxation" that keeps the mind supple

> **Case: The Student Who Read Everything Alike** (Chapter 7): Sertillanges describes the student who treats every book as equally important — reading Plato's Republic with the same attention as a newspaper. The result: nothing is digested, nothing is retained. The student confuses quantity with quality. "Not reading much, but reading well."
> **Key takeaway**: 90% of what you read should be exploratory (fast, low-retention). Only 10% needs the RCR treatment. Choose carefully which 10%.

### 3. The Morning First-Hour Rule

The first hour of the day belongs to your deepest work. Before correspondence, conversation, or news — turn your mind to the thing that matters most. "The morning gives the keynote to the day."

### 4. The Two-Hour Minimum

"Most great men followed some calling. Many have declared that two hours suffice for an intellectual career." If those two hours are jealously guarded, they are sufficient.

### 5. The Plenitude Pulse

There are "privileged days" when everything flows. Recognize them. When they come, extend your work hours. When they don't, maintain the minimum. "The man who works in joy gets twice as much done."

### 6. Note Classification System

- **One notebook per major subject** (or loose-leaf)
- **Write on one side only** (for cutting and rearranging)
- **Index by topic, not by book** — your own system
- **Periodic review** — accumulated notes are only useful if re-read

### 7. The Finish Rule

"Do not stop work without leaving a fixed direction." When you end a session, know exactly where you'll begin tomorrow. This prevents the loss of momentum and the paralysis of the blank page.

> **Case: The Writer's Morning Practice** (Chapter 8): Sertillanges advises finishing each session by preparing the material for the next — arranging notes, writing the opening sentence, leaving "a thread to catch hold of." Without this, the next day begins in confusion and wasted energy.
> **Key takeaway**: A session is not finished until the next session has been prepared.
