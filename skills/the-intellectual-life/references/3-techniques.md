# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *The Intellectual Life* by A. D. Sertillanges — chapters 4, 7, 8.

## Practical Methods

### 1. The RCR Method (Read → Copy → Reflect)

Sertillanges' three-step reading method:
1. **Read** a passage attentively
2. **Copy** the key points (by hand, not by typing)
3. **Reflect** on them — add your own thoughts, connections, criticisms
This is not note-taking — it's assimilation. The physical act of writing embeds the thought.

### 2. The Four Kinds of Reading (Chapter 7)

Know which mode you're in:
1. **Exploratory** — browsing to survey a field; fast, loose, no notes
2. **Assimilative** — RCR method: read, copy, reflect for core texts
3. **Creative consultation** — reading a specific text for a specific output problem; targeted, practical
4. **Reading for pleasure** — the "innocent relaxation" that keeps the mind supple

### 3. The Morning First-Hour Rule

The first hour of the day belongs to your deepest work. Before correspondence, before conversation, before news — turn your mind to the thing that matters most. "The morning gives the keynote to the day."

### 4. The Two-Hour Minimum

Even if you have only two hours a day for intellectual work, that is enough — if those two hours are jealously guarded and ardently used. "Most great men followed some calling. Many have declared that two hours suffice for an intellectual career."

### 5. The Plenitude Pulse

There are "privileged days" when everything flows. Learn to recognize them. When they come, extend your work hours. When they don't, maintain the minimum. "The man who works in joy gets twice as much done."

### 6. Note Classification System

Notes are useless if they can't be found. Sertillanges recommends:
- **One notebook per major subject** (or a loose-leaf system)
- **Write on one side only** (for cutting and rearranging)
- **Index by topic, not by book** — your own system, not the author's
- **Periodic review** of accumulated notes

### 7. The Finish Rule

"Do not stop work without leaving a fixed direction." When you end a session, know exactly where you'll begin tomorrow. This prevents the loss of momentum and the paralysis of the blank page.
