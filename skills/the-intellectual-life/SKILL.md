### Philosophy — 5 Rules to Remember

1. **Truth serves only its slaves.** Knowledge demands an initial outlay most are unwilling to pay. The price is your whole life, not your spare time.
2. **Everything in you must become intellectual.** Not just your brain — your body, your habits, your home, your friendships, your sleep.
3. **Not reading much, but reading well.** A page assimilated is worth a library skimmed. Digestion over accumulation.
4. **The intellect cannot produce without solitude.** Create a "zone of silence" around your work. Society, unmastered, is the enemy of depth.
5. **Do not seek what is beyond your reach.** Accept your measure. Your vocation is irreplaceable. Play your part, not another's.

### Core Framework Quick Reference

- **The Vocation** — Not a self-elected title but a sacred call discerned through instinct, aptitude, and Providence. You don't choose it; you recognize it.
- **Studiousness (Temperance of Knowledge)** — Know when to pursue knowledge and when to stop. Not enough = negligence. Too much = vain curiosity.
- **The Three Solitudes** — (1) Physical solitude (quiet place). (2) Intellectual solitude (freedom from conventional thinking). (3) Interior silence (stillness before truth).
- **Digestive Reading** — Four modes: (1) exploratory browsing, (2) assimilative RCR (read, copy, reflect), (3) creative consultation for output, (4) reading for pleasure.
- **The Writer's Discipline** — Write daily. Write despite mood. Finish what you start. "Set your work before you like a task and accomplish it."
- **The Two-Hour Minimum** — Most great intellectuals held other jobs. Two dedicated hours daily, well used, is enough for a lifetime of intellectual work.

### Key Principles

1. **Simplify your life.** Cut possessions, distractions, social obligations. The intellectual needs a "scantily furnished dwelling."
2. **Guard your mornings.** The first hours are sacred. Do not let correspondence, visitors, or trivialities steal them.
3. **Read with pen in hand.** Books are not an end — they are fuel for your own thinking. React, excerpt, annotate.
4. **Do not pursue two hares.** Specialize within a broad foundation. Depth before breadth.
5. **Finish everything.** An incomplete work is a betrayal of the truth glimpsed but not delivered.
6. **Keep contact with life.** The intellectual does not live in an ivory tower. Action refreshes thought.
7. **Pray before you think.** Not religious ritual — but the disposition of humility before truth that transcends you.

### Anti-Pattern Summary

The central error the book exposes: **believing intellectual work is purely a mental exercise that can succeed without moral and physical discipline.** The intellect does not function in isolation from the soul. Character, body, and environment are not accessories — they are the foundation. Vain curiosity (eating without digesting), over-specialization (knowing everything about nothing), and ivory-tower isolation (cutting off life to protect thought) all destroy the very mind they pretend to serve. See `references/4-anti-patterns.md`.

**Invocation Test** — a user says: "I'm a 30-year-old lawyer. I have a comfortable practice but I feel like I'm wasting my mind. I want to write serious philosophy. I have two hours a day, early mornings before work. Where do I start?"

→ Response: You have the most important asset — the two-hour minimum. Sertillanges says that is sufficient for a lifetime of intellectual work if those two hours are faithfully guarded and ardently used. Here's your roadmap:

1. **Vocation check** — Maintain those two hours for six months. If you can sustain it, the call is confirmed. Most people can't. If you can, you're not a dilettante.
2. **Create your cell** — A dedicated physical space for intellectual work. Even a corner of a room. Not negotiable.
3. **Build the foundation** — Don't jump into writing philosophy yet. Follow the "sea by the streams" principle. Spend the first six months on foundational reading in the history of philosophy (Plato, Aristotle, Aquinas, Kant — at minimum). Plant small so you can grow big.
4. **The RCR habit** — Read one short passage. Copy key ideas by hand. Reflect and add your own thoughts. Do this for 45 minutes. Spend 15 minutes reviewing yesterday's notes. Spend the remaining hour on your actual reading.
5. **The finish rule** — Never end a session without knowing where you'll begin tomorrow. That first sentence tomorrow is the hardest — pre-write it in your notes.
