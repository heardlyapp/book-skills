# Che Guevara's Voice & Application Scenarios

> Source: The Motorcycle Diaries, all entries; Letter to his mother; Photographs

## Authorial Voice

Che Guevara writes as a 23-year-old medical student who becomes a writer along the way. His voice is:

- **Youthful and energetic**: The early entries are full of jokes, adventure, and irreverence. Che is a young man having the time of his life, aware of his own luck and good humor.
- **Observant and empathetic**: He notices details — the texture of a miner's hand, the eyes of a leper, the cold of the Andes night. His empathy is concrete, not abstract.
- **Growing in seriousness**: As the journey progresses, the tone deepens. The jokes become less frequent. The political analysis becomes more central.
- **Poetic and philosophical**: Che was widely read (Neruda, Marx, Darwin, Freud). The diaries are peppered with literary references, philosophical reflections, and poetic descriptions of landscape.
- **Surprisingly gentle**: The man who would become the ruthless guerrilla commander is, in the diaries, a gentle and idealistic young man. The contrast is famous and revealing.

### Classic Che Quotes (from The Motorcycle Diaries)

- "I turned 'I' into 'we.'"
- "The exploitation is unimaginable."
- "How can a person feel cold in a world that is so beautiful?"
- "This wandering around our America has changed me more than I thought."
- "If you have the capacity to look, the world is revealed."
- "The only true way to live is to believe in something bigger than yourself."

## Five Application Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Student Planning a "Meaningful" Gap Year
You want to travel, but you want your trip to matter. You're worried about being a tourist.
**Lesson**: Che's journey is the prototype of meaningful travel. Go with a plan but be ready to abandon it. Travel cheaply — not as a stunt but because poverty reveals reality. Serve somewhere. Don't just observe — participate. And keep a journal. The writing is what makes the experience permanent.

### Scenario 2: Activist Feeling Burned Out
You've been fighting for a cause for years. You're tired. You've lost touch with why you started.
**Lesson**: Re-read Che's early diary entries before he became Che. Remember the moment you first saw the thing that made you commit. That moment — of seeing clearly for the first time — is the fuel that can sustain you for a lifetime. The work is hard, but the vision must stay alive.

### Scenario 3: Privileged Person Wondering What to Do About Inequality
You're not poor. You know you benefit from an unjust system. You want to help but don't know how.
**Lesson**: Che was you — a privileged student who had never known real suffering. He didn't start as a revolutionary. He started as a traveler who let himself be changed by what he saw. The first step is not action — it's exposure. You must put yourself in a position to see what you have been protected from seeing. Then let the seeing change you.

### Scenario 4: Writer Interested in Travel Writing
You want to write about your travels. You're not sure how to make it more than a travelogue.
**Lesson**: Che's diary works because it's not about the places — it's about the transformation. The landscape is backdrop. The real story is the narrator's change. Write about what you see, but more importantly, write about what the seeing does to you. The reader should finish the book changed, not just informed.

### Scenario 5: Someone Questioning Their Career Path
You have a comfortable job but feel something is missing. You're not sure what your purpose is.
**Lesson**: Che started as a medical student — a career he chose for practical reasons. The journey revealed that medicine was not his calling; service to humanity was. The diaries show that purpose is not something you discover by thinking — it's something you discover by doing, by traveling, by meeting people whose lives challenge your assumptions.

> **Case: The Photographs** (Throughout)
> Che carried a camera and took hundreds of photographs during the journey. The images show a young man with an eye for beauty, suffering, and connection. There are photos of Alberto fixing the motorcycle, of indigenous women carrying children, of lepers playing soccer, of the Amazon river at sunset. The photographs add a visual dimension to the diary — they show what Che saw, but also how he saw: with empathy, humor, and a growing sense of purpose.
> **Key takeaway**: Che's photographs are a lesson in attention: he saw the beauty of the world even as he saw its suffering. The two were not contradictory — they were the same reality. The ability to hold both — beauty and injustice, joy and sorrow — is the mark of a complete human being.

> **Case: The Letter to His Mother** (Conclusion)
> Che ends the book with a letter to his mother, written on his 24th birthday in Caracas. He tells her he has changed, that he cannot return to the old life, that he must do something for Latin America. The letter is not a revolutionary manifesto — it is a son telling his mother that he has found his purpose. The personal and the political are one. The book ends not with a conclusion but with a beginning: the journey is over; the work is about to start.
> **Key takeaway**: The most important transformation in The Motorcycle Diaries is not political — it's personal. Che found his purpose. The political commitment followed from the personal awakening. Purpose is not something you invent — it's something you discover through exposure, vulnerability, and the willingness to be changed by what you encounter.
