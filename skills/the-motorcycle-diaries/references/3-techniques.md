# Witnessing Poverty — What Che Saw and How It Changed Him

> Source: The Motorcycle Diaries, Chapters 1, 2, 3, 5

## The Encounters That Mattered

Che's journey was structured around encounters with people whose suffering he could not ignore. Each encounter added a layer to his growing political consciousness.

### Key Encounters

| Encounter | Location | What Che Witnessed | Effect |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|--------|
| The copper miners | Chuquicamata, Chile | 10,000 miners working for pittance, owned by US company | Political awakening began |
| The indigenous woman | Andean highlands, Peru | An elderly Quechua woman sleeping in the cold with no shelter | Emotional shock — "this should not exist" |
| The communist couple | Peru | Intellectuals who gave him books and conversation | Intellectual framework for his emotions |
| The lepers | San Pablo, Peru | Patients isolated from society, treated as untouchables | Service as solidarity |

### The Inadequacy of Charity

Che was not satisfied with charity. He gave money when he had it, treated patients when he could, worked when the opportunity arose. But he saw that individual acts of kindness could not solve problems that were structural. A doctor could treat a patient and send them back to the same conditions that made them sick. Real change required changing the conditions themselves.

> **Case: The Old Woman on the Mountain** (Peru)
> In the high Andes, Che and Alberto encountered an elderly indigenous woman sleeping in the open air with no shelter from the freezing night. She had no family, no home, no resources. Che gave her his blanket and some money. But he wrote: "What good is my blanket against the cold of a whole continent? What good is my few pesos against the poverty of centuries?" The encounter was a turning point: charity is not enough. The system must change.
> **Key takeaway**: The moment Che realized that charity is not enough is the moment he stopped being a philanthropist and started becoming a revolutionary. Individual kindness cannot fix structural injustice. The question is: what do you do when you realize this?

> **Case: The Communist Scholar** (Peru)
> In Peru, Che met a Spanish exile and his wife who were communists. They gave Che books, talked to him about Marxism, and helped him understand the political structure behind the poverty he had been witnessing. Che had been reading Marx before the journey, but now the theory connected with lived experience. The scholar gave Che the language to describe what he had seen. The emotions found their intellectual home.
> **Key takeaway**: Che's political awakening was not just emotional — it was intellectual. The encounter with the Spanish communists gave him the framework to understand what he had experienced. Emotion without analysis leads to despair. Emotion with analysis leads to action.
