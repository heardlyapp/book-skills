# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *The Age of Napoleon* by Will & Ariel Durant — Books I-V.

## The Three Frameworks

### Framework A: The Revolution-to-Empire Arc (Books I-II, V)

The French Revolution began with Enlightenment ideals — liberty, equality, fraternity — and descended into the Terror, then military dictatorship, then empire. Durant traces this arc: the financial crisis of the Old Regime, the summoning of the Estates-General, the fall of the Bastille, the Declaration of the Rights of Man, the radicalization of the Revolution, the Reign of Terror, the corrupt Directory, and finally Napoleon's coup. Each phase consumed the previous one.

> **Case: The Revolution Eats Its Children (Chapter IV):** Danton, Robespierre, and Marat — the three great figures of the Revolution's radical phase — all died at the hands of the same Revolution they created. Marat was assassinated by Charlotte Corday in his bath. Danton was guillotined by Robespierre's Committee of Public Safety. Robespierre himself was guillotined by his former allies. The pattern: the revolution devours those who try to control it.

> **Case: Napoleon's Coup — The 18th Brumaire (Chapter V):** Napoleon returned from Egypt to a France exhausted by years of chaos. The Directory was corrupt and ineffective. Napoleon's coup was not a violent revolution — it was a seizure of power in a vacuum. The people accepted him because he promised order. Durant notes: after the Terror, France wanted stability more than liberty. Napoleon gave them stability, then took everything else.

### Framework B: Napoleon's Art of War (Chapters V, VII, VIII, XXXV-XXXVII)

Napoleon's military system was built on speed, concentration, and the destruction of enemy armies rather than the capture of territory. He centralized command, used the corps system for flexibility, and struck at the enemy's center of gravity. Austerlitz (1805) was his masterpiece. Russia (1812) was his catastrophe. Waterloo (1815) was the final lesson in what happens when speed meets immovable resistance.

> **Case: Austerlitz (Chapter VIII):** December 2, 1805. Napoleon faced the combined armies of Austria and Russia — numerically superior. He deliberately weakened his right flank to invite the allies to attack. When they took the bait and moved to envelop him, he struck through the weakened center with his main force, splitting the enemy army in two. The battle was over in hours. 15,000 allied dead. The Austro-Russian alliance collapsed. It was Napoleon's greatest victory.

> **Case: The Invasion of Russia (Chapter XXXV):** Napoleon invaded Russia in June 1812 with the largest army Europe had ever seen — over 600,000 men. The Russians refused to fight a decisive battle, retreating deeper into their territory. They burned everything behind them. By the time Napoleon reached Moscow in September, his army was already half gone. The city burned. Alexander refused to negotiate. The retreat in winter was a catastrophe: fewer than 40,000 men returned.

### Framework C: Napoleon the Reformer — The Lasting Legacy (Chapters VII, X, XI)

For all his military ambition, Napoleon's most enduring achievements were domestic. The Code Napoleon (1804) unified French law and became the model for legal systems across Europe and the world. The Concordat of 1801 restored peace with the Catholic Church. The educational reforms created the lycée system and centralized the university. The administrative system — prefects, councils, centralized bureaucracy — is still in place in France today.

> **Case: The Code Napoleon (Chapter VII):** Napoleon personally participated in drafting the Code — attending 57 of 84 sessions of the Council of State. The Code established equality before the law, freedom of religion, the right to property, and the abolition of feudalism. It was not perfect (women's rights were limited) — but it was a revolutionary document that spread across Europe. Two hundred years later, it remains the foundation of French civil law and influenced legal systems worldwide.
