# 2 — Key Principles

> Source: *The Age of Napoleon* by Will & Ariel Durant.

## The 7 Principles

### 1. Speed Is the Ultimate Weapon

Napoleon's central tactical insight: move faster than the enemy expects, arrive stronger than the enemy anticipates, strike before the enemy is ready. The Italian campaign (1796-1797) was won by marching, not fighting. His soldiers marched 30 miles a day when the standard was 12. Speed substitutes for mass.

> **Case: The Italian Campaign (Chapter V):** In 1796, the 26-year-old Napoleon took command of the French army in Italy — a demoralized, unpaid, starving force. Within weeks, he had defeated the Piedmontese and Austrians in six battles. His secret: relentless speed. He forced marches, attacked before his enemies could combine, and lived off the land. Soldiers who had been ready to mutiny became the most feared army in Europe.

### 2. The Revolution Consumed Its Children

The Terror did not end because the Revolution succeeded. It ended because the Revolution ran out of people to consume. When Robespierre was guillotined, the revolution turned on itself. Napoleon filled the vacuum.

### 3. Every Strength Pushed to Excess Becomes a Fatal Weakness

Napoleon's ambition was the engine of his rise. It was also the engine of his destruction. He could not stop. He did not know how to consolidate. After each victory, he demanded the next. This is the pattern of hubris that Durant traces across all of history.

### 4. Never Fight the Enemy's Strengths

Napoleon at Austerlitz won by choosing the battlefield. Napoleon in Russia lost by letting the enemy choose it. The Russians knew that fighting their own weather and distance was more effective than fighting the Grand Army.

### 5. Institutions Outlast Individuals

> **Case: The Concordat of 1801 (Chapter VII):** Napoleon made peace with the Catholic Church, ending a decade of conflict. The Concordat recognized Catholicism as the religion of the majority of French people, but the state controlled Church appointments. This pragmatic settlement lasted 104 years (until 1905). Napoleon was not religious — he was strategic. He understood that some institutions are too powerful to fight; they must be co-opted.

### 6. The Leader Sets the Moral Tone

Napoleon's camp was meritocratic — any soldier could rise to marshal. But his court became increasingly monarchical and sycophantic. As the court changed, so did his judgment. The people around him stopped telling him the truth because he stopped wanting to hear it.

### 7. Greatness Is a Judgment, Not a Fact

Durant presents Napoleon as neither hero nor villain, but as a force of nature — morally complex, historically transformative, personally tragic. The lesson: history does not grade on a curve. It evaluates the whole person, the whole era, the whole impact.
