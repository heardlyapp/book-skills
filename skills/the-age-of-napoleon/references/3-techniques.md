# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *The Age of Napoleon* by Will & Ariel Durant.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The Speed Advantage Calculation

Napoleon's tactical principle can be applied to any competitive situation: if you can move faster than your opponent, you can arrive with more force at the decisive point. In business: respond to opportunities faster than competitors. In negotiation: prepare faster than the other side. In execution: compress timelines mercilessly. Speed substitutes for resources.

> **Case: Marengo (Chapter VII):** In June 1800, Napoleon's army was scattered across the Alps. The Austrians attacked his separated corps. Napoleon rushed reinforcements to the sound of the guns, arriving at Marengo in the afternoon to turn a defeat into a victory. Speed of decision-making and speed of march saved the day.
> **Key takeaway**: When you are losing, speed in redeploying resources can turn the tide. The faster you can correct, the more likely you are to win.

> **Case: The Egyptian Debacle (Chapter V):** Napoleon invaded Egypt in 1798 with 35,000 soldiers and 167 scientists and scholars. The military campaign was ultimately a failure — Nelson destroyed the French fleet at the Nile, stranding Napoleon's army. But the scientific expedition was a triumph: the discovery of the Rosetta Stone, the Description de l'Égypte. Napoleon understood that building knowledge was as important as winning battles.
> **Key takeaway**: Even failed campaigns can produce lasting value if paired with intellectual ambition. Create something that survives even the failure of your primary objective.

In any conflict, identify the enemy's center of gravity — the thing that, if removed, causes the system to collapse. Napoleon targeted enemy armies, not enemy capitals. The destruction of the Austrian and Russian armies at Austerlitz mattered more than the capture of Vienna. Apply this: what is the single point of leverage in your current challenge?

### 3. The Battlefield Selection Protocol

Never fight on the enemy's terms. Napoleon chose the time, place, and conditions of every major battle. At Austerlitz, he chose the ground, weakened his own flank deliberately, and used the terrain to hide his main force. In Russia, he had no choice — the Russians refused to give battle on his terms. He lost.

### 4. The Meritocracy Lever

> **Case: The Marshals (Chapter VII):** Napoleon's marshals came from everywhere. Ney was a cooper's son. Murat was a tavern-keeper's son. Lannes was a dyer's apprentice. Junot was a law student. Napoleon promoted based on talent and courage, not birth. This created fierce loyalty and extraordinary performance. The principle: the best way to build a high-performing organization is to promote the best people regardless of their background.

### 5. The Self-Correction Mechanism

Napoleon lost the ability to self-correct because no one would tell him he was wrong. By 1812, his marshals knew the Russian campaign was a disaster before it began, but no one spoke. Durant notes that Napoleon's court became so sycophantic that the intelligence pipeline broke. The technique: create mechanisms for honest feedback that survive your success. A designated "truth-teller" role. Anonymous feedback. Structured dissent.
