# Criticisms and Limitations — Anti-Patterns

## Anti-Pattern 1: The Difference Principle Without the First Principle

Some readers accept the difference principle but ignore the priority of liberty. This leads to authoritarian conclusions Rawls would reject.

**Case: The Trade-Off Trap.** An authoritarian government argues: we need to restrict freedom of speech temporarily to achieve economic growth that will benefit the least advantaged. Rawls says no. Liberty is prior.

**Case: The Singapore Model.** Some point to Singapore as an example of the difference principle working: high growth, rising living standards for the poor, but limited political freedoms. Rawls would reject this as unjust because the first principle is violated.

## Anti-Pattern 2: The Original Position as a Real Event

The original position is a thought experiment, not a historical event. Treating it as real leads to confusion.

**Case: The "Who Signed?" Objection.** Critics ask: "I never signed any social contract. Why am I bound by it?" Rawls' answer: the original position is not a contract you sign. It is a way of testing whether principles are fair.

**Case: The "Impossible" Objection.** "No one can actually forget their identity." True. That is why the veil of ignorance is a thought experiment, not a psychological technique.

## Anti-Pattern 3: The Unlimited Difference Principle

The difference principle does not say all inequalities are justified if they benefit the least advantaged.

**Case: The CEO Pay.** A CEO earns 300 times the average worker. Could this benefit the least advantaged? Possibly — if the CEO's talents are so scarce that without this incentive, the company would fail and jobs would be lost. But the burden of proof is on the inequality.

**Case: The Incentive Argument.** The difference principle does not automatically justify every inequality claimed to be an "incentive." The question is always: does this inequality actually make the least advantaged better off than they would be without it?
