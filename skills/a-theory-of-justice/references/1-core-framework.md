# Justice as Fairness — Core Framework

Rawls' central idea: the principles of justice are those that free and rational persons would accept in an initial position of equality.

## The Original Position

The original position is not a historical event. It is a thought experiment designed to make the constraints on choice fair.

**Case: The Veil of Ignorance.** The parties in the original position do not know: their place in society, their class position, their natural talents, their intelligence, their strength, their conception of the good, their psychological tendencies, their race, their sex, or their generation. They know: general facts about human society, the principles of economics, the basis of social organization, the laws of human psychology.

**Case: The Maximin Rule.** Rawls argues that the parties would choose principles according to the maximin rule: maximize the minimum share. They would choose the arrangement that makes the worst-off person as well off as possible, because behind the veil they might be that person.

## The Two Principles

**First Principle:** Each person has an equal right to the most extensive scheme of equal basic liberties compatible with a similar scheme for all. These liberties include: political liberty (the right to vote), freedom of speech and assembly, liberty of conscience, freedom of thought, freedom of the person, the right to hold personal property, and freedom from arbitrary arrest.

**Second Principle:** Social and economic inequalities are to be arranged so that they are both (a) to the greatest benefit of the least advantaged (the difference principle), and (b) attached to offices and positions open to all under conditions of fair equality of opportunity.

**Case: The Priority Rules.** The first principle is prior to the second. A less extensive liberty cannot be justified by greater economic benefits. Equal liberty cannot be traded for economic growth.

**Case: The Difference Principle Explained.** The difference principle allows inequality, but only if it benefits the least advantaged. A doctor earning more than a janitor is just only if the inequality makes the janitor better off than she would be under strict equality. The logic: incentives for doctors produce more medical services, which benefits everyone, including the janitor.
