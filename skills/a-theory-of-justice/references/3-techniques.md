# Methods and Applications — Techniques

## Technique 1: The Original Position

Use the original position as a decision-making tool: when considering a policy, ask whether you would agree to it if you did not know your own position.

**How to do it:** Imagine you must choose the rules of your society, organization, or family — but you will be assigned a random position within it. What rules would you choose? This eliminates self-serving bias.

**Case: The Veil for Policy.** When evaluating a tax policy, ask: would I support this if I did not know whether I would be wealthy or poor? Most people become more supportive of progressive taxation behind the veil.

**Case: The Organizational Design.** A manager designing a bonus system can use the original position: if you did not know whether you would be the top performer or the bottom performer, what system would you design? This tends to produce fairer systems.

## Technique 2: Reflective Equilibrium

Rawls' method for arriving at justified principles: move back and forth between general principles and specific judgments until they are consistent.

**How to do it:** Start with a general principle. Test it against specific cases. If the principle does not match your judgment about the case, revise either the principle or the judgment. Continue until equilibrium is reached.

**Case: The Prisoner.** You believe in the principle "punishment should be proportional to the crime." But you consider a case: a mother steals food to feed her starving child. Your judgment: she should not be punished. You must revise either the principle (add exceptions) or your judgment (accept that she must be punished). Reflective equilibrium is the process of getting them consistent.

**Case: The Draft.** You believe in equal liberty for all. But you consider a military draft in wartime. Your judgment: conscription is sometimes justified. You must reconcile the principle with the judgment, perhaps by adding a condition about national security.

## Technique 3: The Maximin Rule

When facing uncertainty, choose the option that maximizes the minimum possible outcome.

**How to do it:** List the options. For each option, identify the worst possible outcome. Choose the option whose worst outcome is better than the worst outcome of any other option.

**Case: Career Choice.** If you could be a lawyer (high income but long hours) or a teacher (moderate income but stable), behind the veil you would choose the option with the better minimum outcome. The maximin rule favors the teacher.

**Case: Investment Strategy.** Behind the veil, you would not choose a risky investment that could make you rich or leave you destitute. You would choose the strategy that ensures you have enough regardless of outcomes.
