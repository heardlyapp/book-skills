# Principles of a Just Society

## I. Justice Is Not Efficiency

Rawls argues against utilitarianism, which holds that the just society is the one that maximizes total happiness. Rawls: a society can maximize total happiness by enslaving a minority. That would be efficient — but it would be unjust.

**Case: The Utilitarian Trade-Off.** If an enslaved minority produces enough happiness for the majority, utilitarianism would permit it. Rawls argues that the original position would reject this because no one would agree to be a slave if they did not know they would be a slave.

**Case: The Distribution Problem.** Utilitarianism cares only about the total. It does not care about how happiness is distributed. Rawls argues that distribution matters — and justice requires that the worst-off share be maximized.

## II. Fair Equality of Opportunity

Fair equality of opportunity goes beyond formal equality (everyone can apply for the job). It requires that everyone have the same opportunity to develop their talents.

**Case: The Class Difference.** Under formal equality, a child born to wealthy parents and a child born to poor parents both have the right to apply to Harvard. But their actual opportunity is not equal. Fair equality of opportunity would require that society compensate for disadvantages of birth.

**Case: The Talent Lottery.** Rawls argues that natural talents are arbitrary from a moral point of view. No one deserves to be born with superior intelligence any more than they deserve to be born into a wealthy family. The difference principle uses the distribution of talents for the benefit of everyone.

## III. The Priority of Liberty

Equal liberty is the most important principle. It cannot be sacrificed for economic gain.

**Case: The Trade-Off Rawls Rejects.** An authoritarian government might argue that restricting political freedom will lead to faster economic growth, which will eventually benefit everyone. Rawls rejects this. Liberty is prior.

**Case: The Justification of Liberty.** Liberty can only be restricted for the sake of liberty. A less extensive liberty must strengthen the total system of liberty shared by all. A less than equal liberty must be acceptable to those with the lesser liberty.

## IV. Civil Disobedience

Rawls provides a theory of when civil disobedience is justified: public, nonviolent, conscientious acts against law, appealing to the shared sense of justice.

**Case: The Conditions.** Civil disobedience is justified when: (1) it targets a substantial and clear injustice, (2) legal remedies have been exhausted, and (3) it does not lead to a breakdown of respect for law.

**Case: The Appeal.** Civil disobedience is not self-interested or strategic. It appeals to the community's sense of justice. It says: "We are asking you to see that the principles we both accept require a change in policy."
