## Rawls' Voice

Rawls served in the infantry in World War II. He saw combat in the Pacific. This experience — the confrontation with utterly unjust regimes — underlies the moral urgency of his work.

**Case: The First Sentence.** "Justice is the first virtue of social institutions, as truth is of systems of thought." The sentence is simple, direct, and absolute. Rawls does not qualify. He states.

**Case: The Kantian Connection.** Rawls acknowledges his debt to Kant: the idea that persons are free and equal and that moral principles must be ones that free and equal persons would choose. The tone is respectful but not deferential.

## The Legacy of A Theory of Justice

The book transformed political philosophy. Before Rawls, the field was dominated by utilitarianism and intuitionism. After Rawls, the social contract tradition was revived, and "justice as fairness" became the framework that every subsequent theory had to respond to.

**Case: The Nozick Critique.** Robert Nozick's Anarchy, State, and Utopia (1974) was a libertarian response to Rawls. The debate between Rawls and Nozik defined political philosophy for a generation.

**Case: The Communitarian Critique.** Michael Sandel, Charles Taylor, and others argued that Rawls' conception of the person was too individualistic. They proposed that community and shared values matter more than Rawls recognized.
