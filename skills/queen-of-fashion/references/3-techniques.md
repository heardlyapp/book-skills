## Practical Techniques

### 1. The Fashion Audit

Look at what you wear. Ask: what does this communicate? Who is the intended audience? What message am I sending about my identity, my values, my status?

### 2. The Context Check

A garment that is appropriate in one context is scandalous in another. The chemise dress was fine at the Petit Trianon; at Versailles it was a breach of protocol. Know your context.

> **Case: The Petit Trianon** (Chapter 6): Marie Antoinette's private estate, the Petit Trianon, was where she dressed informally. She wore muslin, played milkmaid, and escaped the court. The public eventually saw this as evidence of her detachment from the suffering of the French people.
> **Key takeaway**: Private behavior that escapes scrutiny is safe. But a queen has no private life. Everything she does becomes public.

### 3. The Image Control

You cannot control how others interpret your choices. The best you can do is be intentional. Know what you are communicating and accept that others may read it differently.

### 4. The Consumption Check

Before spending, ask: what message does this spending send? Marie Antoinette's gowns were not just dresses — they were political statements that the public read as corruption.

> **Case: The Execution Dress** (Epilogue): On the day of her execution, Marie Antoinette wore a simple white dress. No jewels, no pouf, no corset. For the first time, her fashion communicated nothing but her humanity. The simplicity was the most powerful statement of all.

> **Key takeaway**: When status symbols are stripped away, the person remains. Fashion is armor — but it is not identity.
