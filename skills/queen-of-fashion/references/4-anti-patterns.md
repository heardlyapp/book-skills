## 6 Anti-Patterns of Fashion Blindness

### 1. "Fashion Is Trivial"

Fashion is not trivial — it is one of the most powerful tools of communication and control. Ignoring fashion is ignoring a source of power.

### 2. "Clothing Is Just Personal Choice"

Clothing is never just personal. It is social, political, and economic. What you wear communicates your identity, values, and status.

> **Case: The Sumptuary Laws** (Introduction): Before the Revolution, French law dictated what each social class could wear. Silk was reserved for the nobility. Velvet was forbidden to commoners. Fashion was literally a matter of law.
> **Key takeaway**: Clothing has always been regulated because it has always been political. Dress codes are systems of control.

### 3. "The Meaning of Fashion Is Obvious"

The chemise dress was meant to mean "natural simplicity." It was received as "indecent impropriety." The meaning of fashion is never obvious — it depends on context and audience.

### 4. "You Can Control Your Image"

Marie Antoinette could not control hers. The Diamond Necklace Affair proved that once an image escapes into the public, the original owner loses control.

### 5. "Fashion Is Only for Women"

Men's fashion is equally political. The revolutionary government banned silk and lace as decadent — men adopted sober black coats to display republican virtue.

### 6. "Fashion History Is Dead"

Fashion history is alive. The same dynamics — consumption, status signaling, political expression — operate today.

> **Case: The Red Heeled Shoes** (Chapter 2): Red heels were a privilege reserved for French nobility. Commoners could not wear them. When Marie Antoinette's portrait showed her wearing red heels, it was a statement of her rank.
> **Key takeaway**: The smallest details of dress — even the color of a shoe — can carry enormous political meaning.
