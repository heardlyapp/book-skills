## The Weber Framework: Fashion as Political Language

Weber's framework treats every garment as a political statement. Marie Antoinette's wardrobe is not frivolity — it is a conscious language of power, identity, and rebellion. Her fashion choices reflect her struggle for autonomy within the rigid structure of Versailles.

**The Key Insight**: Marie Antoinette was one of history's most powerful fashion communicators — but she lost control of her message. The revolutionaries reinterpreted her style as evidence of corruption.

> **Case: The Pouf à l'Indépendance** (Chapter 3): In 1778, Marie Antoinette wore a pouf celebrating the French naval victory over Britain. The pouf featured a model of a ship riding on a sea of waves. The message: "I am a patriot. I am the queen. I am in control." The public was delighted — for a moment.
> **Key takeaway**: Fashion can make political statements that words cannot. The pouf was a declaration of loyalty and power.

> **Case: The Portrait Scandal** (Chapter 5): In 1783, a portrait of Marie Antoinette in a simple white chemise was exhibited at the Salon. The public was outraged. A queen should not appear in "underwear." The painting was removed. The message of simplicity was received as scandal.
> **Key takeaway**: The intended message does not always reach the audience. What the queen meant as "natural" was seen as "indecent." Fashion is a conversation you cannot control.
Fashion is always a gamble. You cannot control how others read your clothes.
