## 7 Principles of Fashion as Power

### 1. Clothing Is a Language

Every garment communicates. The question is not whether you are making a statement — it is what statement you are making.

### 2. Fashion Is Political

In the court of Versailles, what you wore determined your rank, your access, and your power. Dress codes were political systems.

> **Case: The Corset and Panier** (Chapter 1): The formal court gown required a corset (tight-laced bodice) and panier (wide hoops at the hips). It was physically uncomfortable and nearly immobile. Wearing it was an act of submission to the court's rules.
> **Key takeaway**: Uncomfortable clothing is not an accident — it is a system of control. Those who bear the discomfort demonstrate their willingness to conform.

### 3. Breaking Rules Can Backfire

Marie Antoinette's fashion rebellion (the chemise, the pouf, informal dress at the Petit Trianon) was meant to assert her freedom. But the public interpreted it as frivolity and disrespect.

### 4. Image Escapes Control

The Diamond Necklace Affair proved that Marie Antoinette could not control her own image. The public believed she was capable of the crime — even though she was innocent.

### 5. Consumption Is Political

Marie Antoinette's spending on fashion made her a symbol of the monarchy's excess. In a time of economic crisis, her gowns became evidence of the regime's failure.

### 6. The Body Is a Site of Power

How you present your body — dressed, adorned, covered, exposed — is a statement about who you are and what you deserve. Marie Antoinette's body was a battlefield.

### 7. Fashion History Is Not Trivial

What people wore is a primary source for understanding their values, their constraints, and their political context. Fashion is history.

> **Case: The Rose Bertin Relationship** (Chapter 4): Marie Antoinette's dressmaker Rose Bertin was one of the first celebrity fashion designers. The queen's patronage made Bertin famous — and made the queen vulnerable. The public saw Bertin's influence as proof that the queen was frivolous.
> **Key takeaway**: Even the relationships behind fashion have political implications. Who dresses you shapes how you are seen.
