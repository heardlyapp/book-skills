# Anti-Patterns: Misreading Leonardo

> Source: The Science of Leonardo, throughout

## Leonardo Was an Artist Who Dabbled in Science

The most persistent myth. In fact, Leonardo was a scientist first. His art expressed his scientific understanding.

> **Case: The Mona Lisa's Science** (Chapter 4): The Mona Lisa's smile is not just artistic genius. It's the result of Leonardo's study of light, optics, and human anatomy. The sfumato technique comes from scientific observation of how light scatters.

> **Key takeaway**: Leonardo's art is applied science. His paintings are expressions of his research into nature.

## His Notebooks Were Disorganized

They look chaotic but reflect visual and associative thinking.

> **Case: The Connected Pages** (Chapter 1): Leonardo often put notes on water, anatomy, and flight on the same page. This is not disorganization — it's connection. He recognized the relationships between seemingly different phenomena.

> **Key takeaway**: The notebooks are organized by connection, not by category. That's how a systems thinker works.

## He Was a Failure

Leonardo completed few projects. But his unfinished works changed the world.

> **Case: The Incomplete Legacy** (Chapter 12): His unfinished paintings and unpublished notebooks advanced science and art more than most people's finished works.

> **Key takeaway**: Leonardo's "failures" — unfinished paintings, unpublished notebooks, unbuilt flying machines — contain some of his most important contributions to science and art alike.
