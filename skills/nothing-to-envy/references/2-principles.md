## 7 Principles of Understanding North Korea

1. **The Regime Controls Through Fear, Not Love** — Most North Koreans do not love Kim Jong Un. They fear the consequences of disobedience. The fear is rational.

2. **Songbun Is the System's Backbone** — The hereditary caste system makes resistance nearly impossible. You cannot organize with people you cannot trust.

3. **The Famine Was a Policy Choice** — The Arduous March was not a natural disaster. The regime chose military spending over food aid. An estimated 1-3 million died.

4. **Information Is the Most Dangerous Threat** — The regime's greatest fear is not sanctions or military action — it is its own people discovering the truth.

> **Case: The Secret Radio** (Chapter 8): One of the book's subjects secretly listened to South Korean radio broadcasts. The moment he heard news critical of Kim Jong Il, his entire worldview cracked. He said: "It was like waking up from a dream. I realized I had been lied to my entire life."

5. **Defectors Pay an Impossible Price** — Escape means leaving behind everyone you love, knowing they will be punished for your freedom. Many defectors later suffer from depression, guilt, and trauma.

6. **The Prison Camps Are Genocide** — Three generations imprisoned together. Forced labor, starvation, torture, execution. The camps are not a side effect of the system — they are essential to it.

7. **Ordinary People Make Extraordinary Choices** — Despite the system, individuals find ways to resist: smuggling information, helping prisoners, choosing to defect. These small acts of heroism are the regime's greatest failure.

> **Case: The Family's Choice** (Chapter 11): A family whose son had defected faced collective punishment. They could denounce him publicly — a lie that would save them — or stay silent and accept the consequences. They chose silence. The grandmother was sent to a camp. She died there. The family's quiet dignity was a form of resistance the regime could not control.
