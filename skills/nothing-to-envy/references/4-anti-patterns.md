## 6 Anti-Patterns in Understanding North Korea

### 1. Treating North Korea as a Monolith

The regime is not the country. The government is not the people. Ordinary North Koreans are as diverse in their beliefs as any population.

### 2. Believing the Propaganda

The regime's propaganda is not reality. But neither is it irrelevant — propaganda shapes how people think, even if they don't fully believe it.

> **Case: The Portraits** (Chapter 2): Every home must have portraits of Kim Il Sung and Kim Jong Il. Most people bow to them daily. Some believe. Others perform. The act of bowing — whether sincere or not — reinforces the system.

### 3. Assuming No One Wants to Leave

Many North Koreans want to leave. But the cost of leaving — the punishment of family, the risk of death, the uncertainty of life outside — is prohibitive.

### 4. Focusing Only on the Nuclear Program

The nuclear program is real and dangerous. But it should not be the only lens through which we see North Korea. The human rights crisis is equally urgent.

### 5. Forgetting the Famine

The Arduous March killed millions. The regime has never acknowledged it, never apologized, never changed the policies that caused it.

### 6. Believing Collapse Is Imminent

North Korea has been "about to collapse" for decades. The regime is more stable than outsiders assume. The songbun system, propaganda, and fear-based control are resilient.

> **Key takeaway**: North Korea is not a mystery — it is a system. The system can be understood, and understanding it is the first step toward doing something about the human suffering it creates.
