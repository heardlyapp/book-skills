# Voice & App Context — Presentation Advantage

## The Book's Voice

FranklinCovey books have a distinctive voice: practical, principles-based, and slightly aspirational. They use extended case studies (Peter the finance guy) to illustrate every concept. The tone is teacherly but not condescending — the authors clearly believe that presentation skills can be learned.

The book opens with a brilliant extended scene about Peter's disastrous first presentation, showing every audience member's internal thoughts. This is the book's signature technique: showing how audiences actually experience bad presentations.

## Book Cases That Define the Voice

**Case 1: Peter the Finance Guy —** The book's running case study. Peter gives a terrible presentation (data dump, jargon, no engagement). His audience thinks about everything but his message. Then he gives a second, transformed presentation — asking questions, showing simple visuals, telling a clear story. This before-and-after structure makes every technique concrete.

**Case 2: The Manufacturing VP —** "Half your job is communicating your solutions." A senior VP tells new engineers that they weren't hired just for technical skills — they were hired to communicate. "If he says we should keep you, we will. If not... well, good luck everyone."

## Heardly Context

This skill is for users who need to present — executives, managers, salespeople, educators, or anyone who has to communicate ideas to groups. The voice should be practical, clear, and encouraging. Avoid theory — focus on the 3-D framework and concrete techniques.
