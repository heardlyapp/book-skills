# Key Principles — Presentation Advantage

## I. Connection Is Everything

"You can have the best data and the best intentions, but if you don't connect — with your message, yourself, and your audience — you have nothing." Case: Peter failed the first time because he only had data. The second time, he connected.

## II. Character Is the Foundation of Persuasion

"Can you be trusted?" No amount of slick delivery compensates for lack of integrity. The book argues this is the one thing missing from most presentation books. Case: Stephen Covey's character + competence framework. "You can be the slickest of presenters, but if you're not trustworthy, you will shift no paradigms."

## III. Know Your Purpose: Inform or Persuade

"Is your goal to inform or to persuade?" If you don't know, your audience won't either. Inform = share knowledge clearly. Persuade = change their paradigm and get action. Case: "If your message is merely tactical, find some other way than a presentation."

## IV. The Opening Is Everything

"You have 8 seconds." The average attention span has dropped from 12 to 8 seconds. Start with a hook: a compelling question, a striking fact, a story, or an analogy. Case: Peter's second opening: "Who can tell me what this slide means?" — immediately engaged.

## V. One Message Per Slide

"A slide should be understandable in 5 seconds." One idea, one visual, one point. No "death by bullet point." Case: "Poor PowerPoint is a crime against humanity."

## VI. Delivery Is Learned, Not Born

"Eye contact for 2-3 seconds per person. Movement with purpose. Vocal variety." Great presenters are made, not born. Practice is essential. Case: "Winging it is not a good idea. Mark Twain: 'I never could make a good impromptu speech without several hours to prepare it.'"

## VII. Tell the Story the Data Is Telling

"The real job of a knowledge worker is to communicate." You weren't hired just to do analysis — you were hired to tell the story the analysis reveals. Case: The manufacturing VP: "Half your job is communicating your ideas."
