# Anti-Patterns — Presentation Advantage

## The Central Error: "More Data = Better Presentation"

"The more data you pack in, the more convincing you'll be." Wrong. Information overload causes audiences to check out. Case: Peter's first presentation was a data dump — his audience was thinking about everything except his message.

### Mistake 1: Death by Bullet Point
"Put all your talking points on slides — your audience needs to see them." Bullet points are the worst way to present information. They encourage reading instead of listening. Case: "Poor PowerPoint is a crime against humanity."

### Mistake 2: The Information Dump Opening
"Start with background, then methodology, then findings, then recommendations." This chronological approach loses everyone in the first 30 seconds. Lead with the conclusion. Case: Peter opened with "credit card security issues" — the audience drifted immediately. His second opening used a question that hooked them.

### Mistake 3: Reading Your Slides
"Your slides serve as your notes." No. Slides serve the audience. Your notes are separate. Face the audience, not the screen. Case: "If you're reading your slides, your audience is reading them too — and they can read faster than you can speak, so they'll finish before you do."

### Mistake 4: Talking Above Your Audience
"Use industry jargon to sound smart." Jargon alienates. Use plain language. Case: "EBITDA" and "enhanced authorization data" meant nothing to Peter's non-finance audience. "We're losing money" meant everything.

### Mistake 5: Spinning the Truth
"Put a positive spin on bad news." Spinning destroys credibility. Be honest, direct, and transparent. Case: "We're losing a million dollars" is better than "market conditions have not yet aligned to our projections."

### Mistake 6: Preparation = "I Know This Stuff"
"I know the material, so I don't need to practice." Knowing the material and delivering it effectively are different skills. Case: "I never could make a good impromptu speech without several hours to prepare it" — Mark Twain.
