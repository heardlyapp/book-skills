# Techniques — Presentation Advantage

## The FranklinCovey 3-D Presentation System

### Technique 1: The Key Message Technique
Distill your entire presentation into one sentence. "If your audience remembers only one thing, what should it be?" Write it down. Test it. "If you can't state your message in one clear sentence, your presentation isn't ready." Case: Peter's key message: "Our credit card security is losing us a million a year — and here's how we fix it in 90 days."

### Technique 2: The 8-Second Hook
Open with impact within 8 seconds. Options: a question ("Who can tell me what this slide means?"), a startling fact ("We're losing five times more than our competitors"), a short story, or a provocative statement. "The first 8 seconds determine whether they tune in or tune out." Case: Peter's opening question immediately engaged the room.

### Technique 3: The Problem-Solution Structure
Body structure: "Here's the problem, here's why it matters, here's the solution." Simple, clear, and persuasive. "Don't present data in chronological order — present it in order of significance." Case: Peter's second presentation: "We're losing $1M (problem), this is why (analysis), and here's enhanced authorization (solution)."

### Technique 4: The 5-Second Slide Rule
Every slide should be scannable in 5 seconds. One idea per slide. Use visuals (charts, diagrams, images) over text. "Slides should support your narrative — not BE your narrative." Case: Peter's first slides were dense data dumps. His second used simple comparison charts that everyone understood immediately.

### Technique 5: The "Show, Don't Tell" Visual
Instead of "Our fraud rate exceeds industry average," show a bar chart comparing your company's rate to competitors. "A picture is worth a thousand bullet points." Case: "We're five times worse than the industry average" — one simple chart did what paragraphs of explanation couldn't.

### Technique 6: Eye Contact Rotation
2-3 seconds per person. Rotate through the room. "Eye contact is the bridge between you and your audience. When you look at someone, they feel included." Case: "Don't scan the room like a searchlight — make real, brief contact with individuals."

### Technique 7: The Conversation Frame
"Don't lecture people, talk with them." Ask questions. Invite participation. "The goal is dialogue, not monologue." Case: Peter's transformation from data reader to conversation leader — asking "What does this mean?" instead of "Let me explain what this means."
