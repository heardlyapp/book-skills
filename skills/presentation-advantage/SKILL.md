### Philosophy — 7 Rules to Remember

1. **Connection Is Everything.** "Your presentation only has power when all three connections are present" — with your message, yourself, and your audience.

2. **Character Is the Foundation.** "You can be the slickest of presenters, but if you're not trustworthy, you will shift no paradigms." Character (integrity) + Competence (skills) = Credibility.

3. **Know Your Purpose.** Inform or Persuade? If you don't know, they won't either. "If your message is merely tactical, find some other way."

4. **You Have 8 Seconds.** "Average attention span in 2013: 8 seconds." Your opening must hook them immediately — a question, a fact, a story.

5. **One Idea Per Slide.** "A slide should be scannable in 5 seconds." One idea, one visual, one point. "Death by bullet point" kills engagement.

6. **Delivery Is Learned.** Eye contact (2-3 seconds per person), purposeful movement, vocal variety. "Winging it is not a good idea."

7. **Tell the Story the Data Tells.** "Half your job as a knowledge worker is communicating." Don't dump data — tell the story it reveals.

### Core Framework Quick Reference

- **Who the Authors Are:** Kory Kogon (FranklinCovey global practice leader), Breck England (FranklinCovey writer/researcher), Julie Schmidt (FranklinCovey consultant). All are FranklinCovey leadership experts.
- **The 3-D Model:** Develop (craft the message), Design (create impactful visuals), Deliver (present with excellence). Builds on the foundation of three connections.
- **The Foundation: Three Connections:** Connect with your message (passion, strategic stakes), connect with yourself (character + competence = credibility), connect with your audience (8-second attention, engagement, conversation).
- **The Peter Story:** The book's running case study. Peter the finance guy gives a terrible presentation in Chapter 1 (audience drifts to phones and daydreams). Then he transforms his approach in Chapter 2 using the 3-D system. This before/after is the book's teaching method.
- **The 8-Second Attention Span:** "In 2000: 12 seconds. In 2013: 8 seconds." People check phones 110 times daily. The opening is the most critical moment.
- **The Manufacturing VP Story:** A senior VP tells new engineers: "Half your job is communicating your solutions." He brings in a presentation consultant and says: "If he says we should keep you, we will. If not... well, good luck everyone." Sets up the book's core argument that communication is as important as technical skill.
- **The Spin Cycle Warning:** The book's most unusual chapter for a presentation book — it spends significant space warning against "spin": cherry-picking, doublespeak, and euphemisms. "Lots of communication experts will argue this view is naive. But the age of spin has turned us into skeptics." Case: The "doublespeak" translation table: "It's coming along" = "I haven't started yet." "Interesting idea" = "Useless idea."
- **The Data-to-Story Transformation:** Peter's two presentations side by side — same data, completely different results. First: "I have just a few slides..." (everyone checks phones). Second: "Who can tell me what this slide means?" (everyone engaged). The difference is story structure, not data quality.
- **The "Real Job" Argument:** "You were hired to tell stories." The book argues that every knowledge worker has two jobs: do the analysis AND communicate the results. "You can do the best analysis in the world, but if you can't communicate it, it doesn't matter."
- **Character + Competence:** Quoting Stephen Covey. "Character includes your integrity, your motive, your intent. Competence includes your capabilities, your skills, your track record. Both are vital." The authors argue this is the one thing missing from every other presentation book: "Do a word search on them. Nowhere do they talk about things like integrity, honesty, or trust."
- **The Visual Design Principles:** Simplicity (remove anything that doesn't support the message), clarity (the message should be obvious in 5 seconds), relevance (serve the audience's needs, not your ego). Use images, charts, and diagrams over text. "A picture is worth a thousand bullet points."

### Key Principles

1. **Connection Is Everything.** Message + self + audience.
2. **Character = Foundation.** Without trust, nothing works.
3. **Know Your Purpose.** Inform or persuade.
4. **8 Seconds to Hook.** Opening is critical.
5. **One Idea Per Slide.** 5-second scan rule.
6. **Delivery Is Learned.** Practice. Practice. Practice.
7. **Tell the Data's Story.** Knowledge = worthless if not shared.
