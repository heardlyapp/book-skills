# Principles: The Narrative Fallacy and Hindsight Bias

## Principle 1: The Narrative Fallacy

We love stories. We create narratives to make sense of random events. The problem is that stories impose order on chaos, making unpredictable events seem predictable. This gives us a false sense of understanding.

## Principle 2: Hindsight Is Not Foresight

After an event happens, we can always find reasons why it was inevitable. This is the hindsight bias. It is one of the most dangerous cognitive biases because it makes us overconfident in our ability to predict the future.

> **Case: The 9/11 Attacks** (Chapter 8): After 9/11, many commentators claimed they had predicted it. Before 9/11, virtually no one did. The warnings that existed were vague and unspecific. In hindsight, the scattered clues seemed to point clearly to the attack. But they did not point clearly before it happened.
> **Key takeaway:** Hindsight bias distorts our understanding of history and makes us overconfident. The fact that you can explain an event after the fact does not mean you could have predicted it.

## Principle 3: The Problem of Induction

The fact that the sun has risen every day for thousands of years does not prove it will rise tomorrow. This is the problem of induction. We assume the future will resemble the past, but black swans prove that it often does not.

The narrative fallacy is why we think we understand history better than we do. After World War I, historians created stories that made it seem inevitable. But before it happened, almost no one predicted it. The stories we tell after the fact give us the illusion that we understand causes and effects. In reality, history is far more random than our narratives suggest.

> **Case: The Internet** (Chapter 9): Before the internet became ubiquitous, almost no one predicted its impact. After it transformed the world, we created stories about why it was inevitable. The printing press, the industrial revolution, and the internet were all black swans that seemed obvious only in hindsight.
> **Key takeaway:** Major technological shifts are black swans. Our narratives make them seem predictable, but they were not.
The problem of induction is the philosophical foundation of the black swan problem. David Hume pointed out that we cannot prove the sun will rise tomorrow just because it has always risen. Black swans are the practical manifestation of this philosophical problem. Every day that passes without a black swan makes us more confident that one will not come, when in fact we should be less confident. This is the central paradox of uncertainty.
Taleb also warns against the sin of confirmation bias when evaluating evidence. We look for evidence that confirms our theories and ignore evidence that contradicts them. This is especially dangerous when dealing with rare events, because the absence of a black swan does not confirm that one will never come. Each safe day is not evidence of safety.
