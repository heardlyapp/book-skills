# 2 — Key Principles

> Source: *On Call: A Doctor's Journey in Public Service* by Anthony Fauci — synthesized from his career across all six parts.

## The 8 Operating Principles of a Public-Service Career

### 1. Tell the Truth to Everyone — Equally

The same standard applies whether you're briefing a president, a congressional committee, a room of AIDS activists, or the American public on national TV. Fauci never had a "private truth" for the White House and a "public truth" for the press. Consistency is what earned him the trust of six presidents across both parties.

> **Case: The Parallel Track Conflict** (Part 2, Chapter ~15): In 1989, Fauci publicly supported a "parallel track" for AIDS drug access at a town hall in San Francisco — against the official position of the FDA and the Bush administration. He had already told the president's team privately. When asked publicly, he said the same thing. HHS Secretary Louis Sullivan called him in, furious. Fauci held his ground. Eventually, the parallel track was adopted.

### 2. Build Relationships Before the Crisis

Fauci's access to the Oval Office didn't come from his title. It came from years of face-to-face briefings, handwritten notes, and showing up consistently. He built relationships with George H.W. Bush's personal aide Tim McBride long before he needed an audience with the president.

### 3. Stay in Your Lane — and Never Leave It

Fauci's golden rule: only speak on matters within his scientific expertise. During the 2004 election, when the Chiron vaccine shortage became a political football, he focused on the science of vaccine safety and supply — not the politics. That's why both the Bush and Kerry camps trusted his testimony.

### 4. Don't Overpromise. Underpromise and Overdeliver.

The COVID vaccine timeline: "12 to 18 months." Many thought that was optimistic. It was realistic. Fauci had been through enough pandemics to know that promising miracles destroys credibility when they don't materialize.

### 5. When Attacked, Don't Retaliate. Keep Working.

The hand-to-forehead photo went viral. Right-wing media used it to frame Fauci as the enemy. His response: more briefings, more data, more emphasis on science. "The results speak."

### 6. Serve the Mission Before Your Reputation.

Cliff Lane, Peter Staley, and his wife Christine all urged Fauci to leave the Trump task force to protect his legacy. He refused. "The American people needed someone telling the truth at that podium."

### 7. Earn Trust by Being the Same Person in Every Room.

Fauci was the same with a first-term congressman as with a president. The Jesuit discipline — "precision of thought and economy of expression" — wasn't a tactic. It was who he was. That's why he was trusted across 50 years of political change.

### 8. Find Your Unshakeable Inner Circle.

Christine Grady (wife, bioethicist, nurse), Cliff Lane (deputy, 40+ years of partnership), and Peter Staley (former ACT UP activist turned close friend) formed Fauci's core. When everything outside was noise, this circle kept him grounded.

> **Case: Staley the Convert** (Part 2, Chapter ~17): Peter Staley was one of Fauci's fiercest critics during ACT UP's early years. But over decades, as Staley saw Fauci's commitment to patients through his willingness to meet activists directly, to hold town halls under hostile conditions, and to admit when the system was failing, Staley became one of his most loyal defenders. Their friendship is a case study in turning adversaries into allies through sheer consistency.
