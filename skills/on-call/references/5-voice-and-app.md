# 5 — Voice & App

> Source: *On Call: A Doctor's Journey in Public Service* by Anthony Fauci — voice characteristics and application scenarios.

## Fauci's Voice — What Makes It Distinct

1. **Jesuit precision.** Short sentences. No unnecessary words. "Precision of thought and economy of expression" was drilled into him at Regis High School and Holy Cross. It never left.
2. **Clinician's calm.** The tone of a doctor delivering a diagnosis — direct, factual, but not cold. He makes eye contact with the camera, not the script.
3. **No false emotion.** Fauci doesn't emote for effect. When he's emotional (the COVID vaccine call from Albert Bourla), it's a crack in his professional armor — and that's what makes it powerful.
4. **Classics and poetry.** He quotes *Harrison's Principles of Internal Medicine* as naturally as a 21st-century scientist. He references Jesuit education, Brooklyn street smarts, and 1940s baseball in the same paragraph.
5. **Self-deprecating humility.** He tells the story of his birth — delivered by a tuxedoed OB-GYN who might have come from a Christmas party — with a bemused distance that humanizes the larger-than-life figure.

## Signature Phrases / "Fauci-isms"

> "The patient is no mere collection of symptoms, signs, disordered functions, damaged organs, and disturbed emotions. The patient is human, fearful, and hopeful." — *Harrison's Principles of Internal Medicine* (his constant reference point)

> "You don't have to be a Democrat or a Republican to have a virus infect you." — *His central message on depoliticizing public health*

## Five Application Scenarios

### Scenario 1: The Start-up Founder Facing a Crisis

**The situation:** You're a CEO. Your product has a critical bug that could hurt users. Your investors want you to keep quiet and ship. Your engineering team wants to fix it but needs two weeks.

**Fauci's playbook:**
1. Go to your board privately — show them the data (the bug, the severity, the risk)
2. Frame the fix as protecting the company's long-term credibility
3. Acknowledge uncertainty honestly: "We don't know exactly how many users are affected, but we know enough to act"
4. Give the public (your customers) the diagnosis — even if it's bad
5. Take the short-term hit. Long-term trust is worth more than any quarter.
→ *Reference: The parallel track with AIDS drugs — he chose patients' trust over institutional approval.*

### Scenario 2: The Product Manager Contradicting a VP

**The situation:** VP of Product wants to launch a feature that has known UX issues. You're the PM who ran usability tests showing it fails.

**Fauci's playbook:**
1. Request 15 minutes alone with the VP (Private-First Briefing)
2. Bring the usability results (data, not opinion)
3. Frame it as helping them hit their goals: "I want this launch to succeed. The data shows we need two more weeks."
4. Offer alternatives: Can we launch for power users only? Can we soft-launch?
5. If they override you: state your objection in writing, then execute. If asked publicly, tell the truth.
→ *Reference: The H5N1 private briefing before the pharmaceutical CEO meeting.*

### Scenario 3: The Non-Profit Leader Facing Funding Cuts

**The situation:** Your grant funding is getting cut because "the problem isn't visible right now." You need to persuade the board/donors to keep investing.

**Fauci's playbook:**
1. Use the Preparedness Paradox: "We haven't had an outbreak because we've been prepared. If cuts happen, that luck won't hold."
2. Reference past near-misses (the H5N1 stockpile that was never used but would have saved millions if needed)
3. Share an uncomfortable projection: what happens if the cuts go through and the problem resurfaces
4. Offer a tiered funding option: "If you can't fund the full plan, here's the minimum viable protection"

### Scenario 4: The Individual Contributor Feeling Burnt Out by Criticism

**The situation:** You work on a controversial project. You're being attacked online. Your friends say quit. Your family is worried.

**Fauci's playbook:**
1. Compartmentalize: the criticism and the work live in different boxes
2. Find your inner circle (Christine, Cliff, Peter) — one or two people who see the whole picture
3. Repeat your mission: "Who am I serving? What would happen if I stopped?"
4. Accept that some people will never like you. That's fine.
5. Keep going. The work is the only answer.
→ *Reference: Fauci staying on the task force despite his wife's and closest friends' pleas to quit.*

### Scenario 5: The Government Scientist Navigating Political Pressure

**The situation:** Your political leadership wants you to say something that isn't true. You're a career civil servant.

**Fauci's playbook:**
1. Refuse politely but firmly: "I cannot say that. The data doesn't support it."
2. Offer what you *can* say instead (an honest framing that serves the same political goal)
3. If they override you, document everything
4. If asked by the press, tell the truth — the American people are your patient
5. Accept that you may lose your job. But you won't lose your integrity.
→ *Reference: Every single Fauci-Trump briefing — especially hydroxychloroquine.*
