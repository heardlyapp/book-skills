## Practical Techniques

### 1. The One-Sentence Purpose

Write your purpose in one sentence. "I help [audience] achieve [result] through [method]." If you cannot do it in one sentence, keep working.

### 2. The Originality Gap

Ask: what do I offer that no one else does? If the answer is not clear, find a niche within your field that is underserved.

> **Case: The Niche Finder** (Chapter 5): A speaker who was one of hundreds of communication experts became the go-to person for "presenting data to boards." Same skill, different audience.
> **Key takeaway**: Originality often comes from narrowing your focus, not expanding it.

### 3. The Pizzazz Check

Before any presentation, ask: would I want to listen to myself? If the answer is no, add energy, a story, or a surprising element.

### 4. The IP Creation Exercise

1. List three phrases describing what you do. 2. Combine into one unique phrase. 3. Test on three people. 4. Refine.

### 5. The 60-Second Pitch

Template: "I help [specific audience] achieve [specific result] through my unique approach to [method]."

> **Case: The Pitch That Worked** (Chapter 8): A consultant's original pitch: "I help businesses improve efficiency." After POP: "I help tech startups cut customer onboarding time in half through my Customer Flow System." The second pitch got meetings.
> **Key takeaway**: Specificity is more powerful than generality. A specific audience, specific result, and named method create credibility.

### 6. The Reaction Test

Before using any messaging, test it on three people. If their eyes glaze over, rework it. The reaction test saves you from using ineffective messaging.
