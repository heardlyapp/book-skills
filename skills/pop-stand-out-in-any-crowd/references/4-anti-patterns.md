## 6 Anti-Patterns of Blending In

### 1. "I Will Let My Work Speak for Itself"

Your work will not speak for itself. In a noisy world, you must speak for your work. If you do not, someone else will take the credit.

### 2. "Being Different Is Risky"

Being different is less risky than being invisible. The crowd is not safe — it is invisible.

> **Case: The Consultant Who Played It Safe** (Chapter 3): A consultant blended in with hundreds of others offering the same service. She was competent, experienced, and invisible. She changed her messaging to focus on her unique approach — and her phone started ringing.
> **Key takeaway**: Blending in is not safety — it is invisibility. Originality is not a risk — it is a strategy.

### 3. "Humility Means Staying Quiet"

Confidence is not arrogance. There is a difference between being proud of your work and being arrogant. The humble genius who no one knows about has not helped anyone.

### 4. "I Do Not Need a Brand"

Everyone has a brand. The question is whether you control it or let others define it.

### 5. "Pizzazz Is Not Professional"

Energy and enthusiasm are professional. Deadpan delivery is not more professional — it is less memorable.

### 6. "I Will Just Be Myself"

Being yourself is good advice — but only if you know who you are. Purpose is knowing yourself. Originality is expressing yourself. Pizzazz is showing up as yourself.

> **Case: The Invisible Expert** (Chapter 1): Horn describes a financial advisor who was excellent at his job but invisible in the market. He had no messaging, no brand, no visibility. When he started using POP (Purpose: helping families secure their future. Originality: his "Family First" framework. Pizzazz: community speaking.) his practice grew.
> **Key takeaway**: Excellence without visibility is invisible. Purpose, originality, and pizzazz turn expertise into influence.
