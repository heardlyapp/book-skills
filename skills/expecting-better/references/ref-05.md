# Reference 05: Risk Assessment Framework — Age, Testing, and Making Personal Decisions

## Overview

This reference synthesizes Oster's approach to pregnancy risk assessment: how to think about maternal age, prenatal testing options, miscarriage probabilities, and how to weigh all of this information to make personally appropriate decisions. The core principle: risks are meaningful only in context, and the "right" decision depends on individual values and circumstances — not on rules handed down by authority.

## Maternal Age and Fertility

### The "Cliff at 35" Myth

**The Common Narrative:**
Women are told their fertility "drops off a cliff" at age 35. This creates unnecessary anxiety and pressure.

**Oster's Correction:**
The decline is gradual:
- Ages 20-34: baseline fertility (best)
- Ages 35-39: approximately 90% as fertile as 20-34
- Ages 40-44: approximately 62% as fertile as 20-34

**The origin of the "35" cutoff:**
The number 35 comes from historical data on Down syndrome risk, not fertility. At 35, the risk of Down syndrome was considered high enough to warrant offering amniocentesis. The number stuck and was wrongly extrapolated to fertility.

### Natural Conception vs IVF
- The age effect is stronger for IVF than natural conception
- Women in their late 30s who have already had children have higher success with subsequent pregnancies
- Age affects egg quality (genetic abnormalities) more than the ability to conceive
- Many women successfully conceive naturally in their late 30s and early 40s

## Miscarriage Risk

### Actual Numbers
Oster provides the most realistic numbers available:

**By Week of Pregnancy:**
- Before missed period: 30-50% of all conceptions end naturally (most women never know)
- Weeks 4-6 (just after missed period): ~20-25% miscarriage rate
- Weeks 6-12: ~10-15% miscarriage rate
- Weeks 12-20: ~2-3% miscarriage rate
- After 20 weeks: less than 1%

**By Maternal Age:**
- Under 35: 10-15% miscarriage rate (of recognized pregnancies)
- Age 35-39: 15-25%
- Age 40-44: 30-40%
- Age 45+: 50%+

**Key Insight:** Most miscarriages are due to chromosomal abnormalities in the embryo, not anything the mother did. They are nature's quality control mechanism. There is nothing the woman can do to prevent them.

### What Does NOT Cause Miscarriage
Oster provides a (non-exhaustive) list of things DON'T cause miscarriage despite popular belief:
- Exercise (moderate)
- Sex
- Stress
- Flying
- Lifting (moderate)
- Falls (unless severe enough to cause placental abruption)
- Spicy food
- Most medications (with specific exceptions)
- Grief or emotional shock
- Miscarriage of a previous pregnancy (one miscarriage does not predict another)

## Prenatal Testing

### Types of Tests

**Screening Tests:**
- **Nuchal Translucency (NT)** ultrasound (11-13 weeks): measures fluid at back of fetal neck
- **First trimester blood test** (PAPP-A + hCG levels)
- **Quad screen** (second trimester blood test)
- **NIPT** (Non-Invasive Prenatal Testing): cell-free fetal DNA from maternal blood
  - Very accurate for Down syndrome (detection rate ~99%, false positive rate <0.1%)
  - Less accurate for other chromosomal abnormalities

**Diagnostic Tests:**
- **Chorionic Villus Sampling (CVS)** (10-13 weeks): samples placental tissue
  - Miscarriage risk: approximately 1 in 300 to 1 in 500
  - Can detect chromosomal abnormalities and some genetic disorders
- **Amniocentesis** (15-20 weeks): samples amniotic fluid
  - Miscarriage risk: approximately 1 in 300 to 1 in 500 (modern estimates may be lower: 1 in 500-1,000 with experienced practitioners)
  - Can detect chromosomal abnormalities, neural tube defects, and genetic disorders

### Understanding Test Results

**False Positives vs False Negatives:**
- A screening test (like NIPT or quad screen) can give a "positive" result even when nothing is wrong
- A negative screening result does not guarantee no abnormalities
- Screening positives should always be confirmed with diagnostic testing before making decisions
- The false positive rate varies by test and by the condition being screened for

**Oster's Framework for Test Decisions:**

1. **What are you testing for?** Understand what conditions the test can and cannot detect
2. **What is your baseline risk?** (based on age, family history, etc.)
3. **How does the test change that risk?** A positive screening may change risk from 1/1,000 to 1/20 — still 95% chance everything is fine
4. **What would you do with the information?** This is the most important question. Options:
   - Continue pregnancy regardless (test provides preparation time)
   - Consider termination (test guides decision)
   - Prepare for a special needs child
5. **What are the risks of the test itself?** For diagnostic tests, the procedure risk matters

### The Cost of Uncertainty

Oster emphasizes that the decision to test or not test involves weighing the cost of uncertainty against the cost of information:
- Some women prefer not to know about potential problems
- Others find uncertainty more stressful than any information could be
- Both choices are valid — the right answer depends on the individual

## Personalized Risk Framework

### Oster's Method for Making Any Pregnancy Decision

1. **Identify the question** — Be specific. "Is caffeine safe?" is too broad. "Does 200mg of caffeine daily increase my miscarriage risk by a meaningful amount?" is specific.

2. **Find the baseline risk** — What's the background rate of the bad outcome? E.g., without the behavior, what's the risk of miscarriage in the general population at this stage?

3. **Find the incremental risk** — How much does the behavior change that baseline? E.g., from 15% to 16%? Or from 1 in 10,000 to 1 in 5,000?

4. **Find the benefits** — What does the behavior provide? Relaxation, nutrition, enjoyment, reduced stress?

5. **Weigh costs and benefits** — This is personal. Some people are very risk-averse; others are comfortable with small risks for meaningful benefits.

6. **Make the decision** — There is no single "right" answer. The right answer for YOU depends on your values and circumstances.

### Example: Caffeine Decision

| Factor | Value |
|---|---|
| Baseline miscarriage risk | 15% |
| Risk with 1-2 cups coffee/day | ~15-16% |
| Incremental absolute risk | ~1% |
| Benefit | Enjoyment, routine, caffeine's effects |
| Would you take a 1% risk increase for this benefit? | Personal decision |

## Case 1: Maternal Age Fertility Analysis

Oster's analysis of the historical fertility data demonstrates her methodological approach in action:

**The Question:** How much does fertility decline with age, really?

**The Problem:** Most modern fertility data is confounded by contraception (women choose when to have children, so fertility is observed only in women who are trying — a selected sample).

**Oster's Solution:** She turned to historical populations with reliable data from before the era of effective contraception:
- The Hutterites (a religious community with natural fertility and excellent record-keeping)
- 17th-19th century French village records
- Early 20th century British data

**The Analysis:** She compared fertility rates (births per woman-year) across age groups in these populations:
- Peak fertility: late teens to late 20s
- Gradual decline through 30s
- At 35-39: still 85-95% of peak
- At 40-44: about 60-70% of peak
- Sharp drop only after 45

**The Implication:** The popular "fertility cliff at 35" is not supported by the best available data. The decline is more like a gentle slope. This has enormous implications for women making family planning decisions who might feel rushed by the narrative that they "have to" have children before 35.

## Case 2: The Amniocentesis Risk Calculation

Oster walks through the decision to have amniocentesis as a model for cost-benefit analysis in pregnancy:

**The Numbers:**
- Suppose you're 35, within the standard age for amnio offer
- Your risk of Down syndrome is about 1 in 250 (0.4%)
- The risk of miscarriage from amnio is about 1 in 300 (0.33%)
- These two risks are roughly EQUAL

**The Decision Framework:**
- If you would terminate for Down syndrome: amnio gives you certainty. The trade-off is about 1 procedure-caused miscarriage per 300 women to detect 1 case of Down syndrome per 250 women. Similar magnitude risks.
- If you would NOT terminate: amnio might still be useful (preparation) but the 1 in 300 procedure risk becomes harder to justify
- If you want maximum information regardless: the risk trade-off may be acceptable

**At Age 25:**
- Down syndrome risk: 1 in 1,250
- Amnio risk: 1 in 300
- The procedure risk is about 4x higher than the condition risk
- Most women at 25 would reasonably decline amnio

**At Age 40:**
- Down syndrome risk: 1 in 100
- Amnio risk: 1 in 300
- The condition risk is 3x higher than procedure risk
- Most women at 40 would reasonably accept amnio

**The crucial insight:** The "right" decision changes with age, risk tolerance, values, and what you'd do with the information. There's no universal rule.

## Final Principle

Oster's ultimate message: **The goal is not zero risk.** The goal is informed choice. Pregnancy inevitably involves risk — the risk of miscarriage, of birth defects, of complications. The question is which risks you're willing to accept and which you want to minimize. Different women will make different choices, and that's fine. The data enables that freedom.
