# Reference 02: Diet & Substances — Caffeine, Alcohol, and Food Risks

## Overview

This reference covers Oster's analysis of dietary risks during pregnancy, including caffeine, alcohol, and food-borne illness risks. The central theme: actual risk levels are far lower than commonly believed, and the benefits of some restricted items may outweigh the minimal risks.

## Caffeine

### The Standard Recommendation
Limit caffeine to 200mg/day (roughly 2 cups of coffee). Exceeding this is linked to increased miscarriage risk.

### Oster's Analysis

Oster conducted a thorough meta-analysis of existing studies to evaluate the caffeine-miscarriage relationship:

**The Evidence:**
- Individual studies show inconsistent results: some find no effect, some find a small effect, some find a moderate effect
- When combined in meta-analysis, the data suggests a small increase in miscarriage risk above ~200mg/day
- The effect size: approximately a 1-2% increase in absolute risk (e.g., from a baseline 15% miscarriage rate to 16-17%)
- Below 200mg: no consistent evidence of any increased risk across multiple studies

**Caveats:**
- Many studies fail to control adequately for morning sickness: women with healthier pregnancies have more nausea, and nausea makes coffee taste bad. So women who drink more coffee may be those who are less nauseous — and those are also the women at higher miscarriage risk. This could create a spurious correlation
- The 200mg cutoff is somewhat arbitrary — the evidence doesn't show a sharp threshold effect but a gradual increase

**Oster's Take:** The 200mg limit is reasonable but conservative. She considers it a sensible rule of thumb. Most women who have 1-2 cups of coffee daily are well within safe territory.

### Actionable Guidance
- 1 cup of coffee (95mg) is well within safe limits
- 2 cups (190mg) still under 200mg
- A single espresso (63mg) is fine
- Above 200mg/day: small absolute risk increase, may be acceptable to some
- Consider total caffeine from ALL sources: coffee, tea, soda, chocolate, energy drinks

## Alcohol

### The Standard Recommendation
Zero alcohol throughout pregnancy. The Surgeon General advises complete abstinence.

### Oster's Deep Dive

This is the most controversial analysis in the book. Oster carefully examines the evidence:

**Fetal Alcohol Syndrome (FAS):**
- Caused by heavy, chronic drinking during pregnancy (5+ drinks/day)
- Characterized by distinct facial features, growth deficiency, and neurological problems
- There is NO evidence linking moderate drinking (1 drink/day or less) to FAS

**Studies on Light-to-Moderate Drinking:**

Oster reviews multiple large-scale studies:
- The Millenium Cohort Study (UK): Children of light drinkers (1-2 drinks/week) scored slightly BETTER on developmental tests than children of abstainers — likely because light drinkers tend to be higher socioeconomic status, not a causal benefit
- Danish studies: No difference in IQ, attention, or executive function between children of light drinkers and abstainers at age 5
- Avon Longitudinal Study (UK): No adverse effects at 1-2 drinks per week

**The Key Confounders:**
- Women who drink moderately tend to be older, wealthier, better educated, and have better nutrition
- Women who completely abstain may be heavier drinkers who quit upon learning they're pregnant — making abstainers a mixed group
- Studies that control for these factors consistently find no harm from light drinking

**The Binge Drinking Distinction:**
- Critical: 4+ drinks in a session is clearly harmful
- The shape of alcohol consumption matters as much as total amount
- A glass of wine sipped over dinner is fundamentally different from 5 shots consumed in one hour at a bar
- Binge drinking is linked to FAS and cognitive deficits; light consumption is not

**The Absurdity Argument:**
Oster uses the banana analogy effectively: "no amount proven safe" is a logical trap. By that standard, virtually nothing in pregnancy would be permitted.

**Oster's Personal Decision:**
She had about half a glass of wine 3-4 times per week during her second and third trimesters. She explicitly states this was a personal choice based on her reading of the data.

### Actionable Guidance
- Zero alcohol in first trimester is safest (organogenesis — major fetal development)
- 1-2 drinks per week in first trimester: no evidence of harm from well-controlled studies
- 1 drink per day in second/third trimester: no evidence of harm
- NEVER binge drink (4+ drinks in a session)
- The European comparison is instructive: many European countries allow 1-2 drinks/week and have lower rates of FAS than the US
- Each woman's tolerance for risk differs — the data allows for informed personal choice

## Food Risks

### Deli Meats and Listeria

**The Standard Warning:** Don't eat deli meats due to listeria risk.

**Oster's Analysis:**
- Listeriosis is serious in pregnancy but extremely rare
- The actual risk from eating deli meat: approximately 1 in 8,000 (per serving, assuming contaminated meat)
- Even this is an overestimate — it assumes the meat is contaminated, which is rare
- Many other foods pose similar or higher listeria risks: hummus (recalled), ice cream (multiple outbreaks), frozen vegetables, soft cheeses, bagged salads
- Years with major listeria outbreaks: often traced to produce or dairy, not deli meats

**The Selective Focus Problem:**
Pregnancy food warnings are inconsistent:
- Deli meats: highly warned against
- Ice cream: rarely mentioned (had 2015 Blue Bell listeria outbreak)
- Hummus: not typically warned against (had 2015 Sabra listeria recall)
- Bagged salads: not typically warned against (multiple listeria recalls)

If listeria risk is the concern, being vigilant about ALL sources matters more than banning one food.

### Sushi and Mercury

**The Standard Warning:** No sushi during pregnancy (raw fish).

**Oster's Analysis:**
- **Mercury risk**: The concern is methylmercury in large predatory fish
- **Small fish vs large fish**: Salmon, shrimp, mackerel (low mercury) are safe. Tuna, shark, swordfish (high mercury) should be limited
- **Raw fish vs cooked**: The raw vs cooked distinction matters for food-borne illness risk but not for mercury
- **Mercury levels**: Tuna has moderate mercury; limiting tuna to 1-2 servings per week is reasonable. Salmon has very low mercury; multiple servings per week are fine
- **Nutritional benefit**: Fish provides omega-3 fatty acids important for fetal brain development
- **The Japanese comparison**: Japan has one of the highest sushi consumption rates in the world and does not have higher rates of pregnancy complications or birth defects

**Practical Takeaway:**
- Continue eating sushi with low-mercury fish (salmon, shrimp, eel, crab)
- Avoid or limit high-mercury fish: shark, swordfish, king mackerel, tilefish
- Limit tuna (including in sushi) to 1-2 servings per week
- Make sure the sushi is from a reputable source (food safety standards matter)

## Other Food Concerns

**Cheese**: Soft cheeses (brie, camembert, blue cheese) carry listeria risk, but pasteurized versions are safe. Most US commercial cheeses are pasteurized.

**Eggs**: Raw or undercooked eggs carry salmonella risk. Runny yolks from pasteurized eggs are fine.

**Fish oil supplements**: Safe and may be beneficial for omega-3 intake.

## Case 1: Oster's Cocktail Party Confession

One of the most memorable passages in the book: Oster describes attending an economics cocktail party while researching the alcohol chapter. She accepted a glass of wine and:

> "I was standing there, holding my glass of wine, pregnant, talking to another economist about the data on alcohol in pregnancy. And I thought: there are probably fifty people in this room, forty of them have published papers using multiple regression, and not one of them would think there was anything risky about what I was doing."

This moment crystallizes the book's thesis: the same people who would demand rigorous evidence in their professional lives accept fear-based pregnancy rules without question. The economist's trained instinct to ask "what does the data actually say?" goes out the window when it comes to pregnancy.

## Case 2: The Caffeine Meta-Analysis

Oster aggregated data from all major studies on caffeine and miscarriage:

**Approach:** She didn't trust any single study. Instead, she collected all published studies, evaluated their quality, adjusted for confounders, and combined the results.

**Finding:** When you isolate well-controlled studies (those that adjust for smoking, maternal age, and importantly, nausea), the caffeine effect essentially disappears below 200mg/day. Above 200mg/day, there's a small but detectable effect.

**The methodological insight:** The earliest studies showing large caffeine effects failed to control for nausea. Women with morning sickness both drink less coffee AND have lower miscarriage risk (because nausea is a sign of a healthy pregnancy). This creates a powerful spurious correlation that naive analysis picks up as "caffeine causes miscarriage."

This is Econometrics 101: omitted variable bias. Yet it had been overlooked in decades of pregnancy research.
