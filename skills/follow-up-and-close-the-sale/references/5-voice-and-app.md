> Source: Follow Up and Close the Sale, Introduction through Chapter 14

## Author's Voice

Jeff Shore writes as a sales trainer who has seen it all. His tone is direct, practical, and focused on what works. He does not waste time on theory — every chapter ends with actionable steps. He is sympathetic to the struggles of salespeople (rejection, anxiety, time pressure) but pushes them to be better.

## Signature Phrases

- "Follow up and close the sale" — the title and the method
- "Value-driven contact" — every touch must add value
- "The first follow-up" — the critical window
- "The 5-touch rule" — persistence pays
- "Buyers buy on emotion" — the psychology
- "The close is a service" — not a technique

## Application to 5 Scenarios

### Scenario 1: A new salesperson
**Partial response**: "Master the first follow-up. Within 24 hours, send a value-driven message. This one habit will separate you from 80% of salespeople."

### Scenario 2: An experienced rep struggling with close rates
**Partial response**: "Audit your follow-up. How many touches per prospect? If it is under 5, that is your problem. Plan for 5+."

### Scenario 3: A business owner selling their own service
**Partial response**: "Follow up with every inquiry. Most business owners are bad at follow-up because they are busy. That is your opportunity."

### Scenario 4: A manager training a sales team
**Partial response**: "Build a follow-up system. Define the cadence, the value to provide, and the tracking metrics. System beats talent."

### Scenario 5: Someone who hates sales
**Partial response**: "You hate sales because you think it is manipulation. Shore shows that follow-up done right is service, not pressure."

> **Case: The Voicemail Formula** (Chapter 11): Leave voicemails that do not ask for a call back — provide insight, say you will follow up by email. Lower pressure = higher response.

> **Key takeaway**: Remove the ask. Increase the value. The response rate goes up when the pressure goes down.

> **Case: The Lost Deal** (Chapter 12): A salesperson stopped follow-up after a lost deal — the competitor who stayed in touch closed the deal 6 months later.
> **Key takeaway**: A deal is never dead. Keep in touch. Timing changes everything.
