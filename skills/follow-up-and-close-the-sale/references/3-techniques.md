## Practical Techniques

### 1. The 24-Hour First Follow-Up

Send a follow-up within 24 hours of any contact — meeting, call, demo. Include a recap, next steps, and something of value.

### 2. The Value-First Email Template

Instead of "just checking in," send: "I came across this article/case study and thought of your situation. Thought it might be useful."

### 3. The 5-Touch Plan

Before starting with a prospect, plan for 5 follow-ups over 30-60 days. Each with a specific purpose and value proposition.

> **Case: The Planned Campaign** (Chapter 10): Shore advises creating a "follow-up campaign" for each prospect — a written plan of the 5 contacts you will make. This eliminates the anxiety of "should I call again?"
> **Key takeaway**: Plan your follow-up in advance. A plan eliminates anxiety. Know what you will say before you say it.

### 4. The "Not Now" Response

When a prospect says "not now," respond: "I appreciate you letting me know. When would be a good time to reconnect? And what would make that conversation more valuable for you?"

### 5. The Direct Close Approach

When the prospect is ready, ask directly: "Based on everything we have discussed, it sounds like this is a good fit. Are you ready to move forward?"

### 6. The Voice-to-Value Shift

Never leave a voicemail asking for a call back. Instead say: "I wanted to share something I think you will find useful. I will send it by email. Talk soon."

> **Case: The No-Ask Voicemail** (Chapter 11): Shore tested voicemails that asked for a call back vs. voicemails that offered value without asking. The value voicemails had a higher callback rate — because they did not feel like a sales pitch.
> **Key takeaway**: The less you ask for in a follow-up, the more you get. Remove the ask. Increase the value.
