# 4 — Anti-Patterns

> Source: *Life, on the Line* by Grant Achatz & Nick Kokonas.

## Common Errors

### 1. Trusting Convention Over Your Own Judgment

**The trap:** "No one has ever done it this way. It must be impossible."

**Why it fails:** Every innovation defied convention. No four-star restaurant had bare tables before Alinea. No one opened a restaurant with a 20+ course tasting menu in Chicago. The "it's never been done" argument is not a reason to stop. It is a reason to investigate why.

> **Case: The Business Plan Template (Chapter 13):** Kokonas recognized that Achatz's business plan "was made from an online template." But the vision was original. The plan could be improved. The vision could not be replicated. Do not confuse a flawed plan with a flawed concept.
> **Key takeaway**: A bad plan attached to a great vision is fixable. No plan at all attached to a mediocre vision is not.

> **Case: The Friends Who Said No (Chapter 14):** Everyone Kokonas trusted told him not to invest in a restaurant. "You seem like you have a great life. Why would you want to do that to yourself?" If he had listened to conventional wisdom, Alinea would not exist. The voices of caution are not always wrong — but they are not always right.
> **Key takeaway**: Listen to advice. Consider it seriously. But the final decision is yours. Sometimes the people who love you most will give you the worst advice because they are trying to protect you from risk you need to take.

**Why it fails:** Kokonas insisted on testing the relationship before committing. He asked Achatz if they would be friends. He watched how Achatz handled constructive criticism (the unsalted pasta). Partnerships fail because of trust issues, not business issues.

### 3. Letting Your Physical Condition Define Your Capability

**The trap:** "I cannot do my job right now. I should step away entirely."

**Why it fails:** Achatz could not taste for months. He still ran Alinea's kitchen. He delegated the tasting to his team while staying in charge of everything else. He did not abandon his post. He adapted.

### 4. Being Afraid to Commit Fully

**The trap:** "I'll keep my safety net. I can always go back."

**Why it fails:** Kokonas closed his hedge fund. He did not keep a fallback. Total commitment creates total focus. Half-measures produce half-results. This is not for everyone — but it is the price of building something unprecedented.

### 5. Ignoring the Business Side of a Creative Venture

**The trap:** "The art will speak for itself. The business will work itself out."

**Why it fails:** Achatz could not have built Alinea alone. He needed Kokonas to handle the business, the investors, the spreadsheets, the lease. Creative brilliance without business rigor is a hobby, not a company.
