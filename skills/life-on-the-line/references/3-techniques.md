# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *Life, on the Line* by Grant Achatz & Nick Kokonas.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The Partnership Vetting Conversation

Before committing to a business partner, ask: "Will we be friends? Can we challenge each other? Will we trust each other when things get hard?" Kokonas asked Achatz this before a single dollar was invested. If you cannot say yes to these questions, do not proceed.

> **Case: The Unsalted Pasta (Chapter 13):** Kokonas served Achatz pasta with marinara that he had forgotten to salt. Achatz asked for salt — enthusiastically, with a grin. The moment tested whether they could be honest with each other. Achatz passed. "I smiled — the friendship part wouldn't be a problem."
> **Key takeaway**: How a potential partner handles a mistake or a moment of honest feedback tells you everything about the future of the partnership.

> **Case: The Menu Design (Chapter 16):** Achatz designed a menu with multiple courses, no choices, and no salt on the table. Every convention of fine dining was questioned and redesigned. "Why put salt on the table? It says the food hasn't been seasoned properly." The removal of salt shakers was controversial. It became part of Alinea's philosophy.
> **Key takeaway**: The small conventions you break signal the big changes you are making. Each broken convention is a statement of philosophy.

When designing anything, list every convention in your industry and ask: why does this exist? Achatz questioned tablecloths, salt on tables, serviceware. Kokonas asked why investors always lose money in restaurants. The audit revealed assumptions that could be challenged.

### 3. The Crisis Delegation Protocol

When you cannot perform your core function (Achatz could not taste), identify the people you trust to fill the gap. Communicate clearly what you need from them. Then step back. Protect your energy for recovery. Return when you can contribute — not when you are perfect.

### 4. The "Write It to Believe It" Planning Method

Kokonas did not write the 14-page executive summary to convince investors. He wrote it to convince himself. When you are uncertain, write the plan. The act of writing clarifies your thinking and reveals whether your idea is real or just a fantasy.

### 5. The Impossible Deadline Strategy

Achatz insisted on a nine-month timeline. It forced rapid decisions, eliminated non-essentials, and created urgency. Impossible deadlines are not always reckless — they can force the kind of focus that makes the impossible possible.
