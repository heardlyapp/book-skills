# 2 — Key Principles

> Source: *Life, on the Line* by Grant Achatz & Nick Kokonas.

## The 7 Principles

### 1. Obsession Is the Price of Greatness

Achatz did not get to the top by being balanced. He worked 16-hour days, practiced obsessively, and thought about food constantly. His childhood: standing on a milk crate to reach the griddle, flipping eggs for hours until his arm hurt. Excellence is not a part-time pursuit.

> **Case: The French Laundry Grind (Chapters 7-8):** At The French Laundry, Achatz worked 14-16 hour days, often staying after his shift to practice techniques. He was not the most talented cook in the kitchen — but he was the most determined. Keller noticed. "I learned that talent is not enough. You have to outwork everyone."

### 2. Trust Before Capital

Kokonas asked Achatz if they would be friends before he asked about the money. Partnership is not a transaction — it is a relationship that must survive stress, disagreement, and failure. Trust must come before investment.

### 3. Constraints Are Creative Fuel

Alinea had no tablecloths because Achatz refused to use cheap plywood tables. The constraint — bare tables must be beautiful — drove the entire design. Kokonas calculated that $42,000/year in linen costs could fund mahogany tables. The constraint became a signature.

> **Case: The Nine-Month Timeline (Chapter 15):** Achatz insisted they could open Alinea in nine months. Everyone said it was impossible. Kokonas reluctantly agreed. The impossible deadline forced ruthless prioritization. They opened in nine months. The constraint made them faster, better, and more creative.

### 4. Your Purpose Must Be Stronger Than Your Body

Achatz could not taste for months after treatment. He still ran the kitchen. He relied on his team's palates, his memory of flavors, and his will. When your senses fail you, your purpose must carry you.

### 5. The Partnership Is Tested in Crisis, Not in Success

The true test of Kokonas and Achatz's partnership was not the successful opening — it was the cancer. Kokonas ran the business while Achatz was in treatment. He protected the team. He kept the restaurant alive. The partnership proved itself when it mattered most.

### 6. Question Everything

Achatz questioned the necessity of tablecloths, salt on tables, serviceware design, and the structure of the menu. He did not accept conventions. Every element of Alinea was questioned and redesigned.

### 7. Life Is Lived on the Line

"On the line" — in the kitchen, during service — is where everything is at stake. Achatz extends this: the defining moments of life are the ones where you are fully present, fully committed, and fully at risk. That is where you prove who you are.
