# The Sex/Gender Distinction and Woman as Situation

> Source: The Second Sex, Introduction, Book 1, Part 1

## Core Framework

Beauvoir's most famous claim — "One is not born, but rather becomes, woman" — is the foundation of modern feminist theory. It distinguishes between biological sex (a fact) and social gender (a construction).

### The Argument

Beauvoir argues that throughout Western history, man has been positioned as the Subject, the absolute, the standard. Woman has been defined as the Other — relative to man, dependent on him for her definition. No woman has ever written a book about "man" as a category. Men wrote books about "woman" — because man is the default and woman is the deviation.

### Sex vs Gender

| Aspect | Sex (Biological) | Gender (Social) |
|--------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Basis | Chromosomes, anatomy, hormones | Cultural roles, expectations, norms |
| Determines | Certain physical differences | Entire way of life, status, opportunities |
| Changeable? | Limited | Changeable through social transformation |
| Beauvoir's view | A fact, not a destiny | A situation that can be transcended |

### The Situation

To be a woman is not to have a certain biology — it is to find oneself in a particular social situation. The female body has certain characteristics, but the meaning of those characteristics is determined by society. A woman is not born into a fixed destiny; she becomes what her situation makes her — and she can change that situation.

> **Case: The Historical Absence of Women** (Introduction)
> Beauvoir opens with a provocation: why have there been no great women artists, philosophers, or leaders? Is it because women are naturally inferior? No, she argues — it is because women have been systematically excluded from education, public life, and the means of creative achievement. The absence of great women is not evidence of female inferiority but evidence of female exclusion. The question is not "what have women achieved?" but "what have women been prevented from achieving?"
> **Key takeaway**: The absence of women from history's highest achievements is not a measure of their capacity — it is a measure of the obstacles they faced. When judging women's accomplishments, the question must always be: what was the situation that made those accomplishments possible — or impossible?

> **Case: The Male Gaze and the Definition of Woman** (Book 1)
> Beauvoir analyzes how men have defined woman throughout history. From Aristotle ("woman is a misbegotten male") to Thomas Aquinas ("woman is a defective male") to modern science, men have defined woman in terms of her difference from man. She is the exception, the problem, the mystery. Beauvoir argues: no group has ever defined itself as the Other. Men defined woman as Other because they had the power to do so. This power to name and define is itself a form of domination.
> **Key takeaway**: The power to define is the power to control. When men define what woman "is," they set the terms of women's existence. The first step toward liberation is for women to claim the power of self-definition.
