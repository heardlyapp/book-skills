# Woman as the Other — The Fundamental Category of Human Thought

> Source: The Second Sex, Introduction, Book 1

## The Other as a Category

Beauvoir argues that the Self/Other distinction is one of the most fundamental categories of human consciousness. Every consciousness sets itself apart from an Other. The Self is the subject; the Other is the object. The problem: throughout recorded history, men have claimed the position of the Self for themselves and assigned women the position of the Other — permanently, absolutely, without reciprocity.

### How the Other Operates

| Mechanism | How It Affects Women |
|-----------|---------------------|
| The Self defines the Other | Man defines woman; woman does not define man |
| The Other is the problem | "What is woman?" is the eternal question — "What is man?" is never asked |
| The Other is the mystery | Women are mystified — treated as inscrutable, irrational, unknowable |
| The Other is not a peer | Woman is not man's equal partner but his "other half," his helpmate, his complement |

### The Permanence of Woman as Other

Beauvoir notes that other groups that have been treated as Other — the working class, colonized peoples, racial minorities — have fought back and demanded subject status. Women have done so too, but with less success because:
1. Women have no shared history or geography that unites them
2. Women are distributed throughout the male world, living with the "enemy"
3. Women are raised from birth to accept their Otherness

> **Case: The Myth of the Eternal Feminine** (Book 1)
> Beauvoir examines the concept of the "Eternal Feminine" — the belief that there is an unchanging, transhistorical essence of womanhood that all women share. This myth appears in religion (the Virgin Mary), in literature (Dante's Beatrice, Goethe's Eternal Feminine), and in philosophy (Schopenhauer, Kierkegaard). Beauvoir argues that the Eternal Feminine is a male invention — a projection of male desires and fears onto women. It has no basis in female experience. The Eternal Feminine is not what women are but what men want women to be.
> **Key takeaway**: Every claim about "what women are" should be met with the question: who said this, and what interests does it serve? The Eternal Feminine is a myth that serves those who benefit from keeping women in their traditional place.

> **Case: The Two Sides of the Myth** (Book 1)
> Beauvoir shows that the male myths of woman are deeply contradictory. Woman is pure and impure, Madonna and whore, life-giving and death-bringing, the source of all comfort and the source of all temptation. Men simultaneously worship women and despise them, put them on pedestals and lock them in houses. The contradiction is not a flaw in the myth — it's the point. The myth of woman serves male needs, and those needs are contradictory. Woman is whatever man needs her to be at any given moment.
> **Key takeaway**: The contradictory myths about women — pure and impure, nurturing and devouring, spiritual and carnal — reveal not the nature of women but the confusion of men who want to control what they cannot understand and cannot live without.
