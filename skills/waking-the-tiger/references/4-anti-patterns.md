# What Goes Wrong — Anti-Patterns

Levine's approach is as much about what NOT to do as what to do. Many common approaches to trauma actually make it worse.

## Anti-Pattern 1: Catharsis Without Completion

The most dangerous mistake: encouraging the client to fully relive the traumatic event in the belief that "getting it out" will heal it.

**Case: The Vietnam Veteran.** Levine describes working with a veteran who had been in multiple cathartic therapies. Each time, he would vividly relive the battle, screaming, crying, shaking. Each time, he felt worse afterward. The problem: catharsis without completion re-traumatizes. The nervous system is re-activated but not allowed to discharge. The veteran was not healing — he was drilling the trauma deeper.

**Case: The Reliving Trap.** Levine writes: "Some cathartic methods that encourage intense emotional reliving of trauma may be harmful. I believe that in the long run, cathartic approaches create a dependency on continuing catharsis and encourage the emergence of so-called 'false memories.'" The key distinction: reliving the trauma floods the nervous system. Completing the defensive response discharges it. They look similar but produce opposite results.

## Anti-Pattern 2: The Neo-Cortex Takeover

The very thing that makes humans intelligent — our capacity for rational thought — is also what makes us vulnerable to trauma. The neo-cortex overrides the instinctual response.

**Case: The Rationalization Reflex.** "I should be over this by now." "It wasn't that bad." "Other people have it worse." These rationalizations are the neo-cortex trying to manage an instinctual response with logic. It does not work. The body does not care about rational arguments. The energy is still there.

**Case: The "Get On With Your Life" Trap.** This is the most common advice given to trauma survivors. It is also the most damaging. Levine writes: "Getting on with life' is a powerful but misguided solution. The energy does not go away because you ignore it." It goes underground and emerges as symptoms — anxiety, depression, chronic pain, relationship problems.

## Anti-Pattern 3: The Fixed-Freeze State

The immobility response is designed to be temporary. When it becomes chronic, the person lives in a state of permanent freeze.

**Case: The Perpetual Startle.** The chronically traumatized person startles at ordinary sounds. A door closing. A car backfiring. A touch on the shoulder. The freeze response has become default mode. The person is not living — they are surviving. Hypervigilance is the constant scanning for threat. Numbness is the opposite side of the same coin.

**Case: Mrs. Thayer.** A client, Mrs. Thayer, had been in a minor car accident months earlier. She came to Levine because she could not stop scanning the road for danger. Her neck was chronically tight. She could not relax. The accident was minor, but the freeze response had become stuck. Her body was still waiting for the crash that had already happened.

## Anti-Pattern 4: Misidentifying the Source

Trauma can be caused by events that do not seem "traumatic" in the conventional sense. A fall. A medical procedure. A difficult birth. A frightening experience that the conscious mind has forgotten.

**Case: The Dental Procedure.** Many people are traumatized by dental procedures — not because they are painful, but because they involve helplessness, invasiveness, and sometimes the sensation of suffocation (from the rubber dam, the impression material, or the dental dam). These traumas are often unrecognized because "it was just the dentist."

**Case: The Birth Trauma.** A difficult birth can be traumatic for both mother and child. The mother may feel helpless, out of control, and terrified. The baby may experience the birth as overwhelming and inescapable. These early traumas can set patterns of reactivity that last a lifetime. They are not always remembered, but they are always felt.

## Anti-Pattern 5: Medication as the Only Solution

Medication can be helpful in managing trauma symptoms, but it is not a cure. The trapped energy is still there — it is just suppressed.

**Case: The Symptom Mask.** A client taking benzodiazepines for anxiety after a car accident. The medication reduces the anxiety. But when the client stops taking it, the anxiety returns. The medication has not resolved the trauma — it has only suppressed the symptoms. The frozen energy remains.

**Case: The Window of Tolerance and Drugs.** Levine acknowledges that medication can be useful in the early stages of trauma work, particularly when the client is too activated to do body-based work. But the goal is not to keep the client medicated indefinitely. The goal is to help the client develop the capacity to self-regulate — to expand the window of tolerance so that the medication is no longer needed.
