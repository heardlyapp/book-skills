# The Principles of Trauma Healing

## I. Trauma Is Not a Sentence — It Is a Message

The most important principle of Levine's approach: trauma is not permanent damage. It is a physiological response that has not completed its cycle. Like a note of music that has been struck and not allowed to fade, it continues to vibrate.

**Case: Nancy.** Levine introduces Nancy, a client who experienced a minor car accident. Years later, she still could not relax in cars. She had panic attacks. Her doctors told her "it was all in her head." Levine explains: it was not in her head — it was in her nervous system. The energy from the accident had never discharged. When Levine helped Nancy gently track her body sensations — the tightness in her shoulders, the urge to brace — she began to tremble. The trembling was the discharge. Within a few sessions, the panic attacks stopped.

**Case: Bob Barklay.** Bob was in a near-fatal motorcycle accident. Afterward, he could not sleep, could not concentrate, and had violent outbursts. He was diagnosed with PTSD. Medication helped temporarily but did not resolve the underlying condition. Working with Levine, Bob began to track the sensations of the accident — not the story, but the body's memory of the impact. As he allowed himself to feel the vibration of the crash in his body, his nervous system began to complete the cycle. His sleep improved. His outbursts stopped.

## II. The Felt Sense — The Body's Inner Voice

The "felt sense" is the key to Somatic Experiencing. It is the body's subtle awareness of its own internal state — not emotion, not thought, but direct sensation.

**Case: The Medusa Complex.** Levine uses the myth of Medusa to illustrate the trauma response. In Greek myth, looking at Medusa turns you to stone. This is what freeze feels like — the body becomes rigid, frozen, stone-like. The felt sense of this state is the key to healing. The client does not need to fight Medusa or slay her. They need to feel the stone-ness, and then feel the thaw.

**Case: The Shaking That Heals.** When clients in Levine's practice begin to emerge from the freeze state, they often shake involuntarily. The shaking can be gentle or intense. Modern culture tells us this is bad — "control yourself," "stop trembling." Levine says the opposite: the shaking is the healing. "When the animal emerges from the frozen state, it shakes vigorously. This shaking is the discharge of the enormous energy that was mobilized for survival."

## III. The Three Responses — Fight, Flight, Freeze

All mammals have three responses to threat. Two are well-known. One is neglected but crucial.

**Case: The Flood of Energy.** When a human is threatened, the nervous system mobilizes enormous energy. Heart rate increases. Muscles tense. Pupils dilate. Blood vessels constrict. The body is preparing to fight or flee. But if neither is possible — if the threat is overwhelming or inescapable — the body defaults to the third option: freeze.

**Case: The Child Under the Knife.** A child undergoing surgery experiences the freeze response. The anesthesia causes immobility. The body associates this helplessness with danger. Even if the child has no memory of the surgery, the body remembers. Later in life, the child may develop phobias of doctors, panic in medical settings, or generalized anxiety. The freeze response was the best available option at the time — but it became stuck.

## IV. Symptoms Are the Energy Trying to Complete

The flashbacks, the nightmares, the hypervigilance — these are not signs of damage. They are the organism's desperate attempt to complete what was interrupted.

**Case: The Flashback.** A war veteran hears a car backfire and is instantly on the ground, heart pounding, in full combat mode. This is not a memory — it is a re-experiencing. The nervous system is trying to complete the defensive response that was interrupted in the actual battle. The problem is not that the response happens. The problem is that it never completes.

**Case: The Panic Attack.** A woman who was assaulted begins having panic attacks in crowded spaces. Her body is still trying to escape from the assault. The panic attack is the fight-or-flight response activating in a context where neither fighting nor fleeing is appropriate. The energy that was mobilized years ago is still trying to discharge.

## V. Titration — The Gentle Approach

Levine's method uses "titration" — the careful, gradual introduction of small amounts of traumatic activation. The term comes from chemistry, where a solution is added drop by drop. The same principle applies to trauma.

**Case: The Window of Tolerance.** Every person has a "window of tolerance" — the range of activation within which they can function. Trauma narrows this window. A traumatized person goes from calm to overwhelmed very quickly. Titration works by touching the edge of the activation and then returning to resource — expanding the window gradually.

**Case: The Gradient Descent.** Levine describes working with a client who had been in a severe car accident. Instead of asking her to relive the crash, he asked her to notice a small sensation of tightness in her chest. Just that. Then he asked her to notice what happened next — a slight warmth, a release. Then back to a place of safety. Drop by drop, the frozen energy thawed. No catharsis. No reliving. Just gentle, repeated pendulation between activation and resource.
