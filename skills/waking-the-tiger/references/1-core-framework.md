# The Somatic Framework — Core Concepts

Peter Levine's central insight: trauma is not primarily a psychological problem but a physiological one. The body holds the key to healing.

## The Impala Principle

The book opens with a vivid scene: a herd of impala grazing peacefully. A cheetah attacks. The young impala falls to the ground, stone-still — not pretending to be dead, but entering an involuntary altered state called the "immobility response." If the impala survives, it will shake off the frozen energy and return to grazing as if nothing happened. It has completed the cycle.

**Case: The Impala That Survives.** The impala freezes when the cheetah makes contact. Inside, its nervous system is still supercharged at 70 miles per hour. But when the threat passes, the animal trembles, shakes, takes deep spontaneous breaths, and the energy discharges. "The animal will literally shake off the residual effects of the immobility response and gain full control of its body. It will then return to its normal life as if nothing had happened."

**Case: The Human Who Does Not Complete.** Humans do the same thing — we freeze in response to overwhelming threat. But unlike the impala, we do not complete the cycle. Our neo-cortex intervenes: we stop ourselves from shaking because it looks undignified. We suppress the trembling because we are supposed to be in control. We "get on with our lives" without discharging the frozen energy. That energy stays trapped in the nervous system, creating the symptoms of trauma.

## The Triune Brain Model

Levine uses the triune brain model to explain why trauma happens:

- **Reptilian Brain (R-Complex):** Controls instinctive survival responses — fight, flight, freeze. Operates below conscious awareness. The most ancient part of the brain, shared with reptiles and all mammals.
- **Limbic System (Mammalian Brain):** Emotions, social bonding, attachment, memory. Trauma disrupts the normal functioning of this system.
- **Neo-Cortex (Human Brain):** Language, logic, self-awareness, planning. The newest part of the brain. Its capacity to override instinct is both a gift and a curse.

**Case: The Neo-Cortex Override.** When a human is in a car accident, the reptilian brain mobilizes enormous energy for fight or flight. But the neo-cortex recognizes that fighting the car or fleeing the moving vehicle is not a viable option. It overrides the instinct. The energy that was mobilized has nowhere to go. It stays frozen. That frozen energy is the trauma.

**Case: The Unfreezing That Must Happen.** Levine's method (Somatic Experiencing) works by helping the client access the reptilian and limbic levels of the brain through the felt sense — not through talking about the trauma, but through tracking bodily sensations. The neo-cortex must step aside and let the older brain structures complete their work.

## Trauma Is Energy, Not Story

This is Levine's most radical claim: you do not need to remember or narrate the traumatic event to heal from it. In fact, talking about it can retraumatize. What matters is discharging the frozen energy through the body.

**Case: The Auto Accident Client.** Levine describes working with clients who have been in serious car accidents. They don't need to tell the story of the crash in detail. They need to track the sensations in their body — the tightness in the chest, the tremor in the legs, the impulse to push against the floor. As they stay with these sensations without being overwhelmed, the nervous system completes its discharge. The client shakes, trembles, takes deep breaths. The energy moves. The trauma resolves.

**Case: The Surgical Patient.** Many people are traumatized by surgery — not because of the pain, but because of the helplessness and the invasion of the body. Levine notes that the anesthesia interrupts the body's natural response to threat. The patient goes under conscious and wakes up with the trauma energy still frozen. Healing requires reconnecting with the body's experience during and after the procedure — not the memory of it, but the sensation of it.

## When to Use This Framework

- When you or someone you know is experiencing symptoms that seem to have no explanation
- When talk therapy has not resolved the effects of a traumatic event
- When you feel "stuck" in patterns of hypervigilance, numbing, or reactivity
- When working with accident survivors, violence survivors, or medical trauma
