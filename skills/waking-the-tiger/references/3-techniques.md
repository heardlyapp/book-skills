# Somatic Experiencing — Practical Techniques

Levine's book is a theory of trauma and a practical manual for healing. These are the core techniques.

## Technique 1: Tracking the Felt Sense

The fundamental skill of Somatic Experiencing. You cannot heal trauma by thinking about it. You must learn to sense your body from the inside.

**How to do it:** Find a comfortable seated or lying position. Close your eyes. Bring your attention to your body. Notice any sensations: warmth, coolness, tingling, pressure, tightness, numbness, vibration. Do not judge or change them. Simply observe.

**Case: The Airplane Exercise.** Levine includes an exercise: "Pretend you are in an airplane flying at 30,000 feet. Notice what happens in your body. Now pretend you are sitting on the front step of a friend's house on a warm summer evening. Notice the difference." Most people notice their body tightens in the airplane scenario and relaxes on the front step. This is the felt sense in action. The body responds to the imagination as if it were real.

**Case: The Driving Exercise.** "Pretend you are driving your car on the freeway. Traffic isn't bad, but a car suddenly cuts in front of you, forcing you to brake. Notice what happens in your body." The felt sense records the startle response — the clench, the brace, the held breath. Then notice what happens when the danger passes. The body begins to release. This natural release is the healing mechanism. Trauma happens when the release is blocked.

## Technique 2: Pendulation

Pendulation is the rhythmic movement between activation (the trauma energy) and resource (safety, grounding, positive sensation). Healing happens in the oscillation between these two poles.

**How to do it:** Start by finding a resource — a memory, image, or sensation that feels safe and pleasant. Feel it fully in your body. Then, gently touch the edge of the traumatic activation — a small sensation of tightness or discomfort. Stay with it briefly, then return to the resource. Repeat, gradually expanding the window of tolerance.

**Case: Nancy's Pendulation.** Nancy, the car accident survivor, tracked a tightness in her chest (the activation). Then Levine asked her to notice the warmth of her hands on her lap (the resource). She moved between the two — tightness, warmth, tightness, warmth. Each time, the tightness softened slightly. She was thawing the freeze without being overwhelmed by it.

**Case: The Ocean Rhythm.** Levine compares pendulation to the rhythm of the ocean: "The waves come in and go out. The tide rises and falls. This natural rhythm is reflected in our nervous systems." The goal is not to eliminate the activation but to learn to ride its natural rhythm. The wave of activation comes, peaks, and recedes. If you can stay with it without panicking, it will complete its cycle.

## Technique 3: Titration — The Smallest Possible Dose

Do not work with the whole trauma at once. Work with the smallest piece that the nervous system can handle. In chemistry, titration involves adding a solution one drop at a time until the reaction is complete. Same principle here.

**How to do it:** When you feel a sensation related to the trauma, do not dive into the full story. Stay with just the sensation — the tightness in the throat, the pressure in the chest, the trembling in the legs. Let that sensation unfold at its own pace. If it becomes too intense, return to resource.

**Case: The Surgical Trauma Client.** A client who had been traumatized by surgery did not need to relive the operation. Levine asked her to notice just the slight queasiness in her stomach. That was the entry point. Over several sessions, she tracked the queasiness, felt it spread, noticed images arising, and eventually completed the discharge through spontaneous shaking and tears. No catharsis. No reliving. Just gentle, body-based processing.

**Case: The Accident Victim's Leg Tremors.** A man who had been in a motorcycle accident felt tremors in his legs when he thought about the crash. Levine did not ask him to describe the crash. He asked him to simply notice the tremors and let them happen. The tremors intensified, then spread through his body, then subsided. He felt a wave of warmth and relief. The energy had discharged.

## Technique 4: Resourcing

Before working with traumatic activation, you must establish a resource — a felt sense of safety, strength, or connection that the client can return to when the activation becomes too intense.

**How to do it:** Ask yourself: What is a place, person, memory, or sensation that makes me feel safe, strong, or peaceful? It could be a childhood memory, a favorite spot in nature, the feeling of a loved one's hand, the image of a protective figure. Feel it in your body. Let it anchor you.

**Case: The Protectress.** A client who had been assaulted felt completely helpless. Levine asked her to remember a time when she had felt powerful — she recalled winning a race in high school. The felt sense of that memory (the exhilaration, the strength in her legs) became her resource. Each time the trauma work became intense, she returned to this sensation. Over time, the resource of her own strength became stronger than the trauma of the assault.

**Case: The Tree.** Another client found resource in the image of a large tree behind her childhood home. She could feel the solidity of the trunk, the spread of the branches, the deep roots in the earth. This felt sense of groundedness and stability became her anchor. She could access it whenever the trauma work became destabilizing.

## Technique 5: Completion of the Defensive Response

The final step: helping the body complete the fight or flight response that was interrupted at the time of the trauma. This is not about actually fighting or fleeing — it is about allowing the body to feel the impulse to do so, and letting that impulse complete itself.

**How to do it:** In a safe, supported context, notice the impulse in your body to push, pull, run, or strike. Let the movement happen, but in slow motion, in small doses. A push against a wall. A slow-motion run. A gentle strike against a pillow. The goal is not to re-enact the trauma but to complete the nervous system's response.

**Case: The Pushed Wall.** A client who had been pinned in a car accident felt an impulse to push against the crushed dashboard. In session, Levine had her place her hands against a wall and slowly push. As she pushed, she felt the tension in her arms release. She began to shake. Tears came. The "pushing" that was interrupted by the accident was finally completed — not in a car, but safely in a therapy room.

**Case: The Slow-Motion Run.** A man who had been mugged felt an impulse to run. In session, he allowed himself to move his legs in slow-motion running movements while lying on a mat. The felt sense of running, even in slow motion, allowed the flight response to complete. His chronic leg tension resolved.
