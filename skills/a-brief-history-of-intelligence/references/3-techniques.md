# 3 — Mammalian and Primate Brains

> Source: "A Brief History of Intelligence" by Max Bennett

## Breakthroughs 3 & 4: The Social Brain

Mammals added the neocortex, enabling flexible, context-dependent behavior. Primates further expanded it, especially the prefrontal cortex, enabling theory of mind and complex social reasoning.

> **Case: The Neocortex Innovation** (Chapter on Mammalian Brain): "The neocortex was the mammalian breakthrough. Unlike the older brain structures that are specialized for specific tasks, the neocortex is flexible. It can learn new patterns, adapt to new situations, and integrate information from multiple sensory streams." Bennett explains that the neocortex is what makes mammals capable of learning and adaptation beyond instinct.
> **Key takeaway**: The neocortex's flexibility is what makes mammals so adaptable. It's not pre-wired for specific behaviors — it learns from experience. This is the breakthrough that enables complex social behavior, play, and parental care.

> **Case: Theory of Mind in Primates** (Chapter on Primate Brain): "Primates, especially great apes, can understand that others have beliefs, desires, and intentions different from their own. This theory of mind is the foundation of social intelligence — deception, cooperation, teaching, and complex group dynamics." Bennett traces how the enlarged prefrontal cortex in primates enabled this social cognition.
> **Key takeaway**: Theory of mind is not unique to humans — it exists in rudimentary form in other great apes. What makes humans special is the degree to which we can model others' minds and use that understanding for cooperation and culture.
