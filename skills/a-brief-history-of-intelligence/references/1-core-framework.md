# 1 — The Five Breakthroughs of Brain Evolution

> Source: "A Brief History of Intelligence" by Max Bennett

## The Layered Brain

Bennett's central framework: intelligence evolved through five distinct breakthroughs over 600 million years. Each breakthrough added a new capability layer without replacing the previous one.

> **Case: The First Breakthrough** (Chapter 1 — The World Before Brains): "Life existed on Earth for over three billion years before the first nervous system. The first breakthrough was simple: the ability to detect and respond to the environment. Neurons evolved to coordinate movement in bilateral organisms." Bennett traces how a few specialized cells became the foundation of all intelligence.
> **Key takeaway**: Intelligence didn't emerge from nothing. The first nervous system solved a concrete problem: coordinating movement in creatures that needed to navigate a complex environment. Every subsequent breakthrough built on this foundation.

> **Case: The Five Breakthroughs Framework** (Introduction): "There are five key innovations in the evolution of intelligence: first nervous systems (600M years ago), vertebrate brains (500M), mammalian brains (250M), primate brains (60M), and human brains (300K years ago). Each solved a specific challenge." Bennett presents this as the organizing framework for the entire book.
> **Key takeaway**: The five breakthroughs are not arbitrary. Each corresponds to a major evolutionary transition and a specific adaptive challenge. By understanding the problems evolution solved, we can understand what "intelligence" actually is.
