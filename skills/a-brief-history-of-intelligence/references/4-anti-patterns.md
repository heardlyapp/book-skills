# 4 — Human Intelligence and Common Misconceptions

> Source: "A Brief History of Intelligence" by Max Bennett

## Anti-Pattern: The Ladder Fallacy

One of the most persistent misconceptions about evolution: thinking it is a ladder with humans at the top. Bennett argues this distorts both our understanding of evolution and our approach to AI.

> **Case: The Ladder of Progress** (Introduction — A Final Point About Ladders): "It is tempting to see evolution as a progression from 'lower' to 'higher' forms of intelligence — from bacteria to fish to mammals to humans. But this is wrong. Every living species today is equally evolved. Evolution has no direction, only adaptation to changing environments." Bennett explicitly warns against the ladder fallacy.
> **Key takeaway**: The ladder fallacy leads to two errors: (1) underestimating the intelligence of other animals, which have evolved to solve their own problems perfectly well, and (2) overestimating human uniqueness, when in fact our capabilities are extensions of capabilities present in other species.

## Anti-Pattern: Underestimating Animal Intelligence

A related mistake: assuming that many cognitive abilities are uniquely human when they appear in simpler forms throughout the animal kingdom.

> **Case: Tool Use and Problem-Solving** (Chapter on Primate and Bird Intelligence): "Tool use was long thought to be uniquely human, then uniquely primate. But New Caledonian crows manufacture and use tools, solving problems that require causal reasoning. Octopuses navigate complex environments, solve puzzles, and learn from observation." Bennett presents these examples throughout the book.
> **Key takeaway**: Many "uniquely human" capabilities exist in simpler forms across the animal kingdom. What makes humans different is not the presence of these capabilities but their degree and combination, and especially the ability to transmit knowledge across generations.
