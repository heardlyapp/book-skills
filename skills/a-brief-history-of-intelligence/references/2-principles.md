# 2 — The First Brains and Basic Learning

> Source: "A Brief History of Intelligence" by Max Bennett

## Breakthroughs 1 & 2: Nervous Systems and Vertebrate Brains

The earliest brains enabled simple learning — habituation, sensitization, and the beginnings of associative learning. The vertebrate brain added the hindbrain, midbrain, and forebrain, enabling more sophisticated behaviors.

> **Case: The Sea Slug** (Chapter 2 — The First Neurons): "The sea slug Aplysia has only 20,000 neurons — far simpler than the human brain's 86 billion. Yet it is capable of learning: habituation (learning to ignore irrelevant stimuli) and sensitization (learning to be more responsive to dangerous stimuli). These are the foundations of all learning." Bennett uses the sea slug to show how basic learning mechanisms evolved long before complex brains.
> **Key takeaway**: The most fundamental forms of learning — getting used to things and becoming alert to danger — are ancient and simple. They don't require a neocortex. They are the building blocks upon which all more complex learning is built.

> **Case: The First Reinforcement Learning Algorithm** (Chapter 2): "The first RL algorithm was built in 1951 by Marvin Minsky. But nature had been running RL algorithms for 500 million years. The vertebrate brain's dopamine system is a biological RL system — it rewards actions that lead to positive outcomes and punishes those that don't." Bennett connects dopamine to the evolution of reinforcement learning.
> **Key takeaway**: Reinforcement learning is not a recent AI invention. It is one of the oldest and most fundamental learning mechanisms in biology. The dopamine system in vertebrates and the basal ganglia system in mammals are biological implementations of RL.
