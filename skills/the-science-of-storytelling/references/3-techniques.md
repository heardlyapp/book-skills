## Practical Techniques

### 1. The Question Hook

Begin with a question (explicit or implicit) that makes the reader want to know the answer. "What happens next?" is the engine of narrative.

### 2. The Flawed Character Template

Create a character with: a want (conscious goal), a need (unconscious, opposite of want), and a flaw (the barrier to getting what they need).

> **Case: The Character Worksheet** (Chapter 4): Storr provides a template: What does the character consciously want? What do they actually need? What is the flaw that prevents them from getting it? Answer these three questions, and you have a character.
> **Key takeaway**: Character is structure. A well-designed character generates plot automatically.

### 3. The Three-Part Attention Control

Each scene should: (1) Establish where we are and whose mind we are in. (2) Create a question. (3) Deliver a small payoff while setting up the next question.

### 4. The Change Arc Test

At the end of your story, ask: is the character different from how they started? If the answer is no, the story is not finished.

### 5. The Moral World Check

Every story creates a moral world. What does your story say about what matters? What values does it demonstrate?

### 6. The Show-Don't-Tell Principle

Instead of "He was angry," show: "His fists clenched. His jaw tightened. He looked away." The reader's brain simulates the clenching, the tightening, the looking away — and experiences the anger.

> **Case: The Show vs Tell Example** (Chapter 6): Storr compares two descriptions. "John was sad" (tell) vs "John stared at the rain streaming down the window. His shoulders slumped." (show). The second activates the reader's simulation.
> **Key takeaway**: Show, do not tell. The reader's brain simulates specific details, not abstractions.
