## 6 Anti-Patterns of Bad Storytelling

### 1. "Plot Comes First"

Plot does not come first. Character comes first. Plot is what happens when a character tries to get what they want.

### 2. "Happy Characters Are Interesting"

Happy people do not generate stories. Stories come from struggle, conflict, and dissatisfaction.

### 3. "The Reader Will Figure It Out"

Readers are not mind readers. You must control what they imagine. Ambiguity is not depth — it is confusion.

> **Case: The Confused Reader** (Chapter 3): Storr critiques stories where the author leaves out crucial information. The reader cannot simulate what they cannot imagine. The story fails.
> **Key takeaway**: The reader can only simulate what they can imagine. You must give them the material to build the simulation.

### 4. "All Description Is Good Description"

Every sentence should serve the story. Description that does not advance character, conflict, or change is wasted.

### 5. "Happily Ever After Is Satisfying"

A happy ending is satisfying only if the character earned it through struggle. A free happy ending is unsatisfying.

### 6. "Stories Are Just for Entertainment"

Stories are how humans make meaning. They are not a luxury — they are a necessity.

> **Case: The Overwritten Scene** (Chapter 7): Storr shows a scene with too much description. Every detail was realistic — but none served the story. The reader's attention was scattered, not focused.
> **Key takeaway**: Every detail must serve the story. If it does not advance character, conflict, or change, cut it.
