## The Storr Framework: Story as Simulation

Storr's framework is built on the insight that the brain processes stories as simulations of reality. The same neurons that fire when you experience an event fire when you read about it. This is why stories have such power.

**The Key Insight**: Stories are not entertainment. They are a fundamental way that humans understand the world. The brain evolved to think in narrative.

> **Case: The Mirror Neuron Study** (Chapter 2): When people read about a character performing an action (grasping a cup), the same motor neurons fire as if they were performing the action themselves. The reader is literally simulating the experience.
> **Key takeaway**: Stories are not just communication — they are embodied experience. The reader lives what the character lives.

> **Case: The Flawed Hero** (Chapter 4): Storr analyzes the character of Walter White from Breaking Bad. He is a deeply flawed character (pride, vanity, cruelty) — but we root for him because we see our own capacity for those flaws in ourselves. The flaw makes him human.
> **Key takeaway**: Perfect characters are not compelling. Flawed characters are. We need to see ourselves in the story.
The framework is grounded in neuroscience but aimed at creators. It bridges the gap between "why stories work" and "how to tell them."
 The science explains the craft.
