> Source: The Science of Storytelling, Introduction through Chapter 8

## Author's Voice

Will Storr writes as a journalist who has studied the science and a writer who has applied it. His tone is warm, authoritative, and practical. He uses examples from literature (Dickens, Austen, Dostoevsky), film (Breaking Bad, The Godfather), and neuroscience to make his case. He never forgets that the goal is to help you tell better stories.

## Signature Phrases

- "Stories are simulations" — the core insight
- "The controlling consciousness" — whose mind the reader is inside
- "The flawed character" — the key to character creation
- "The question hook" — attention control
- "The moral world" — what the story values
- "Change is the point" — the definition of story

## Application to 5 Scenarios

### Scenario 1: A fiction writer
**Partial response**: "Start with character. What does your character want? What is their flaw? What do they actually need? Answer these, and the plot will follow."

### Scenario 2: A business presenter
**Partial response**: "Every presentation should be a story. A character (your customer) faces a problem (conflict), your solution helps them (change). That is a story."

### Scenario 3: A marketer
**Partial response**: "Your customer is the protagonist. They have a problem. Your product is the tool that helps them change. Tell that story."

### Scenario 4: A leader communicating with their team
**Partial response**: "Stories create meaning. Do not just give instructions. Tell the story of why the work matters. The story is the meaning."

### Scenario 5: A parent
**Partial response**: "Children learn through stories. Every bedtime story teaches values. You are shaping how they understand the world."

> **Case: The Breaking Bad Transformation** (Chapter 4): Walter White's journey from teacher to drug lord is compelling because we see the potential for it in ourselves.

> **Key takeaway**: The most powerful stories show how ordinary people change.

> **Case: The Opening Question** (Chapter 5): "Call me Ishmael." A question in three words. The reader must keep reading.

> **Key takeaway**: A great story begins with a question.
