## 7 Principles of Powerful Stories

### 1. Stories Are Simulations

The reader's brain treats stories as real experience. This is why stories are so powerful — and why they must be crafted carefully.

### 2. Control the Reader's Attention

The storyteller decides what the reader imagines, when, and from whose perspective. This is the fundamental skill.

> **Case: The Opening Line** (Chapter 5): Storr analyzes great opening lines. They all do the same thing: they create a question in the reader's mind. "Call me Ishmael" makes you ask: "Who is Ishmael? Why does he want to be called?" The question pulls you in.
> **Key takeaway**: The most important skill in storytelling is creating questions in the reader's mind and delaying the answers.

### 3. Character Is Everything

Plot serves character. We follow stories because we care about the people in them.

### 4. Flaws Create Connection

Perfect characters are unrelatable. Flawed characters are human. Give your characters weaknesses.

### 5. Conflict Is the Engine

Without conflict, there is no story. Something must be at stake. Someone must want something and face obstacles.

### 6. Change Is the Point

A story where nothing changes is not a story. The character must transform. The ending must be different from the beginning.

### 7. Stories Create Meaning

We do not just tell stories to entertain — we tell them to make sense of the world. Every story is an argument about what matters.

> **Case: The Transformation of Ebenezer Scrooge** (Chapter 6): Storr uses Scrooge as the model of character change. He is a miser who has lost his connection to humanity. Through the story, he faces his past, present, and future — and transforms. The change is the story.
> **Key takeaway**: A character who does not change is not a character — it is a cardboard cutout. Change is the measure of a story.
