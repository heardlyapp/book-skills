# Techniques — The Little Book of Common Sense Investing

## How to Invest Like Jack Bogle

### Technique 1: The Gotrocks Test
Imagine you and your family own 100% of every stock. Now ask: "How much of that return am I keeping after fees?" If the answer is less than 100%, you're paying too much. The family's share shrank from 100% to 60% because of Helpers. Bogle's test: minimize Helpers, maximize retained return.

### Technique 2: The Cost Calculator
Compare two funds with different expense ratios over 30 years at a 7% return. Fund A: 0.05% ER. Fund B: 1.2% ER. On a $100,000 investment: Fund A grows to ~$760,000. Fund B grows to ~$540,000. That's $220,000 for "nothing you can see, feel, or touch."

### Technique 3: The Three-Fund Portfolio
Bogle's minimalist portfolio: (1) Total Stock Market Index Fund, (2) Total International Stock Index Fund, (3) Total Bond Market Index Fund. Allocate by age: bonds = your age. Rest in stocks. Rebalance once a year. That's it.

### Technique 4: The Dividends Test
Before buying any stock or fund, ask: "What would this investment be worth if I lived entirely off the dividends?" If the answer is uncomfortable, the investment is too speculative. Bogle: dividends are "the engine of long-term returns."

### Technique 5: The Survivorship Bias Check
When someone shows you a fund's track record, ask: "What happened to the funds that didn't survive?" Of the 355 equity funds from 1970, only 9 outperformed and survived to 2016. The rest closed or underperformed. Ignore the survivors — they are misleading by definition.

### Technique 6: The Reversion Prediction
When a fund is on a hot streak, predict: it will underperform in the next five years. When a fund is in a cold streak, predict: it will outperform. Bogle's data shows this pattern is more reliable than any analyst's projection. "Reversion to the mean is the iron law."

### Technique 7: The $10,000/50-Year Challenge
Take any investment strategy. Calculate the terminal value of a $10,000 investment over 50 years at 7% gross return. Then subtract the fees and taxes. If the result is significantly less than $294,000 (the 0% cost scenario), the strategy is failing Bogle's common sense test.
