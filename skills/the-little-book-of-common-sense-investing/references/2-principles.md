# Key Principles — The Little Book of Common Sense Investing

## I. Get What You Don't Pay For

Bogle's mantra: "In investing, you get what you don't pay for." The less you pay in fees, the more of the market's return you keep. A 2.5% expense ratio on a $10,000 investment over 50 years consumes 75% of potential terminal wealth — leaving $76,000 instead of $294,000. "The magic of compounding returns is matched by the tyranny of compounding costs."

## II. Investment Return Beats Speculative Return

The market's long-term return comes from two sources: dividends + earnings growth (investment return) and changes in valuation (speculative return). Investment return is predictable (6-8% real). Speculative return is a zero-sum game — one investor's gain is another's loss. "In the short run, the market is a voting machine. In the long run, it's a weighing machine."

## III. Active Management Is a Loser's Game After Costs

"Before costs, beating the market is a zero-sum game. After costs, it's a loser's game." Over 15 years ending 2016, only 16% of active large-cap funds beat the S&P 500. From 1970-2016, only 9 of 355 equity funds survived and outperformed (2.5%). Peter Lynch warned: "Investors as a group consistently underperform the market averages."

## IV. Past Performance Doesn't Predict Future Returns

Reversion to the mean: funds that outperform revert to underperformance, and vice versa. Chasing past performance (the "selection penalty") is the single biggest mistake investors make. The Fidelity Magellan fund earned 16% annually under Lynch, but the average Magellan investor earned only 7% — because they bought after the run and sold after the drop.

## V. Costs Compound Like Returns

The arithmetic is inexorable: high costs consume a disproportionate share of returns. When market returns are high (10%), a 2% fee consumes 20% of the return. When returns are low (4%), that same 2% fee consumes 50%. "The lower returns expected in the coming decade make it even more important to control costs."

## VI. Taxes Are Costs, Too

Active funds with high turnover generate capital gains taxes that further reduce returns. Index funds, with turnover near zero, defer taxes indefinitely. "The average actively managed fund turns over its portfolio at a rate approaching 100% annually. That's a tax disaster."

## VII. Simplicity Is the Master Key

"Profit from the majesty of simplicity and parsimony." One total stock market index fund. One total bond market index fund. That's enough. Add international if you wish. "The winning strategy is to own all of the nation's publicly held businesses at very low cost and hold them forever."
