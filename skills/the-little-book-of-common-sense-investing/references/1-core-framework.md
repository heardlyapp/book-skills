# Core Framework — The Little Book of Common Sense Investing

## Introduction: The Index Revolution

John C. Bogle founded Vanguard in 1974 and created the world's first index mutual fund in 1975. His thesis: "The index fund is the only investment that essentially guarantees that you will capture your fair share of the returns that business earns." Simple arithmetic dictates that low-cost index funds outperform the vast majority of actively managed funds over time.

## Chapter 1: A Parable — The Gotrocks Family

The Gotrocks family owns 100% of every stock in America. They collect all dividends and earnings growth. Harmony reigns. Then "Helpers" arrive — brokers, money managers, consultants — each taking a cut. The family's share of the pie shrinks from 100% to 60%. The uncle: "Get rid of all your Helpers. Get rid of all your brokers. Get rid of all your money managers. Get rid of all your consultants. Then our family will again reap 100 percent of the pie."

"Successful investing is about minimizing the share of the returns earned by our corporations that is consumed by Wall Street, and maximizing the share that is delivered to Main Street."

## Chapter 2: Rational Exuberance

**The two returns:** Investment return (dividends + earnings growth) is what the market gives you. Speculative return (changes in P/E ratio) is what the market takes away. Over the long term, investment return dominates (6-8% annual). Speculative return is a zero-sum game. "In the short run, the stock market is a voting machine. In the long run, it's a weighing machine." — Benjamin Graham.

## Chapter 3: Cast Your Lot with Business

Own the entire stock market, not individual stocks. The S&P 500 has returned ~10% annually over the past century (7% real). The total stock market index captures even more diversification. "The index fund is designed to be held for a lifetime."

**The data:** Over the 15 years ending 2016, only 16% of actively managed large-cap funds outperformed the S&P 500. Among mid-cap funds: 6%. Among small-cap: 11%. The longer the time horizon, the worse active management performs.

## Chapter 4: How Most Investors Turn a Winner's Game into a Loser's Game

**The relentless rules of humble arithmetic:** "Before the deduction of the costs of investing, beating the stock market is a zero-sum game. After the deduction of costs, it is a loser's game."

Costs compound just like returns. A 2% annual fee consumes about 40% of a 7% return over 30 years. Bogle's rule of thumb: "In investing, you get what you don't pay for."

**The tyranny of compounding costs:** On a $10,000 investment earning 7% over 50 years: at 0% costs = $294,000. At 2.5% costs (typical active fund) = $76,000. The investor loses nearly 75% of potential wealth to costs.

## Chapter 5: Focus on the Lowest-Cost Funds

The correlation between expense ratios and returns is negative and powerful. The lowest-cost quartile of funds consistently outperforms the highest-cost quartile. "Costs matter. They always matter." Vanguard's average expense ratio: 0.12%. Industry average: 0.70%+.

## Chapter 6: Dividends Are the Investor's Best Friend

From 1900-2016, dividends accounted for roughly 40% of the S&P 500's total return. Reinvested dividends are the engine of long-term compounding. The average stock pays ~2% dividend yield, plus earnings growth of ~5%, giving a fundamental return of ~7% before speculation.

## Chapter 7: The Grand Illusion

Fund investors do worse than the funds they invest in. Why? Because they chase past performance — buying high, selling low. "The average fund investor underperforms the average fund by about 3% per year." This is the "timing penalty" and "selection penalty" combined.

**The Fidelity Magellan example:** Magellan earned 16% annually under Peter Lynch (1977-1990). But the average Magellan investor earned only 7%. Because investors piled in after the great returns and sold after the poor ones.

## Chapter 8: Taxes Are Costs, Too

Index funds are extremely tax-efficient — low turnover means few capital gains distributions. Active funds trade constantly, generating taxable gains that are passed to shareholders. "The tax cost of owning an actively managed equity fund over a lifetime can reduce terminal wealth by an additional 2% or more per year."

## Chapter 9: When the Good Times No Longer Roll

Future returns will be lower than past returns. Bogle projected (in 2017) that the S&P 500 would return roughly 4-6% annually over the next decade, not the 10% of the past century. Lower returns make costs matter even more — when returns are low, the share consumed by fees is proportionally larger.

## Chapter 10: Selecting Long-Term Winners

Of the 355 equity funds that existed in 1970, only 9 survived and outperformed the market through 2016. That's 2.5%. "Past performance does not predict future returns." The few winners are impossible to identify in advance.

## Chapter 11: Reversion to the Mean

Funds that outperform in one period tend to underperform in the next. "Regression to the mean is the iron law of fund performance." The top-performing quartile over five years becomes the bottom quartile in the subsequent five years with remarkable consistency.

## Chapters 14-20: Bonds, Asset Allocation, Simplicity

**Bond index funds:** Same logic applies — low-cost bond index funds outperform most active bond funds.

**Asset allocation:** The split between stocks and bonds is the single most important investment decision. A simple 60/40 portfolio of stock and bond index funds has historically returned ~8% with lower volatility than stocks alone.

**The majesty of simplicity and parsimony:** One fund. Or two (stock + bond). Or three (+ international). That's all you need. "The winning strategy for investing in stocks is to own all of the nation's publicly held businesses at very low cost. By doing so you are guaranteed to capture almost the entire return."
