# Key Principles — The Truths We Hold

## I. Speak Truth — Especially When It's Hard

The book's title and central thesis. Harris: "We cannot solve our most intractable problems unless we are honest about what they are." She applies this to the criminal justice system, immigration, economic inequality, and race. "Truth matters. What we say. What we mean. The value we place on our words — and what they are worth to others."

## II. Be a Prosecutor with a Conscience

Harris insists that prosecutors can be forces for justice, not just punishment. "I wanted to be on the front lines of criminal justice reform." The Back on Track program proved recidivism could be cut by focusing on education and jobs. The innocent woman freed on Friday because one intern cared. "People who cared could do justice."

## III. Government Should Serve People, Not Institutions

Harris' approach to the foreclosure crisis: when the banks offered $2 billion, she refused. "That's not enough." She walked away. The banks tried to go over her head. She held. Final: $18.4 billion. "The data didn't lie. And we didn't blink."

## IV. Your Family Defines You, Not Your Biography

Harris' blended family: Doug, Cole, Ella, Maya, her mother. "Momala" — not stepmother. "I never wanted them to think of themselves as 'yours' and 'mine. We were a team." Her mother Shyamala raised her as a single mom, carried her in a stroller to civil rights marches, and taught her: "Fight systems in a way that causes them to be fairer."

## V. Diversity Is a Strength, Not a Threat

Harris grew up in a Black church, went to a desegregated school, spent Thursdays at Rainbow Sign (a Black cultural center), visited India every few years. Her mother: "She knew her adopted homeland would see Maya and me as black girls, and she was determined to make sure we would grow into confident, proud black women."

## VI. Fight with Joy

Harris' 9-year-old godson cried on Election Night 2016. Her response: "Superheroes fight back." Rainbow Sign was full of laughter, food, and art — "It was where I learned that artistic expression, ambition, and intelligence were cool." Doug proposed on Valentine's Day. "I knew he was the one when he laughed at all my jokes."

## VII. Democracy Requires Participation

Thurgood Marshall: "We cannot play ostrich." Harris: "Democracy is not a state. It is an act." The Women's March on January 21, 2017 proved that millions of people could rise up against what was wrong. The book's closing call: "We must continue to organize, resist, and hope. That is our inheritance. That is our charge."
