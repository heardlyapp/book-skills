# Core Framework — The Truths We Hold

## Preface: Election Night 2016

Kamala Harris was elected to the U.S. Senate on November 8, 2016 — the same night Donald Trump won the presidency. Her nine-year-old godson Alexander asked with tears: "Auntie Kamala, that man can't win. He's not going to win, is he?" She told him superheroes fight back. "That's right. And they fight back with emotion. But they always fight back."

Gave her victory speech without the draft she'd prepared (which assumed Hillary would win). "Do we retreat or do we fight? I say we fight. And I intend to fight!" Went home, ate an entire family-size bag of Doritos without sharing.

## Chapter 1: For The People (Becoming a Prosecutor)

**First day at the Alameda County DA's office (1988):** Harris was the first to arrive. Only one woman among the interns — Amy Resner. Harris asked for her number immediately. They remain close friends: "In that male-dominated environment, it was refreshing to have at least one female colleague."

**The innocent woman in jail:** As an intern, Harris reviewed a drug bust case where an innocent bystander was swept into jail. It was late Friday. Without intervention, she'd spend the weekend in jail. Harris begged the clerk to call the judge back. "My God, she could lose her kids." The judge returned. She was freed. "I never did get to meet her, but I'll never forget her."

**The "prison's revolving door":** As DA, Harris saw the same men and women cycled through the system. "I wanted to stop the revolving door — to prevent people from entering it in the first place." She created the "Back on Track" program: first-time, nonviolent drug offenders earn a high school diploma and get a job. Recidivism: less than 10%.

## Chapter 2: A Voice for Justice (Attorney General of California)

**First woman, first Black person, first South Asian — California AG:** Elected 2010. "I knew it mattered whose voice was represented."

**The foreclosure crisis:** Harris refused to settle with the big banks for the $2-4 billion initially offered. She walked away from negotiations. The banks called her bluff. She held. Final settlement: $18.4 billion for California homeowners — the largest state-level settlement in history.

**Home Foreclosure Hotspot Map:** Harris' office created a data-driven tool showing foreclosure concentration by neighborhood. "The data didn't lie." She used it to target banks that had engaged in fraudulent robo-signing.

## Chapter 3: Underwater (Family Roots)

**Shyamala Gopalan (mother, 1938-2009):** Arrived in Berkeley from India at 19 for a PhD in nutrition and endocrinology. Became a breast cancer researcher. "She had only two goals in life: to raise her two daughters and to end breast cancer." Met Donald Harris at Berkeley through the civil rights movement.

**Donald Harris (father):** Jamaican immigrant. Professor emeritus of economics at Stanford. Jazz collection filled a wall — Thelonious Monk, John Coltrane, Miles Davis. "Run, Kamala. As fast as you can. Run!"

**The divorce:** When Harris was 5, her parents separated. "They didn't fight about money. The only thing they fought about was who got the books." Her mother raised her and sister Maya as a single mom. Shyamala: "Fweedom!" a toddler Kamala yelled when asked what she wanted.

**Rainbow Sign:** A Black cultural center in Berkeley where Harris spent Thursdays. Shirley Chisholm, Alice Walker, Maya Angelou, Nina Simone — all performed there. "It was where I learned that artistic expression, ambition, and intelligence were cool."

## Chapter 4: Wedding Bells (Meeting Doug Emhoff)

**The blind date:** A friend set them up. Doug called Kamala and left a message so long she had to fast-forward through it. "He had this deep, smooth voice — like a DJ from a quiet jazz station." First date: they talked for hours. "I knew he was the one when he laughed at all my jokes."

**The blended family:** Doug's children, Cole and Ella, from his first marriage. Kamala became "Momala." "I never wanted my stepchildren to think of themselves as 'yours' and 'mine.' We were a team." Doug proposed on Valentine's Day.

## Chapter 5-6: I Say We Fight / We Are Better Than This

**The Senate race:** California's "jungle primary" — Harris vs. Loretta Sanchez. The Kamoji campaign bus (with an emoji of her face on the back). Election Night 2016: bittersweet.

**DACA confirmation hearing:** Harris questioned John Kelly (nominee for Homeland Security). She pressed him on DACA protections. Kelly deflected. She voted against him. First 100 days: immigration arrests increased 37%.

**The "prosecutor cross-examination" style:** Harris' signature Senate committee technique — asking pointed, layered questions that cornered witnesses. Became famous during the Kavanaugh hearings and Barr testimony.

## Chapter 7-8: Every Body / The Cost of Living

**Health care:** Harris argues health care is a right, not a privilege. Medicaid expansion, Medicare for All. The maternal mortality crisis: Black women are 3-4x more likely to die from pregnancy-related causes than white women.

**Economic justice:** The middle class living paycheck to paycheck. Student loan debt crisis. For-profit college scandals. "Wages haven't risen in forty years."

## Chapter 9-10: Smart on Security / What I've Learned

**Foreign policy:** Harris' seat on the Intelligence Committee. Russia, China, North Korea. Her philosophy: "We must be smart on security — not just tough."

**Her mother's death (2009):** Shyamala died of colon cancer. Kamala was at her side. "I was fortunate to be with her, to hold her hand, to tell her I loved her." The most formative influence of her life.

**Closing:** "The truths we hold — these are the values that guide us: justice, equality, dignity, opportunity. We must speak truth, even when it's hard. We must fight, even when it seems impossible. Because America has always been about the work."
