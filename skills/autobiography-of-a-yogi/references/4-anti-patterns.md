# 4 — Miracles and Materialism

> Source: "Autobiography of a Yogi" by Paramahansa Yogananda

## Anti-Pattern: Dismissing the Miraculous

The book is filled with accounts of supernatural phenomena — levitation, materialization, healing, resurrection, and communion with advanced spiritual beings. Yogananda anticipated that Western readers would struggle with these stories.

> **Case: The Saint with Two Bodies** (Chapter 3): "A saint named Swami Pranabananda appeared to be in two places at once. Yogananda met him in person while the saint was also seen by others in another city. This bilocation was not a trick — it was a demonstration of the power of a realized soul who had transcended identification with the physical body." This early chapter sets the tone for the miraculous throughout the book.
> **Key takeaway**: Yogananda does not ask you to believe blindly. He presents these accounts as evidence of the potential of the human spirit when it is fully realized. The miracles are not the point — they are illustrations of what is possible when the soul knows its true nature.

## Anti-Pattern: Skepticism as a Spiritual Block

Yogananda argues that closed-minded skepticism is as dogmatic as blind faith. The spiritual path requires an open mind and a willingness to investigate.

> **Case: The Skeptical Scientist** (Chapter on Science and Spirituality): "A Western scientist visited Yogananda and demanded proof of yogic powers. Yogananda calmly demonstrated phenomena that the scientist could not explain. The scientist's assumptions about reality were challenged. Yogananda's point: science and spirituality are not enemies. They are both investigations into the nature of reality." He argues for their compatibility.
> **Key takeaway**: Yogananda was not anti-science. He believed that yoga was a science — a systematic method for investigating the inner world. The true opposition is not between science and spirituality but between open inquiry and closed dogma on both sides.
