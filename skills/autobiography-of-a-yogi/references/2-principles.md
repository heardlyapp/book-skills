# 2 — The Guru and Kriya Yoga

> Source: "Autobiography of a Yogi" by Paramahansa Yogananda

## The Central Relationship

The guru-disciple relationship is the heart of the book. Sri Yukteswar was not just a teacher — he was a spiritual master who guided Yogananda with wisdom, discipline, and profound love.

> **Case: Sri Yukteswar** (Chapter 12 — Meeting with the Master): "When Yogananda met Sri Yukteswar for the first time, the master said: 'O little one, you have come.' He then gave Yogananda a spiritual initiation that lasted hours and touched the deepest levels of his being. This is the moment the entire book has been building toward." The meeting is described with extraordinary detail and emotion.
> **Key takeaway**: The guru-disciple relationship in yoga is not casual. The guru is not a coach or therapist but a living embodiment of the Divine. The recognition between guru and disciple is mutual and transcends the physical. Sri Yukteswar saw Yogananda's past lives and knew this was the disciple he had been waiting for.

> **Case: Kriya Yoga Initiation** (Chapter on Kriya Yoga): "Sri Yukteswar initiated Yogananda into Kriya Yoga — an ancient meditation technique that had been lost for centuries and was revived by Mahavatar Babaji. Kriya accelerates spiritual evolution by circulating energy through the spine's seven chakras. One year of Kriya practice equals one year of natural spiritual evolution." Kriya Yoga is the central teaching of the book.
> **Key takeaway**: Kriya Yoga is presented as the most scientific and efficient method for spiritual growth. It is not a devotional practice or a philosophical system — it is a technique. A practical method that can be learned and practiced. This is what made Yogananda's teaching so attractive to Westerners.
