# Key Principles — The Office BFFs

## I. The Right Collaboration Multiplies Everything

Jenna and Angela could have been rivals (same show, both women in a male-heavy cast). Instead, they became best friends. The show was better for it. The magic on screen came from real friendship off screen. "Angela became, and still is, that person for me. When I share with her, I see things more clearly."

Greg Daniels deliberately blurred the line between writers and actors. B. J. Novak, Mindy Kaling, Paul Lieberstein, Mike Schur were both. "Greg was interested in seeing what would happen if he made the two groups more fluid." Result: real-life moments became storylines. John doing impressions → "bears, beets, Battlestar Galactica." Steve's hockey → "Threat Level Midnight."

## II. The Best Work Comes from Trust and Permission

Greg asked Jenna: "Why do you think Pam and Roy haven't gotten married yet?" She told him the Jet Ski story. He lit up: "We must add this to the show!" It appeared in a talking head and stayed alive through "Cocktails" in Season 3.

Angela and Jenna pitched "Boys and Girls" (Jan's Women in the Workplace seminar) to Greg. He grabbed B. J. and started building it on the spot. "It was so cool to see them work right there in the moment."

Angela's Sprinkles Post-it note during the pilot became a multi-season storyline. "This is just one example of why our show was so creatively fulfilling."

## III. Friendship Is the Real Product, Fame Is a By-Product

Steve Carell to Jenna and Angela when they were goofing around singing Laverne & Shirley: "No matter what happens with the show... you will have this. This friendship is what you will take with you."

Jenna almost missed Halloween with her kids. Angela mobilized her neighbors, bought candy, created a trick-or-treating route — all in two days. "You will not miss Halloween on my watch."

Angela was in Jenna's wedding. Their kids go to the same school. Isabel gives Harper a hug at recess. "The show was a family — but Angela is my real family."

## IV. Closeness Is Built in the Margins (Between the Cues)

The friendship formed in the in-between moments: bench week (hours sitting together in warehouse background), hair and makeup trailer at 5 AM, lunch in Jenna's trailer, car rides home talking the entire way.

"No FreeCell, no journals, no partition between us — we gabbed every chance we got. We told each other our life stories on that bench... I can say with absolute certainty, this was the birthplace of our best friendship."

## V. Support Each Other Through the Hard Parts

Jenna returned 5 weeks after C-section — no paid maternity leave. Angela: got her postpartum underwear, made sure props gave her a stroller, talked to her every day. "The truth is it felt like a double standard that other cast members had their schedules accommodated for films, but Jenna was not given the same consideration after having a baby."

Rainn helped Angela through karaoke anxiety. He saw her trembling and dropped to one knee to sing with her. "If you watch that scene, you will see a huge smile comes across my face. That is not Angela Martin smiling at Dwight Schrute. That is Angela Kinsey smiling at Rainn Wilson for saving her butt."

Phyllis spent an entire shoot day crying alone (the oven mitt scene). Jenna: "When the scene was finally over, I gave her a big hug."

## VI. The Magic Was Intentional (Not Accidental)

Greg Daniels had a specific vision: "It's kind of like a tofu hot dog. It's good food that's made to look like bad food." He hired a documentary cinematographer (Randall Einhorn) who said the emotional scenes should be shot from farther away, not closer.

Carey Bennett visited a real paper company, took hundreds of photos, made a slideshow. The entire look of the show came from that research. "I never assume I know what I'm talking about. I always try to go to the source."

The writers' room had hundreds of notecards on walls — one per potential storyline idea. "My imagination would churn, thinking of all the ways these cards might turn into an episode."

## VII. What Happens on a Hit Show Doesn't Stay on the Show

After The Office, Jenna was surprised: "I learned how little the writers and producers wanted to hear my thoughts about my character. The message was 'The creator has a vision; the writers write it, the actors perform it. Everyone stay in your lane.'"

The Jenna (adjustable leg wrap for microphone packs) was used on Splitting Up Together too. "When I walked on set the first day... our sound team was the same team from The Office. Nick pulled out the Jenna. He had saved it."

Angela's real Yankee Swap parties (still happening, years after the show). Andy Buckley (David Wallace) once brought a squirrel-shaped squirrel feeder. "I fought a friend’s preteen son for a dill pickle dip set. And won."
