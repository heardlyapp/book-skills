# 1 — The Final Voyage

> Source: "The Gales of November" by John U. Bacon

## Timeline

**November 9, 1975**: The Fitzgerald departs Superior, Wisconsin, loaded with 26,000 tons of taconite. Weather forecast: severe storm warning.

**November 10, Morning**: The Fitzgerald meets the Arthur M. Anderson, a sister ship, for the journey across Lake Superior. Storm intensifies.

**November 10, Afternoon**: Winds reach 50+ knots. Waves 25+ feet. The Fitzgerald is taking on water. List develops. Captain McSorley slows the ship.

**7:00 PM**: McSorley radios the Anderson: "We're holding our own."

**7:10 PM**: The Anderson loses the Fitzgerald on radar. No distress call. Nothing.

> **Case: The Last Words** (Chapter 7): Captain McSorley's last radio transmission: "We're holding our own." He said it calmly. No panic. No distress. Ten minutes later, the Fitzgerald was gone. The Anderson's captain, Bernie Cooper, tried to raise them. Nothing. "That silence was the worst moment of my life."
> **Key takeaway**: The crew of the Fitzgerald died with no warning. They were holding their own one minute and gone the next. The suddenness is the most haunting aspect of the tragedy.

> **Case: The Discovery** (Chapter 12): The wreck was found days later. It had split in two. The bow section was upright. The stern was upside down and mangled. "The wreck told a story of catastrophic damage, but it couldn't tell us exactly what happened."
> **Key takeaway**: The wreck answers some questions but raises others. The ship broke apart on the surface or during the descent. We may never know which came first — the flooding or the breakup.
