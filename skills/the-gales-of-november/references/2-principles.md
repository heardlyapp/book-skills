# 2 — Theories and Investigation

> Source: "The Gales of November" by John U. Bacon

## The Three Main Theories

### Theory 1: Rogue Wave
A massive, unexpected wave — perhaps 35-40 feet — overwhelmed the ship. The deck was flooded. Water entered through vents and hatches.

### Theory 2: Shoaling (Bottom Contact)
The Fitzgerald passed over an area where the bottom rises from 300 feet to 100 feet. The ship may have bottomed out due to heavy seas, causing hull damage.

### Theory 3: Hatch Cover Failure
The Coast Guard's official conclusion. Hatch covers were improperly secured. Waves broke them open. The hold flooded. The ship sank.

> **Case: The Coast Guard Report** (Chapter 14): The Coast Guard concluded the most likely cause was "ineffective hatch closures." But the report was controversial. Families felt it was incomplete. Bacon writes: "The Coast Guard needed a cause. But they may have been too quick to settle on one."
> **Key takeaway**: Official investigations don't always find the truth — they find the most plausible explanation given the evidence available. The families of the Fitzgerald crew never got closure.

> **Case: The Rogue Wave Evidence** (Chapter 15): In the 1970s, rogue waves were not scientifically accepted. They were considered sailor's tales. Today, satellite data proves rogue waves exist and are more common than thought. "If the Fitzgerald encountered a rogue wave, it would explain the suddenness of the sinking and the lack of a distress call."
> **Key takeaway**: Science evolves. What was dismissed in 1975 is now accepted. The Fitzgerald's sinking may need to be re-evaluated with modern understanding of rogue waves.
