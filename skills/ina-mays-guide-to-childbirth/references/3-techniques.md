# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *Ina May's Guide to Childbirth* by Ina May Gaskin — Part II, Chapters 3, 4, 6-9.

## Practical Tools

> **Case: Kathryn's Bathtub Breakthrough** (Part I, Kathryn B. Van de Castle's Story, pages ~339-341): Kathryn labored for seven hours in a warm tub. She entered the tub at 1 centimeter dilation. She emerged seven hours later fully dilated at 10 centimeters. The water provided privacy, warmth, and support. Her body opened in conditions where it felt safe. Ina May notes: water births and long baths are powerful natural tools precisely because they satisfy the conditions of Sphincter Law.
> **Key takeaway**: A warm bath is not just comfort — it's a physiological intervention that helps your cervix open.

### 1. The Sphincter Law Audit

**Before you enter a birth environment, ask:**
- Am I in a private space where I won't be interrupted or observed by strangers?
- Do I feel safe and relaxed here?
- Are the lights dim and the noise level low?
- Am I surrounded by people I trust?
- If the answer to any of these is "no," the environment is working against your body's ability to open.

### 2. The Fear/Pain Cycle Break

**When fear rises:**
1. Breathe slowly and deeply — Ina May calls this "making low-pitched moans" to stay calm
2. Change position — walk, squat, lean, rock. Never stay still during a contraction
3. Use water — warm baths and showers are potent natural pain relievers
4. Tell your support person what you need — don't expect them to read your mind
5. Repeat an affirmation: "This is power. This is my baby working. My body was made for this."

### 3. The "Rush" Reframe

**When you feel a contraction coming:** consciously rename it. Say "rush" instead of "contraction" or "pain." Ina May found that the language women used shaped their experience. "Rush" suggests energy, power, progress. Practice this before labor.

### 4. The Upright Position Rule

**Never labor on your back if you can help it:** The supine position reduces blood flow to the uterus, works against gravity, and narrows the pelvic outlet. Ina May recommends: walking, leaning forward during contractions, sitting on a birth stool, squatting, hands-and-knees, side-lying. Gravity is your assistant.

### 5. The Sisterhood Strategy

**When labor stalls:** call in someone who believes in you. The "sisterhood birth" (Mary Shelton story) shows that a calm, experienced, loving presence can restart stalled labor. Ina May has a list of nearby women who are "energy donors" for this purpose.

### 6. The VBAC Preparation

**For women planning a VBAC:**
1. Choose a caregiver with a high VBAC success rate and a low repeat cesarean rate
2. Wait for labor to start spontaneously — avoid induction if possible
3. Stay at home as long as safely possible in early labor
4. Use continuous one-to-one support (doula, partner, midwife)
5. Keep movement and upright positions throughout labor
6. Avoid epidurals if possible — they can slow labor

### 7. The Placenta Check

**The third stage of labor:** Don't rush the placenta. Ina May recommends waiting for signs of separation, gentle traction, and immediate breastfeeding to stimulate the final contraction. The cord should never be pulled hard — gentle patience is safer.

> **Case: Karen's Unmedicated Third Stage** (Part I, Karen Lovell's James's Birth): After James was born, Karen felt the placenta slide out "like a squishy blob" with barely any effort. The midwives didn't pull. They didn't rush. They waited for her body to deliver the placenta naturally. The result: minimal blood loss, a comfortable mother, an unhurried welcome.
> **Key takeaway**: The third stage of labor is a birth too. Patience and gentleness apply here as much as anywhere.
