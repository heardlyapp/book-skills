# Philip Fisher's Voice & Application Scenarios

> Source: Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits, all parts; Preface by Ken Fisher; Developing an Investment Philosophy

## Authorial Voice

Philip Fisher writes with the quiet confidence of a man who has spent decades refining a single approach. His voice is:

- **Systematic but not mechanical**: He provides a 15-point checklist but insists the real work is in the art of follow-up questions, not the rote application of the checklist.
- **Contrarian without being combative**: He knows his views on dividends, diversification, and market timing go against conventional wisdom. He states them plainly without attacking the opposition.
- **Patient and long-term oriented**: Every sentence assumes the reader is investing for decades, not months. There is no urgency, no FOMO, no "this time it's different."
- **Practical rather than theoretical**: Unlike academic finance, Fisher's advice is grounded in real conversations with real people at real companies. Scuttlebutt is fieldwork, not modeling.
- **Morally grounded**: Points 14-15 (management integrity and candor) are not optional. If management hides bad news, the rest of the analysis is worthless because the data can't be trusted.

### Classic Fisher Quotes

- "The stock market is filled with individuals who know the price of everything but the value of nothing."
- "If you have done a thorough enough job of selecting a stock in the first place, there is virtually no time to sell it."
- "The scuttlebutt method is simply about finding out from real, 'Main Street' sources if a firm is strong or weak."
- "It is better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price." (This idea, later attributed to Buffett, originated with Fisher.)
- "I have already made up my mind — don't confuse me with facts." — Fisher's warning against the most common investor bias, placed in the book's dedication.
- "Never sell a company that you have bought on correct judgment simply because the price has gone up."

## Five Application Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Individual Investor Evaluating a Stock
You've found a company with growing revenues and a product you personally love. Everyone on social media says it's a buy.
**Solution**: Don't buy yet. Apply the 15 points through scuttlebutt. Find three competitors and call them posing as a customer. Find a former employee on LinkedIn and ask about management culture. Read the suppliers' annual reports to see if they mention your target as a key customer. This week of fieldwork is worth more than a year of reading analyst reports. (Read references/1-core-framework.md + 2-principles.md)

### Scenario 2: Long-Term Holder Facing a Correction
You own a growth stock that's dropped 40%. Your portfolio is down. Should you cut losses?
**Solution**: Ask yourself Fisher's three-question test: (1) Was my original purchase a mistake? (2) Have the company's fundamentals permanently deteriorated? (3) Is there a dramatically better opportunity? If the answer to all three is no, hold. The drop is noise. If the fundamentals have changed, sell — regardless of the price. (Read references/3-techniques.md + 4-anti-patterns.md)

### Scenario 3: New Investor Building a Portfolio
You have $10,000 to invest and want to start building a long-term portfolio. Everyone says buy an index fund.
**Solution**: Fisher would not disagree with index funds for most people. But if you want to beat the market, go the opposite direction from the crowd. Start with 3-5 companies you can research deeply. Read everything about one industry. Call their competitors. Go to a trade show. The concentrated, researched approach beats the diversified, ignorant approach every time. (Read references/4-anti-patterns.md, Don't #6)

### Scenario 4: Growth Investor Confused About Dividends
Your portfolio has a mix of dividend payers and growth stocks. A dividend increase from one of your holdings feels good.
**Solution**: Run the dividend test: Can this company reinvest its cash at attractive returns? If yes, you'd prefer they kept the money (avoiding the tax drag). If no, the dividend is a reasonable return of capital. Don't confuse the emotional satisfaction of a dividend check with sound investment logic. (Read references/3-techniques.md, The Dividend Controversy)

### Scenario 5: Value Investor Considering Growth Stocks
You're a Graham-style value investor but want exposure to faster-growing companies.
**Solution**: The 15 points work for value too — Ken Fisher proved this with Nucor. Apply them as a filter for business quality, then use your value discipline on the price (the fourth dimension). The combination of Fisher's quality framework and Graham's price discipline is the sweet spot — which is exactly what Buffett achieved by being "15% Fisher and 85% Graham." (Read references/1-core-framework.md + 5-voice-and-app.md)

> **Case: Fisher Teaching at Stanford** (Preface by Ken Fisher)
> For a brief period (1961-1962), Fisher taught the only dedicated investment course at Stanford's Graduate School of Business. His student Jack McDonald later took over the course and taught it for decades, using Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits as a textbook. McDonald's assessment: Fisher's major contribution was "the first person to link the models of sustainable growth with the concept of competitive advantage." Today this is standard — then it was revolutionary. The 15 points were the first systematic attempt to identify competitive advantage before the term existed.
> **Key takeaway**: Fisher invented the concept of competitive advantage as an investment framework. The 15 points were the original moat analysis — decades before Warren Buffett popularized the term.

> **Case: The Cornell Student Who Knew Nothing About Scuttlebutt** (Preface by Ken Fisher)
> Ken Fisher describes his first attempt to apply the 15 points as a college graduate. He tried to research Pacific Lumber, a local publicly-traded stock. The few people he approached weren't impressed with "some wanna-be kid-sleuth seeking competitive detail who was clearly ill-prepared." He gave up. It took years of practice to learn the craft — learning how to ask questions in a way that got real answers. The method works, but it requires practice.
> **Key takeaway**: Scuttlebutt is a skill that must be developed through practice. First attempts will fail. That's normal. The key is to keep going, get better at asking, and learn that the best questions come from listening to the answers to your previous questions.
