# The Scuttlebutt Method — Deep Research Through Primary Sources

> Source: Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits, Chapter 2; Preface by Ken Fisher; Developing an Investment Philosophy

## The Core Principle

Scuttlebutt is Fisher's term for gathering information about a company from the people who know it best: its competitors, customers, suppliers, former employees, and industry experts. The method rejects the conventional reliance on financial statements, analyst reports, and stock price movements in favor of direct human intelligence.

### The Scuttlebutt Protocol

| Source | What They Know | How to Approach |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|
| Competitors | The target's weaknesses, vulnerabilities, market share trends | Pose as a potential customer evaluating all options in the industry |
| Customers | Product quality, service levels, pricing power | Ask about satisfaction, switching costs, alternatives considered |
| Suppliers | Order volumes, payment behavior, production capacity utilization | Inquire about industry trends, who's ordering what |
| Former employees | Internal culture, management quality, hidden problems | Network through industry contacts; ask about "the good old days" |
| R&D community | Innovation pipeline, technical competence, hiring trends | Attend trade shows, read technical journals, talk to university researchers |
| Industry experts | Long-term trends, competitive dynamics, regulatory changes | Join industry associations, subscribe to trade publications |

### The Questioning Technique

Fisher's method had three layers:
1. **Prepared questions** — A typed list on yellow paper with space for notes. These provided structure and showed the target that Fisher was serious.
2. **The craft questions** — Standard follow-ups that anyone can learn. "Why do you say that?" "Can you give me an example?" "Who else should I talk to?"
3. **The art questions** — Completely improvised follow-ups based on what the person just said. Fisher's best questions were never written down in advance. They emerged from listening.

> **Case: The Scuttlebutt Chapter Is Only Three Pages — And the Most Important in the Book** (Preface by Ken Fisher)
> Ken Fisher notes that the scuttlebutt chapter is only three pages long but among the most important in the book. His father "simply skipped the craft part of what otherwise might have been in the book. He just assumed it." The craft — learning how to ask the right follow-up question based on the answer you just received — takes time to develop. Ken took years to learn it effectively.
> **Key takeaway**: The method is simple to describe and hard to execute. The skill is in the follow-up question that you can't prepare because you don't know what the answer will be. This is why scuttlebutt is an art, not a checklist.

> **Case: Fisher's First Investment Through Scuttlebutt** (Chapter 2)
> In the scuttlebutt chapter, Fisher describes his early experience researching a promising food machinery company. Instead of relying on published reports, he called its competitors and suppliers. He asked them about the company's products, service, and market position. The competitors' grudging respect for the target told him more than any financial statement could. This single investment proved the method and shaped his entire career approach.
> **Key takeaway**: The first scuttlebutt test is the most informative: call a company's competitors and ask them who they worry about most. If they name your target without hesitation or qualification, you've found something worth investigating further.
