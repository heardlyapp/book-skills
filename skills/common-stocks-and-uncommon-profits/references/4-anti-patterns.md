# Anti-Patterns — The Five Don'ts and Five More Don'ts

> Source: Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits, Chapters 8-9

## The Ten Investor Errors

Fisher devoted two full chapters to what NOT to do. These ten errors are as relevant today as when he wrote them in 1958.

### Chapter 8: Five Don'ts for Investors

| # | Don't | The Fallacy | Fisher's Correction |
|---|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| 1 | Don't buy into promotional companies | Hype and promise masquerade as potential | Only invest in companies that can pass the 15-point test today, not "once they get going" |
| 2 | Don't ignore a good stock just because it trades "over the counter" | Fear of illiquidity / lack of prestige | The OTC market (now smaller-cap stocks) contains some of the best growth opportunities |
| 3 | Don't buy a stock just because you like the "tone" of its annual report | Form over substance | Beautiful reports can hide terrible businesses. Scuttlebutt, not PR, reveals the truth |
| 4 | Don't assume a high price for a stock is an indication of its ultimate peak | Anchoring to price | Great businesses become worth much more over time. Price is not value |
| 5 | Don't quibble over eighths and quarters | Penny-wise, pound-foolish | The best trades are the ones where you don't worry about getting the last 1% — you focus on getting the company right |

### Chapter 9: Five More Don'ts for Investors

| # | Don't | The Fallacy | Fisher's Correction |
|---|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| 6 | Don't overstress diversification | Safety through dispersion | Owning 50 stocks guarantees average results. A concentrated portfolio of 5-10 deeply researched holdings is safer |
| 7 | Don't be afraid to buy on a war scare | Panic selling on geopolitical events | Wars are terrible but historically create buying opportunities. The market recovers |
| 8 | Don't forget your most valuable asset — time | Impatience and short-term thinking | The younger you are, the more you benefit from compounding. Time in the market beats timing the market |
| 9 | Don't follow the crowd | Herd mentality | The consensus is usually wrong at the extremes. Fisher was a lifelong contrarian |
| 10 | Don't fail to recognize that money is not the only consideration | Obsession with returns over everything else | Investing should enable a good life, not consume it. Don't let the pursuit of returns destroy your judgment |

> **Case: The IPO Tech Bubble — Eliminated by Don'ts 1 and 9** (Preface by Ken Fisher)
> The internet IPO boom of 1999-2000 was precisely the kind of "promotional company" environment Fisher warned against in Don't #1. Companies with no sales force, no profit margins, no plan to achieve profitability, and no research capability were bid up to insane valuations. The crowd (Don't #9) believed the narrative. Scuttlebutt would have revealed that competitors weren't scared, customers weren't impressed, and suppliers' other customers were ordering more. Every don't in the book was violated simultaneously.
> **Key takeaway**: When the crowd is most enthusiastic and the promotional noise is loudest, that's when the don'ts are most important. The IPO bubble violated all ten don'ts — and all ten predicted the crash.

> **Case: The Diversification Trap** (Chapter 9, Don't #6)
> Conventional wisdom says diversify across 30+ stocks to reduce risk. Fisher argued the opposite: holding too many stocks forces you to spread your research too thin. You end up owning companies you don't understand. Real safety comes from deep knowledge of a few companies, not shallow knowledge of many. Warren Buffett later echoed this: "Diversification is protection against ignorance. It makes little sense if you know what you're doing."
> **Key takeaway**: The "safety" of diversification is an illusion if it means you don't really understand any of your holdings. Fisher's ideal was 5-10 stocks, each subject to thorough scuttlebutt. That portfolio, he argued, was safer than 50 stocks owned without real knowledge.
