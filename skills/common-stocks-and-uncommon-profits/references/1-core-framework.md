# The 15-Point Framework for Identifying Superior Growth Companies

> Source: Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits, Part One, Chapters 3-4; Also the Preface by Ken Fisher

## The Core Framework

Philip Fisher's 15 Points are the heart of his investment system. They form a systematic checklist for evaluating whether a company has what it takes to deliver exceptional long-term growth. The points are organized into three categories: functional factors, people factors, and business characteristics.

### The 15 Points

| # | Category | Point | Key Question |
|---|----------|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Product | Market Potential | Does the company have products/services with sufficient market potential to sustain growth for years? |
| 2 | Product | R&D Effectiveness | Does management have a determination to develop new products that continue this potential? |
| 3 | Product | R&D Efficiency | How effective is the company's research effort relative to its size? |
| 4 | Sales | Sales Organization | Does the company have an above-average sales organization? |
| 5 | Profit | Profit Margin | Does the company have a worthwhile profit margin? |
| 6 | Profit | Margin Improvement | What is the company doing to maintain or improve profit margins? |
| 7 | People | Labor Relations | Does the company have outstanding labor and personnel relations? |
| 8 | People | Executive Relations | Does the company have outstanding executive relations? |
| 9 | People | Management Depth | Does the company have depth in its management? |
| 10 | Finance | Cost Controls | How good are the company's cost analysis and accounting controls? |
| 11 | Moats | Industry Position | Are there aspects of the business — peculiar to its industry — that give it an advantage over competitors? |
| 12 | Strategy | Earnings Outlook | Does the company have a short-term or long-term outlook regarding profits? |
| 13 | Finance | Equity Dilution | Will the company's growth require sufficient equity financing to dilute the value of existing shares? |
| 14 | People | Management Integrity | Does management talk freely to investors about its operations when things are going badly as well as when they are going well? |
| 15 | Strategy | Unique Integrity | Does the company have a management of unquestionable integrity? |

### How the Points Work Together

Points 1-3 establish the product/market opportunity. Points 4-6 verify that the company can execute commercially. Points 7-9 ensure the people are capable and stable. Points 10-13 check financial discipline. Points 14-15 are the governance gate — without integrity, none of the other points matter because the numbers can't be trusted.

> **Case: Enron, Tyco, and WorldCom Elimination** (Preface by Ken Fisher)
> Ken Fisher argues that the 15 points plus scuttlebutt would have eliminated every scandal stock of the 2000-2002 bear market. Competitors weren't scared of Enron. Customers weren't impressed with Tyco's products. Suppliers of WorldCom saw that orders weren't real. The 15 points test for fundamental business reality that can't be faked. "Not a one could have passed the test via scuttlebutt." The fraud stocks had no real sales organization (Point 4), no plan to achieve profitability (Point 5), no fundamental research (Point 3), and no ability to exist without future equity financing (Point 13).
> **Key takeaway**: The 15 points are an anti-fraud filter. If a company can't pass even half the points via scuttlebutt verification, you don't need to see the financial statements to know something is wrong.

> **Case: Nucor Steel — Same 15 Points, Different Styles** (Preface by Ken Fisher)
> In 1976, Ken Fisher discovered Nucor, a tiny low-cost steel vendor with great management, innovative technology, and lower production costs. He bought it as a value play. His father Philip, applying the same 15 points, bought it as a growth play. Ken sold years later at a huge profit; Philip held for decades, selling at a much larger profit by which time Nucor had become the second-largest U.S. steel manufacturer. The 15 points worked for both because they identify business quality, not investment style.
> **Key takeaway**: The 15 points work for value AND growth investors. If the business is truly outstanding, both approaches will profit — but the longer you hold a great business, the more you profit.
