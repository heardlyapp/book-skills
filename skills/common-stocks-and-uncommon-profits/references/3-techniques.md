# Timing, Dividends, and the Conservative Framework

> Source: Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits, Chapters 5-7, and Conservative Investors Sleep Well

## When to Buy

Fisher's entry principle: **buy on temporary difficulties, not during prosperity.** The best bargains appear when a genuinely great company encounters a fixable problem that Wall Street overreacts to.

### The Three Types of Buying Opportunities

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Temporary earnings dip | A cyclical or fixable problem reduces profits for 1-2 quarters | A failed product launch in an otherwise strong product line |
| Industry-wide pessimism | Whole sector falls out of favor, taking good companies down with bad | A competitor's scandal tarring the entire industry |
| Market correction | General market decline creates lower prices across the board | Bear market punishing all stocks indiscriminately |

## When to Sell

Fisher was famous for holding stocks for decades. He identified only **three valid reasons to sell**:

1. **The original purchase was a mistake.** — You misjudged the company's quality. Accept the error and exit.
2. **The company's fundamentals have permanently deteriorated.** — The competitive advantage is gone, key management left, the business model is broken.
3. **You found a dramatically better opportunity.** — Not slightly better. Not moderately better. Dramatically better.

**Never sell because:** the price went up (success is not a reason to sell), the price went down (unless the reason is reason #2), you've held it long enough (there is no time limit), you need to rebalance (diversification is not the goal), or the market is going to crash (you can't predict the market).

## The Dividend Controversy

Fisher believed dividends were overrated for growth investors. His logic:

- A dollar reinvested in a business earning 15-20% on equity is worth more to you than a dollar paid in dividends (which are then taxed).
- If a company needs to borrow money or issue stock to fund growth while paying dividends, it's destroying shareholder value.
- The real return comes from retained earnings deployed at high rates, not from the dividend check.

## The Four Dimensions of a Conservative Investment

From Part Two (Conservative Investors Sleep Well):

| Dimension | Focus | Key Question |
|-----------|-------|-------------|
| First | Low-cost production and strong marketing | Does the company produce at lower cost than competitors? Can it sell effectively? |
| Second | People: management depth and R&D capability | Is the management team deep enough to survive the loss of any one person? |
| Third | Investment characteristics of the business | Does the business have unique features that make it hard to compete with? |
| Fourth | Price | What price must you pay? Even the best company can be a bad investment if bought at too high a price. |

> **Case: The Raychem Story** (Part Three, Chapter 4)
> Fisher held Raychem through a period where the company's growth slowed and its stock crashed. Rather than selling, he investigated whether the fundamentals had permanently deteriorated. He concluded they hadn't — the company's core technology advantage and management remained intact. Raychem eventually recovered and provided enormous returns to those who held on. Had he sold on the bad news, he would have locked in losses in a company that was still excellent.
> **Key takeaway**: Price volatility is not fundamental deterioration. Fisher's first question when a stock falls is not "should I sell?" but "has the company changed?" and "can I find a better place for this capital?"

> **Case: Fisher's Experiment with Market Timing** (Part Three, Chapter 2)
> Fisher experimented with trying to time the market — selling holdings in anticipation of a downturn, intending to buy back later. He concluded it was a fool's errand. He couldn't predict the timing, he missed the best days of the rebound, and the tax cost destroyed his returns. After this experiment, he never attempted market timing again and instead focused entirely on business quality and holding for the long term.
> **Key takeaway**: Market timing is a tax-inefficient distraction. The effort spent trying to predict the market would be better spent researching one more company through scuttlebutt.
