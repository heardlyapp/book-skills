# Principles: Core Mindfulness Attitudes

> Source: The Mindful Way through Depression, Chapters 2-4

## Beginner's Mind

Seeing things as if for the first time. Depression's pattern is over-familiarity — we've been sad before, so we assume we know what's coming. Beginner's mind says: maybe this moment is different. Each moment is truly new.

> **Case: The Raisin Exercise** (Chapter 2): Participants eat a raisin as if they've never seen one. They notice its texture, wrinkles, the taste unfolding on the tongue. This simple exercise demonstrates the difference between automatic pilot (eating without noticing) and mindful awareness (full presence with curiosity).

> **Key takeaway**: Depression makes you feel like you've seen it all before. But this moment is genuinely new. You can approach it fresh.

## Non-Judging

Observing without immediately labeling good or bad. Depression is saturated with judgment: "I shouldn't feel this way," "This is wrong." Non-judging doesn't mean you can't want things to improve — it means you stop fighting the present moment.

## Acceptance

Allowing things to be as they already are. Not resignation — the willingness to be with what is. Paradoxically, acceptance is the first step toward genuine change.

> **Case: The Open Door** (Chapter 4): You can't close a door on a room you've never entered. Avoiding difficult emotions gives them more power. Turning toward them with kindness dissolves their grip. Acceptance doesn't mean liking the sadness — it means letting it be here.
