# Reference: Poverty and Resilience

## Overview
The novel does not romanticize poverty. It shows it clearly: hunger, exhaustion, and the constant scraping.

## Case: The Hunger
The Nolans are always hungry. They eat stale bread. They water down the milk. They save pennies. Francie's hunger is physical and constant.

## Case: The Work
Katie scrubs floors. Johnny sings in bars. Francie works after school. Everyone works. Survival requires every ounce of energy.

## Case: The Pride
The Nolans are proud. They do not beg. They do not complain. They keep their dignity even when they have nothing else.

## Case: The Community
The tenement community is a world of its own. Neighbors help each other. Everyone is poor. Everyone understands.

## Case: The Escape
Francie's escape is not guaranteed. She must work for it. She must be lucky. The novel does not promise success — it shows the effort.

## Case: The Christmas
The Nolans cannot afford a Christmas tree. Johnny brings one home anyway — a discarded one he found. He makes it beautiful. The tree represents the love that survives poverty.

## Case: The Savings
Katie keeps a savings can. The pennies are for the children's education. No matter how hungry they are, she will not open it.
Poverty in the novel is not a backdrop. It is a character. It shapes every decision, every sacrifice, every hope. Francie's resilience is forged in hunger.
The novel honors the poor by telling the truth: poverty is brutal, but people are resilient.
