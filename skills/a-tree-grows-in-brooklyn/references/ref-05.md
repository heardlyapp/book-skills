# Reference: The Tree

## Overview
The Tree of Heaven is the novel's central symbol. It represents survival against all odds.

## Case: The Introduction
The novel opens with the tree. It grows in boarded-up lots, out of rubbish heaps, through cement. It does not need sun, water, or earth. It survives.

## Case: The Parallel
Francie is like the tree. She grows in poverty, neglect, and hardship. She survives. She thrives.

## Case: The Cutting
The Nolans cut down the tree in their yard. It seems dead. But it grows back. The roots are too deep. The tree will not die.

## Case: The Meaning
The tree represents the immigrant experience, the poor, the forgotten. They grow where they are planted. They survive.

## Case: The Legacy
The Tree of Heaven is not considered beautiful. But it is beautiful because it survives. Francie is the same.

## Case: The Seeds
The tree's seeds are everywhere. The tree does not need to be planted. It plants itself. It will grow wherever it lands.

## Case: The Brooklyn Tenements
The tree is the only thing that grows in the tenement yards. It is the symbol of the people who live there — the poor, the immigrants, the forgotten.
The Tree of Heaven is the novel's argument: survival is not about where you start. It is about what you are made of.
Francie is the tree. She grows through cement.
The tree does not apologize for growing. Neither does Francie.
That is the novel's message: grow where you are planted. Survive. Thrive.
The Tree of Heaven: it grows in Brooklyn. And so do the best of us.
