# 2 — Key Principles

> Source: *Red Notice* by Bill Browder.

## The 7 Principles

### 1. Sunlight Is the Only Disinfectant

In a system where every institution is corrupt, the only protection is exposure. Browder's entire career in Russia was built on this principle. He shined light on corporate abuse, government theft, and human rights violations. The light was often the only thing that worked.

> **Case: The Gazprom Campaign (Chapter 10):** When Gazprom was stealing from minority shareholders through transfer pricing, Browder went public. He calculated that Gazprom was selling gas at a tenth of the market price to a shell company owned by the same management. He published the analysis. The scandal forced Gazprom's management to change course. Share prices tripled. Transparency worked.
> **Key takeaway**: Even the most powerful oligarchs fear public exposure. Publishing the math made the theft undeniable.

> **Case: Natalia's Phone Call (Chapter 30):** When Browder called Sergei's mother to express condolences, Natalia was inconsolable. "Sergei was her only child and meant everything to her." Browder promised her he would step into Sergei's shoes and make sure the killers faced justice. This personal vow became the engine of the entire Magnitsky Act campaign.
> **Key takeaway**: A promise to the victim's family is the most powerful motivator. It turns an abstract campaign into a sacred duty.

Browder won many battles. But each victory made the opposition more determined. They didn't just want to stop him — they wanted to destroy him, his company, and anyone who helped him.

### 3. The Legal System Can Be Hijacked

The $230 million theft was done through courts, not despite them. Browder warns that when criminals control the judiciary, the law becomes a weapon against the innocent.

### 4. One Person's Story Changes Everything

"The statistics of one" is the most powerful tool in human rights. Sergei Magnitsky was not a number. He was a 37-year-old lawyer, a father of two, a husband, a son. His mother Natalia's grief — being told by a prison official "he died of pancreonecrosis" in a monotone — made the injustice real in a way that statistics never could.

### 5. Personal Sacrifice Is the Price of Change

Browder lost his business, his freedom to travel, his ability to see his son on alternating weekends, and his peace of mind. He gained a legacy that changed international law. He would tell you it was worth it.

### 6. Find Champions Inside the System

Browder could not pass the Magnitsky Act alone. He needed Senator John McCain and Senator Ben Cardin. They became the champions because Browder had a compelling story, irrefutable evidence, and relentless persistence.

### 7. Never Stop. They Are Waiting for You to Give Up.

The Russian government assumed Browder would eventually stop campaigning. Seven years after Sergei's death, the Magnitsky Act was law. Similar laws were passed in the UK, Canada, and EU. He never stopped.
