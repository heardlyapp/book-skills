# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *Red Notice* by Bill Browder.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The "If I Disappear" Protocol

Have a documented, verified package of evidence that can be released immediately if you are arrested, kidnapped, or killed. Sergei's handwritten prison diary was released within hours of his death. The rapid release made it impossible for the Russian government to control the narrative.

> **Case: Sergei's Prison Diary (Chapter 30):** During his year in Butyrka, Sergei recorded every denial of medical care. He wrote down which guard refused him, what date, and what his symptoms were. The diary proved that the Interior Ministry's claim that "he never complained about his health" was a lie. The diary was released to the press the same day his death was announced.
> **Key takeaway**: The most powerful evidence is evidence collected in real-time. Sergei's diary was not a reconstruction — it was a contemporaneous record.

> **Case: The Gazprom Math (Chapter 10):** Browder calculated that Gazprom was selling gas to a management-owned shell company at one-tenth of the market price. The math was simple, irrefutable, and publishable. When the Financial Times ran the story, the board had to respond. The numbers themselves became the weapon.
> **Key takeaway**: In financial crime, the math is the evidence. Make it simple enough that anyone can understand it.

In a corrupt system, being completely transparent about your business operations is a defense against false accusations. Browder's Hermitage Capital maintained rigorous books, published its methodology, and welcomed scrutiny. When the Russian government accused him of tax evasion, the evidence was already public — and it showed the accusation was false.

### 3. The Coalition-Building Method

Identify potential allies across political lines. Browder found champions in John McCain (Republican) and Ben Cardin (Democrat). He worked with human rights organizations, journalists, and politicians of all parties. A broad coalition is harder to discredit than a partisan one.

### 4. The Personal Story Arc

When presenting a human rights case, structure it as a story: (1) The victim's normal life before the injustice. (2) The crime. (3) The cover-up. (4) The fight for truth. (5) The call to action. Sergei was a "devoted family man" before "Russian police stole $230 million and he exposed them" → "They arrested, tortured, and killed him" → "They lied about it" → "I have the evidence" → "Help me pass the Magnitsky Act."

### 5. The International Leverage Model

When domestic remedies fail: (1) Get the story into international media. (2) Find a political champion in a powerful country. (3) Craft legislative remedy. (4) Build bilateral pressure on the offending regime. (5) Repeat in other countries.
