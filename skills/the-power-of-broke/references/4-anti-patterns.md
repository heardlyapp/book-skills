> Source: The Power of Broke, Chapters 1-10 — 6 anti-patterns of bootstrapping

## Anti-Pattern 1: Waiting for Funding

"I'll start when I get investors." Daymond's response: by the time you get investors, you will have spent years waiting. You could have spent those years building.

> **Case: The Dreamers** (Chapter 9): Daymond describes entrepreneurs who spend years writing business plans and pitching investors instead of building a product and selling it. They become professional fundraisers instead of business builders.
> **Key takeaway**: If you can start without funding, you should. Funding amplifies momentum — it does not create it.

## Anti-Pattern 2: Spending Before Earning

The temptation to invest in "professional" everything—website, logo, office space, equipment—before making a single sale.

> **Case: The Office Space** (Chapter 5): Daymond worked from his mother's house for years. He didn't need an office. He needed a sewing machine. Spending money on things that don't directly generate revenue is not investment — it's avoidance.
> **Key takeaway**: Every dollar spent is a dollar not invested in growth. Spend only on what directly generates revenue.

## Anti-Pattern 3: Ignoring the Numbers

"You can't manage what you can't measure." The broke entrepreneur knows every number because survival depends on it.

> **Case: The Shark That Got Away** (Chapter 9): Daymond rejected a deal on Shark Tank because the entrepreneur didn't know his cost per acquisition. Great product, no understanding of the business. The product failed.
> **Key takeaway**: Your passion for your product is not a substitute for knowing your numbers.

## Anti-Pattern 4: Broke + Lazy = Dead

Being broke without the hustle to match is fatal. The Power of Broke only works if you are willing to work harder than anyone else.

> **Case: The Entitled Founder** (Chapter 7): Daymond describes a would-be entrepreneur who wanted to start a business but refused to do the grunt work — cold-calling, selling, cleaning, organizing. He wanted to be the "visionary." He never started.
> **Key takeaway**: Being broke is not enough. You must also have the hustle. Broke without hustle is just poor.

## Anti-Pattern 5: Not Understanding Your Customer

"If you don't know who you're selling to, you can't sell to them." The broke entrepreneur must understand the customer intimately because there is no money to waste on broad marketing.

> **Case: The Wrong Audience** (Chapter 6): Daymond launched a product aimed at an audience he didn't understand. It failed. He learned: start with the customer, not the product.
> **Key takeaway**: Your first job is not to create a product. It is to understand your customer. The product comes second.

## Anti-Pattern 6: Quitting When It Gets Hard

"Most businesses fail because the founder stops trying." Daymond's message: persistence is the only thing that separates successful entrepreneurs from unsuccessful ones.

> **Case: The First Rejection** (Chapter 8): Daymond was rejected by every retailer before Macy's said yes. If he had stopped after the first, tenth, or hundredth no, FUBU would not exist.
> **Key takeaway**: The only no that matters is the last one — when you stop trying.
