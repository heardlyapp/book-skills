> Source: The Power of Broke, Introduction through Chapter 10 — 7 principles of the broke advantage

## Principle 1: Necessity Is the Mother of Reinvention

When you have no money, you are forced to find creative solutions. These solutions — born of scarcity — are often more innovative than what money would have bought. Necessity doesn't just mother invention — it mothers reinvention.

> **Case: The First FUBU Hats** (Chapter 1): Daymond needed hats for his fledgling brand but had no money to manufacture them. He went to a local hat store, bought generic blank hats, and hand-sewed the FUBU logo onto each one. The result: a unique, handcrafted product that customers loved. If he had money to mass-manufacture, the hats would not have had the authenticity that made them special.
> **Key takeaway**: Scarcity produces authenticity. When you can't buy a solution, you invent one — and that invention often becomes your competitive advantage.

## Principle 2: Hustle Is a Force Multiplier

Hustle multiplies whatever resources you have. If you have no money, hustle is your only currency. If you have some money, hustle makes it go further. Daymond defines hustle not as desperation — but as the disciplined willingness to do whatever it takes.

> **Case: The Cold Call That Changed Everything** (Chapter 3): Daymond cold-called a buyer at Macy's for months. He was told no repeatedly. He kept calling. Eventually, the buyer agreed to meet. That meeting led to FUBU's first major department store deal. The hustle cost nothing but time. The return was worth millions.
> **Key takeaway**: Hustle is not shouting "I want it." Hustle is consistently showing up, consistently asking, consistently improving until the answer changes from no to yes.

## Principle 3: Sweat Equity Is Real Equity

The time and effort you invest in your business is equity. Daymond argues that founders who bootstrap — who trade sweat for capital — are often more valuable than founders who raise money. They have learned every aspect of the business.

> **Case: The Sewing Machine** (Chapter 2): Daymond learned to sew, to cut patterns, to manage inventory, to negotiate with suppliers, to sell, to market — all because he had no money to hire experts. By the time he needed to hire, he knew enough to know who was good. The bootstrapped founder, Daymond argues, knows the business from the inside out.
> **Key takeaway**: Sweat equity is not inferior to financial equity. It is more valuable because it comes with knowledge.

## Principle 4: Know Your Numbers Cold

One of Daymond's key requirements for entrepreneurs on Shark Tank: they must know their numbers. Cost, margin, break-even, customer acquisition cost, lifetime value. The Power of Broke forces you to know these because you cannot afford to be wrong.

> **Case: The Pitch That Failed** (Chapter 9): Daymond describes a Shark Tank pitch where an entrepreneur had a great product but didn't know how much it cost to acquire a customer. "If you don't know that number," Daymond told him, "you don't know if you have a business." The entrepreneur didn't get a deal.
> **Key takeaway**: In a bootstrapped business, every dollar is visible. There is no room for ignorance about the numbers. Know them cold.

## Principle 5: Your Brand Is Your Story

A brand is not a logo or a product. It is the story people tell themselves about you. The Power of Broke means your story is authentic — you are not a corporation pretending to be cool. You are the real thing.

> **Case: The FUBU Name** (Chapter 5): The name FUBU — For Us, By Us — told a story in four letters. It said: this brand represents our community, our culture, our values. No amount of advertising could have communicated that message more powerfully. The name itself was the brand.
> **Key takeaway**: Your brand is not what you say about yourself. It is the story your customers tell themselves about you. Make that story real.

## Principle 6: Momentum Creates Its Own Resources

Daymond argues that you don't need resources to start. You need to start to create momentum, and momentum attracts resources. Customers, partners, investors, and talent are all drawn to momentum.

> **Case: The Saks Fifth Avenue Order** (Chapter 7): After the $800K trade show order, FUBU had momentum. Saks Fifth Avenue reached out to Daymond — not the other way around. The momentum from one big sale created the next. The business grew because it was growing.
> **Key takeaway**: Don't wait for resources to start. Start with what you have. Momentum will bring the resources.

## Principle 7: Failure Is Tuition

Daymond has failed multiple times — businesses that didn't work, investments that went bad, products that flopped. He frames every failure as tuition for a lesson he needed to learn. The cost of the lesson is the tuition. The lesson itself is the value.

> **Case: The Failed Brand** (Chapter 10): Daymond launched a brand that didn't resonate. It failed after two years. He lost money. But he learned a critical lesson: a brand must have a reason to exist beyond the founder's desire. That lesson informed every subsequent decision.
> **Key takeaway**: If you learn from failure, the tuition was worth it. If you don't learn, you paid for nothing.
