## 1. The Broke Budget

**The technique**: Your budget determines your business. When you have no money, every dollar must work. The Broke Budget means: spend only on what you absolutely cannot do yourself.

**How to do it**: List every expense. For each one, ask: "Can I do this myself? Can I barter for it? Do I actually need it?" Cut everything that doesn't directly generate revenue or product. The Broke Budget is not about being cheap — it's about being focused.

## 2. The Cold-Call Marathon

**The technique**: Daymond's signature move — call every potential customer, partner, and contact until someone says yes. Expect rejection. Treat it as a numbers game.

**How to do it**: Set a daily quota. 50 calls. 100 calls. Track responses. Learn from rejections. Refine your pitch. The next call might be the one.

## 3. The $40 Booth Strategy

**The technique**: Start small. The smallest possible entry point. A $40 booth at a trade show. A table at a street fair. A stall at a flea market. Prove your concept at the lowest possible cost.

**How to do it**: Find the cheapest possible venue where your target customer will be. Show up. Sell. Learn. Validate.

## 4. The Grassroots Brand Campaign

**The technique**: Build your brand by being where your customers are — not through advertising, but through presence. Wear your own brand. Tell your story. Talk to people.

**How to do it**: In the early days, you are your brand. Wear your product. Talk about it. Answer questions. Hand out samples. People buy from people, not from logos.

> **Case: The Guerilla Billboard** (Chapter 6): Daymond couldn't afford billboard advertising, so he made his own. He and his friends wore FUBU shirts everywhere. They put stickers on telephone poles. They handed out flyers at concerts. Every person wearing a FUBU shirt was a walking billboard — and they didn't cost a dime.
> **Key takeaway**: Your customers are your best advertisers. When you can't buy attention, earn it through authenticity and word of mouth.

> **Case: The Street Corner Pitch** (Chapter 4): Daymond spent hours on street corners in Queens, approaching strangers to sell FUBU shirts. He learned more about his customer in one afternoon of street selling than he could have learned from a $50,000 marketing study. The street was his focus group.
> **Key takeaway**: The cheapest marketing research is talking to people. Go where your customers are. Listen to them.

## 5. The Sweat Equity Calculator

**The technique**: Calculate the value of your sweat equity. Every hour you work instead of paying someone is an investment. Track it.

**How to do it**: Multiply your hours by the market rate for that work. That's your sweat equity contribution. It is as real as cash. Do not discount it.
