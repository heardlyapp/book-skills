# Principles: Character, Contracts, and Crisis

> Source: Civilian Warriors, Chapters 5-11

## Character Counts

Prince devotes Chapter 11 to this principle. Blackwater hired former SEALs, Marines, Rangers, and special operators. The quality of the person was everything. He argues that the "mercenary" label is applied by people who have never met the contractors — who are motivated by patriotism as much as pay.

> **Case: The Six Geneva Conditions** (Chapter 11): Under Additional Protocol I of the Geneva Conventions, a mercenary must meet all six conditions: specially recruited to fight, directly participating in hostilities, motivated primarily by private gain, not a national of any party, not a member of armed forces, and not sent by a state. Blackwater's men were American citizens, former military, serving defensive roles for lawful U.S. agencies. They met almost none of these conditions.

> **Case: Frank Gallagher** (Introduction): The head of Bremer's security detail. A barrel-chested former operator who took responsibility for the ambush response. Before the mission, he told his team: "We promised to have a cup of mead in Valhalla later this evening." This gallows humor is characteristic of the culture.

## Speed Over Perfection

In crisis, hesitation kills. The Bremer ambush was survived because of constant drills, real-time radio coordination, and split-second decisions made without deliberation.

> **Key takeaway:** In high-stakes environments, speed of execution matters more than perfection of planning. Train until the response is automatic.
