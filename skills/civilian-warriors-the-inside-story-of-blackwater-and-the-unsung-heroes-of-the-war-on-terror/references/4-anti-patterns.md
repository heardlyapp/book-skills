# Anti-Patterns: What Undermines Security Operations

> Source: Civilian Warriors, Chapters 14-16

## The "Mercenary" Smear

The most persistent attack on Blackwater was labeling its contractors "mercenaries." This is legally inaccurate but politically effective.

> **Case: The Geneva Convention Distinction** (Chapter 11): Prince explains that under Additional Protocol I, a mercenary must meet six specific conditions. Blackwater's contractors were American citizens, former U.S. military, working for U.S. agencies, in defensive roles. They met almost none. But the label stuck because it was useful politically — Democrats needed a villain for the Iraq War.

> **Key takeaway:** When the media calls you a name, correcting the record is necessary but not sufficient. The accusation sticks regardless of its accuracy.

## Nisour Square

September 16, 2007. Blackwater contractors opened fire in a Baghdad square, killing 17 Iraqi civilians. The event that triggered Congressional investigations, Iraqi government outcry, and ultimately Blackwater's loss of its State Department contract.

Regardless, the damage was done. Blackwater personnel were prosecuted, the company lost its contracts, and the brand became toxic.

## Political Blowback

When a company becomes a political target, standard crisis management isn't enough. The rules of engagement change.

> **Key takeaway:** If you're providing services in a politically charged environment, expect to become a target yourself. Build relationships on both sides of the aisle. Have a contingency plan for when the political winds shift.
