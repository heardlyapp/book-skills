# Core Framework: Building Blackwater

> Source: Civilian Warriors, Chapters 1-4

## The Great Dismal Swamp

Erik Prince bought 6,000 acres in North Carolina's Great Dismal Swamp to build a tactical training facility. He started with a shooting range, then added buildings, vehicles, and ultimately a helicopter fleet. The company grew from teaching local police to supporting U.S. special operations. At its peak, Blackwater had the largest privately owned tactical training facility in the world.

> **Case: The First Contract** (Chapter 4): Blackwater's first major break came after the USS Cole bombing. The Navy needed anti-terrorism training and Blackwater had the only suitable facility. Prince had built capability before there was demand. When the market appeared, he was ready. This is a core business lesson: invest in infrastructure before the contract.

> **Case: The Columbine Connection** (Chapter 3): After the Columbine school shooting, schools everywhere needed active shooter training. Blackwater became a leader in this space — but Prince found the margins thin and the work emotionally draining. This taught him a critical lesson: focus on high-value government contracts, not low-margin commercial work.

## The Bremer Ambush (Introduction)

December 6, 2003. Paul Bremer insisted on seeing Secretary Rumsfeld off at Baghdad airport despite the danger. Blackwater's convoy was ambushed on the return journey. The lead SUV was hit by an IED, AK-47 fire rattled the vehicles. Blackwater helicopters provided cover. No one was injured. The code "Tuna! Tuna! Tuna!" meant drive through the ambush — execute the emergency protocol immediately without hesitation.

> **Key takeaway:** When the plan fails, execute the emergency protocol. No deliberation. No second-guessing. Trust the training.
