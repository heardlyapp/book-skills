> Source: Lords of Finance, Chapters 7-15 — specific techniques, tools, and methods used (or that should have been used) by interwar central bankers

## 1. The Deflation vs. Devaluation Decision Matrix

Every country after WWI faced the same choice: deflate (contract the money supply, lower prices, accept unemployment) or devalue (reduce the currency's gold value, protect employment, penalize savers). The correct choice depends on:

| Factor | Choose Deflation | Choose Devaluation |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Debt burden | Low | High |
| Trade competitiveness | Strong | Weak |
| Political stability | High | Fragile |
| External obligations | Large | Manageable |
| Labor market flexibility | High | Rigid |

**Application**: Britain chose deflation when it should have chosen devaluation (its trade competitiveness was permanently damaged by the war). France chose devaluation — deliberately undervaluing the franc — and prospered.

> **Case: Britain's Wrong Choice** (Chapter 12): Churchill agonized for months. His dinner debate with Keynes, McKenna, Niemeyer, and Bradbury on March 17, 1925, went past midnight. McKenna's final verdict: "There is no escape. You will have to go back; but it will be hell." Churchill went back. It was hell — a decade of unemployment above 10%.
> **Key takeaway**: When the choice is painful either way, choose the pain that falls on the group most able to absorb it.

## 2. Currency Stabilization — The Scarcity Principle

Schacht ended Germany's hyperinflation not with gold but with a commitment to scarcity. The Rentenmark's issuance was rigidly capped at 2.4 billion. He turned down every loan request — from government agencies, municipalities, banks, and industrialists. His office was a converted broom closet. His secretary was his only employee. He made no speeches. He just refused to print more.

> **Case: Schacht's Rentenmark** (Chapter 10): On November 20, 1923, Schacht fixed the conversion rate at 1 trillion old marks to 1 Rentenmark. He had deliberately waited until the old currency had fallen another 80% — allowing the government to buy back its trillions of debt for pennies. The result: within weeks, food reappeared in shops, queues melted away, and British Ambassador d'Abernon wrote of "astonishing appeasement and relief brought about by a touch of the magical wand of Currency Stability."
> **Key takeaway**: Stabilizing a collapsing currency requires demonstrated discipline, not theoretical backing. Scarcity builds credibility faster than gold.

## 3. Open Market Operations — The Strong Innovation

Benjamin Strong invented the modern tool of central banking — open market operations. Before Strong, central banks changed credit conditions only by adjusting the discount rate (the rate at which they lent to member banks). Strong realized that by buying or selling government securities from the Fed's portfolio, he could directly and immediately alter the quantity of money in the banking system.

**The technique**: When the Fed buys securities, it pays with newly created reserves, expanding the money supply. When it sells, it withdraws reserves, contracting it.

> **Case: The 1924 Easing** (Chapter 12): Strong used open market purchases to ease U.S. credit conditions in 1924, partly to help Britain prepare for its return to gold. This was the first coordinated central bank operation in history. It worked — but it also fueled speculation that would become the 1929 bubble.
> **Key takeaway**: A powerful new tool is also a dangerous one — because you don't yet understand its side effects.

## 4. The Dawes Plan — Kick the Can Down the Road

Owen Young's approach to the impossible reparations problem was brilliantly pragmatic: avoid the total figure altogether. Instead of asking "How much can Germany pay?" (a question no one could agree on), he asked "How much should Germany pay in the first year?" and built an escrow mechanism to prevent the mark from being destabilized.

**The structure**:
- Year 1: $250M → Year 5: $600M (graduated ramp)
- German payments go into a special escrow account
- Agent-General for Reparations (an American) decides when funds can be transferred abroad
- $200M international loan to jump-start the economy
- Reichsbank placed under a 14-member board (7 foreigners, 7 Germans)

> **Case: The London Conference** (Chapter 11): Montagu Norman and Thomas Lamont of J.P. Morgan dictated terms to the French. They insisted French troops leave the Ruhr before any loan was made. The French prime minister Herriot burst into tears of frustration. Lamont tightened the screws by hinting that Morgans might not roll over France's own loans. The bankers won.
> **Key takeaway**: When a problem seems unsolvable, change the question. Don't ask "how much" — ask "how much right now."

## 5. The Gold Standard Escape Hatch — Suspending Convertibility

When the pressure became unbearable, central banks could — and did — suspend gold convertibility. Every belligerent did it in 1914. Germany did it preemptively in July 1914, saving its gold reserves. Britain abandoned gold in 1914 and returned in 1925. The U.S. never suspended (it had too much gold), and Britain finally abandoned gold for good in 1931.

**The technique**: When a central bank faces a run, it simply stops honoring its legal obligation to exchange paper notes for gold. This preserves reserves and buys time. The cost: the currency loses its "badge of honor."

> **Case: Britain's 1931 Abandonment** (Chapters 20-21): The run on sterling in 1931 forced Britain off gold permanently. Norman was in Canada recovering from a nervous breakdown. The decision was made by the cabinet, not the Bank. Britain never returned to the gold standard.
> **Key takeaway**: When a rule is destroying you, break it. The rules are for normal times; in a crisis, survival comes first.

## 6. Building International Central Bank Cooperation

Norman's vision of a "league of central bankers" was ahead of its time but never fully realized. His "most exclusive club" (the four central bankers) did coordinate on specific issues — the Dawes Plan, the return to gold, the 1927 rate cut — but it was personality-driven, not institutionalized.

> **Case: The 1927 Rate Cut** (Chapter 15): Norman, Strong, Schacht, and Moreau met secretly on Long Island in July 1927 — actually Strong's apartment at 903 Park Avenue — to coordinate a reduction in interest rates. The U.S. cut rates to help Britain. The effect: fuel on a stock market fire.
> **Key takeaway**: Personal relationships between central bankers can achieve things formal diplomacy cannot — but they also create blind spots and groupthink.
