## 7 Principles for Navigating Financial Decision-Making

### Principle 1: Question Dogma When Everyone Agrees

The gold standard commanded near-universal assent among bankers after WWI. The Cunliffe Committee, every central banker, and every finance minister believed returning to prewar gold parity was essential. Keynes alone called it a "barbarous relic." The consensus was wrong — and it cost millions of jobs.

> **Case: The Cunliffe Committee** (Chapter 9): In 1918, every single witness before the committee favored return to gold at the prewar rate. Not one dissenter. The committee's report became holy writ for British monetary policy for the next decade. No one questioned whether the prewar world had permanently changed.
> **Key takeaway**: When you hear "everyone knows this is true," ask what evidence would change your mind.

### Principle 2: Creditor Nations Must Recycle Surpluses

After WWI, the U.S. accumulated $4.5 billion of the world's $6 billion in monetary gold — 75% of the total. The Federal Reserve, led by Strong, "sterilized" these inflows, preventing them from circulating to Europe. This starved European economies of the liquidity needed to rebuild and maintain the gold standard.

> **Case: Strong's Gold Sterilization** (Chapter 9): Strong refused to let U.S. gold inflows expand American credit, correctly fearing inflation. But by hoarding the gold, he made it impossible for Europe to rebuild its reserves. The global system became a poker game where one player had all the chips.
> **Key takeaway**: Surplus nations have a responsibility to recycle capital — hoarding creates systemic risk.

### Principle 3: Debt Forgiveness Is Cheaper Than Collection

The interlocking debts of WWI — Germany owed $12.5B in reparations, Allies owed $12B to the U.S. — created a "vicious cycle of financial claims." The U.S. insisted on collecting war debts at 80 cents on the dollar (Britain) or 40 cents (France). This forced the Allies to insist on maximum reparations from Germany, which in turn destroyed the German economy and paved the way for Hitler.

> **Case: The Balfour Note** (Chapter 8): Britain offered to collect only as much from its debtors as the U.S. collected from Britain, effectively proposing a linked settlement. The U.S. rejected it, insisting war debts and reparations were separate. "Uncle Shylock" became the American image in Europe.
> **Key takeaway**: Insisting on collecting every dollar of debt can cost more — in political stability, trade, and human welfare — than forgiving it.

### Principle 4: Devaluation Is Not Defeat — It's Adjustment

Britain's refusal to devalue the pound after WWI was the single most consequential monetary decision of the 1920s. The choice was framed as a moral question — "honoring obligations" versus "cheating creditors." In reality, devaluation was the rational adjustment to a new economic reality.

> **Case: France vs. Britain** (Chapters 9, 13): Britain deflated (prices down 50%, unemployment above 10%) to return to gold at $4.86. France devalued the franc to one-fifth of its prewar value and kept unemployment low. French exports boomed; British industry stagnated. The "virtuous" policy destroyed the real economy; the "irresponsible" one preserved it.
> **Key takeaway**: When the world changes, refusing to adjust the price of your currency (or your product, or your strategy) is not virtue — it's denial.

### Principle 5: Watch the Person, Not Just the Policy

The four central bankers' decisions were deeply shaped by their personalities, health, and psychology. Norman's manic depression, Strong's tuberculosis, Schacht's fragile ego, Moreau's xenophobia — these human factors mattered more than any economic model.

> **Case: Strong's Death and the Fed Vacuum** (Chapters 16-17): After Strong died in 1928, the Fed fell into the hands of the Federal Reserve Board — a "body of startling incompetence" according to Galbraith. Adolph Miller argued with himself. Daniel Crissinger, a childhood friend of Harding's, was "utterly devoid of global or economic banking sense." The paralysis of leadership directly contributed to the Fed's failure to stop the 1929 bubble.
> **Key takeaway**: Institutionalize your thinking. If your organization depends on one person's judgment, it's not a system — it's a personality cult.

### Principle 6: Inflation Destroys the Middle Class — Extremism Follows

Nobody in power understood this fundamental truth in 1923. The hyperinflation wiped out the savings of Germany's professional classes — civil servants, doctors, teachers, professors — who had invested their lives in government bonds and bank deposits. These became worthless overnight. The middle class was pauperized, radicalized, and ready to listen to extremists.

> **Case: German Hyperinflation, 1923** (Chapters 7, 10): A kilo of butter cost 250 billion marks. Workers were paid daily and given half-hour breaks to rush out and spend their wages before the money lost value. A Texan hired the full Berlin Philharmonic for $100. "Legless war veterans rode the sidewalks on rolling planks" while foreign speculators lived in duplex apartments with two maids. The depression of the Mittelstand directly enabled the rise of Nazism.
> **Key takeaway**: The social cost of inflation is not abstract — it creates the conditions for political catastrophe.

### Principle 7: Financial Crises Are Psychological — Greed to Fear Is the Arc

The book documents a pattern that repeats across centuries: a surge of optimism → cavalier risk-taking by bankers → mania → sudden shock → panic → forced liquidation → contagion → collapse. The first documented bank panic was in A.D. 33 when Tiberius had to inject gold into the Roman system.

> **Case: The 1929 Crash and Banking Panics** (Chapters 16-20): The Wall Street boom turned to panic in October 1929. The Creditanstalt failed in May 1931. The Danatbank failed in July 1931. Bank runs spread across Germany, then to London, then to the U.S. The Bank of England had to borrow $650 million from France and the U.S. to prevent its gold reserves from being depleted. Norman predicted "the capitalist system throughout the civilized world will be wrecked within a year."
> **Key takeaway**: When bankers stop trusting each other, the system stops working — and restoring trust is the hardest job in finance.
