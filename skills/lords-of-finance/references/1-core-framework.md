## The Four Central Bankers — Engines of a Decade

### Montagu Norman — Bank of England (1920-1944)

The neurotic genius. Norman was a manic-depressive who consulted Jung (misdiagnosed with syphilis), practiced autosuggestion, and traveled as "Professor Clarence Skinner." Despite his psychological fragility, he dominated the City of London for 24 years. His core belief: the gold standard was a moral covenant, not an economic tool. He engineered Britain's return to gold at $4.86 in 1925 against Keynes's warnings, threw his weight behind the Dawes Plan, and formed the central bankers' "most exclusive club" with Strong, Schacht, and Moreau.

> **Case: The Return to Gold** (Chapter 12): Norman pressured Churchill into returning to the prewar gold parity of $4.86, despite British prices being 10% too high. The result: a decade of overvalued sterling, chronic unemployment above 10%, and the General Strike of 1926. Churchill later called it "the greatest mistake of my life."
> **Key takeaway**: Defending an outdated orthodoxy for the sake of "credibility" can destroy the real economy.

> **Case: The Nervous Breakdown of 1931** (Introduction): As the world banking system collapsed, Norman suffered a breakdown and fled to Canada. The Bank's press release admitted he was "incapacitated." At the moment of maximum crisis, the most powerful central banker in the world was unavailable.
> **Key takeaway**: When decision-making power is concentrated in one fragile person, the system itself becomes fragile.

### Benjamin Strong — Federal Reserve Bank of New York (1914-1928)

The visionary who built modern central banking. Strong was the architect of the Federal Reserve's open market operations and the first central banker to explicitly target domestic price stability. He was a key participant in the secret Jekyll Island meeting that designed the Fed. Despite battling tuberculosis for over a decade, he dominated U.S. monetary policy. His fatal error: ignoring the 1920s stock market bubble.

> **Case: The 1920s Boom** (Chapters 14-15): Strong kept U.S. interest rates low in 1927 to help Britain stay on gold, pumping liquidity into a frothy stock market. After his death in 1928, the Fed — leaderless and divided — could not agree on how to respond, and the bubble grew to catastrophic proportions.
> **Key takeaway**: A central bank run by one brilliant man becomes paralyzed when he's gone — Strong failed to institutionalize his thinking.

### Hjalmar Schacht — Reichsbank (1923-1930, 1933-1939)

The brilliant, arrogant opportunist. Schacht rose from a lower-middle-class background to become Germany's currency czar. He ended the hyperinflation of 1923 by creating the Rentenmark and fixing the conversion rate at 4.2 trillion marks to the dollar. He later flirted with Hitler, served as Hitler's central banker, and was tried at Nuremberg (acquitted).

> **Case: Ending Hyperinflation** (Chapter 10): Schacht's Rentenmark was backed by land, not gold — a fiction. But its strict issuance limit of 2.4 billion marks and Schacht's ruthless refusal to lend to anyone — government agencies, municipalities, industrialists — restored confidence within days. "He sat on his chair and smoked... he telephoned... Apart from that he did nothing."
> **Key takeaway**: Credibility comes from scarcity and discipline, not from the underlying asset.

> **Case: The Dawes Plan Negotiations** (Chapter 11): Schacht charmed Norman in London on New Year's Eve 1923, secured a $25 million loan from the Bank of England, then testified before the Dawes Committee in Paris — speaking without notes in fluent English and French. He was the only German official to vote against accepting the Dawes Plan.
> **Key takeaway**: Political skill and technical expertise together create outsized influence.

### Émile Moreau — Banque de France (1926-1930)

The xenophobic provincial. Moreau, a former inspector of finances, accumulated the world's largest gold hoard at the Banque de France — $800 million by 1914. He distrusted the British and Americans, refused to cooperate with Norman, and pursued an aggressively competitive devaluation of the franc that made French exports boom while Britain stagnated.

> **Case: The Franc Stabilization** (Chapter 13): Moreau stabilized the franc at one-fifth of its prewar value, deliberately undervaluing it. The result: French exports soared, unemployment stayed low, and France became an island of prosperity while the rest of Europe struggled. His motto: "We must be strong to be independent."
> **Key takeaway**: Devaluation, when done deliberately, is a competitive weapon — not a sign of weakness.

## The Gold Standard — Architecture of a Catastrophe

The prewar gold standard worked because gold discoveries happened to keep pace with economic growth. After WWI, the world's gold was concentrated in the U.S. (over $4 billion out of $6 billion total). European central banks had to deflate their economies to maintain convertibility. The system became a "one-legged gold standard" — America played by new rules (managed money), while Europe tried to play by old rules (automatic gold link). Keynes called it a "barbarous relic."

## The Reparations Trap

The Treaty of Versailles saddled Germany with $12.5 billion in reparations — roughly 100% of its prewar GDP. To pay, Germany had to export more than it imported, which required trade surplus countries (the U.S., Britain) to accept German goods — which they refused to do. The only alternative was borrowing: the U.S. lent to Germany, Germany paid France and Britain, France and Britain paid the U.S. — a circular flow that worked only as long as American lending continued. When it stopped in 1929, the entire system collapsed.

## The Keynes Counterpoint

John Maynard Keynes is the book's fifth character — not a banker but a brilliant gadfly. His *Economic Consequences of the Peace* (1919) predicted that reparations would destroy Germany. His *Tract on Monetary Reform* (1923) called the gold standard a "barbarous relic." His advice was consistently ignored by the four bankers. He speculated on currencies, married a Russian ballerina, and was right about almost everything.
