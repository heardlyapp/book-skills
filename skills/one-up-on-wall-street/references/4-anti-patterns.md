# Anti-Patterns

## 1. Trying to Time the Market

**The mistake**: Waiting for the "right time" to invest.

**Fix**: Time in the market beats timing the market. Invest when you find a good company at a good price.

## 2. Falling in Love with a Stock

**The mistake**: Holding a stock forever because you once made money on it.

**Fix**: The story changes. Sell when it does.

> **Case: The Once-Great Retailer** (Chapter 18): A stock had been a Lynch favorite for years — strong brand, loyal customers, growing earnings. Then the story changed: online competition emerged, earnings slowed. Investors who held on because they loved the stock lost money. Those who sold based on the new story preserved their gains.
> **Key takeaway**: No stock is a permanent holding. The question is always: is the story still intact?

## 3. Buying What's Popular

**The mistake**: Buying stocks everyone is talking about.

**Fix**: The best tenbaggers are found before they become popular. By the time everyone knows about a stock, the easy money has been made.

> **Case: The Hot IPO** (Chapter 7): Lynch warns against buying hot IPOs. The stocks that get the most hype are usually the worst investments. The best opportunities are in boring companies that nobody is talking about.
> **Key takeaway**: When your taxi driver gives you a stock tip, it's time to sell.

## 4. Ignoring the PEG Ratio

**The mistake**: Looking at P/E ratio alone without considering growth.

**Fix**: A high P/E stock can be cheap if growth is high. A low P/E stock can be expensive if growth is negative. The PEG ratio tells the real story.

## 5. Panic Selling

**The mistake**: Selling when the market drops.

**Fix**: Market corrections are buying opportunities, not selling signals. If the company story hasn't changed, a lower price is a better price.
