# Techniques: The Stock Research Process

## The Two-Minute Drill Process

1. Start with a company whose products/services you know and like
2. Determine which of the six categories it belongs to
3. Find the P/E ratio and growth rate — calculate the PEG
4. Check the balance sheet for debt levels
5. Look for insider buying — executives buying their own stock is the best signal
6. Check the story — is the company still doing what made it successful?
7. The final question: can I explain this to a 10-year-old in two minutes?

## Stock Categories at a Glance

| Category | PEG Target | Key Question |
|----------|-----------|--------------|
| Slow Grower | N/A (check dividend) | Is the dividend safe? |
| Stalwart | 1.0-1.5 | What's the next catalyst? |
| Fast Grower | Under 1.0 | Can growth continue? |
| Cyclical | Varies | Where are we in the cycle? |
| Turnaround | N/A | Is the turnaround real? |
| Asset Play | Below book value | What are assets worth? |

## When to Sell Checklist

- [ ] The story has changed (new competition, industry disruption)
- [ ] Earnings growth has slowed for two consecutive quarters
- [ ] The PEG ratio is above 1.5
- [ ] The company is diversifying into unrelated businesses
- [ ] Insiders are selling heavily
- [ ] The stock represents too large a percentage of your portfolio

> **Case: The Chrysler Turnaround** (Chapter 13): Lynch invested in Chrysler when it was near bankruptcy. The story was a turnaround — a classic American automaker with a new CEO (Lee Iacocca) and a plan. Lynch checked: debt manageable? Cash position improving? New products coming? Yes to all. The stock returned 15x.
>
> **Key takeaway**: Turnarounds can produce the biggest returns — but only if you confirm the turnaround is real. Check the cash position, debt levels, and new product pipeline.

> **Case: The Stock That Was Too Complicated** (Chapter 11): A reader asked Lynch about a company with a complex business model involving joint ventures, subsidiaries, and multiple industries. Lynch couldn't explain it in two minutes. His advice: don't invest in what you can't understand. The stock later collapsed.
>
> **Key takeaway**: Complexity is not sophistication. If you can't explain a business simply, it's usually because the business is not simple — and not an ideal investment.
