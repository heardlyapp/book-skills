> Source: Wintergirls, Laurie Halse Anderson (2009). A novel about anorexia, grief, and recovery.

# Core Framework — The Voice, the Numbers, and the Wintergirl Pact

## The Model

The novel is driven by the internal voice of Lia's eating disorder. The voice counts, judges, controls. The story is Lia's battle against the voice — and her decision to live.

> **Case: The Wintergirl Pact** (Lia's memory): Lia and Cassie made a pact when they were younger. They would be "Wintergirls" — cold, untouchable, perfect. They would be the thinnest girls in school. The pact was a symptom of their shared disease. Cassie kept the pact until it killed her. Lia is still alive, haunted by the weight of her survival.
> **Key takeaway**: Pacts made in the depths of disease are not promises to keep. They are cries for help. You can break a pact to stay alive.

> **Case: The Voice** (Throughout): Lia hears a voice that is not her own. It tells her to skip meals, to exercise more, to lie to her parents. The voice is relentless. It never sleeps. It is the disease speaking. Lia knows it is not her real voice. But she cannot always resist it.
> **Key takeaway**: The voice is not you. It is the disease. Naming it — recognizing it as separate — gives you power over it.

> **Case: Cassie's Last Call** (Opening): Cassie calls Lia thirty-three times on the night she dies. Lia does not answer. She lets the calls go to voicemail. Cassie dies alone in a motel room. Lia's guilt is overwhelming. She replays the night over and over. The unanswered calls become the central wound of the novel.
> **Key takeaway**: The guilt of not answering is heavier than any grief. If someone you love is reaching out, answer. If you did not answer, forgive yourself. You did not know.
