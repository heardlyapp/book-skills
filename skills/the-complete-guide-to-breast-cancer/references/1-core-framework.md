# Core Framework — The Complete Guide to Breast Cancer

## The Authors' Unique Perspective

Two doctors who have both had breast cancer. "We are both doctors — Trish is a GP and Liz is a breast cancer surgeon. We met on Twitter and became 'virtual chemo buddies.'" Their dual perspective as doctors and patients makes this guide uniquely practical. Case: "This is the book we wish we had been able to buy the day we were diagnosed."

## Chapter 1: Joining the Cancer Club

"For the rest of your life, you will be in one of two phases: having active treatment, or living after active treatment." The authors normalize the experience: shock, horror, disbelief are normal. "Getting breast cancer is not your fault." Case: "The lifetime risk of a woman developing breast cancer is 1 in 8."

## Chapters 3-4: Information and Emotions

Gathering information is empowering. "You probably don't even know where to start reading." Coping emotionally is equally important. "We know what it's like to have your world collapse." Case: The two phases — active treatment vs. survivorship — help patients understand what to expect.

## Chapters 6-13: Medical Treatments

Clear explanations of surgery (lumpectomy vs mastectomy), chemotherapy, radiotherapy, hormone therapy, targeted therapies (Herceptin), and breast reconstruction. "Doctors tell you what will happen, but it is patients who will show you how to cope." Case: The authors describe each treatment from both a medical and a personal perspective.

## Chapters 14-18: Living Through Treatment

Support systems, coping with body changes, treatment-induced menopause, relationships and sex, staying healthy during and after treatment. Case: Practical tips for managing side effects and maintaining quality of life.

## Chapters 19-23: Special Populations

Breast cancer in pregnancy, in men, in LGBT+ people, in older people, and secondary (metastatic) cancer. The book is inclusive. Case: "Men get breast cancer too, although it is rare."

## Chapter 24: Moving Forward

Life after treatment. Dealing with the fear of recurrence. Finding a new normal. Case: "Most people who get breast cancer go on to live for many years."
