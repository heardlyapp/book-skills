# How Leaders Stay in Power — The Strategies of Political Survival

> Source: The Dictator's Handbook, Chapters 3-6

## The Leader's Toolkit

Regardless of system type, all leaders use the same basic strategies to stay in power. The difference is in how they implement them.

### The Four Essential Strategies

| Strategy | Small Coalition (Dictatorship) | Large Coalition (Democracy) |
|----------|------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Controlled revenue | Steal from the treasury, sell natural resources, take foreign aid | Tax the population at moderate rates, borrow money |
| Distribute rewards | Give cash, land, and privileges to coalition members | Provide public goods (education, health, infrastructure) |
| Control the flow of information | Censor the media, ban opposition, control the internet | Accept a free press, tolerate criticism, spin the news |
| Manage succession | Designate an heir, eliminate rivals, purge the military | Hold regular elections, follow constitutional rules |

### The Revenue Trap

Foreign aid, oil, and natural resource revenues are particularly dangerous because they allow a leader to survive without taxing the population — which means the population has no leverage over the leader. This is the "resource curse": countries with abundant natural resources tend to be less democratic because the leader doesn't need to provide public goods in exchange for tax revenues.

> **Case: The Resource Curse** (Chapter 4)
> Countries that rely on oil, diamonds, or mineral exports for revenue have smaller winning coalition problems. The leader takes the resource revenue and distributes it to a small coalition. He doesn't need to tax the population — so the population has no representation. This is why oil-rich countries tend to be autocratic. The Saudi royal family does not need Saudi citizens to pay taxes because oil revenue funds the government. And because citizens don't pay taxes, they have no basis for demanding representation.
> **Key takeaway**: The relationship between taxation and representation is not just a slogan — it's a structural feature of political systems. Leaders who don't need to tax don't need to represent. Foreign aid and natural resources break the link between leaders and citizens.

> **Case: The Problem of Succession** (Chapter 6)
> Dictators face a chronic problem: when the leader dies or is removed, the entire coalition is at risk. The new leader may purge the old coalition and install their own. This creates intense loyalty among the old coalition (they are fighting for their survival) and intense instability during succession (all coalitions are provisional until the new leader consolidates power). Democracies solve this problem through institutionalized succession: elections, term limits, and constitutional rules that protect the coalition regardless of who wins.
> **Key takeaway**: Instability in autocratic regimes is not a bug — it's a feature of the system. The constant threat of purges and succession crises keeps coalition members loyal to the current leader. In democracies, the safety of the coalition (you won't be killed if your party loses) allows peaceful transfer of power.
