# Anti-Patterns — Common Misconceptions About Politics

> Source: The Dictator's Handbook, Chapters 1, 7, 10

## The Five Political Myths

### 1. The "Bad Leader" Myth
**Error**: "If only we could elect better people, the country would be governed better."
**Correction**: Leaders respond to incentives, not character. A good person in a system that rewards corruption will become corrupt. A bad person in a system that rewards honesty will become honest. Change the system, not the leader.

### 2. The "Ideology Matters" Myth
**Error**: "Leaders are motivated by ideology (communism, capitalism, nationalism)."
**Correction**: Leaders use ideology to justify their actions, but the actions themselves are determined by coalition maintenance. Stalin's ideology did not prevent him from signing a pact with Hitler. Nixon's conservatism did not prevent him from creating the EPA. Watch what leaders do, not what they say.

### 3. The "Foreign Aid Works" Myth
**Error**: "We give foreign aid to help poor countries develop."
**Correction**: Foreign aid that goes to the central government strengthens the dictator's coalition. It makes the country less democratic and the population worse off. If you want to help, bypass the government.

### 4. The "Democracy = Elections" Myth
**Error**: "If a country holds elections, it's a democracy."
**Correction**: Elections without a large selectorate are not democracy. In Russia, Putin wins elections because the winning coalition is small (siloviki, oligarchs). The people vote, but their votes don't determine the outcome because the coalition is already decided.

### 5. The "War Is Irrational" Myth
**Error**: "Wars are caused by irrational leaders or nationalist fervor."
**Correction**: Wars are rational for leaders in small-coalition systems when they face domestic instability. A leader who is about to be deposed may start a war to rally the selectorate and demonstrate competence. The war is not about the enemy — it's about the leader's survival.

> **Case: Stalin's Terror** (Chapter 7)
> Stalin's purges (1936-1938) killed hundreds of thousands of Communist Party members, military officers, and government officials. From a character perspective, this was insane. From a selectorate perspective, it was rational. Stalin had a small winning coalition. He feared that any member of that coalition could replace him. By periodically purging the coalition — executing some members and promoting new ones — he kept everyone uncertain of their position and intensely loyal. The terror was not a sign of madness — it was a strategy of political survival.
> **Key takeaway**: Actions that seem irrational from the outside (purges, wars, corruption) are often perfectly rational from the perspective of political survival. The leader's rationality is not the same as the public's rationality.

> **Case: The North Korean Famine** (Chapter 5)
> In the 1990s, North Korea suffered a famine that killed perhaps 1 million people. The regime had food in storage. But it chose not to distribute it widely. Why? Because the leader (Kim Jong-il) needed to keep his winning coalition (the military and party elite) fed and loyal. The population — outside the coalition — was expendable. From a humanitarian perspective, this was monstrous. From a selectorate perspective, it was rational. The leader who feeds his coalition survives; the leader who feeds his population and starves his coalition is deposed.
> **Key takeaway**: Leaders will sacrifice the population to save the coalition. This is not because leaders are evil (though they often are) but because the institutional structure incentivizes this behavior. The only way to prevent such outcomes is to change the structure — to make the winning coalition large enough that keeping the population alive is in the leader's interest.
