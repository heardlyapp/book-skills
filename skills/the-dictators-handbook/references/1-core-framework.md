# The Selectorate Theory — How Leaders Really Stay in Power

> Source: The Dictator's Handbook, Chapters 1-2

## Core Framework

The selectorate theory is a game-theoretic model of political survival. It explains why leaders behave the way they do — not based on their character or ideology but on the mathematical structure of the political system they operate in.

### The Three Key Variables

| Variable | Symbol | Definition | Example |
|----------|--------|------------|---------|
| The Selectorate | S | Everyone who has a say in choosing the leader | Voters (democracy); party members, generals (dictatorship) |
| The Winning Coalition | W | The minimum number of supporters the leader needs to stay in power | 50%+1 (democracy); a few key patrons (dictatorship) |
| The Loyalty Norm | W/S | The ratio of coalition size to selectorate size | Higher ratio = less loyal; lower ratio = more loyal |

### Small Coalition vs. Large Coalition Systems

| Aspect | Small Coalition (Autocracy) | Large Coalition (Democracy) |
|--------|---------------------------|---------------------------|
| Size of W | A few dozen to a few thousand | 50%+ of the electorate |
| How leader keeps power | Private rewards to coalition members | Public goods to the population |
| Leader's spending | On patronage, bribes, military loyalty | On roads, schools, healthcare, security |
| Corruption level | Extremely high | Low to moderate |
| Tax burden | Low | High |
| Leader's tenure | Long (hard to dislodge) | Short (easy to replace) |
| Policy quality | Poor | Good |

> **Case: Why Dictators Provide Poor Public Services** (Chapter 1)
> A dictator doesn't need to provide good schools, roads, or healthcare because his winning coalition is small. He needs to keep a few generals, party officials, and wealthy families happy. He gives them money, privileges, and power. The rest of the population gets nothing — and it doesn't matter because they can't remove him. This is not a failure of governance — it's the system working exactly as the incentives dictate.
> **Key takeaway**: When you wonder why a dictator doesn't build schools, the answer is not that he's evil or incompetent. The answer is that his coalition doesn't need schools. His coalition needs cash. And that's what he gives them.

> **Case: Why Democracies Do Provide Public Goods** (Chapter 1)
> In a democracy, the winning coalition is huge — 50%+ of voters. The leader cannot possibly bribe all of them individually. Instead, the leader provides things that benefit everyone: clean water, public education, infrastructure, security, rule of law. These are "public goods" — they benefit the whole population, and the population rewards the leader with continued support. The leader provides public goods not because he is virtuous but because his political survival depends on satisfying a large coalition.
> **Key takeaway**: Democracy produces better outcomes not because democratic leaders are better people but because the institutional incentives force them to serve the many rather than the few. Virtue is not the cause of good government — coalition size is.
