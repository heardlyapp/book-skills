# Core Framework — Rising Strong

## The Rising Strong Process

The book maps what happens after we fall — whether the fall is a major failure (getting fired, a divorce) or a moment of disappointment (a rejection, an argument). The key insight: "We much prefer stories about falling and rising to be inspirational and sanitized. This gold-plating grit is dangerous." The real process involves three stages.

## The Reckoning (Chapter 4)

"Recognizing when you're hooked by emotion and getting curious." The first step is simply noticing that you're emotionally activated — your heart is racing, your jaw is tight, you're replaying a conversation. "The reckoning is the moment you become aware of what you're feeling and start asking 'Why?'" Case: Brené describes being in a meeting where a comment hit her "like a punch in the gut" — her internal reaction (defensiveness, story-making) without external response.

## The Rumble (Chapters 5-6)

"Getting honest with yourself about the story you're making up." Humans are story-making machines — when something happens, we immediately create a narrative. The rumble is separating fact from fiction. "The story I'm making up is..." becomes a key phrase. Case: The "Sewer Rats and Scofflaws" chapter — Brené recounts a conflict with a colleague where she made up a story about being disrespected. Rumbling revealed the real issue was her own fear of not being enough.

## The Revolution (Chapters 7-11)

"Turning the learning into action." After reckoning with the emotion and rumbling with the story, the revolution is writing a new ending. This means living differently, making different choices, and integrating the learning. Case: "Composting Failure" (Chapter 9) — "Failure isn't something to be avoided or sanitized. It's raw material. You have to let it decompose and turn into something fertile."

## Key Supporting Concepts

**Gold-Plating Grit.** The cultural tendency to sanitize failure stories. "To strip failure of its real emotional consequences is to scrub the concepts of grit and resilience of the very qualities that make them both so important — toughness, doggedness, and perseverance." Case: Ashley Good's Fail Forward work with Engineers Without Borders — 14 stories of failure in a glossy report.

**The Badassery Deficit.** Real courage is not swagger or bluster. "To me the real badass is the person who says, 'Our family is really hurting. We could use your support.'" Case: The man who tells his son "It's okay to be sad. We all get sad."

**Consolable vs. Inconsolable.** A key distinction. Consolable moments: we can be comforted. Inconsolable moments: we can't be reached. The rising strong process works for the consolable — for the inconsolable, professional help is needed.
