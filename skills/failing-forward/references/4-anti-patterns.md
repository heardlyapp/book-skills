# Anti-Patterns

## 1. Making Failure Identity

**The mistake**: "I failed" becomes "I am a failure."

**Fix**: Separate action from identity. The business failed. The relationship ended. The project was late. None of these define you.

> **Case: The Entrepreneur's Identity Trap** (Chapter 3): After his business failed, a founder told himself "I'm not cut out for business." He stopped trying. Years later, he realized his failure was about the specific business model, not his capability. By making failure identity, he prevented himself from learning the real lesson.
> **Key takeaway**: "I failed at X" is accurate. "I am a failure" is a story you tell yourself. Reframe: "My approach didn't work. I can try a different approach."

## 2. The Blame Reflex

**The mistake**: "It was the market / the economy / my team / my partner."

**Why it fails**: Even if external factors contributed, blaming them gives away your power to learn and improve.

**Fix**: Focus on your contribution — even if it's only 5%. That 5% is where your growth lives.

## 3. Waiting for the "Right" Failure

**The mistake**: Believing some failures are "acceptable" and others are "fatal."

**Fix**: Every failure has a lesson. The "worst" failures often contain the most valuable lessons. Don't create a hierarchy of failures — create a practice of learning from all of them.

> **Case: The Public Failure** (Chapter 6): A CEO made a strategic error that cost the company millions. He could have hidden or resigned. Instead, he presented the failure to his board with his analysis of what went wrong. The board respected his honesty. The company learned. He kept his job.
> **Key takeaway**: How you handle failure matters more than the failure itself. Transparency in failure builds trust.

## 4. Stopping at One Failure

**The mistake**: One failure is taken as evidence that the path is wrong.

**Fix**: Fail 10 times. Then fail 10 more. Each one is a data point. The only true failure is the one that makes you stop.
