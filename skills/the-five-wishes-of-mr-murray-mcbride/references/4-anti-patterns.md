# 4. Anti-Patterns — Critiques of the Novel

> Source: "The Five Wishes of Mr. Murray McBride" by Joe Siple (2018). Critiques of the book.

## Critique 1: The Emotional Manipulation

**What it looks like**: The novel is designed to make the reader cry. Some may find it emotionally manipulative.

**Why it works**: The novel earns its emotion through character development and careful pacing. The tears are not cheap — they are the result of investment in characters the reader has come to love.

> **Case: Murray's Death** (Final chapters): Murray dies peacefully after fulfilling his wishes. The death is not sudden or tragic. It is quiet and earned. The reader cries not because of shock but because of love.
> **Key takeaway**: Earned emotion is not manipulation. The difference is whether the reader feels cheated or grateful for the experience.

## Critique 2: The Sentimentality

**What it looks like**: The story is too sweet, too perfect, too neatly resolved.

**Why it matters**: Some readers prefer grittier, more ambiguous fiction. The Five Wishes is unapologetically sentimental. It is a book that wants to make you feel good.

## Critique 3: The Predictability

**What it looks like**: The five wishes structure makes the story predictable.

**Why it works**: Predictability is not a flaw in comfort fiction. The reader knows there will be a happy-sad ending. The pleasure is in the journey, not the destination.

> **Case: The Perfect Ending** (Epilogue): The novel ends with Jason as Prospero, performing his greatest trick, the two empty seats finally explained. The ending is satisfying and complete — but also neat. Everything ties up perfectly.
> **Key takeaway**: A perfect ending is a choice, not a flaw. Some stories aim for emotional fulfillment, not aesthetic ambiguity.
