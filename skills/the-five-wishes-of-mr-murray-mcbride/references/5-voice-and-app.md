# 5. Voice, Application, and Key Quotes

> Source: "The Five Wishes of Mr. Murray McBride" by Joe Siple (2018). Voice and application.

## Joe Siple's Voice

Siple writes with warmth and simplicity. His prose is accessible, direct, and emotionally transparent. He does not hide his intentions — he wants to move you.

## 5 Application Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Making Your Own Wishes
**The situation:** Someone wants to identify what matters most to them.
**The book's approach:** Make a list of five wishes. Not material things — experiences, relationships, acts of meaning. Ask: what would I want if I had limited time?

### Scenario 2: Connecting with an Older Person
**The situation:** Someone wants to connect with an elderly relative.
**The book's approach:** Sit on a bench. Listen. Be present. The most important gift is attention.

### Scenario 3: Facing Regret
**The situation:** Someone has a broken relationship they want to heal.
**The book's approach:** Reach out. It is never too late. Forgiveness is for you.

### Scenario 4: Finding Purpose at Any Age
**The situation:** Someone feels too old to start something new.
**The book's approach:** Murray starts his wish journey at 100. You are never too old to begin.

### Scenario 5: Appreciating Simple Pleasures
**The situation:** Someone is chasing big goals and forgetting small joys.
**The book's approach:** The ocean is the same ocean it always was. Simple pleasures do not diminish with age.

## Key Quotes

> "It is never too late to make a wish."

> "A life is not measured by its length but by its depth."

> "The most important things cannot be wished for alone."

> "Time is not the enemy. Regret is."

> **Case: The Two Empty Seats** (Prologue and Epilogue): The novel opens with two empty seats at Prospero's show. The reader spends the entire book wondering who they are for. The reveal — they are for Murray and the person Murray wished would be there — is the emotional payoff of the entire story.
> **Key takeaway**: A mystery planted in the opening and resolved in the closing creates narrative satisfaction.

> **Case: The Magician's Name** (Epilogue): Jason's stage name is Prospero, the wizard from Shakespeare's The Tempest. The reference enriches the story: like Prospero, Jason learned to create storms and calm them. The name is a tribute to the man who taught him.
> **Key takeaway**: Literary references in fiction should serve the story, not show off the author's reading.
