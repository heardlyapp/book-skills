# Anti-Patterns — What Blocks Love

> Source: The 5 Love Languages, Chapters 1, 2, 10, 11

## The Five Relationship Traps

### 1. The Assumption Error
**Pattern**: "I love my partner, so they should feel loved."
**Why it fails**: You're loving in your language, not theirs. The feeling of being loved depends on receiving love in a language you can understand. Giving love in your own language and expecting your partner to feel it is like speaking English to someone who only speaks Mandarin and expecting them to understand.

### 2. The Scorekeeping Trap
**Pattern**: "I did X for them, so they should do Y for me."
**Why it fails**: This turns love into a transactional exchange. Real love serves without keeping count. When you give to create debt, you're not loving — you're trading. Chapman's advice: give freely, and ask for what you need directly rather than trying to create reciprocity.

### 3. The "In Love" Addiction
**Pattern**: "I don't love them anymore because I don't feel 'in love.'"
**Why it fails**: The euphoric "in love" state always ends. People who chase this feeling from partner to partner are addicted to a temporary state. Real love is the choice to love after the euphoria fades. If you leave every relationship when the excitement fades, you never experience real love.

### 4. The Blame Loop
**Pattern**: "If only they would change, I would be happy."
**Why it fails**: Waiting for your partner to change before you fill their love tank is a recipe for stalemate. Someone has to start. Chapman's challenge: fill your partner's tank for one month without asking for anything in return. See what happens. Often, the partner's behavior changes in response.

### 5. The Self-Sufficiency Lie
**Pattern**: "I don't need anyone. I can take care of myself."
**Why it fails**: Humans are wired for connection. The love tank is not a luxury — it's a need. Denying the need for love doesn't eliminate it; it just leads to an empty tank and unrecognized depression or resentment.

> **Case: The Couple Who Almost Divorced Over Dishes** (Chapter 7)
> A couple came to Chapman on the verge of divorce. The wife's complaint: her husband never helped with housework. She felt unloved and disrespected. The husband's defense: he worked 60-hour weeks to provide for the family. He didn't see why he should have to do housework too. The issue wasn't the dishes — it was that dishes were her love language. When the husband understood that loading the dishwasher was as meaningful to her as saying "I love you" was to him, he started doing the dishes. The marriage was saved.
> **Key takeaway**: The thing you're fighting about is probably not the thing you're fighting about. Surface conflicts (dishes, money, time) are often love language conflicts. Look at the complaint through the love language lens, and the real issue becomes clear.

> **Case: The Wife Who Needed Touch, Not Talk** (Chapter 8, Physical Touch)
> A wife complained that her husband never said "I love you." The husband said "I love you" every day — but he said it while walking out the door, in a flat tone, without eye contact. The wife's love language was Physical Touch; she needed him to hold her when he said it. He started saying "I love you" while holding her, and she felt loved for the first time in years. The words hadn't changed — the context had.
> **Key takeaway**: The same words can land differently depending on the context and the physical delivery. For someone whose primary language is Physical Touch, an "I love you" said from across the room is not the same as one said while holding them.
