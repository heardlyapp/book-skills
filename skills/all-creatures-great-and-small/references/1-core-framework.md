## The Three Pillars of Practice

**Pillar 1: Technical Skill.** Herriot was a skilled veterinarian — but he learned by doing, not by reading. The gap between what the textbooks described and what he actually faced was enormous.

**Case: The First Calving.** Herriot's first solo calving was nothing like the textbook illustration. The cow lay on a cobbled floor in a frozen barn, not a gleaming operating theater. Herriot worked for two hours, exhausted and panicked. But he succeeded. The skill was not from the books — it was from the struggle.

**Case: The Difficult Birth.** When the calf was in the wrong position, Herriot had to reach into the cow's uterus, loop a rope around the calf's jaw, and pull. The pressure was agonizing. The cow's contractions squeezed his arm. He nearly gave up. But he persisted.

**Pillar 2: People Skills.** The farmers Herriot served were suspicious of strangers, tight with money, and deeply knowledgeable about their animals.

**Case: The Taciturn Farmer.** A farmer would watch silently as Herriot examined his cow. He would not offer information or opinion. Herriot learned to work in silence, to respect the farmer's knowledge, and to earn trust gradually.

**Case: The Farmer Who Would Not Pay.** Some farmers refused to pay until they saw results. Herriot learned to accept this. He treated the animal first, trusted the farmer to pay if the treatment worked, and accepted that sometimes he would not be paid.

**Pillar 3: Emotional Resilience.** Herriot could not save every animal. He had to learn to accept loss without being defeated.

**Case: The Euthanasia.** Herriot describes putting down animals that could not be saved. Each time hurt. He learned to do it humanely, with compassion, and to move on to the next case.

**Case: The Loss of a Favorite.** When a regular patient died, Herriot felt the loss. But he did not let it stop him from treating the next animal that needed him.
