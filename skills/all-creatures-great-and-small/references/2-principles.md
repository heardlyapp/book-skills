# Principles from the Dales

## I. Show Up, No Matter What

Herriot's most defining quality: he showed up. At 2 AM, in a blizzard, on Christmas morning, after no sleep — he went where he was needed.

**Case: The Night Call.** Farmers would call at all hours. Herriot would drag himself from bed, drive miles on icy roads, and treat the animal by lantern light. He never complained.

**Case: The Desperate Farmer.** When a farmer called for help, he was often desperate. His animal was suffering. His livelihood was at stake. Herriot understood this and responded accordingly.

## II. Never Stop Learning

Herriot graduated from veterinary school, but his real education began when he started practicing.

**Case: The Old Farmer's Wisdom.** An elderly farmer would know more about calving than a young veterinarian. Herriot learned to listen, to observe, and to defer to experience.

**Case: The Case You Have Never Seen.** Every year brought new cases, new challenges, new diseases. Herriot never stopped learning because the work never stopped changing.

## III. Treat the Person, Not Just the Animal

The animal was the patient. But the farmer was the client. Herriot had to treat both.

**Case: The Grieving Farmer.** When a beloved animal died, the farmer grieved. Herriot learned to give space for that grief, to offer comfort without pretending.

**Case: The Embarrassed Dog Owner.** When a dog bit someone, the owner was embarrassed. Herriot learned to defuse the situation with humor, to protect the animal from the owner's anger.

## IV. Humor Is a Survival Tool

Herriot's practice was full of absurd situations. He learned to laugh.

**Case: Tristan's Disasters.** Tristan Farnon was a constant source of comic relief. His schemes to avoid work, to impress women, to escape Siegfried's anger — they kept the practice from becoming too serious.

**Case: The Mad Farmer.** A farmer who talked to his animals as if they were people. Another farmer who kept a pig in the house. Herriot met them all and found humor in each encounter.
