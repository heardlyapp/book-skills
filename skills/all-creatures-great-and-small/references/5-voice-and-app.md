James Herriot's voice is one of the most beloved in English literature — warm, self-deprecating, and deeply humane.

## Herriot's Voice

Herriot writes as if he is telling stories to a friend. His sentences are simple, his humor is gentle, and his compassion is evident on every page.

**Case: The Opening Line.** "They didn't say anything about this in the books, I thought, as the snow blew in through the gaping doorway and settled on my naked back." The reader is immediately with him — in the cold, in the struggle, in the joke.

**Case: The Yorkshire Accent.** Herriot's dialogue captures the Yorkshire dialect perfectly. His characters speak in a distinctive voice that is part of their character. A farmer who says "Nay, lad" or "Tha'll manage" is immediately real.

## The Legacy

All Creatures Great and Small has sold over 60 million copies. It has been adapted into two films and a television series that ran for 90 episodes. In 2020, a new television adaptation introduced Herriot to a new generation.

**Case: The Universal Appeal.** Herriot's stories cross cultural boundaries. A Japanese reader can appreciate the beauty of the Yorkshire Dales and the humor of Tristan Farnon as much as a British reader.

**Case: The Lasting Impact.** Herriot's books have inspired generations of veterinarians. Many entered the profession because of his stories. The book's message — that a life of service to animals and people is a good life — continues to resonate.
