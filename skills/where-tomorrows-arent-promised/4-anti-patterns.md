# Anti-Patterns: What Not to Do (And Why)

> Source: Where Tomorrows Aren't Promised, Chapters 1-30

## Anti-Pattern 1: Romanticizing the Streets

**The Trap:** The streets are presented as glamorous — dope boys with new sneakers, cars, and respect. Kids from the projects see money, clothes, and status and want in.

**Carmelo's Reality:** He watched the dope boys from his window. They had money, but they also had shootouts, prison, and early death. "The sound of discharging firearms had become as routine as hail in January." The principal of his elementary school was shot and killed walking through the projects looking for a missing student. A nine-year-old girl from New York was murdered in Murphy Homes and left in a trash bin.

**The pattern to avoid:** Do not confuse the appearance of success (money, clothes, cars) with actual success (freedom, longevity, peace of mind).

> **Case: The New KJs (Chapter 12)**
> Carmelo wanted nice clothes to "feel good." He says: "If you ever hear a person passing judgment on a dude from the projects dressed in expensive clothes from head to toe, know that maybe it's the only way he knows how to make himself feel good. In a neighborhood devoid of therapy, positive reinforcement, or opportunity, those new Nikes may have made his cold night a little warmer."
>
> **Key takeaway**: Understand the psychological need behind the behavior before judging it. But also: find healthier ways to meet that need.

## Anti-Pattern 2: The Victim Narrative

**The Trap:** Carmelo had every reason to play the victim card. Father dead at two. Racist institutions. Living in the "crack capital of America." Stepfather who resented him. Multiple murders of loved ones.

**What He Did Instead:** He acknowledged the pain but never used it as an excuse. "I never talked about struggling. Why should I? We all knew we were struggling. We all knew we were dealing with things."

**The pattern to avoid:** Using legitimate hardship as justification for inaction, resentment, or self-destructive behavior. Pain is real. What you do with it is a choice.

> **Case: The Question Marks on the Wall (Chapter 18)**
> Carmelo scribbled "Why me?" and endless question marks on his wall. He was depressed. But he also got up every day and went to the gym. "I relied on sports to distract me from everything that was around me. If I played ball and only played ball, so much that I didn't have time to focus on my world, then I would be okay."
>
> **Key takeaway**: Acknowledge your pain. But do not let it become your identity. Process it, use it, move through it.

## Anti-Pattern 3: Lying to Yourself About Danger

**The Trap:** Believing that "it won't happen to me" or that you are immune to the dangers of your environment.

**Carmelo's Reality:** "Bullets don't have a name." He saw Luck — his cousin, his mentor, the best basketball player he knew — shot 27 times and killed. He saw Wood — his guardian, the man who taught him to read the streets — shot in the head in a car.

**The pattern to avoid:** Denial is not strength. Acknowledging danger is not fear — it is survival. The people who survive dangerous environments are the ones who are most honest about the danger.

> **Case: Luck's Murder (Chapter 17)**
> Luck was coming back to Baltimore. He was turning his life around. He went to grab his things from New York and was shot 27 times by his baby's mother's boyfriend. "He was dead on the scene." Carmelo had been confident that Luck would be fine.
>
> **Key takeaway**: Danger does not care about your plans. It does not care about your talent or potential. Acknowledge it, respect it, and act accordingly.

## Anti-Pattern 4: Taking the Easy Way Out

**The Trap:** When things get hard, the easy path is tempting. Transfer to Dunbar. Stay in Baltimore. Skip the hard classes. Ignore the rules you don't like.

**Carmelo's Reframe:** "Looking for the easy way out had never worked for me. I had always been better at dealing with all my problems head-on."

**The pattern to avoid:** The easy path is almost never the growth path. It feels good in the short term and costs you in the long term.

> **Case: Dunbar vs. Towson Catholic (Chapter 20)**
> Carmelo could have transferred to Dunbar, where he would have been "king." He spent months dreaming about it and even started attending classes there. But he realized: "I couldn't just run away from those people like that, proving them right. Eventually, I realized that running was the easy way out."
>
> **Key takeaway**: Ask yourself: am I leaving because it is the right move, or because it is the easy move? If it is the easy move, stay and fight.

## Anti-Pattern 5: The Comparison Game

**The Trap:** Comparing yourself to others who seem to have it easier, are more favored, or are more naturally talented.

**Carmelo's Experience:** At Towson Catholic, he was constantly compared to Darnell Hopkins. Darnell was the ideal Black student — clean-cut, well-dressed, never questioned authority. Carmelo was measured against him constantly.

**The pattern to avoid:** Comparison either inflates your ego (when you come out ahead) or crushes your spirit (when you come out behind). Neither is useful. Focus on your own trajectory.

**What Carmelo did instead:** He used comparison as fuel, not as measurement. "If [Darnell] ran two sprints, I had to run four sprints. If he did one hundred push-ups, I was right behind him doing two hundred."

> **Case: The Darnell Dynamic (Chapter 20)**
> "Darnell was the barometer I was measured against. The definition of how young Black men should act." But rather than resenting Darnell, Carmelo used the dynamic to push himself. They became friends and pushed each other. "We constantly used our competitive edge to push each other forward."
>
> **Key takeaway**: Comparison is only useful when it drives you to be better, not when it makes you feel worse. Use the gap as fuel, not as shame.

## Anti-Pattern 6: Trusting Empty Promises

**The Trap:** Believing people's words without verifying their track record or incentives.

**Carmelo's Lesson:** Larry Brown, coach of the Detroit Pistons, looked Carmelo in the eye and said, "We are taking you with the number two pick, you have my word. You are the best, it's a no-brainer. I can't wait to work with you!" Detroit sent Carmelo merchandise: jerseys, shirts, a velour tracksuit. His family started planning where to live.

Then Detroit drafted Darko Miličić.

**The pattern to avoid:** Words are cheap. People change their minds. Incentives shift. Trust actions, not promises. Have a backup plan.

> **Case: The Draft Betrayal (Chapter 30)**
> "I'm big on loyalty. It matters to me. Above all, a man should always be loyal." But Larry Brown was not loyal. He made a promise and broke it. Carmelo was devastated. "I was literally hurt at that moment... Could he just lie straight to you like that?"
>
> **Key takeaway**: Your loyalty is a reflection of your character, not others'. Keep your word. But do not expect others to keep theirs. Protect yourself accordingly.

## Anti-Pattern 7: The Isolation Spiral

**The Trap:** When you are depressed, grieving, or overwhelmed, the instinct is to withdraw. Carmelo's instinct was to lie in bed, stare at the ceiling, and throw a ball against the wall.

**What Saved Him:** He forced himself to go to the gym. He found community — Robert C, Pioneer Homes, his teammates. He made sure he was never alone for too long. "I used to go to sleep hopeful, but I woke up every morning, like, Damn, why do I have to go through this?... And still, as always, I relied on sports to distract me."

**The pattern to avoid:** Isolation amplifies pain. Community dilutes it. When you want to be alone most is when you most need to be around people.

> **Case: The Empty Bedroom (Chapter 18)**
> After Deek and Luck died within a short period, Carmelo was consumed by depression. He stayed in bed. He drew on the walls. He wrote DMX lyrics. He did not talk to anyone about it. "Vulnerability was a big no-no in my community... Healing wasn't an option." His salvation was the structure of sports — he had to show up to practice.
>
> **Key takeaway**: When you cannot process through conversation (and many people cannot), let structure and routine carry you. Show up. Go through the motions. Movement is medicine.

## Anti-Pattern 8: Mistaking the Streets for Family

**The Trap:** The streets offer belonging — especially to young people who lack positive attention at home. The dope boys, the older hustlers — they welcome you, they share their money, they make you feel important.

**Carmelo's Observation:** The streets take more than they give. He watched "Baltimore basketball players who went from being top recruits to corner boys slinging nickel bags of smack." He watched Luck — "the best basketball player in the neighborhood" — become consumed by alcoholism and violence, then get murdered.

**The pattern to avoid:** The streets offer counterfeit belonging. They make you feel loved while leading you toward destruction. Real family — blood or chosen — holds you accountable and wants you to grow.

> **Case: The Baltimore Pipeline (Chapter 27)**
> "Bay spent a lot of time telling me how fans and haters tore Baltimore athletes apart... Or they ended up working on trash trucks or hanging around the Rec, talking about how good they were back in the day. A lot of times, their downfalls weren't even their own fault. They just had bad advice from people who were trying to use them."
>
> **Key takeaway**: Not everyone who claims to love you wants the best for you. Evaluate people by the outcomes they produce in your life, not how they make you feel in the moment.

## Anti-Pattern 9: Suppressing All Vulnerability

**The Trap:** "Men don't cry." Carmelo was taught this by Luck, by Wood, by the streets. Vulnerability was weakness. Showing emotion was dangerous.

**The Cost:** Carmelo admits: "I was depressed at a time when depression wasn't a topic, at least not in my community. I can't lie, I wanted to go speak to somebody. There were times when I wanted to tell somebody what I was feeling, but I didn't know how."

**The pattern to avoid:** Complete suppression of vulnerability is not strength — it is self-harm. There is a middle ground between being emotionally open with everyone and being closed to everyone.

> **Case: The Hidden Depression (Chapter 18)**
> "My insides were broken into pieces. On the outside, it looked like I was on top of the world." Carmelo kept the smile on. "I didn't feel I really had much of a choice. People were always used to me smiling, and they probably couldn't imagine that I was going through hell."
>
> **Key takeaway**: Find at least one person or one outlet where you can be real about how you feel. The alternative is carrying a weight that will eventually break you.

## Anti-Pattern 10: Believing the System Will Save You

**The Trap:** Schools, police, government programs — these institutions are supposed to help. Carmelo learned early that they are often designed to maintain the status quo.

**His Education:** Black veterans were denied G.I. Bill benefits. The principal of his school was killed while looking for a missing student. Police couldn't even enter Murphy Homes without an armored car. The judge who found Kevin Teague's attackers not guilty was "the system in a nutshell if you were Black and from Red Hook."

**The pattern to avoid:** Institutions cannot be relied upon to fix problems they helped create. You must build your own support systems and pathways.

> **Case: The Bad Judge (Chapter 2)**
> Kevin Teague, a Black postal worker, was beaten nearly to death by four white men. Despite needing 13 stitches and suffering severe eye damage, the judge found all four attackers not guilty. "That's the system in a nutshell if you were Black and from Red Hook."
>
> **Key takeaway**: Understand how systems actually work (not how they claim to work) and plan accordingly. Build alternative paths and rely on your own network.
