# Techniques: Practical Tools from the Streets and the Court

> Source: Where Tomorrows Aren't Promised, Chapters 1-30

## Technique 1: Mental Rehearsal — Rehearsing the Future

Wood taught Carmelo to mentally rehearse every situation before it happened. "Mentally rehearsing the present and the future — always, always on point."

**How to do it:**
- Before entering any situation, visualize the possible scenarios
- Ask: Who will be there? What is their role? What baseline behavior is normal?
- Ask: What could go wrong? What would I do if X happens?
- Mentally walk through your response to each scenario

Carmelo applied this to basketball (anticipating defensive schemes) and to the streets (knowing when danger was coming before it arrived).

> **Case: The Block Surveillance (Chapter 16)**
> Wood would sit in his car parked in front of Carmelo's house, watching the block. He taught Carmelo to notice everything: "knowing my surroundings, knowing the cars that come through, knowing what's happening during the day as well as the night, knowing who doesn't belong around here."
>
> **Key takeaway**: Mental rehearsal turns uncertainty into preparedness. You cannot predict everything, but you can be ready for anything.

## Technique 2: The Art of Fighting Back — Strategic Aggression

Carmelo's mother taught him that passivity invites predation. When Avon stole his keys in Baltimore, she made him fight — not win, but fight. "If you are not going fight every single day, boy, you going to cry every single day."

**How to use it:**
- When something is taken from you (opportunity, respect, resources), do not passively accept it
- Fighting back does not mean physical violence — it means active resistance
- The goal is often not to win immediately but to establish that you are not prey
- People remember effort more than outcome: "In the end, people would remember my effort more than my size"

> **Case: The Gold Chain (Chapter 3)**
> Carmelo's best friend Tyree stole the gold chain from Carmelo's dead father. "I never caught him wearing it after that, but I knew he had it and was hiding it from me. Even knowing how important it was to me, my best friend stole my most prized possession." Carmelo vowed: "I took that loss hard, and I did not like how it felt. The feeling of defeat doesn't sit well with me. That's the reason I fought so hard for my keychain."
>
> **Key takeaway**: Losses are data. They teach you what you cannot tolerate. Use them to sharpen your resolve, not to fuel despair.
>
> **Key takeaway**: Betrayal from someone close is a different category of pain. Acknowledge it, learn from it, and adjust your trust accordingly.

## Technique 3: Finding Your People — The Outsider's Strategy

Every time Carmelo entered a new environment (Baltimore, Towson Catholic, Oak Hill, Syracuse), he was an outsider. He developed a repeatable strategy for building community:

1. **Find the familiar.** At Syracuse, he connected with Billy Edelin (also from Oak Hill) and through him found Pioneer Homes — a housing project that felt like home.
2. **Serve.** At Robert C, he was the ball boy for Luck's St. Frances games. At Bay's events, he worked the concession stand. Service creates connection.
3. **Be curious about others.** He asked questions, listened, and learned. He did not try to impress.
4. **Bring people together.** At Syracuse, Carmelo made a point of connecting with everyone — "gangster, youth pastor, or the best hooper on campus." He was known for bringing nerds to the best parties.

> **Case: Pioneer Homes at Syracuse (Chapter 28)**
> Carmelo felt lost on Syracuse's big campus. He found his way to Pioneer Homes, the projects next to campus, through Billy Edelin's friends. "I started becoming comfortable down at Pioneer. I would go after class, before practice, walk around, go mess with the people in the projects, and catch game from the older cats. Over time, Pioneer became my second home. After a while, I stopped hanging on campus altogether."
>
> **Key takeaway**: When you are an outsider, find the sub-community that matches your background. Build from familiarity outward.

## Technique 4: Reading the Room — The Observer's Edge

Carmelo's hyper-awareness made him an expert reader of people and situations. He could tell from "their vibe, their energy, and the looks they gave" whether drivers wanted their windows cleaned when he was squeegeeing.

**How to develop this skill:**
- Watch before you act. In any new environment, spend the first period observing
- Notice who has influence, even if they are not in formal power
- Pay attention to what people do, not what they say
- Trust your gut feeling about people — "that sickly feeling" that precedes bad news

> **Case: The Squeegee Hustle (Chapter 12)**
> "We started learning what people wanted. You could tell who wanted their windows washed without them saying anything — by their vibe, their energy, and the looks they gave. Some people wanted to help us out, and others seemed like they wanted to run us over."
>
> **Key takeaway**: Reading people is a skill you can practice in everyday interactions. The more you observe, the better you predict.

## Technique 5: Compartmentalization — The Smile That Carries Weight

Carmelo became a master of presenting one version of himself while experiencing something entirely different internally. "I was a one-trick illusionist, seeming to be perfectly okay while hiding all my hurt and fear."

**How to use it (without becoming emotionally disconnected):**
- Choose when and where to process pain. The basketball court, the practice gym, the training session — these are for performance
- The locker room, the car, late night alone — these are for processing
- Carmelo's method: music (DMX's albums were his therapy), writing rap lyrics on the wall, drawing cartoon characters surrounded by question marks
- Do not let the compartment become a prison. Carmelo's smile was real — he chose joy — but he also acknowledged his depression privately

> **Case: The Question Marks on the Wall (Chapter 18)**
> After Deek and Luck died, Carmelo couldn't process his pain through conversation. "Nobody never questioned me about what I wrote. I used to get in trouble for writing on the wall, but nobody ever really asked about what I was writing on the wall." He scribbled DMX lyrics, questions like "Why me?", and endless question marks.
>
> **Key takeaway**: Find a private outlet for the weight you cannot share. Writing, drawing, music — the medium matters less than having one.

## Technique 6: Gratitude as Armor — Choosing Joy Deliberately

"I chose to be grateful, happy, and content with my surroundings — and to constantly bring joy to whoever was in my presence."

This is not toxic positivity. It is a conscious countermeasure to an environment of loss. Carmelo had lost so much that the default state was depression. Choosing joy was an active, daily decision.

**How to practice it:**
- Focus on what you have, not what you have lost
- Look for moments that make you smile, even small ones
- Share joy with others — it multiplies when you give it away
- Recognize that happiness is not a permanent state; it is a choice you make repeatedly

> **Case: The Green Hornet (Chapter 29)**
> Carmelo's car was a beat-up '98 Chrysler Concord with bald tires and a makeshift CD player. He loved it. "Bumping music that way was awkward at first, but you learned to love it, or at least I did." When he drove it through a blizzard back to Syracuse and got stranded, he didn't complain about the car — he was grateful for the freedom it gave him.
>
> **Key takeaway**: Gratitude for small things builds resilience against big disappointments. Your first reliable car, your first paycheck — celebrate them.

## Technique 7: Processing Trauma Through Craft — The Practice as Therapy

Carmelo did not have access to therapy. Basketball became his processing mechanism. When he was depressed, he went to the gym. When he was angry, he went to the gym. When he didn't understand the world, he went to the gym.

The key insight: the craft itself becomes the container for emotional processing. You do not need to talk about your feelings if you can express them through your work.

**How to apply this:**
- Identify a craft (writing, coding, music, sport, art) that absorbs your full attention
- When emotions are overwhelming, go to the craft
- Do not force the processing — let it happen naturally through the rhythm of practice
- The craft provides structure when your internal world feels chaotic

> **Case: The Bleak Bedroom (Chapter 18)**
> After Deek's death, Carmelo lay in bed, throwing a ball at the wall, thinking about how Deek would have yelled at him for it — but Deek was dead. His response: "I'd crank my music... That was my medicine." Then he'd go to the gym. Basketball filled the space that grief had opened.
>
> **Key takeaway**: When words fail, action speaks. Let your craft carry what you cannot articulate.

## Technique 8: Mentorship Triangulation — Learning from Multiple Sources

Carmelo did not have one mentor. He had a network: his mother for values, Michelle for street wisdom, Luck for internal toughness, Wood for situational awareness, Bay for career navigation, Corbett for basketball fundamentals, Wise for discipline and opportunity.

Each mentor filled a specific gap. No single person provided everything.

**How to build your own mentorship network:**
- Identify areas where you need growth (values, skills, confidence, strategy)
- Find people who excel in one area, not necessarily all areas
- Learn from them specifically about their strength
- Rotate: as you grow, your mentorship needs change

> **Case: The Coaches Pipeline (Chapters 8, 19)**
> Mr. Wise at Robert C saw Carmelo's talent and pushed him to play against older kids. "Playing with other kids is too easy for you, Carmelo! Time to step it up!" Then Wise passed him to Corbett at Mount Royal: "Carmelo, you got some real talent, and I can't do nothing else with you. You're going with Corbett. I need you over here with him, so that you can reach all of your potential."
>
> **Key takeaway**: Great mentors know when to pass you to the next level. They do not hoard your growth.
>
> **Key takeaway**: A single mentor is rarely enough. Build a constellation.

## Technique 9: The Name as Identity — Owning Your Story

Carmelo tried to shed his name. At school, he called himself Tyrone Johnson. He was suspended. His mother found out and was furious. She told him: "Your father would say... You'd better cherish his name."

From that moment, Carmelo stopped running from his identity. Carmelo is not a common Black name, but he made it iconic. He made it stand for something.

**How to apply this:**
- Your background is not a liability — it is your differentiator
- The things that make you different (your name, your neighborhood, your accent) are your brand
- Do not try to fit in by erasing who you are
- Make your name mean something through your actions

> **Case: Tyrone Johnson vs. Carmelo Anthony (Chapter 9)**
> When his mother discovered he had been calling himself Tyrone at school, she marched him to the principal's office. "My son Tyrone? I don't have a son named Tyrone, and my name isn't Johnson. This boy's name is Carmelo!" He told her why he hated his name. Her response changed everything: "Nobody has it because you are special. If your father knew that you were saying that... You wouldn't be saying that. He was an amazing person. You'd better cherish his name."
>
> **Key takeaway**: Your name, your identity, your story — these are not for others to approve or reject. They are yours to own and build upon.

## Technique 10: The Hustle Mastery — Creating Opportunity from Nothing

Before basketball became his career, Carmelo learned to create income from whatever was available. Squeegeeing on MLK and Mulberry. Selling candy at Orioles and Ravens games. Working the concession stand at basketball tournaments.

Each hustle taught him something: reading people, managing money, understanding value exchange, and the importance of having "a floor plank" where he could store his earnings.

**The rules of squeegeeing (applicable to any hustle):**
1. Always have a partner (safety and accountability)
2. Always split the money evenly (fairness prevents conflict)
3. Tuck bills into your sock (protect your earnings)
4. Stay on your side (know your boundaries; do not encroach on others)

> **Case: The Floor Plank (Chapter 12)**
> "I had a loose floor plank in my room. I could lift it up and store all of my squeegee proceeds there, along with the allowance I would get sometimes. It was a pretty good hiding place. I never stacked up a fortune, but I definitely had something to hold on to."
>
> **Key takeaway**: The habit of saving — even small amounts — is more important than the amount. A hiding place, a savings account, a system. Build the habit early.
