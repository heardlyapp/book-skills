# Core Framework: The History and Nature of Money

## What Is Money?

"Money is not a thing — it's a technology for transferring value across time and space." Lyn Alden traces the evolution of money from primitive barter systems through commodity money (gold, silver), representative money (paper backed by gold), to fiat money (government currency backed by nothing).

> **Case: The Gold Standard Era** (Chapters 2-3): For centuries, major currencies were backed by gold. Prices remained stable over long periods. A dollar in 1913 was worth roughly what a dollar was worth in 1800. Since the US left the gold standard in 1971, the dollar has lost over 85% of its purchasing power.
> **Key takeaway**: Fiat money gives governments flexibility to respond to crises — but at the cost of long-term purchasing power. The tradeoff is real.

## The Three Properties of Good Money

| Property | What It Means | Example |
|----------|---------------|---------|
| Store of value | Maintains purchasing power over time | Gold (millennia), Bitcoin (digital scarcity) |
| Medium of exchange | Accepted for transactions | US dollar (widely accepted) |
| Unit of account | Used to price goods and services | Dollar, Euro (most convenient) |

> **Case: The 1971 Nixon Shock** (Chapter 3): When President Nixon ended dollar convertibility to gold, the world moved to a pure fiat system. This enabled more flexible monetary policy but also unleashed decades of inflation. The dollar lost value faster in the 50 years after 1971 than in the 200 years before.
> **Key takeaway**: The monetary system we have today is historically unusual. Most of human history had harder money. Understanding this helps you make better financial decisions.
