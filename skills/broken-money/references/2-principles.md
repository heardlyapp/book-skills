# Key Principles

## 1. All Fiat Currencies Eventually Lose Value

"Throughout history, every government-issued fiat currency has eventually depreciated against hard assets." The Roman denarius, the French assignat, the German mark, the US dollar — all have lost purchasing power over time.

## 2. Banks Create Money When They Lend

"Fractional reserve banking means that for every dollar deposited, banks can lend multiple dollars against it." This expands the money supply and drives economic growth — but also creates systemic fragility.

## 3. Inflation Is a Hidden Tax

"When the money supply expands faster than the economy's productive capacity, each unit of currency buys less." This is not an accident — it's a feature of the system that benefits debtors (including governments) at the expense of savers.

> **Case: The 2008 Financial Crisis** (Chapters 9-10): The housing bubble and subsequent crash illustrated how fractional reserve banking, when combined with excessive leverage, can trigger systemic collapse. Banks had lent far more than they held in reserves. When housing prices fell, the whole system teetered.
> **Key takeaway**: Banking crises are not anomalies — they are features of the fractional reserve system. Understanding the mechanism helps you prepare for the next one.

## 4. The Cantillon Effect Benefits the Connected

"New money enters the economy at discrete points — banks, government, financial institutions — and spreads outward." Those closest to the money creation benefit most, because they spend it before prices rise. Those furthest away (ordinary savers) bear the cost.

## 5. Sound Money Is Political

"Hard money limits government's ability to spend." This is why governments prefer fiat: it gives them flexibility. But that flexibility comes at the cost of your purchasing power.

> **Case: The Bitcoin Emergence** (Chapters 11-12): Bitcoin was created in response to the 2008 financial crisis. It offers a fixed supply schedule (only 21 million will ever exist) and operates without a central authority. Whether it succeeds as money depends on adoption, but its existence represents a technological challenge to the fiat system.
> **Key takeaway**: Bitcoin is not just an asset — it's a monetary experiment. Its fixed supply makes it the hardest money ever created. But its volatility and regulatory uncertainty make it risky.
