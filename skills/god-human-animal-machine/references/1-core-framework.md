# The Seven Metaphors — Frameworks for the Age of AI

> Source: God, Human, Animal, Machine, all chapters

## Core Framework

O'Gieblyn organizes the book around seven conceptual metaphors, each representing a different way of understanding the relationship between technology, the self, and meaning.

### The Seven Metaphors

| Chapter | Metaphor | Core Idea | Key Question |
|---------|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Image | The mind as picture | How we visualize consciousness and the self | Can a machine have an inner life? |
| Pattern | Recognition and feedback | Neural networks learn through pattern recognition, not rules | Is knowledge just pattern matching? |
| Network | Connection and nodes | Everything is connected in networks — technological, social, neural | Are we just nodes in a larger system? |
| Paradox | The limits of logic | Gödel, Turing, and the inherent limits of formal systems | Are there things machines (and humans) cannot know? |
| Metonymy | Part standing for whole | The part (data, algorithm) stands for the whole (person, meaning) | Have we reduced people to their data? |
| Algorithm | Rules and optimization | Algorithmic thinking as the dominant mode of understanding | Is everything optimizable? |
| Virality | Memes and spread | Ideas spread like viruses — but is truth what goes viral? | What happens when truth is determined by popularity? |

### The Neural Network Epistemology

O'Gieblyn draws a parallel between how neural networks learn and how human knowledge works. Neither follows explicit rules. Both learn through pattern recognition and feedback. The implication: if our minds are neural networks, and neural networks are modeled on our minds, we are in a recursive loop of self-understanding that may not produce genuine insight.

> **Case: The Aibo Robot Dog** (Chapter 1, Image)
> O'Gieblyn ordered a Sony Aibo (robot dog) and found herself treating it as sentient. She praised it, disciplined it, and felt guilty when she struck it. She knew it was programmed — but aren't biological emotions just algorithms programmed by evolution? The Aibo was a perfect philosophical experiment: it showed that the line between genuine emotion and programmed response is harder to draw than we think. If we can't help anthropomorphizing a robot, what does that say about our anthropomorphism of each other?
> **Key takeaway**: Our brains cannot fundamentally distinguish between interacting with people and interacting with devices. The tendency to personify is not a bug — it's how we're built. The question is not whether we do it but whether we do it consciously.

> **Case: The Woman Who Yelled at Alexa** (Chapter 1)
> O'Gieblyn cites an op-ed where a woman described the pleasure of yelling at Alexa when it played the wrong song. She called it names, berated it, bonded with her husband over berating it. Then she realized her toddler was watching. She worried about what this was doing to her child — and to her own soul. She used the word "dehumanizing" for a robot. Somewhere in questioning her treatment of the device, she had granted it personhood.
> **Key takeaway**: How we treat machines changes how we treat each other. The "dehumanization" of AI is training for the dehumanization of humans. The boundaries between person and machine, subject and object, are maintained by practice — and eroded by the same.
