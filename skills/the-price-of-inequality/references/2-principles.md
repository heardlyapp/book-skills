# Key Principles

## 1. Inequality Is a Political Choice

"Every major increase in inequality can be traced to specific policy decisions: tax cuts for the rich, deregulation, weakened unions, and a shrinking social safety net."

## 2. Rent Seeking, Not Value Creation

"Much of the wealth at the top comes not from creating value but from extracting it. Monopoly profits, high-frequency trading, and exploiting information advantages are forms of rent seeking."

> **Case: The Lobbying Machine** (Chapter 4): Wall Street spent billions on lobbying to deregulate finance. They got what they wanted: laws that allowed predatory lending, excessive risk-taking, and complex financial products. Then the 2008 crisis hit, and taxpayers bailed them out. Profits were private; losses were socialized.
> **Key takeaway**: Much inequality is created through political power. The wealthy use their resources to shape laws in their favor, then call the result "free markets."

## 3. The Middle Class Has Been Stagnating

"Since 1980, productivity has doubled. Median wages have barely budged. All the gains went to the top."

## 4. Inequality Undermines Growth

"High inequality actually slows economic growth. When most people have less money, consumer demand weakens, investment falls, and the entire economy suffers."

> **Case: The Education Gap** (Chapter 5): Children born into wealthy families get better schools, tutors, test prep, and college admissions. Children born into poverty get failing schools, no resources, and limited opportunities. The system reproduces itself. "Inequality of opportunity" means the American Dream is a myth for millions.
> **Key takeaway**: In an unequal society, your future is determined by your parents' wealth. The promise of "equal opportunity" is hollow when children face radically different starting points.
