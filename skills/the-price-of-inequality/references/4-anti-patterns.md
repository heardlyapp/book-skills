# Anti-Patterns

## 1. Believing Inequality Is Natural

**The mistake**: Thinking inequality is the inevitable result of differences in talent and effort.

**Fix**: Inequality varies enormously across time and countries. If it were natural, it would be constant. It's not — it's policy.

> **Case: The Two Koreas** (Chapter 2): North and South Korea share the same people, same culture, same history. One is dramatically unequal and poor. The other is relatively equal and prosperous. The difference is not human nature — it's policy choices and political systems.
> **Key takeaway**: If the same people with the same culture can produce radically different outcomes, inequality is clearly not determined by inherent human traits.

## 2. Believing Trickle-Down Economics

**The mistake**: Thinking tax cuts for the rich benefit everyone.

**Fix**: 40 years of data shows the opposite. Tax cuts for the rich concentrate wealth at the top. Middle-class tax cuts and public investments are what actually grow the economy.

## 3. Blaming the Poor

**The mistake**: Believing poverty is caused by poor choices or laziness.

**Fix**: Most inequality is structural, not behavioral. Two children born into different zip codes have wildly different life prospects regardless of personal effort.

> **Case: The Zip Code Destiny** (Chapter 9): A child born in a wealthy zip code will on average live 10-15 years longer, earn 3x more, and achieve higher education than a child born just a few miles away in a poor zip code. This is not about individual choices — it's about the system.
> **Key takeaway**: Where you're born determines your future more than your effort does. Justice requires a system that doesn't punish people for their birthplace.
