# Techniques: Understanding and Addressing Inequality

## Measuring Inequality

| Metric | What It Tells You | U.S. Status |
|--------|-------------------|-------------|
| Gini Coefficient | Overall income distribution | Highest among developed nations |
| Top 1% Share | Share of income going to richest | ~20% of all income |
| CEO-to-Worker Pay Ratio | How CEO pay compares to median worker | ~300:1 (was ~30:1 in 1970s) |
| Intergenerational Mobility | How easy it is to escape poverty | Much lower than Europe |
| Wealth Concentration | Share of assets owned by top | Top 1% owns more than bottom 90% |

> **Case: The CEO Pay Explosion** (Chapter 7): In the 1970s, CEOs earned about 30 times the average worker. Today, they earn over 300 times. Did CEOs become 10x more productive? No. They simply had the power to set their own pay through friendly boards.
> **Key takeaway**: CEO pay is not a market outcome — it's a power dynamic. When CEOs control the board, they control their compensation. The result: enormous wealth extraction at the top.

## Policy Solutions from the Book

1. Progressive taxation — higher rates on top incomes and wealth
2. Stronger financial regulation — break up too-big-to-fail banks
3. Invest in education — equalize opportunity from birth
4. Strengthen unions — restore workers' bargaining power
5. Universal health care — remove the burden of medical debt
6. Anti-monopoly enforcement — break up concentrated industries

> **Case: The Nordic Model** (Chapter 8): Scandinavian countries have much higher equality than the U.S. — but also higher productivity, higher innovation, and happier citizens. They prove that equality and prosperity are not trade-offs.
> **Key takeaway**: Equality doesn't mean stagnation. Countries with less inequality often outperform the U.S. on every measure of well-being.
