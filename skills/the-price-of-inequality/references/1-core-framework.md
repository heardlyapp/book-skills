# Core Framework: Inequality as Policy Choice

## The Central Thesis

"Inequality is not the inevitable result of market forces. It is the result of policy choices made by those in power. Different choices would produce a more equal, more prosperous society."

> **Case: The Postwar Boom vs Today** (Chapter 1): From 1945 to 1980, income grew across all brackets — the rich got richer, but so did everyone else. Since 1980, almost all income growth has gone to the top 1%. The difference isn't human nature — it's policy.
> **Key takeaway**: America was more equal in the 1950s-70s than today. If inequality were "natural," this wouldn't change so dramatically across time. Policy drove the shift.

## Key Concepts

| Concept | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| Gini Coefficient | Statistical measure of income inequality |
| Rent Seeking | Profiting through exploitation of market power, not productive innovation |
| Market Fundamentalism | Belief that unfettered markets always produce the best outcomes |
| Inequality of Opportunity | Life outcomes determined by birth, not merit |
| Trickle-Down Economics | Theory that tax cuts for the rich benefit everyone (Stiglitz: disproven) |

> **Case: The Financial Sector** (Chapter 2): The financial sector exploded from 3% to 8% of GDP — but much of this growth was rent seeking, not value creation. Predatory lending, excessive trading, and opaque products transferred wealth from ordinary people to bankers.
> **Key takeaway**: Not all economic growth is equal. A growing financial sector can actually destroy more value than it creates by extracting from the real economy.
