## Core Framework: Racism, Identity, and the System of Advantage

Beverly Daniel Tatum opens her landmark work by establishing two foundational concepts that underpin everything that follows: a definition of racism as a "system of advantage based on race," and a psychological framework for understanding identity as a socially constructed, multi-dimensional experience. These two ideas together form the lens through which all subsequent analysis—of childhood, adolescence, adulthood, and cross-racial dialogue—must be viewed.

### The System, Not Just the Individual

The common dictionary definition of racism—racial prejudice—is insufficient, Tatum argues. Drawing on the work of sociologist David Wellman, she defines racism as "a system of advantage based on race." This shifts the focus from individual attitudes to structural arrangements. Prejudice can be held by anyone; racism requires systemic power. In the United States, that power has historically and institutionally been held by White people.

This distinction is critical for several reasons. First, it explains why well-intentioned individuals who are "not prejudiced" can still perpetuate racism. Second, it accounts for the persistence of racial inequality even as explicit prejudice declines. Third, it reveals why people of color, who can certainly hold prejudiced views, cannot be "racist" under this definition—they do not control the institutions that systematically disadvantage Whites.

The metaphor Tatum introduces to capture this is the "moving walkway" at an airport. Active racists walk forward on the conveyor belt. Passive racists stand still—but they still arrive at the same destination. Only those actively walking in the opposite direction, faster than the belt, are antiracist. The walkway is the system; you cannot opt out of it. You can only choose which direction to travel.

### White Privilege: The Invisible Knapsack

To make this system visible, Tatum draws on Peggy McIntosh's "White Privilege: Unpacking the Invisible Knapsack." McIntosh identified a range of everyday privileges that come with being White in the United States—the ability to shop without being followed, to find hair products that match your hair type in any drugstore, to speak in a meeting without your opinion being labeled the "White viewpoint." These privileges are unearned, often unnoticed by those who receive them, and systematically denied to people of color.

White privilege does not mean that every White person is wealthy or has an easy life. A White woman on welfare still benefits from being White—she is more likely to get an apartment without a deposit, more likely to be given the benefit of the doubt by an employer, more likely to be seen as an individual rather than a representative of her race. The privilege operates alongside other dimensions of identity: class, gender, sexual orientation, ability, religion. But it operates.

### The Complexity of Identity

Chapter 2 introduces a framework for understanding identity that is as important as the racism framework. Tatum asks her audiences to complete the sentence "I am ___" in sixty seconds. The pattern is revealing: people of color typically mention race; women typically mention gender; Jewish people typically mention religion. White people rarely mention being White; men rarely mention being male; Christians rarely mention being Christian.

What this reveals is that dominant identities are invisible to those who hold them, while targeted identities are perpetually salient. As Audre Lorde observed, a "mythical norm" exists in America—White, thin, male, young, heterosexual, Christian, financially secure—and those who deviate from it know it. Those who match it rarely think about it.

Tatum draws on Jean Baker Miller's framework of domination and subordination to explain the psychological dynamics at play. Dominants set the parameters; subordinates learn to read dominants for survival. Dominants have the luxury of not thinking about the relationship; subordinates have no such luxury. This asymmetry is the psychological soil in which racism grows.

### Case 1: The Well-Intentioned Teacher

> **Case:** A White middle school teacher says to a Black student, "I don't see color—I treat all my students the same." She believes she is being fair and progressive. Under Tatum's framework, what is happening here?

The teacher is operating from a "prejudice only" understanding of racism. By declaring colorblindness, she denies the Black student's lived experience of being racially targeted in a White-dominated society. She may not be actively racist (not walking forward), but by refusing to see race, she is standing still on the conveyor belt—passive racism. The system of advantage continues to operate, and her silence does nothing to interrupt it. Moreover, she misses the opportunity to affirm the student's racial identity, to validate experiences of discrimination, and to create a truly identity-safe classroom. Tatum's framework would suggest the teacher instead acknowledge race, learn about racial identity development, and actively counter the negative messages the Black student receives from the broader culture.

### Case 2: The Diversity Workshop That Backfired

> **Case:** A corporate DEI workshop defines racism as "prejudice plus power." Several White participants react defensively: "I have no power. I'm not a CEO. I don't control hiring. This definition makes me feel blamed for things I didn't do." The facilitator is stuck.

Tatum encountered this exact response in her teaching. Her solution is twofold. First, shift to Wellman's "system of advantage" definition, which feels less accusatory and more structural. Second, use the McIntosh privilege exercise to make the system visible without personal blame. Ask: "Even if you don't feel powerful, can you see advantages that come with being White in this society?" Most White people can. Acknowledge that these advantages are unearned and often invisible, but real. Then redirect: "Now that you see the system, what are you doing to interrupt it?" The goal is not guilt but responsibility. The moving walkway metaphor helps: even standing still, you are moving with the system. What would it look like to walk in the opposite direction?

### Case 3: The "I Am" Exercise in a Classroom

> **Case:** A college instructor leads the "I am ___" exercise. A White male student writes "curious, hardworking, funny, tired" but never mentions his race or gender. A Black female student writes "Black, woman, smart, determined." When asked to share, the White student notices the difference and asks, "Why didn't I think of my Whiteness?"

This is the exercise Tatum describes in Chapter 2, and it powerfully demonstrates the asymmetry of identity salience. The White student's Whiteness is invisible to him because it matches the norm. The Black student's Blackness is salient because it is marked as "other." The teachable moment: explain that this is not a personal failing but a structural one. The dominant group's experience is treated as universal. The "mythical norm" is White, so White goes unremarked. Invite the student to explore what it means to be White—not with guilt, but with curiosity about the system that makes his race invisible. This is the first step in White identity development: moving from Contact (naive unawareness) to something more conscious.
