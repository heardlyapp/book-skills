## Anti-Patterns: What Doesn't Work in Cross-Racial Dialogue and Antiracist Practice

Tatum's book is as much a catalog of failed approaches as it is a guide to effective ones. Many common interventions—well-intentioned, widely used—are counterproductive. This reference documents the most important anti-patterns and explains why they fail, using Tatum's framework.

### Anti-Pattern 1: Mandating Colorblindness

**The pattern:** "I don't see color. I treat everyone the same." "Children are colorblind—let's not ruin it." "Race shouldn't matter."

**Why it fails:** Colorblindness denies the reality that race does matter in a racist society. When a White teacher tells a Black student "I don't see color," she is telling the student that the part of his identity that is most salient to him—and most targeted by others—is invisible to her. This is not comforting; it is invalidating. It also prevents the teacher from seeing and addressing the ways racism operates in her own classroom. The child who says "I don't see color" has already learned that race is taboo. Colorblindness is not an achievement; it is a symptom of a culture that has failed to teach children how to talk about difference.

**Tatum's alternative:** "I see that you are Black, and I want to understand your experience. Race matters in this society, and I want to make sure this classroom is a place where all identities are affirmed."

### Anti-Pattern 2: One-Shot Diversity Training

**The pattern:** A half-day workshop on unconscious bias. Everyone sits in a conference room, hears a presentation, does an exercise, and goes back to work. The organization checks the DEI box for the year.

**Why it fails:** Tatum's framework makes clear that racial identity development is a lifelong process. You cannot move someone from Contact to Autonomy in four hours. One-shot training may produce a temporary "awareness bump," but without sustained engagement, participants revert to baseline. Worse, it can create a false sense of progress—the organization feels it has "done something" while the underlying structures of advantage remain untouched.

**Tatum's alternative:** Sustained intergroup dialogue over a full semester or equivalent (the Michigan IGR model). Or the Welcome Table's three-phase process: trust-building, collaborative action, and equity plan. The key is ongoing engagement with trained facilitation.

### Anti-Pattern 3: Treating All Racism as Individual Bigotry

**The pattern:** When a racial incident occurs, the organization's response is to find and punish the "bad apple." The problem is framed as one individual's prejudice rather than a systemic issue.

**Why it fails:** Racism is not only—or even primarily—about individual attitudes. As Tatum's "moving walkway" metaphor illustrates, passive racism is the default state in a racist society. Finding and firing one bigot does nothing to change the conveyor belt. In fact, it can make things worse by creating the illusion that "the problem has been solved" while the structural conditions that produced the incident remain intact. The Texas A&M incident Tatum describes is instructive: the student body president's response was not to find the perpetrators but to call out the culture of silence that enabled them.

**Tatum's alternative:** Address both individual and systemic dimensions. Acknowledge the harm. Investigate the structural conditions that allowed the incident to occur. Create ongoing dialogue structures so that the response is not just reaction but transformation.

### Anti-Pattern 4: Placing the Burden of Education on People of Color

**The pattern:** "Help me understand: why do Black people feel X?" "Can you explain this to my team?" "Tell me what I'm doing wrong."

**Why it fails:** The "system of advantage" framework makes clear that people of color are also systematically disadvantaged in the workplace and classroom. Asking them to educate Whites about racism replicates the power dynamic: the subordinate group serves the dominant group's learning needs while the dominant group avoids doing its own work. This is exhausting for people of color and avoids the discomfort that is necessary for White growth.

**Tatum's alternative:** Do your own learning. Read the books. Attend the workshops. Find White antiracist role models who can guide you through the stages of White identity development. When you need to ask a person of color a question, frame it respectfully and acknowledge the burden: "I'm trying to understand something. I've read X and Y, but I still have a question. Would you be willing to share your perspective? And please feel free to say no if this is not a good time."

### Anti-Pattern 5: Defensive Denial of Incidents

**The pattern:** A racial incident is reported. The organization's first response is to deny, minimize, or question the credibility of the report. "Are you sure that's what they meant?" "Maybe you misunderstood." "Let's not jump to conclusions."

**Why it fails:** Joseph Benigno, the Texas A&M student body president, identified this anti-pattern clearly: "An attitude of denial is dangerous. It inhibits our ability to learn from what happened." Denial validates the perpetrator and invalidates the victim. It communicates that the organization is not a safe place to report racial harm. It prevents the organization from addressing the underlying conditions that enabled the incident.

**Tatum's alternative:** Start with acknowledgment and belief. "Thank you for telling us. We take this seriously. We will investigate and follow up." Then create space for dialogue—not just investigation—so that the community can process the incident and identify systemic changes needed.

### Anti-Pattern 6: Misunderstanding Self-Segregation as the Problem

**The pattern:** School administrators see Black students sitting together in the cafeteria and treat it as a problem to solve. They break up the group, encourage mixing, or ask teachers to intervene.

**Why it fails:** The clustering is not the problem—it is the symptom. Black students sitting together is a healthy coping response to an environment that devalues their identity. Treating it as a behavioral problem misunderstands the developmental psychology at work. As Tatum explains, adolescence is a period of identity exploration, and peer support is essential. The Black table is a refuge from the microaggressions and stereotypes that pervade the rest of the school day.

**Tatum's alternative:** Address the conditions that make the cafeteria the only safe space. Desegregate tracking. Diversify the curriculum. Train teachers in antiracist pedagogy. Create structured intergroup dialogue so that cross-racial connection happens in classrooms, not just in forced mixing. The cafeteria clustering will resolve itself when the school environment no longer requires a refuge.

### Anti-Pattern 7: Pursuing Diversity Without Equity

**The pattern:** An organization focuses on numerical representation—hiring more people of color, admitting more diverse students—without changing the culture or structures that disadvantage those same people.

**Why it fails:** Diversity without equity creates a "revolving door" effect. People of color enter an organization that was designed for White norms and are expected to adapt. Microaggressions, biased evaluation, and exclusion from networks cause them to leave at higher rates. The organization celebrates its diversity numbers while maintaining the system of advantage.

**Tatum's alternative:** Pair diversity goals with equity and inclusion strategies. Audit policies and practices for disparate impact. Invest in retention, not just recruitment. Create affinity spaces and mentoring programs. Train managers in inclusive leadership. Measure outcomes, not just inputs.

### Anti-Pattern 8: Pursuing Empathy Without Action

**The pattern:** A well-intentioned group holds listening sessions, shares stories, builds empathy across lines of difference—but never translates that empathy into structural change.

**Why it fails:** Empathy is necessary but not sufficient. Tatum's epilogue is frankly titled "Signs of Hope, Sites of Progress," but she is clear that hope must be accompanied by action. The Welcome Table model explicitly includes an "equity action plan" phase. The Atlanta Friendship Initiative pairs are community leaders who can leverage their networks. Empathy that does not lead to action can become a substitute for action—a way of feeling good without doing good.

**Tatum's alternative:** Build action into the design from the beginning. Every dialogue should include a question: "What will we do now?" Every empathy-building exercise should be followed by: "What is one change we can make in our institution?" Relationship and action are not sequential—they are iterative. Action deepens relationship, and relationship sustains action.
