## 6 Anti-Patterns of Development Thinking

### 1. "The Poor Are Irrational"

The poor are not irrational — they are constrained. Behavior that looks irrational (not buying bed nets, not saving) is often the result of limited options and narrow margins.

### 2. "Free Goods Are Wasted"

The evidence shows the opposite: free distribution increases use without increasing waste. People value free goods as much as purchased ones.

### 3. "Microfinance Is the Solution"

Microfinance helps some people but is not transformative. It is a financial tool, not a development strategy. The hype exceeded the evidence.

> **Case: The Microcredit Meta-Analysis** (Chapter 8): Multiple RCTs across countries found that microcredit has modest positive effects on business investment and self-employment — but does not dramatically reduce poverty or transform communities.
> **Key takeaway**: Microcredit is a useful tool, not a silver bullet. The evidence tempered the hype.

### 4. "Build More Schools"

Building schools does not improve education if teachers do not show up and students do not learn. Quality is as important as access.

### 5. "Poor People Do Not Plan Ahead"

The poor plan ahead as much as anyone — but they have less room for error. A small shock (illness, theft, weather) can derail months of planning.

### 6. "One Solution Works Everywhere"

Context matters. An intervention that works in Kenya may not work in India. RCTs measure local effects. Scaling requires adaptation, not replication.

> **Case: The Textbook Study** (Chapter 5): In Kenya, providing textbooks to schools did not improve test scores. The textbooks were too advanced for most students. The assumption that more resources = better learning was wrong.
> **Key takeaway**: Resources alone do not improve education. Match the resource to the student's level.
