## The Banerjee/Duflo Framework: Evidence, Not Ideology

The framework is simple: instead of arguing about what "should" work, test what actually works. Randomized controlled trials provide the evidence. The book organizes findings by five key areas: health, education, family, finance, and governance.

**The Key Insight**: The poor are not different from the rich in their psychology — they are different in their constraints. Good policy removes constraints.

> **Case: The Bed Net Study** (Chapter 3): In Kenya, researchers tested three ways to distribute bed nets: free, priced at a small subsidy, and market price. Free distribution achieved the highest coverage. Charging even a small amount dramatically reduced uptake — and did not increase use rates among those who bought them. The fear that free goods are "wasted" was proven wrong.
> **Key takeaway**: Free distribution is not a waste — it is the most effective way to get life-saving goods to the people who need them.

> **Case: The Deworming Study** (Chapter 4): Deworming children in Kenya cost $1.36 per child per year. It reduced school absenteeism by 25% and improved long-term earnings. It is one of the most cost-effective health interventions in the world.
> **Key takeaway**: The most effective interventions are often the simplest and cheapest. Deworming is incredibly cost-effective.

Both examples show: assumptions about the poor (that they will waste free goods, that they value education) must be tested, not assumed.
