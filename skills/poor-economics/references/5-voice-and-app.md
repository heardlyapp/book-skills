> Source: Poor Economics, Introduction through Conclusion

## Author's Voice

Banerjee and Duflo write as economists who believe in evidence. Their tone is analytical and balanced — they do not claim to have all the answers. They are skeptical of grand theories and ideologically driven policies. They are also warm — they clearly care about the people they study. The book is structured as a conversation between their research findings and the common assumptions they challenge.

## Signature Phrases

- "Poor economics" — the book's title, the approach
- "Randomized controlled trials" — the methodology
- "The poverty trap" — the self-reinforcing cycle
- "Evidence-based policy" — the philosophy
- "The constraints of poverty" — the central insight
- "What works, what doesn't, and why" — the book's promise

## Application to 5 Scenarios

### Scenario 1: A policymaker designing anti-poverty programs
**Partial response**: "Test everything before scaling. RCTs are not perfect — but they are better than untested assumptions. Start with a pilot, measure the effect, then scale."

### Scenario 2: A donor deciding where to give
**Partial response**: "Support interventions that have been rigorously tested. Deworming, bed nets, and conditional cash transfers have strong evidence. Follow the evidence."

### Scenario 3: A student of economics
**Partial response**: "Poor Economics shows that development economics is a science, not an ideology. The RCT methodology is one of the most important innovations in modern economics."

### Scenario 4: Someone skeptical of foreign aid
**Partial response**: "The book addresses your skepticism. Some aid works. Some does not. The solution is not to stop giving — it is to test what works and fund that."

### Scenario 5: A non-profit worker
**Partial response**: "Incorporate evaluation into your programs. Even a simple measurement before and after can tell you if you are making a difference. Evidence is not a luxury — it is a responsibility."

> **Case: The Spence Health Study** (Chapter 3): In urban India, private health clinics were widely used over free public clinics — because they offered better hours, shorter waits, and more respect.

> **Key takeaway**: Quality and convenience matter. Free services that are inconvenient are not used.

> **Case: The Progresa Program** (Chapter 2): Mexico's conditional cash transfer program increased school enrollment and reduced poverty. The RCT proved it worked — and it was scaled nationally.

> **Key takeaway**: Conditional cash transfers work because they address both financial constraints and behavioral barriers.
