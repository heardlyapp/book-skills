## Practical Techniques

### 1. The RCT Mindset

Before implementing a policy, ask: "Can we test this?" Even a small pilot with random assignment can tell you whether a program works before scaling.

### 2. The Constraint Check

When people do not behave as expected, ask: "What constraints are they facing?" Not "what is wrong with them?" The poor know what they need — they are blocked by lack of access, not lack of knowledge.

> **Case: The Savings Study** (Chapter 7): Poor people want to save, but informal savings methods (cash under the mattress, livestock) are insecure and leaky. The simple intervention of a lockable savings box increased savings. The problem was not motivation — it was technology.
> **Key takeaway**: Provide the right tools, and people will use them. The poor want to save — they need savings accounts that work.

### 3. The Small Barrier Removal

Identify and remove tiny barriers. A chlorine dispenser at the water source is more effective than distributing bottles. Deworming at school is more effective than asking parents to take children to clinics.

### 4. The Pilot, Then Scale

Do not implement a nationwide program without testing it first. Pilot on a small scale, measure the effect, refine, then scale. This is how evidence-based policy works.

> **Case: The Pratham Education Program** (Chapter 5): Pratham, an Indian NGO, developed a program that grouped children by learning level (not age) and taught them at their level. An RCT found that the program dramatically improved learning outcomes. It was scaled across India.
> **Key takeaway**: Test first, scale second. Pratham's program worked because they tested it rigorously before expanding.
