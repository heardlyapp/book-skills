# Defending Reason, Science, and Humanism

> Source: Enlightenment Now, Chapters 1, 17, 18, 19, 20, 23, 24

## The Toolkit — Why Enlightenment Values Work

### The Case for Reason
Reason is not perfect — but it is better than the alternatives. Faith (believing without evidence) can produce good lives but terrible politics. Intuition (trusting your gut) gets many things wrong (see cognitive biases). Tradition (doing what was always done) prevents adaptation and progress. Reason — evidence, logic, open debate — is the only reliable method for understanding reality.

### The Case for Science
Science is the most successful method ever devised for producing reliable knowledge. It has given us antibiotics, vaccines, computers, the internet, and the data in this book. The critique of science (postmodernism, anti-vaccine movements, climate denial) is not a critique of science itself but of specific institutions or applications.

### The Case for Humanism
Humanism is the value that all other values serve. The goal is human flourishing — not national greatness, not divine glory, not ideological purity. Humanism is universal: it applies to all humans, not just your tribe.

> **Case: The Critique of Postmodernism** (Chapter 19)
> Pinker devotes significant space to critiquing the academic movements — postmodernism, critical theory, social constructionism — that deny the possibility of objective truth and claim that science is just another form of power. He argues that these ideas are not just academically silly — they are politically dangerous. If truth is just power, then there is no basis for arguing against those who deny climate science, vaccine safety, or election results. The post-truth era was not caused by social media alone — it was also caused by intellectuals who spent decades undermining faith in truth.
> **Key takeaway**: The denial of objective truth is not a sophisticated philosophical position — it's a self-destructive one. If there is no truth, there is no basis for science, democracy, or human rights. The academic movements that undermined truth are partly responsible for the political movements that now threaten Enlightenment institutions.

> **Case: The Unfinished Project** (Chapter 24)
> Pinker acknowledges that progress is not complete. Climate change is real, inequality exists, democracy is backsliding. But he argues that these are problems to be solved by more Enlightenment, not reasons to abandon it. The same tools that gave us progress — reason, science, humanism — can solve the problems created by that progress. The Enlightenment is not a finished project — it's an ongoing one. The choice is not between progress and catastrophe. It's between enlightened responses to our problems and unenlightened ones.
> **Key takeaway**: The fact that problems still exist does not mean progress has failed. It means the project continues. The right response to the challenges of the 21st century is not to abandon reason, science, and humanism — it's to apply them more rigorously to the problems they have revealed.
