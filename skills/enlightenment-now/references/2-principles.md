# The Psychology of Pessimism — Why We Think Things Are Worse

> Source: Enlightenment Now, Chapters 2, 3, 22

## The Perception-Reality Gap

Pinker's opening puzzle: by every objective measure, the world is better than ever. But surveys show that people believe the world is getting worse. The gap between data and perception is huge — and has real political consequences.

### The Cognitive Biases That Drive Pessimism

| Bias | How It Works | Effect on Perception |
|------|-------------|---------------------|
| Availability heuristic | Events that are easily recalled (vivid, recent, emotional) seem more common | Plane crashes dominate news; car crashes don't. You overestimate dramatic deaths and underestimate mundane ones |
| Negativity bias | Bad events have more psychological impact than good ones | One criticism outweighs five compliments. One disaster outweighs a hundred small improvements |
| The "golden age" fallacy | The past is remembered as better than it was | Nostalgia erases the suffering, disease, and violence of the past |
| Media distortion | "If it bleeds, it leads" — media preferentially reports bad news | The news is a firehose of disaster. Gradual improvement does not get headlines |
| Declinism | The belief that things are on a downward trajectory | Every generation thinks the next is worse. It has always been wrong |

### The Media Amplification Loop

Pinker argues that the media, social media, and political competition amplify these biases. The result: a population that is systemically misinformed about the state of the world.

> **Case: The Misinformed Public** (Chapter 2)
> Pinker cites surveys showing that in most countries, a majority believes the world is getting worse. When asked about specific trends (poverty, war, illiteracy), respondents systematically guess that things are much worse than they actually are. In one survey, only 5% of respondents correctly identified that extreme poverty had fallen — the majority thought it had increased. This misinformation is not random — it is structured by the biases described above.
> **Key takeaway**: Public opinion about the state of the world is not just wrong — it's systemically wrong in a pessimistic direction. This has real consequences: people who think the world is getting worse are more likely to support authoritarian leaders who promise to "fix" things, more likely to reject globalization and trade, and more likely to despair about the future.

> **Case: The Doomsayer's Track Record** (Chapter 2, 21)
> Pinker documents prominent predictions of catastrophe that were wrong: Paul Ehrlich (The Population Bomb, 1968 — predicted mass starvation in the 1970s), the Club of Rome (Limits to Growth, 1972 — predicted resource exhaustion by 2000), the Malthusian tradition (every generation since 1798). Each prediction was confident, widely believed, and completely wrong. The pattern: the predictor identifies a real problem (population growth, resource consumption), extrapolates it linearly, ignores human ingenuity and adaptation, and predicts catastrophe by a specific date. When the date passes without catastrophe, the predictor either sets a new date or is forgotten.
> **Key takeaway**: The track record of pessimistic predictions is terrible. This does not mean that all predictions of catastrophe are wrong — but it does mean that the default assumption should be skeptical. The doomsayer sells more books than the optimist — but the optimist has been right more often.
