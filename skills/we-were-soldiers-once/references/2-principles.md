# Key Principles — We Were Soldiers Once...And Young

## I. War Is About the Man Next to You

Moore and Galloway: "We discovered in that depressing, hellish place, where death was our constant companion, that we loved each other. We killed for each other, we died for each other, and we wept for each other." The book's central truth: in battle, "our world shrank to the man on our left and the man on our right and the enemy all around."

## II. Leaders Must Be Visible Under Fire

Moore commanded from the front, not the rear. He was in the command post at LZ X-Ray, but he was exposed — sharing the danger, making decisions in real time. When Charlie Company was being overrun, he told Captain Edwards: "Your company will have to fight on alone for the time being." It was a terrible decision, but a necessary one.

## III. The Best Plans Collapse on Contact

The NVA plan was to draw the Americans into battle and learn their tactics. The American plan was to use airmobility to strike fast. Both plans worked — and both failed. "Both sides claimed victory and both sides drew lessons, some of them dangerously deceptive."

## IV. Courage Is Not the Absence of Fear

"We were dry-mouthed and our bowels churned with fear, and still the enemy came on in waves." The bravest men in the book are the ones who admitted fear and fought anyway. Lieutenant Geoghegan, who died helping a wounded man. Sergeant Jemison, who took three bullets and kept fighting.

## V. The Cost of War Is Carried Forever

"The dead did not get up and dust themselves off and walk away. The wounded did not wash away the red and go on with life, unhurt. Those who were, miraculously, unscratched were by no means untouched. Not one of us left Vietnam the same young man he was when he arrived."

## VI. Both Sides Were Brave

Moore insisted the book honor the NVA soldiers. "They, too, fought and died bravely. They were a worthy enemy." The dedication includes both American and NVA dead. This is not weakness — it is respect.

## VII. The Lessons of War Are Dangerous

The US military learned that airmobility could win battles — leading to a strategy of attrition that bled America for a decade. The NVA learned they could stand and fight the Americans — and keep coming. Both drew the wrong conclusions from Ia Drang. Sometimes the lesson of a battle is that there is no single lesson.
