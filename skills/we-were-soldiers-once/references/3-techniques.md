# Techniques — We Were Soldiers Once...And Young

## How to Lead Under Fire (Based on Moore & Galloway)

### Technique 1: Be Present
Moore commanded from LZ X-Ray, not from a headquarters miles away. When Charlie Company was being overrun, he was on the radio, making decisions in real time. "I held the lives of all these men in my hands." The technique: when the crisis is worst, that's where the leader needs to be — not hiding from it.

### Technique 2: Know Your Men's Names
Moore had memorized his officers' names, hometowns, families. The dedication lists every fallen soldier by name and hometown. "These men were not statistics." The technique: lead people, not units.

### Technique 3: The "Broken Arrow" Protocol
When the situation was desperate, Moore authorized the call for maximum air support. Every available aircraft in South Vietnam responded. The technique: have a pre-planned emergency protocol for the worst-case scenario — and don't hesitate to activate it.

### Technique 4: Fix Bayonets
When the reconnaissance platoon — only 22 men — was ordered into the gap, they fixed bayonets and charged. The technique: when the line is about to break, the only option is to attack. A desperate counterattack can succeed where a desperate defense fails.

### Technique 5: The Radio Method
Moore maintained constant communication with his company commanders, even when they were pinned down. Captain Edwards called for reinforcements while bleeding from a shoulder wound. Moore had to refuse — and had to tell him directly. The technique: communicate directly, honestly, and immediately — even when the news is bad.

### Technique 6: After-Action, Not After-Excuse
Moore spent decades analyzing what went right and wrong at Ia Drang. The lost platoon. The friendly fire. He did not excuse himself. "I have lived with those decisions for the rest of my life." The technique: own your mistakes. The men who died deserve that much.

### Technique 7: Honor Both Sides
The book dedicates equal respect to the NVA dead. Moore insisted. "They were a worthy enemy." The technique: respecting your adversary does not diminish your own sacrifice — it honors the humanity of everyone who fought.
