# 2 — Key Principles

> Source: *Inevitable* by Mike Colias.

## The 7 Principles

### 1. The Destination Is Inevitable. The Path Is Messy.

Colias is clear: EVs will eventually dominate. But the transition is full of false starts, price wars, broken chargers, and political fights. Don't confuse the messy path with a wrong direction.

### 2. Your Core Competency Can Become Your Core Liability.

GM and Ford had a century of combustion-engine expertise. In the EV world, mastery of cylinder blocks, pistons, and valve trains was worthless — worse, it created internal resistance to change.

> **Case: Toyota's Hydrogen Bet** (Chapter 8): Toyota bet heavily on hydrogen fuel cells rather than battery EVs. The bet was rational — hydrogen had advantages in range and refueling speed — but it isolated Toyota from the industry's direction. By the time Toyota reversed course and committed to EVs, it was years behind. Its core competency in hybrid technology became a trap.

### 3. The Battery Cost Wall Requires an Incremental Grind.

Batteries account for as much as one-third of an EV's cost. Claims about imminent breakthroughs never pan out. The real cost reduction comes from years of incremental improvements — better cell chemistry, smarter pack design, efficient manufacturing.

### 4. Government Intervention Is Necessary but Not Sufficient.

The $7.5 billion from the infrastructure bill for charging stations helped break the chicken-and-egg deadlock. But it didn't solve it alone. Entrepreneurs like David Jankowsky (Francis Energy) and Carter Li (SWTCH) had to do the work on the ground.

### 5. Partner with the Disruptor.

Ford and GM both ended up doing deals with Tesla for Supercharger access. It was humiliating. It was also the right call.

### 6. China Is Not a Normal Competitor.

> **Case: Chinese Government Support** (Chapter 2): China's government provides cheap capital, fast regulatory approvals, and a massive domestic market that absorbs production volume while costs come down. Western automakers cannot match this combination. The response must be strategic — not protectionist.

### 7. The Middle Is Where the Real Work Happens.

The early adopters (tech-savvy, affluent, willing to tolerate hassles) already have their EVs. The mainstream buyer — apartment dwellers, rural drivers, pickup truck owners — is a tougher sell. Winning them requires solving different problems.
