# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *Inevitable* by Mike Colias — Introduction, Chapters 1-15, Conclusion.

## The Three Frameworks

### Framework A: The Incumbent's Dilemma (Introduction, Chapters 1, 6, 8)

Legacy automakers face a cruel paradox: their core competency (combustion engines) is becoming irrelevant. The transition requires a complete rewiring of supply chains, engineering teams, and corporate culture — while their existing business is still generating profits that fund the transformation.

> **Case: Ford's Mach-E Teardown** (Introduction): Jim Farley ordered his engineers to tear down a Mustang Mach-E and a Tesla Model Y side by side. The result was humiliating: Tesla held a $3,000-$4,000 cost advantage simply through smarter engineering. The Mach-E had 40% more fasteners, 70 pounds of excess wiring harnesses, and brackets holding up parts that didn't need holding up. Ford's engineers had been telling Farley there was no difference between the two. They were wrong.

> **Case: GM's Compliance Car Era** (Chapter 1): Before the Hummer EV, GM's electric vehicle efforts — the EV1, the Volt, and the Bolt — were what insiders called "compliance cars." They were designed just well enough to meet regulatory requirements but not good enough to attract real customers. Barra broke this pattern with the Hummer EV: a $113,000 statement vehicle with 1,000 horsepower and "Crabwalk mode." From dumpy to dramatic — but still not profitable.

### Framework B: The Chicken-and-Egg Problem (Chapters 5, 6, 10)

Without charging infrastructure, consumers won't buy EVs. Without EVs on the road, companies won't build charging stations. Tesla solved this by building its own proprietary Supercharger network — a "walled garden" that became a competitive advantage. Everyone else had to follow.

> **Case: The Supercharger Deal** (Chapter 5): By 2023, Tesla had 60% of the US's fast chargers. Ford customers had access to zero of them. Jim Farley drove past a Tesla Supercharger station with his family — his Mach-E couldn't use it. "What the hell, Dad! Why can't we stop there?" That humiliation led Farley to call Elon Musk and negotiate a deal: Ford customers would get access to 12,000 Superchargers. GM followed two weeks later. The industry's pride was swallowed by pragmatism.

### Framework C: The China Factor (Chapters 2, 7, 13)

Chinese EV makers are not just competitors — they are a fundamentally different kind of threat. With government backing, full supply chain control, and a massive domestic market, they can out-invest and out-produce any Western automaker.

> **Case: BYD's Ascent** (Chapter 2): BYD started as a battery maker. By the mid-2020s, it was roughly the size of Tesla, with a vertical integration that Western automakers could only dream of. It controlled minerals, cells, packs, motors, and final assembly — the entire chain. Chinese government support meant cheap capital, fast factory approvals, and a domestic market approaching 40% EV penetration. Cargo ships laden with Chinese EVs have landed in Europe. North American factories are under discussion.
