# 5 — Voice & App

> Source: *Inevitable* by Mike Colias.

## Colias' Voice

Colias writes as a seasoned Wall Street Journal automotive reporter. His style is narrative journalism — vivid scenes, direct quotes, and a reporter's insistence on letting the facts tell the story. He does not cheerlead for EVs or attack their critics. He presents the transition as a complex, human story full of flawed executives, struggling engineers, and uncertain outcomes.

> **Case: The President Drives the Hummer** (Chapter 1): President Biden, 78, flooring a $113,000 Hummer EV to 60 mph in three seconds is the kind of scene that captures the absurdity and ambition of the EV transition. "This sucker's something else!" Biden gushed. Mary Barra sat beaming in the back seat. A decade earlier, GM had killed the Hummer brand. Now the president was using its rebirth to sell his agenda.

> **Case: Farley's Honesty** (Chapter 10): Colias presents Farley not as a visionary but as a leader willing to confront ugly truths. "Farley smelled bullshit" when his engineers told him the Mach-E was as efficient as the Model Y. His willingness to verify — and his willingness to share the humiliating results with his team — made the transformation possible.

## Five Scenarios

### 1. The Legacy Company Leader Facing Disruption

Your core product is being made obsolete. Your engineers tell you you're fine. Run a teardown. Find the real gap. It's probably bigger than you think.

### 2. The Infrastructure Entrepreneur

You're trying to build something that doesn't make sense without demand. Use government money strategically. Find partners. Accept that you'll lose money for years.

### 3. The Strategist Competing with China

You can't beat Chinese companies at their own game. You need to find a different advantage: brand, service, software, or a protected market.

### 4. The Executive Managing a Painful Transition

You need to be honest with your organization about the scale of change. Farley's teardown was humiliating. It was necessary.

### 5. The Investor Told the Market Is "Mature"

EV growth slowed in 2024. This was not the end of the transition. It was the beginning of the hard part: winning over mainstream consumers.
