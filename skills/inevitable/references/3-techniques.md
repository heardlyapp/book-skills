# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *Inevitable* by Mike Colias.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The Teardown Audit

When you suspect your organization is in denial about its competitive position: tear down your product and a competitor's product side by side. Quantify the gap. Ford's Mach-E teardown revealed a $3,000-$4,000 disadvantage in hidden design inefficiencies.

> **Case: The Dearborn Autopsy** (Introduction): Jim Farley assembled his engineering team in a warehouse with a cut-open Model Y and Mach-E. The exercise was humiliating — but it was the only way to get the organization to admit the truth. "The CEO felt like he had been lied to."

> **Case: Doug Field's Diagnosis** (Introduction): Doug Field, who had worked at Tesla on the Model 3, identified the problems immediately: excess fasteners, overweight wiring harnesses, brackets that served no purpose. The gap wasn't in technology — it was in design discipline.

### 2. The Walled Garden Strategy

When you need to solve a chicken-and-egg problem: build your own infrastructure first, even if it's expensive. Tesla's Supercharger network was a massive investment — but it became one of the strongest competitive advantages in the industry.

### 3. The Partnership-With-the-Rival Move

When a competitor has something you need: suppress your pride and make the deal. Ford and GM both partnered with Tesla for Supercharger access. The alternative — building their own networks — would have taken years and billions more.

### 4. The Incremental Cost Reduction Plan

When a component is too expensive: don't wait for a miracle breakthrough. Focus on incremental improvements across design, manufacturing, and supply chain. Farley saved $3,000-$4,000 per vehicle by re-engineering the wiring and fasteners alone.

### 5. The Government Leverage Play

When private investment alone can't solve the problem: use government programs strategically. Farley and Barra used the Biden infrastructure bill's $7.5 billion charging fund as leverage to accelerate their EV strategies.
