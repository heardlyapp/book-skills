# Key Principles — We Are Displaced

## I. Displacement Is Not a Choice — Survival Is

Malala: "We had no other option. It was not safe to stay. But where would we go?"
Zaynab: "What kind of choice was this? It was like a doomsday for our region. It wasn't a choice: It was survival."
Ajida: "We had no choice but to run."

No one leaves home by choice. The decision is between life and death. The only "choice" is survival.

## II. The Paradox of Refugee Gratitude

Farah: "I call it refugee gratitude. But I also felt angry that one person could decide where a whole group of people could and couldn't live."
Marie Claire: "For every amazing accomplishment, their happiness is weighed down by the very real trauma that got them here."
Malala: "I am incredibly grateful to the UK for the warm welcome... But not a day goes by when I don't miss my home."

Refugees feel both: gratitude for safety AND grief for what was lost. Neither feeling invalidates the other.

## III. Education Is Not Optional — It Is Survival

Malala: "A ban on girls' schools meant a ban on my dreams, a limit on my future."
Muzoon: "If I did not continue studying, I could lose my chance to go to college. I could lose my chance of a future."
Najla: "The school was my door to see the world."
Zaynab: "I finished my treatment by mid-December... I was about to turn nineteen — this was the best birthday present ever."
Marie Claire: "I convinced him I could do it — I was a good student and spoke decent English. I just needed a chance."

Education is not a luxury. For displaced children, it is the one thing that differentiates a future from a dead end.

## IV. Resilience Is Not Inborn — It Is Forged

Najla ran away at 14 to fight for education. Father didn't speak to her for a year. She didn't give up.
Muzoon went tent-to-tent in a refugee camp convincing parents not to marry off their daughters.
Malala was shot in the head. She did not break.
Marie Claire watched her mother murdered. She still graduated high school 5 months after arriving in America.

These girls did not start out extraordinary. Circumstance demanded they become extraordinary.

## V. Home Is a Memory, Not a Place

María: "I have never felt 'at home' in any other place than the one I keep alive in my head, from when I was a child."
Zaynab: "Leaving Yemen somehow felt as if I were leaving her [her grandmother]."
Malala: "The home I knew no longer existed except in my dreams."
Ajida: "There is nothing left there for us except sadness."

For displaced people, home becomes a memory. The physical place may still exist, but it will never be the same.

## VI. Every Refugee Has Two Stories: The One They Tell and the One They Don't

Malala: "I don't want to keep telling my story."
Marie Claire's family rarely spoke of their mother's murder.
Farah's parents never used the word "refugee."
Jennifer: "I heard the pain in her heart and saw the tears in her eyes. When she spoke, I felt her trauma as well as her triumph."

What refugees choose to share is only the surface. The deep grief and trauma remain private — often for years or forever.

## VII. One Person Can Make a Difference

Jennifer saw a photo on the news. She googled "refugee and volunteer." She showed up. She changed Marie Claire's life.
Jérôme Jarre co-created the Love Army, raised money, built 4,000 shelters and 81 deepwater wells — all through social media.
Muzoon, a teenage girl in a refugee camp, went tent-to-tent and changed the lives of girls she met.
"You can help by donating money, of course, but also by giving time and attention. Research organizations in your community, like Jennifer did." — Malala
