# Techniques — Literacy, Storytelling, and Reading the World

## Practical Methods

> **Case: The Stolen Letter**: Jim finds a letter from Miss Watson's lawyer discussing the possibility of selling him. This letter is a document of his own commodification. He reads it, memorizes it, and uses the information to plan his escape. The letter is both a weapon (knowledge is power) and a wound (he is discussed as property in writing that he was not meant to see). Jim's ability to read the letter — which he was never supposed to be able to read — gives him an advantage that the white characters do not know he has.
> **Key takeaway**: Information that you were not meant to have is often the most valuable. Learn to read what is written about you, even when it was not meant for your eyes.

> **Case: Naming as Power**: Throughout the novel, names matter. Jim is called many things by white characters — boy, n****r, Jim. Each name carries a specific weight of diminishment. Everett's choice to title the novel "James" — not "Jim" — is a powerful statement. Jim is what white people call him. James is who he is. The act of claiming your own name is the first act of reclaiming your humanity.
> **Key takeaway**: Names matter. Accepting the name others give you is accepting their version of you. Claiming your own name is an act of self-creation.

> **Case: The Journal**: Jim keeps a journal — a dangerous object. If discovered, it could get him killed. But he writes in it anyway because writing is how he maintains his sense of self. "If I stop writing," he thinks, "I will become what they say I am." The journal is an act of resistance. It is proof that he exists beyond how he is seen. It is also a record of the truth that will survive him.
> **Key takeaway**: Writing is an act of self-preservation. When the world tells you that you do not exist as a full human, writing proves otherwise. Keep a record. It may outlast the system that denies your humanity.
