# Don't Shoot the Dog!: The New Art of Teaching and Training

## Introduction: The Laws of Behavior Change

Karen Pryor opens with a radical idea: most of us try to change behavior using methods that don't work. We threaten, coerce, punish — then wonder why the behavior continues. The problem isn't the subject; it's our approach.

The laws of reinforcement are as fundamental as the laws of physics. They underlie all learning and teaching situations. Yet most people apply them backwards — they punish what they don't want instead of reinforcing what they do want.

Pryor learned this firsthand working with dolphins at Sea Life Park in Hawaii (1963). Dolphins cannot be coerced — they simply swim away. The only tool available was positive reinforcement (a bucket of fish). She discovered that this approach worked not just for dolphins, but for dogs, children, colleagues, spouses, and even herself.

This skill gives you the complete system: the five training tools, the ten rules of shaping, the eight methods for eliminating unwanted behavior, and the clicker training technology.

## The Five Training Tools

### 1. Reinforcement: Better Than Rewards

The most fundamental concept. A reinforcer is any event that increases the likelihood of a behavior occurring again. It must happen in real time — instantly, as the behavior occurs.

**Key distinctions**:
- A **reward** is something given (often delayed, often unrelated to behavior)
- A **reinforcer** is something that increases behavior frequency (must be immediate, must be contingent on behavior)
- What you *think* should be reinforcing may not be. The definition is empirical: if behavior doesn't increase, it wasn't reinforcing.

**Book case — Wall Street lawyers**: Pryor describes how even Wall Street lawyers respond to well-timed positive reinforcement. When a lawyer made a good point during a tense negotiation, immediate recognition ("Excellent point") increased that behavior. The opposite — ignoring good points and only criticizing bad ones — is the default and creates hostile environments.

**Book case — Dolphin training origin**: At Sea Life Park, Pryor and her team could not use leashes, bridles, or force. Only positive reinforcement (fish). This forced the purest application of reinforcement principles. The result: dolphins performed complex behaviors reliably and enthusiastically.

### 2. Shaping: Developing Super Performance

Shaping is the process of reinforcing successive approximations toward a target behavior. You don't wait for the perfect behavior — you reinforce small steps in the right direction.

**The 10 Rules of Shaping**:
1. Raise criteria in sufficiently small increments
2. Train one aspect at a time
3. Put current level on a variable schedule before raising criteria
4. When introducing new criteria, temporarily relax old ones
5. Stay ahead — plan your shaping program
6. Don't change trainers midstream
7. If one approach isn't working, try another
8. Don't interrupt gratuitously
9. If behavior deteriorates, go back to kindergarten
10. End each session on a high note

**Book case — Chicken training**: Pryor describes how training a chicken to perform a simple behavior (pecking a target) teaches you everything about shaping. You cannot force a chicken. You must break the behavior into tiny steps and reinforce each one. This experience is so powerful that one dolphin researcher said: "Nobody should be allowed to have a baby until they have first been required to train a chicken."

**Book case — Olympic coaches**: Pryor notes that many Olympic coaches now use shaping instead of browbeating. They break complex athletic performances into tiny, reinforceable components. The result: faster skill acquisition and better performance.

### 3. Stimulus Control: Cooperation Without Coercion

Stimulus control means the behavior occurs on cue and only on cue. Four criteria:
1. Behavior always occurs immediately upon presentation of the cue
2. Behavior never occurs in absence of the cue
3. Behavior never occurs in response to another stimulus
4. No other behavior occurs in response to this cue

**Book case — Dolphin cues**: Dolphin trainers establish stimulus control so precisely that a hand signal from 50 yards away produces exactly one behavior. The animal doesn't anticipate, doesn't offer random behaviors. Perfect stimulus control.

**Book case — Musical conductors**: Symphony conductors display beautiful stimulus control. A single gesture from the conductor produces a specific response from 100 musicians simultaneously. The training that made this possible is invisible but fundamental.

### 4. Untraining: Eight Methods

Eight methods for eliminating unwanted behavior, from most to least destructive:

| Method | Description | Effectiveness |
|--------|-------------|---------------|
| 1. Shoot the Animal | Remove the subject | Works immediately, teaches nothing |
| 2. Punishment | Add aversive after behavior | Usually doesn't work long-term |
| 3. Negative Reinforcement | Remove aversive when correct | Works but creates avoidance |
| 4. Extinction | Withhold reinforcement | Slow but effective |
| 5. Incompatible Behavior | Train something that can't coexist | Very effective |
| 6. Put on Cue, Never Give Cue | Make it a controlled trick | Elegant, for trainers |
| 7. Shape the Absence | Reinforce everything except the bad | Kind and effective |
| 8. Change the Motivation | Address root cause | Most fundamental and kindest |

**Book case — Barking dog**: A dog that barks all night can be handled by each method. Method 1: debark the dog (physical). Method 2: hit or spray (punishment). Method 4: ignore the barking (extinction). Method 5: teach "speak" on cue, then reinforce quiet (incompatible). Method 8: address why the dog is barking — loneliness, boredom, territorial anxiety.

**Book case — Messy roommate**: Method 1: change roommates. Method 2: yell and threaten. Method 4: silently clean up and say nothing. Method 5: reinforce any behavior that keeps laundry contained. Method 7: reinforce every clean area. Method 8: find out why the roommate is messy — depression? stress? never learned?

### 5. Clicker Training: A New Technology

The clicker is a marker signal that tells the subject the exact instant they've done something right. It creates an incredibly precise communication channel.

**Why it works**: The clicker bridges the time gap between the behavior and the reinforcer. The click marks the exact instant of correct behavior; the treat follows. This precision accelerates learning dramatically.

**Book case — Hollywood animal training**: Movies like *Babe* used clicker training to get pigs, ducks, and other "untrainable" animals to perform on cue. The breakthrough: positive reinforcement works on ANY species. The clicker makes it precise enough for film.

**Book case — The Great Internet Canine Hot Dog Challenge**: Pryor describes how clicker training spread from dolphin tanks to dog parks through the internet. Dog owners worldwide discovered that clicker training was faster, more reliable, and more fun than traditional choke-chain methods. A grassroots movement was born.

## The Fundamental Principle

Pryor's most important insight: **The subject teaches the trainer.** If training isn't working, look at the trainer's technique, not the subject's stubbornness. Training is a two-way communication loop. The trainer must adapt to the subject.

This principle applies everywhere — with pets, children, employees, and yourself. When behavior doesn't change, the problem is usually timing, criteria, or the reinforcer — not the subject.

## Listen and Execute

You now have the complete system. The key is practice: start with a simple behavior and shape it step by step.

**Start today**:
1. Pick one unwanted behavior you're currently punishing without results
2. Identify which of the eight methods might work better
3. For a new behavior you want to teach: break it into tiny steps
4. Watch for any behavior you like and reinforce it immediately
5. If training isn't working, look in the mirror, not at the subject
