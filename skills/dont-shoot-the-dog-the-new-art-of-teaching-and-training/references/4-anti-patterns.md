# Anti-Patterns: What Not to Do

## Overview

Pryor's book is as much a diagnosis of why common training methods fail as it is a guide to what works. The most pervasive anti-patterns come from our habit of using punishment, our failure to understand timing, and our tendency to blame the learner instead of the training method.

## Anti-Pattern 1: Defaulting to Punishment

When something goes wrong, the instinct is to punish. A dog chews a shoe — scold. A child talks back — ground them. An employee misses a deadline — reprimand. Punishment feels like action. It feels like teaching. But it's rarely effective for long-term behavior change.

**Why it fails:** Punishment suppresses behavior temporarily but doesn't eliminate it. The dog stops chewing shoes when you're watching but continues when you're gone. The employee finds ways to avoid detection rather than improving performance. Punishment also creates side effects: fear of the punisher, aggression, and avoidance of the training situation entirely.

**Case — Prison as "education":** Pryor points out that sending people to prison is often called "teaching them a lesson." But the lesson taught is usually how to be a better criminal. The behavior the punishment aims to change (crime) occurs long after the punishment, so there's no temporal connection. Prison serves retribution, not behavior change.

**Case — The dog who "knows better":** Owners often say "my dog knows better" when the dog performs a behavior the owner doesn't want. The dog doesn't "know better" — the dog knows the behavior produces a result. The owner has reinforced the behavior in some way, or the punishment hasn't been consistent enough.

**Fix:** Before punishing, ask: "What am I reinforcing by accident?" The unwanted behavior is being maintained by something. Identify and remove that reinforcement before adding punishment.

## Anti-Pattern 2: Poor Timing

Reinforcement that arrives too late reinforces the wrong behavior. This is the single most common technical error in training. People praise or correct long after the behavior has occurred, accidentally strengthening whatever is happening at the moment of reinforcement.

**Why it fails:** If you praise a dog for coming when called, but the praise comes when the dog has already started sniffing a bush, you've reinforced sniffing bushes. If you tell a child "good job" after they've already run off to play, you've reinforced playing.

**Case — The Training Game timing lesson:** In the whistle game, trainers immediately discover the power of timing. Blow the whistle as the subject turns away from the goal, and the subject learns to turn away. The group watches the behavior spiral away from the goal — caused entirely by bad timing.

**Case — Shaping destruction:** If a dog barks at the door and you yell "quiet" 10 seconds later, the dog may associate the yell with what it's doing at that moment — looking at you or walking away from the door. You've accidentally reinforced everything except what you wanted.

**Fix:** Use a marker signal (clicker, whistle, word) that can be delivered at the exact instant of behavior. Deliver the actual reinforcer (treat, praise) after the marker. The marker bridges the timing gap.

## Anti-Pattern 3: Raising Criteria Too Fast

The most common amateur mistake: asking for too much progress too quickly. The trainer sees the subject is "almost there" and raises the criteria dramatically. The subject fails repeatedly and loses motivation.

**Why it fails:** When the subject can't earn reinforcement, two things happen: the behavior stops, and the subject becomes frustrated. Frustration leads to avoidance, aggression, or giving up entirely. It can take longer to recover from a too-fast criteria raise than it would have taken to train correctly the first time.

**Case — The jumped horse:** A rider raising jump heights all at once instead of incrementally. The horse fails, the rider gets frustrated, the horse begins refusing jumps. "Raise criteria in increments small enough that the subject always has a realistic chance for reinforcement" — Law #1 of shaping.

**Case — The frustrated student trainer:** In a classroom Training Game, a student raised criteria too quickly. The "animal" froze, then showed signs of anger and frustration. The trainer had to "go back to kindergarten" — dropping criteria to an easily achieved level — before progress could resume.

**Fix:** If the subject fails twice in a row, your criteria are too high. Drop back to the last successful level. Re-establish success. Then raise criteria by a smaller increment.

## Anti-Pattern 4: Using Reward/Punishment Language

Pryor makes a strong case for abandoning the language of "reward" and "punishment" in favor of reinforcement terminology. Reward and punishment carry moral and emotional baggage that interferes with clear thinking about behavior.

**Why it fails:** "Reward" implies something earned or deserved. "Punishment" implies wrongdoing. Both carry judgments that get in the way of observing behavior objectively. A reward might not reinforce; a punishment might inadvertently reinforce.

**Case — The ice cream "reward":** Giving a child ice cream to make up for scolding is a reward that reinforces whatever happened before the ice cream — including the behavior that triggered the scolding. The parent's intention (comfort) and the effect (reinforcement of misbehavior) are disconnected.

**Case — The compliment that hurts:** Praise from the wrong person or for the wrong reason may actually be aversive. A teacher's praise might guarantee ridicule from classmates. The reinforcer must be reinforcing to THAT individual — not what you think should be reinforcing.

**Fix:** Use the definition: reinforcement increases behavior. If the behavior doesn't increase after your "reinforcer," you didn't reinforce. Change your approach. Let the behavior, not your intention, tell you what works.

## Anti-Pattern 5: Talking Instead of Training

When shaping human behavior, most people talk about it. "I'm going to reinforce you for doing X." This destroys the training. The subject becomes aware of being trained, which creates resistance.

**Why it fails:** Telling someone you're going to reinforce them is bribing or promising, not reinforcing. On learning of your plans, the person may rebel immediately and escalate misbehavior. Training should be nonverbal — it's behavior you're changing, not ideas.

**Case — The messy roommate:** If you tell your roommate "I'm going to reinforce you for putting laundry in the hamper," the roommate will likely throw their clothes on the floor out of spite. The right approach: quietly put a hamper where clothes tend to accumulate. Occasionally express appreciation when clothes appear in the hamper. The behavior changes without the person knowing they're being trained.

**Case — The bragging trainer:** Pryor warns that trainers who succeed in shaping someone's behavior must never brag about it. "Wise parents never go around talking about what a good job they did raising their kids. The kids deserve the credit." If you reveal that you shaped someone, you create resentment that can undo the changes.

**Fix:** Shape silently. Never announce your training plan. The behavior changes are real — the subject doesn't need to know how they happened.

## Anti-Pattern 6: Assuming Verbal Communication Is Necessary

The biggest cultural bias in training: we assume learning requires language. In fact, much of the most effective training happens nonverbally. The subject doesn't need to understand why they're doing something — they just need to feel the reinforcement.

**Why it fails:** Verbal instructions bypass the behavioral learning system. The subject can "know" what to do without being able to do it. Behavioral learning happens through experience, not explanation.

**Case — The intellectually trained who can't perform:** An athlete who understands exactly what to do but can't execute under pressure. A musician who knows music theory but can't play. The knowledge is verbal; the skill is behavioral. Training must address behavior, not knowledge.

**Case — Ron Schusterman the psychologist:** A psychologist who studied animal behavior rashly claimed he "could not be trained." Pryor's colleagues shaped him to walk around with his hands clenched behind his back — a behavior he performed without awareness. When the room burst into applause, he realized he HAD been trained. The lesson: training happens at a behavioral level regardless of intellectual awareness.
