# Anti-Patterns: Common Training Mistakes

This reference documents the mistakes Karen Pryor identifies as most common — and most damaging — in attempts to change behavior.

---

## Anti-Pattern 1: Punishment as First Resort

**The problem**: When behavior goes wrong, the first instinct is punishment. Scold, spank, dock pay, yell. This is humanity's favorite method, and it almost never works long-term.

**Why it fails**: Punishment teaches avoidance, not correct behavior. The subject learns to avoid getting caught, not to stop the behavior. It damages the relationship. It requires perfect timing and consistency (which almost no one achieves). And it escalates — when a small punishment doesn't work, we use a bigger one, creating a spiral of increasing aversives.

**What to do instead**: Before punishing, ask: "What behavior do I want instead?" Then reinforce that. Use the eight methods systematically — punishment is Method 2 of 8, and usually not the best choice.

**Book case — The bad report card**: Parents punish by taking away privileges. The next report card is also bad, so they take away more. The spiral continues. Meanwhile, the child hasn't learned better study habits (replacement behavior). The real problem — perhaps a learning disability, bullying, or lack of study skills — goes unaddressed.

**Book case — Prison**: Pryor notes that prison is the ultimate punishment method. Yet recidivism rates prove it doesn't work. Prisoners don't learn new behaviors; they just wait out their sentence. The behavior (theft, violence, etc.) is likely to recur.

## Anti-Pattern 2: Delayed Reinforcement

**The problem**: Reinforcement must be immediate — within one second of the behavior. Most people reinforce behaviors long after they occur ("You did such a good job on that project last month"), which has almost no effect.

**Why it fails**: The brain connects the reinforcer to whatever is happening AT THAT MOMENT. If you praise a child for last week's behavior, the brain associates the praise with whatever the child is doing right now — watching TV.

**What to do instead**: Catch behavior in the act. Praise the moment it happens. For delayed delivery (e.g., you can't give a dolphin a fish mid-jump), use a marker signal (clicker, whistle, "Yes!") that bridges the gap.

**Book case — Dolphin training with markers**: When a dolphin jumps, the trainer can't deliver a fish mid-air. The whistle marks the exact moment of the correct behavior. The dolphin learns to associate the whistle with the fish seconds later. The marker signal solves the timing problem.

**Book case — The "good job" that means nothing**: Pryor describes parents who tell children "You did a good job today" at bedtime — hours after the actual behavior. The child doesn't know what behavior is being reinforced. The parent might as well have said nothing.

## Anti-Pattern 3: Inconsistency

**The problem**: Sporadic reinforcement of unwanted behavior is the most powerful way to maintain it. Variable ratio schedules (unpredictable reinforcement) are the most resistant to extinction.

If you sometimes give in to a tantrum, you've created a powerful training schedule: the child learns to keep tantruming because "maybe this time I'll get what I want."

**What to do instead**: Be consistent. If you decide to extinguish a behavior, never reinforce it again. Every reinforcement during extinction resets the clock.

**Book case — The intermittent tantrum**: A child who tantrums for candy in the grocery store. The parent gives in one time in five. The child has learned on a variable ratio schedule — the most durable schedule in existence. The tantrum behavior will be incredibly hard to eliminate.

**Book case — The barking dog**: If the dog barks to go outside, and you sometimes let it out and sometimes don't, the dog learns "maybe if I bark longer, it will work." The barking becomes persistent. A consistent rule (bark = ignored, sit quietly = door opens) is more effective.

## Anti-Pattern 4: Assuming You Know What's Reinforcing

**The problem**: We project our own preferences onto others. We assume praise is reinforcing (for some people it's embarrassing). We assume money is reinforcing (for some people it's not worth the hassle). We assume a specific treat is reinforcing (the animal may be full, or dislike it).

**The error**: Reinforcers are defined empirically. If the behavior doesn't increase, it wasn't reinforcing.

**What to do instead**: Observe. Test different reinforcers. Ask (if possible). Watch for what the subject chooses when given options.

**Book case — The Wall Street lawyer**: Pryor describes how even tough Wall Street lawyers respond to specific, well-timed reinforcement. But the reinforcer isn't generic praise — it's acknowledgment of a specific contribution. One lawyer might respond to public recognition; another prefers private acknowledgment.

**Book case — The pushy dog**: A dog that jumps on visitors may be seeking attention (even scolding is attention). The owner thinks yelling is punishment. The dog experiences it as reinforcement. The dog's behavior tells you what's really reinforcing: if jumping increases, the "punishment" is actually reinforcement.

## Anti-Pattern 5: Over-Correction and Nagging

**The problem**: When a behavior doesn't change immediately, the natural response is to increase pressure. Nag more. Punish harder. Threaten bigger consequences. This escalates without solving the problem.

**The cost**: Nagging damages relationships. The subject becomes resentful. The nagging itself becomes the problem — the subject stops hearing the message and just hears the noise.

**What to do instead**: If the behavior isn't changing, change your approach. Maybe the reinforcers are wrong. Maybe the criteria are too high. Maybe the motivation needs to change. Nagging is a sign that you haven't found the right method.

**Book case — The messy roommate**: Nagging doesn't work. The roommate may become passive-aggressive, deliberately leaving messes to assert independence. The better approach: reinforce clean-ups when they happen (even rarely), create an environment where clean is easy (more hampers), or address the underlying cause (depression? stress?).

**Book case — The surly bus driver**: Arguing with a surly bus driver is pointless. You won't change their behavior in 30 seconds. The best approach is extinction (ignore the surliness) and report the behavior to the company later. Nagging only reinforces the driver's negative attention-seeking.

## Anti-Pattern 6: Forgetting the "Incompatible Behavior" Option

**The problem**: Most people try to eliminate unwanted behavior directly (punishment, yelling, removal) without considering replacement. They focus on what they don't want rather than what they do want.

**The solution**: Identify a behavior that cannot coexist with the unwanted behavior and reinforce that instead.

**Book case — The dog greeting guests**: Instead of yelling at the dog for jumping on visitors, teach the dog to sit when people enter. Sitting and jumping are incompatible. The dog can't do both. The owner reinforces the sit. The jumping disappears without any punishment.

**Book case — The interrupting child**: Instead of punishing interruptions, teach the child to say "excuse me" and wait. The incompatible behavior (polite interruption) replaces the unwanted one (rude interruption). The reinforcement comes in the form of getting attention for the polite request.
