# Seven Principles in Depth

## Overview

Pryor's framework works because it's built on fundamental laws of behavior that apply across species. These seven principles translate the science of reinforcement into practical tools for everyday life.

## Principle 1: Positive Reinforcement Beats Punishment Every Time

Most people default to punishment — yelling, scolding, timeouts, fines — because it creates immediate suppression of behavior. But suppression is not elimination. Punished behavior returns when the punisher is absent, and punishers create side effects.

**Why it works:** Reinforcement builds behavior from the ground up. The subject wants to perform because the behavior produces good results. Punishment creates a subject who wants to avoid the punisher — and that desire often manifests as aggression, fear, or sneakiness.

**Case — The scolded dog that chews shoes when alone:** The dog learns not to chew shoes when the owner is present (punishment) but chews shoes when alone (no punishment). The behavior hasn't changed — only the circumstances under which it's performed. Positive reinforcement for chewing appropriate toys would eliminate the shoe-chewing permanently.

**Case — The crabby boss:** An executive known for criticizing employees created an environment of fear and minimal risk-taking. When coached to reinforce small positive initiatives with acknowledgment, the team's output increased dramatically. The same manager achieved better results without the side effects.

## Principle 2: Shape Behavior in Small Steps

The biggest mistake in training is asking for too much too soon. A tennis student can't hit a perfect serve on day one. A puppy can't walk perfectly on a leash. Break the end goal into tiny achievable steps and reinforce each one.

**Why it works:** Small steps ensure the learner always has a realistic chance for reinforcement. Success builds confidence and motivation. Failure creates frustration and avoidance.

**Case — Training a chicken to dance:** Pryor's example of shaping a chicken to spin in circles is the clearest illustration. Start with any movement to the left. Reinforce. Gradually require more rotation. At each step, the chicken can easily earn the reinforcer. Within an hour, the chicken is spinning. The same principle applies to teaching a child to tie shoes, an employee to write reports, or yourself to exercise regularly.

**Case — The high school Training Game:** When a trainer raised criteria too fast, the "animal" — a human volunteer — became frustrated and angry. The trainer had to "go back to kindergarten" and review with easily earned reinforcers. The class learned: criteria too high = frustration. Small steps = progress.

## Principle 3: Timing Is the Only Thing That Matters

Reinforcement must occur in the instant the behavior is taking place. One second of delay and you may reinforce a different behavior entirely. This is why clicker training is so powerful — the click can be delivered at the exact millisecond.

**Why it works:** The brain connects events that happen close together in time. If reinforcement is delayed, the brain connects it to whatever is happening at the moment of delivery — not to the behavior you wanted to reinforce.

**Case — The Training Game timing failure:** When the trainer blew the whistle just as the subject turned away from the target, the subject learned to turn away. Everyone watching could see the cause and effect: bad timing created bad behavior. The fix: blow the whistle while the movement is occurring, not after.

**Case — The light switch dilemma:** Beth tried to shape a subject to turn on a light switch. The subject had his hands in his pockets and couldn't get them out. Beth couldn't wait for the hands to come out — nothing happened. She changed tactics: she shaped the subject to rub his back against the wall near the switch, eventually making contact.

## Principle 4: Extinction First — Stop Feeding the Behavior

Before trying any active method, consider extinction: stop reinforcing the unwanted behavior. If you feed a behavior with attention, reaction, or engagement, your first step should be to stop feeding it.

**Why it works:** Behavior that no longer produces reinforcement will eventually disappear. The challenge: extinction burst — behavior initially gets worse before it gets better. Most people give up during the burst, inadvertently training persistence.

**Case — The arguing participant:** At an est training session, one participant argued relentlessly. The group's silence provided zero reinforcement for arguing. When he finally stopped, the group's applause reinforced silence. The behavior changed without a single word of confrontation.

**Case — The interrupting lawyer:** A Wall Street senior partner who interrupted colleagues was ignored when he interrupted. When he let someone finish, he received attention. Interrupting decreased. The key: the team had to be consistent. One slip — one interruption that got attention — would undo progress.

## Principle 5: Use Incompatible Behaviors for Quick Wins

The most elegant solution to unwanted behavior is to train a behavior that physically cannot coexist with the problem. It's a positive approach — you're teaching something new rather than eliminating something old.

**Why it works:** Instead of fighting the unwanted behavior, you replace it. The subject doesn't have to stop doing X; they just start doing Y, and X naturally disappears.

**Case — Dog barking at the door:** Instead of scolding the dog for barking, teach "go to your mat" with reinforcement. When the doorbell rings, send the dog to the mat. The dog can't bark and lie on the mat simultaneously. Barking disappears.

**Case — Child interrupting:** Instead of telling a child "don't interrupt," teach "put your hand on my arm when you need me." The child uses the physical signal instead of speaking. Both behaviors (hand on arm and waiting) are incompatible with interrupting.

## Principle 6: Put Problem Behaviors on Cue

Make the unwanted behavior a controlled "trick." If you can cue it, you can control it. This technique works for behaviors that are difficult to eliminate but easy to manage.

**Why it works:** The behavior becomes part of your training repertoire rather than a problem to fight. The subject gets to perform the behavior on request, which satisfies the need without causing disruption.

**Case — Dog that jumps on people:** Teach "jump up" as a cued behavior. The dog learns to jump only when told. Then you can ask for a sit (incompatible) when greeting people.

**Case — Cat that gets on the kitchen counter:** Instead of constantly chasing the cat off, teach "counter" as a cue. Now you can control when the cat jumps up — and you can make the counter less interesting by only having the cat jump up when you choose.

## Principle 7: Variable Reinforcement Creates Unbreakable Habits

Continuous reinforcement (rewarding every time) establishes behavior quickly. But to make behavior permanent, switch to variable reinforcement — sometimes reinforcing, sometimes not. The subject can't predict when the next reinforcer will come.

**Why it works:** Variable schedules create behavior that is resistant to extinction. The subject keeps trying because "this might be the time." This is why slot machines are addictive — and why the same principle can make good habits unshakable.

**Case — Gambling and variable schedules:** Slot machines pay out on a variable schedule. The gambler never knows when the next win will come. This unpredictability creates the strongest possible behavioral persistence. The same mechanics that make gambling addictive can make exercise, study, or work habits permanent.

**Case — The living statues horses:** Circus horses trained to hold poses for increasingly longer periods were maintained on a variable schedule. The horse never knew when the next treat would come, so it kept holding the pose. The audience saw motionlessness; the trainer saw variable reinforcement at work.
