# The Eight Untraining Methods

This reference provides a detailed treatment of each of the eight methods for eliminating unwanted behavior, with Pryor's commentary and book cases.

---

## Method 1: Shoot the Animal

**What it is**: Remove the subject or physically prevent the behavior.

**When it works**: When the behavior is dangerous or the relationship is unsalvageable. Capital punishment, firing an employee, divorcing a spouse, sending a child to their room, tying the dog up.

**Why it's problematic**: It teaches the subject nothing. The behavior will likely recur if the restraint is removed. No learning happens during restraint.

**Book case — The urinating cat**: Pryor describes a cat that developed a habit of urinating on stove burners overnight. No deterrent worked. She never caught the behavior happening. She couldn't decipher the motivation. After trying everything, she took the cat to the pound. Method 1 was the only practical solution.

**Book case — The sheep-killing dog**: Pryor notes that in some cases — a dog that kills sheep, for example — Method 1 (putting the dog down) is the worldwide standard and often the only realistic option. The behavior is so ingrained and dangerous that training is not practical.

---

## Method 2: Punishment

**What it is**: Adding an aversive consequence following the behavior.

**Why it's popular**: It's humanity's favorite method. When something goes wrong, we think first of punishment. Scold, spank, dock pay, fine, torture, invade.

**Why it usually fails**:
- It must be immediate (rarely is)
- It must be consistent (rarely is)
- It creates avoidance of the punisher, not cessation of the behavior
- Subjects learn to do the behavior when punishment is unlikely
- It escalates — when a small punishment doesn't work, we use a bigger one
- It damages relationships

**Book case — The bad report card**: Parents punish a bad report card by taking away the bike. The next report card is also bad, so they take away the skateboard too. Escalation. But the real problem — the learning difficulty, the lack of motivation, the social issues — goes unaddressed. Punishment treats the symptom, not the cause.

**Book case — Prison as punishment**: Pryor notes that prison doesn't actually reform criminal behavior. It merely prevents it temporarily. "We know that very often that is far from the case; all we can be sure of is that he cannot rip off your TV while he is locked up."

---

## Method 3: Negative Reinforcement

**What it is**: Removing an aversive stimulus when the desired behavior occurs.

**The classic example**: A car that buzzes when you don't buckle your seatbelt. The buzzer stops when you buckle up. The behavior (buckling) is reinforced by the removal of the aversive (buzzer).

**Drawbacks**: The subject has to be exposed to the aversive in the first place. The subject learns to escape or avoid, but the relationship is damaged.

**Book case — Shock collars**: A shock collar that delivers a painful shock when the dog barks, and stops when the dog stops barking, is negative reinforcement. The dog learns to stop barking to avoid the shock. But the dog also learns to associate the owner's presence with possible pain. Trust is damaged.

---

## Method 4: Extinction

**What it is**: Withholding the reinforcer that maintains the behavior.

**How it works**: All behavior that continues is being reinforced somehow. Find the reinforcer and remove it. The behavior will eventually stop.

**The extinction burst**: Behavior often increases dramatically before it decreases. The subject "tries harder" to get the reinforcer. This is the critical moment — if you give in, you've reinforced persistence.

**Book case — The whining child**: A child whines for candy. If parents give in one time in ten, the child has been reinforced for persistence (variable ratio schedule — the most resistant to extinction). The correct approach: never reinforce whining. It will get worse before it stops (extinction burst), but it will stop.

**Book case — The pushy bus driver**: Pryor describes ignoring a surly bus driver's rudeness. The driver's behavior (being surly) is often maintained by getting a reaction from passengers. Withholding that reaction (extinction) is more effective than arguing.

---

## Method 5: Train an Incompatible Behavior

**What it is**: Teach a behavior that cannot coexist with the unwanted behavior.

**The beauty**: Instead of trying to eliminate a behavior, you replace it with something better. This is the most positive of the "active" methods.

**Book case — The scratching cat**: Instead of punishing the cat for scratching the sofa, provide an attractive scratching post and reinforce using it. Scratching the post and scratching the sofa are incompatible — the cat can't do both at once.

**Book case — The messy roommate**: Instead of fighting about the laundry, throw in a load and do it together. The roommate learns to keep laundry contained because the contained approach is reinforced. Doing laundry and not doing laundry are incompatible.

---

## Method 6: Put the Behavior on Cue

**What it is**: Bring the unwanted behavior under stimulus control, then never give the cue.

**This is the dolphin trainer's most elegant method.** If you can get the behavior on cue, you control when it happens. If you never give the cue, it never happens.

**Book case — Dolphin splashing**: If a dolphin splashes water at the trainer (annoying but not dangerous), the trainer can put splashing on cue. The dolphin learns to splash only when the signal is given. When the signal is never given, the dolphin stops splashing. The dolphin is not frustrated — it's a trained behavior that simply isn't called for.

**Book case — The barking dog**: Train the dog to "speak" on cue. Then train "quiet" as a separate behavior. Now you control the barking. You can use "quiet" before the barking even starts.

---

## Method 7: Shape the Absence

**What it is**: Reinforce everything that is NOT the unwanted behavior.

**Kind and effective**: You don't punish the bad; you reward everything else. The subject gradually does more of the good stuff.

**Book case — The grumpy relative**: Instead of fighting about the grumpiness, reinforce every moment of pleasant interaction. A smile, a kind word, a helpful gesture — each gets reinforcement. The grumpiness receives no attention and undergoes extinction. Over time, pleasant behavior becomes more frequent.

**Book case — The noisy kids in the car**: Instead of yelling at the kids for being noisy (which is attention and therefore reinforcing), silently ignore the noise and immediately pay attention when they're quiet for even a moment. The quiet moments get longer over time.

---

## Method 8: Change the Motivation

**What it is**: Address the root cause. Change the environment so the unwanted behavior no longer serves a purpose.

**This is the most fundamental and kindest method.** If you understand why the behavior happens, you can remove the reason for it.

**Book case — The barking dog (root cause)**: A dog that barks all night may be lonely, bored, under-exercised, or fearful. Give the dog more exercise, company, and a comfortable sleeping area. The barking stops because the dog's needs are met. Much kinder than a shock collar.

**Book case — The cat on the table**: Cats jump on tables because they're high and warm. Provide an equally appealing alternative — a cat tree by a window, a heated bed. Or move the bird feeder away from that window so the cat can't see outdoor activity from the table. Change the environment, and the behavior changes naturally.

---

## Choosing a Method

Pryor emphasizes: you are the trainer. You must decide. The choice depends on:
- Urgency (is the behavior dangerous?)
- Relationship (can you afford to damage it?)
- Time (how long do you have?)
- Subject (what works for a dolphin may not work for a child)
- Your values (how do you want to train?)
