# Anti-Patterns: Misunderstanding Evolution

> Source: Climbing Mount Improbable, Chapters 5, 7-8

## The "Irreducible Complexity" Fallacy

The argument that a system needs all parts at once to function, so it couldn't have evolved step by step. Dawkins refutes this by showing parts serve different functions in intermediate stages.

> **Case: The Bacterial Flagellum** (Chapter 5): Creationists often cite the flagellum as irreducibly complex. Dawkins shows that a subset of its proteins forms the Type III secretion system — a simpler structure used by bacteria to inject toxins. Parts were co-opted, not created from scratch.

> **Case: The Eye** (Chapter 1): Light-sensitive patch → cup eye → pinhole eye → lens eye. Each stage is useful on its own. The human eye is not irreducible — it has blind spots and imperfections that a real designer would have avoided.

> **Key takeaway**: "Irreducible complexity" fails because parts can have different functions in intermediate forms. Evolution is a tinkerer, not an engineer.

## The Argument from Design

"This looks designed, so it must have a designer."

> **Case: The Pitcher Plant** (Chapter 1): The pitcher plant is a modified leaf that evolved gradually. Other plants show the intermediate stages between flat leaf and deep, insect-trapping pitcher.

> **Key takeaway**: The design argument fails because we can observe and reconstruct the intermediate steps.

## Confusing Chance with Selection

Mutations are random. Selection is not. Cumulative selection creates order, not chaos.
