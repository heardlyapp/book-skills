# Reference: The Brain as Prediction Machine

## Overview
Hawkins's central insight: the brain is not a computer that processes information — it is a prediction machine that constantly anticipates what it expects to perceive.

## Case: The Prediction Loop
The brain's prediction loop: 1) The neocortex predicts what it expects to see/hear/feel next. 2) The prediction is compared to actual sensory input. 3) If the prediction is correct, the input adds little new information. 4) If the prediction is wrong, an error signal drives learning. Most of what you perceive is your brain's best guess — only the errors are new information.

## Case: The Visual System Example
Vision is not passive. Your brain predicts the visual scene before you see it. When you walk into a familiar room, your brain has already predicted what you will see. That is why you notice a new object but do not consciously perceive things that have not changed. You see what you expect to see.

## Case: The Role of Movement
Movement is essential for perception. Moving your eyes, head, or body changes the sensory input, allowing the brain to test its predictions. If you sit perfectly still and stare straight ahead, the sensory input is constant, and the prediction error is low. Movement is the brain's way of generating new data to test its models.

## Case: The Common Algorithm
All sensory modalities — vision, hearing, touch, language, music — run on the same neocortical algorithm. The difference is not the processing but the input. The same computational principles that allow you to recognize a face allow you to recognize a melody, a sentence, or a texture.
