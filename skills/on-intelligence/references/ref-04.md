# Reference: Intelligence and AI

## Overview
Hawkins's theory has profound implications for artificial intelligence. He argues that current AI is not on the path to general intelligence.

## Case: Current AI vs. Brain-Based AI
Deep learning excels at pattern matching but does not learn sequences, make predictions in the brain's way, or build world models. Hawkins argues that deep learning is a useful tool but not a path to AGI. Brain-based AI requires systems that learn continuously, make predictions, and build models using principles derived from the neocortex.

## Case: HTM for AI
Hierarchical Temporal Memory (HTM) is Hawkins's attempt to implement the brain's algorithm in software. HTM systems learn sequences, detect anomalies, and make predictions. They have been applied to anomaly detection in data centers, stock market prediction, and robotics. HTM is a proof of concept that the brain's algorithm can be implemented computationally.

## Case: The Future of AGI
Hawkins predicts that general intelligence will emerge from systems that embody the principles of the neocortex: sequence learning, hierarchical memory, sparse representations, and prediction as the primary function. The timeline is uncertain, but the principles are clear. AGI will not be achieved by making deep learning models bigger — it will require a fundamentally different architecture.

## Case: SDRs in AI
Sparse distributed representations are one of Hawkins's key contributions. SDRs are noise-resistant and handle overlapping concepts naturally. They could be the representation format that bridges the gap between current AI and brain-based intelligence.
