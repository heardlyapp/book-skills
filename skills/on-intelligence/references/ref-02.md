# Reference: The Cortical Algorithm

## Overview
The neocortex runs a single algorithm. Hawkins's Hierarchical Temporal Memory (HTM) theory describes how it works.

## Case: Learning Sequences
The cortex learns by storing sequences of patterns. A sequence of words forms a sentence. A sequence of movements forms a dance. A sequence of notes forms a melody. The brain stores these sequences as patterns of connections between neurons. When a sequence is partially observed, the brain recalls the rest.

## Case: Invariant Representations
How do you recognize a face regardless of angle, lighting, or expression? The brain learns invariant representations by observing sequences of changing inputs. As you move your head, the image on your retina changes. The brain learns that these different images represent the same object. Invariant representations emerge from movement.

## Case: Sparse Distributed Representations
The brain encodes concepts using SDRs: a small fraction of a large population of neurons. An SDR for "apple" activates a specific small set of neurons. SDRs are mathematical: they overlap, allowing the brain to represent similarity. Two things that are similar (apple and orange) have more overlapping SDRs than two things that are different (apple and shoe).

## Case: Hierarchy of Memory
The neocortex is hierarchically organized. Lower levels learn short sequences of simple patterns (edges, tones). Higher levels learn long sequences of complex patterns (faces, melodies, concepts). The hierarchy allows the brain to learn at multiple timescales simultaneously.
