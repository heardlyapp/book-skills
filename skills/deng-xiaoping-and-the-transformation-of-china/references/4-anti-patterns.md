## 6 Anti-Patterns of Reform Failure

### 1. "Ideology Comes First"

Deng's success came from putting results before ideology. Leaders who prioritize doctrinal purity over practical outcomes fail.

### 2. "Reform Everything at Once"

Trying to change everything simultaneously creates chaos. Deng sequenced his reforms carefully.

### 3. "Ignore the Opposition"

Deng did not ignore conservatives — he compromised with them, delayed reforms they could not accept, and moved forward where he could. Managing opposition is essential.

> **Case: The Conservative Backlash** (Chapter 17): After the initial reforms, conservative leaders pushed back against "spiritual pollution" and "bourgeois liberalization." Deng did not fire them — he slowed down, waited, and then moved forward again when the moment was right.
> **Key takeaway**: Reform is not a straight line. Sometimes you must slow down to ultimately move faster.

### 4. "The Leader Knows Everything"

Deng did not pretend to know everything. He experimented, learned, and adjusted. The SEZs were experiments — some worked better than others.

### 5. "Change Without Stability"

Economic reform requires political stability. Deng maintained party control even while liberalizing the economy. Reform without stability is revolution.

### 6. "Transform Without Talent"

Deng invested heavily in education and technical training. He understood that transformation requires capable people, not just good policies.

> **Case: The Great Leap Forward vs. Deng's Reform** (Chapter 1): Mao's Great Leap Forward (1958-61) was ideologically pure — rapid collectivization, backyard steel furnaces, communes. It caused a famine that killed millions. Deng's approach was the opposite: practical, experimental, gradual. The comparison proves the power of pragmatism.
> **Key takeaway**: The worst disasters come from ideological purity. The best results come from practical experimentation.
