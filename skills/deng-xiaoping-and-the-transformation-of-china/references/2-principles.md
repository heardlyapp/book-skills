## 7 Principles of Deng's Leadership

### 1. Resilience Under Pressure

Deng was purged three times — each time he came back stronger. Purge → Exile → Comeback. The pattern defined his life.

### 2. Pragmatism Over Ideology

The "black cat, white cat" philosophy: results matter more than doctrinal purity. Deng's willingness to experiment with capitalism within a communist system is his signature.

> **Case: The SEZ Experiment** (Chapter 12): When Deng proposed Special Economic Zones in 1979, conservative leaders were horrified — they looked capitalist. Deng's response: "Let's try them and see." The SEZs succeeded beyond expectations. Shenzhen grew from a fishing village to a city of millions.
> **Key takeaway**: Experimentation before commitment. Deng tested new policies on a small scale before expanding them.

### 3. Strategic Sequencing

Deng did not reform everything at once. First agriculture, then industry. First countryside, then cities. First economy, then (never) politics. The sequence reduced disruption.

### 4. Know When to Compromise

Deng compromised with the US on Taiwan, with the UK on Hong Kong, with conservatives on the pace of reform. He was a master of the art of the possible.

### 5. Build a Legacy Team

Deng groomed successors (Hu Yaobang, Zhao Ziyang, Jiang Zemin). He knew his reforms would outlast him. Building institutions and training successors is essential.

### 6. Accept Unintended Consequences

The economic reforms created inequality, corruption, and environmental damage. Deng acknowledged these costs — but argued that the benefits outweighed them.

### 7. Maintain Order

Deng believed that economic reform required political stability. The Tiananmen crackdown was consistent with this principle. Whether one agrees with it or not, the logic was: reform cannot happen in chaos.

> **Case: The Hong Kong Negotiation** (Chapter 18): Deng negotiated the return of Hong Kong with Margaret Thatcher in 1982. He was flexible on the details (one country, two systems) but firm on the principle (Hong Kong must return). The compromise preserved Hong Kong's economic system while satisfying China's sovereignty.
> **Key takeaway**: Compromise on means, not ends. Deng was flexible about how to achieve his goals — but never wavered on the goals themselves.
