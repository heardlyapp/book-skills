# Techniques: Storytelling, Novelty, Jaw-Dropping Moments, and Multisensory Design

## Storytelling Framework (Chapter 2)

The book identifies three types of stories that TED speakers use:

| Type | Best for | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Personal story** | Building trust and emotional connection | "Why I care about this topic" |
| **Third-party story** | Making abstract concepts concrete | "I met someone whose life changed when..." |
| **Brand/customer story** | Business persuasion | "A customer told me..." |

**The pathos-logos combination**: "Stories are just data with a soul." The most persuasive talks combine emotional narrative (pathos) with evidence (logos). Leading with story humanizes the data.

> **Case: Steven Johnson's "Where Good Ideas Come From"** (Chapter 2): Author Steven Johnson opened his TED talk with a story about how he would wake up, work, walk, and have epiphanies during his walks — not at his desk. He used this personal story to introduce the science of the "slow hunch": that great ideas don't arrive in a flash but emerge over time through incubation.
> **Key takeaway**: The personal anecdote made the cognitive science accessible and memorable. The story IS the data's delivery system.

**The 30-second rule**: You have 30 seconds to hook the audience. Open with a story, a provocative question, or a surprising fact. Never open with "Hello, my name is..." or an agenda slide.

## Novelty Injection (Chapter 4)

"Novelty is the single most effective way to capture a person's attention." The technique is straightforward: before every presentation, ask yourself one question:

> **"What will my audience learn from me that they couldn't get from Google?"**

If the answer is nothing, your talk needs fundamental restructuring.

**Techniques for novelty**:
1. **Reveal a fresh statistic** — A fact that recontextualizes a known problem
2. **Introduce an unfamiliar expert** — A researcher or case study your audience hasn't heard of
3. **Challenge conventional wisdom** — "Everything you know about X is wrong"
4. **Bring your audience into an unfamiliar world** — Take them somewhere they've never been (Robert Ballard took TED underwater)

> **Case: Robert Ballard's Ocean Revelation** (Chapter 4): The deep-sea explorer who discovered the Titanic told TED: "50% of the United States lies beneath the sea." "The greatest mountain range on Earth is underwater." "Most of our planet is in eternal darkness." Every fact was a revelation.
> **Key takeaway**: Don't just inform — reveal. The audience should feel like they've discovered something, not just learned it.

## Jaw-Dropping Moment Design (Chapter 5)

A jaw-dropping moment is one carefully designed element that becomes the memory anchor of your entire talk. "These moments are not accidental. They are carefully, consciously designed and rehearsed."

**Design process**:
1. Identify your single most powerful data point, demonstration, or argument
2. Strip away ALL supporting context — deliver it raw
3. Design the build-up: pause, eye contact, then deliver
4. Let the moment breathe — silence after the reveal
5. Anchor it: repeat it, reference it, close with it

## Multisensory Techniques (Chapter 8)

**Slide design rules**:
- One image per slide
- Max 5 words per slide
- No bullet points
- Images should be metaphorical, not literal

**Vivid language toolkit**:
- Replace abstractions with concrete objects ("The numbers climbed like a rocket")
- Use sensory verbs (glimmered, thundered, dissolved) instead of analytical verbs (increased, decreased, correlated)
- Describe one specific scene rather than summarizing a general trend

**The rule of three for memory**:
- People remember groups of three
- Structure your three main points as a repeating pattern
- End each section by returning to the same phrase or image
