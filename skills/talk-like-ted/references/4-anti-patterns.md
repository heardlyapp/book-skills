# Anti-Patterns: Common Mistakes Speakers Make

## 1. The Data Dump

**The mistake**: Presenting raw data, charts, and tables without a narrative frame. The speaker assumes that if the information is important, the audience will pay attention.

**Why it fails**: The brain does not process raw data emotionally. Without emotional engagement, information goes into short-term memory and disappears within minutes. "Stories are just data with a soul."

**Fix**: For every data point, ask: "What story does this tell? Whose life does this change?" Lead with the story, support with the data.

> **Case: Hans Rosling's Data Storytelling** (Chapter 2): Statistician Hans Rosling became one of TED's most popular speakers not by showing dry spreadsheets, but by animating global health data with bouncing bubbles that transformed statistics into a compelling narrative about global development.
> **Key takeaway**: Even the most data-heavy presentation can be compelling if the data tells a story. The format matters as much as the content.

## 2. Death by Bullet Points

**The mistake**: Using slides as speaker notes — full text, bullet points, and data tables projected on screen. The speaker reads from the slides.

**Why it fails**: "PowerPoint slides shouldn't be documents projected on a screen." When an audience reads and listens at the same time, the brain's processing capacity is split — neither channel works well. The audience scans the slide and tunes out the speaker.

**Fix**: One image per slide. No more than 5 words. Use slides for emotional impact, not information transfer.

## 3. The Corporate Voice

**The mistake**: Speaking in jargon, passive voice, and generic corporate language. "Our company leverages synergistic solutions to maximize stakeholder value."

**Why it fails**: The brain cannot process abstract, multi-syllabic language as quickly as concrete, simple language. Abstract language also signals inauthenticity — the audience doesn't feel they're talking to a person.

**Fix**: Stay in your lane. Speak as yourself. Use the language you'd use in a conversation with a smart friend.

## 4. Forced Humor

**The mistake**: Opening with a joke that has nothing to do with the topic. "A funny thing happened on the way to the conference..." (audience groans).

**Why it fails**: Jokes are high-risk. They create pressure (will the audience laugh?), break the speaker's rhythm if they don't land, and often have nothing to do with the content.

**Fix**: The book's approach to humor is "Lighten Up" (Secret 6), not "Tell Jokes." Use surprise instead. Show a self-deprecating slide. Make an unexpected observation. Wit tied to content always works better than jokes disconnected from it.

## 5. The Never-Ending Talk

**The mistake**: "Since I have 30 minutes, I'll use all 30 minutes." Adding more content doesn't add more value.

**Why it fails**: Cognitive science proves the brain's attention span maxes at 18-20 minutes. Every minute beyond 18 reduces retention. More time = less impact.

**Fix**: The 18-minute rule is the target. If you can't say it in 18 minutes, you haven't found your core message yet.

> **Case: Bill Gates and the Mosquitoes** (Chapter 5): In one of TED's most famous jaw-dropping moments, Bill Gates released a jar of mosquitoes into the audience while giving a talk about malaria. "There's no reason only poor people should have the experience," he said. The audience gasped, then realized the mosquitoes were malaria-free. The moment became the most talked-about element of his entire presentation.
> **Key takeaway**: A single, well-designed moment is worth a thousand slides. The mosquito release was shocking, memorable, and perfectly tied to Gates' message about malaria eradication.

## 6. Telling Without Showing

**The mistake**: Describing a concept verbally without engaging the audience's senses. "Our product is innovative and user-friendly."

**Why it fails**: Abstract claims trigger skepticism. Concrete demonstrations trigger belief.

**Fix**: Use the multisensory approach. Show, don't tell. Use demonstrations, customer testimonials, role-playing, or visual metaphors to make the abstract concrete.
