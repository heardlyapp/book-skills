# Core Framework: The Yellow House — Narrative Structure and Themes

## The Four Movements

Broom structures her memoir as a musical composition in four movements, each creating a different emotional relationship to the house.

### Movement I: The World Before Me
**Emotional register**: Nostalgia, wonder, reconstruction
**The house**: Full, alive, the center of the world
**Time**: Before Sarah is born — 1961 through the 1970s

This movement reconstructs a world the author never directly experienced but absorbed through family stories. It establishes the Yellow House as a character and New Orleans East as a place worth knowing.

### Movement II: The Grieving House
**Emotional register**: Ambivalence, claustrophobia, impending loss
**The house**: Aging, sinking, too small
**Time**: 1970s through 1990s — Sarah's childhood and adolescence

The house is "grieving" because it already knows what's coming. This movement captures the tension between love for home and the need to escape.

### Movement III: Water
**Emotional register**: Chaos, terror, splintering
**The house**: Destroyed, flooded
**Time**: August-September 2005 — Hurricane Katrina

The most visceral section. Told through multiple family member voices. The water itself becomes a character.

### Movement IV: Do You Know What It Means?
**Emotional register**: Grief, erasure, endurance
**The house**: Gone. An empty lot.
**Time**: 2005 to the present

The aftermath. The long struggle to recover, to be recognized, to hold onto a place that no longer exists.

## Book Cases

**Case 1 — Ivory Mae's story**: The arc of Ivory Mae Broom's life is the arc of the book. She bought the house, filled it with children, defended it, lost it, and spent her final years in a FEMA trailer on the same spot. Her refusal to abandon the property is heartbreaking and heroic.

**Case 2 — The Avis map**: Broom describes a map from Avis Rent a Car that shaded the French Quarter in turquoise but literally cut off New Orleans East. The map is a physical artifact of erasure: her neighborhood, her home, reduced to blank space. This image recurs throughout the book as a central metaphor.

**Case 3 — Carl's vigil**: Sarah's brother Carl still sits on the empty lot, a skinny man in shorts, white socks pulled up to his knees, a gold front tooth. Friends visit with coolers of beer. The only structure on the lot is a bright blue porta potty from the City of New Orleans. Carl is "babysitting ruins."

## Key Metaphors

- **The house**: Not a building but a character, a body, a memory
- **Maps**: Who is included, who is erased, who decides
- **Water**: Both literal flood and the abstraction of erasure
- **The sinking ground**: New Orleans East was built on drained swampland — always precarious, always unstable
- **Yellow**: The color of the house, the color of divine clarity (Van Gogh), the color of memory
