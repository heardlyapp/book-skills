# The Key Characters and Their Relationships to the House

This reference profiles the main figures in *The Yellow House* and their connections to the central subject.

---

## Ivory Mae Broom — Sarah's Mother

The protagonist of the house's story. Ivory Mae bought the Yellow House in 1961 and spent the rest of her life in relationship with it — raising twelve children, maintaining the property (as best she could on limited means), and ultimately refusing to leave after Katrina.

**Her relationship to the house**: The house was her life's work. It represented her independence, her family, her place in the world. When the house was destroyed, a part of her was destroyed too.

**Book case — The FEMA trailer**: After Katrina, Ivory Mae returned to a gutted house. She moved into a FEMA trailer on the same lot. She stayed there for years. She told Sarah she "didn't want to start over" — she wanted to stay on the land that was hers.

---

## Sarah Broom — The Author

The narrator and guide. Sarah is the youngest of Ivory Mae's twelve children. She was born in 1977, after most of her siblings had already left home. The Yellow House was both her whole world and a place she needed to escape.

**Her relationship to the house**: Deeply ambivalent. She loved the Yellow House — its sounds, its smells, its particular way of being. But she also felt suffocated by it. She left New Orleans for college and never really returned. The book is her attempt to reconcile these feelings.

**Book case — Leaving and returning**: Broom writes about leaving New Orleans and building a life elsewhere. But the house kept calling her back. The memoir is itself an act of return — not to the physical house, which is gone, but to the memory of it.

---

## Carl (Rabbit) — Sarah's Brother

The guardian of the ruins. Carl still lives on the property, sitting on an ice chest, watching over the empty lot where the Yellow House used to be.

**His relationship to the house**: Unconditional loyalty. Carl never abandoned the Yellow House. He stayed before Katrina (working his maintenance job at NASA), stayed during (surviving on a bridge overpass), and stayed after (babysitting ruins).

**Book case — The vigil**: Years after Katrina, Carl can still be found on Wilson Avenue, sitting on an ice chest or at a pecan-colored dining table (the only surviving piece of furniture), holding court with friends. He is the living link between the Yellow House that was and the empty lot that remains.

---

## Simon Broom — Sarah's Father

Married Ivory Mae after she bought the house. Worked as a laborer at NASA's Michoud Assembly Facility in New Orleans East. Died when Sarah was very young.

**His relationship to the house**: He was the steadying force. After he died, the house began to decline more rapidly. His absence is felt throughout the book — a ghost in the Yellow House.

---

## The Twelve Siblings

The house held twelve children. The book weaves together many of their voices, particularly during the Katrina section. Each sibling experienced the house and the disaster differently.

**Book case — Michael's story**: Michael, one of Sarah's brothers, was stranded with fifteen people in the Lafitte Projects after Katrina. His account of foraging for food in chest-deep water, turning down rescue helicopters, and dousing in Listerine to mask the smell is one of the most visceral passages in the book.

**Book case — Troy's fear of flying**: After evacuation, Troy (another brother) had never flown before. On the flight to Texas, he chanted "Dammit, dammit, dammit" the entire time, his leg shaking furiously. A small, human detail amid the catastrophe.

---

## The Yellow House Itself

The true central character. The house is described with the same depth and detail as any human. It is not a setting — it is a protagonist.

**Its biography**:
- 1961: Purchased by Ivory Mae
- 1960s-1970s: Full of children, full of life
- 1980s-1990s: Aging, settling, sinking into the ground
- 2005: Flooded by Katrina. Gutted.
- Present: An empty lot on Wilson Avenue

**Its character**: Modest, resilient, too small but somehow enough. Painted yellow — the color of Broom's mother's choosing. A shotgun house, meaning one room deep, a direct line from front to back. The kind of house that forces intimacy.
