# Anti-Patterns: What the Memoir Critiques

This reference documents the systemic failures and wrongs that Broom's memoir exposes.

---

## Anti-Pattern 1: The Neglect of New Orleans East

**The problem**: New Orleans East was systematically excluded from the city's self-image. Tourist maps don't include it. City guides don't mention it. It is "a blank space on someone's mental map."

**Why it matters**: When a hurricane destroyed the neighborhood, there was no public outcry to rebuild it. The neighborhood that was already invisible became even more invisible. The recovery effort prioritized the French Quarter, the Garden District, and other tourist-facing areas.

**Book case — The Avis map**: The physical artifact of this neglect. A rental car map that literally cut off New Orleans East as if it didn't exist.

**Book case — The disaster bus tours**: After Katrina, tour buses drove tourists through devastated neighborhoods. Broom wonders whether those buses ever made it to her street.

---

## Anti-Pattern 2: The Failure of the Levees

**The problem**: The levees that broke during Katrina were known to be inadequate. They were designed for a Category 3 storm, not a Category 5. The federal government had been warned repeatedly.

**Why it matters**: Katrina was not a natural disaster. It was a man-made disaster layered on top of a natural one. The water that destroyed the Yellow House was not God's water — it was the government's water.

**Book case — The breached levee**: Broom traces the specific failure: the levee at the Industrial Canal, the floodwall that gave way, the water that rushed into New Orleans East and stayed for weeks.

---

## Anti-Pattern 3: The Bureaucratic Aftermath

**The problem**: After the flood, the survivors faced an impossible bureaucracy. Insurance companies denied claims. FEMA was overwhelmed. Rebuilding permits were tied up for years.

**Why it matters**: The bureaucracy was a second disaster. Many families simply gave up. Those who wanted to rebuild faced years of paperwork, inspections, and denials.

**Book case — Ivory Mae's FEMA trailer**: The most powerful symbol of this failure. A proud woman who owned her home for forty years, living in a temporary trailer on the ruins of that home, waiting for help that never came.

---

## Anti-Pattern 4: The Erasure of Working-Class Black Neighborhoods

**The problem**: New Orleans East was a predominantly Black, working-class neighborhood. After Katrina, there was a clear pattern: wealthier, whiter neighborhoods were rebuilt first. The neighborhoods that were already neglected before the storm were neglected after.

**Why it matters**: Reconstruction was not an even process. It revealed the city's priorities: tourist dollars over resident lives, property values over human dignity.

**Book case — The city's recovery plan**: New Orleans East was barely mentioned in the official recovery plans. Broom documents the meetings, the reports, the promises that went unfulfilled.

---

## Anti-Pattern 5: The "Blank Slate" Narrative

**The problem**: After Katrina, there was a narrative that New Orleans could start fresh — rebuild better, smarter, more beautifully. But a "blank slate" requires erasing what existed before.

**Why it matters**: The "blank slate" narrative denies the value of what was lost. It treats neighborhoods like New Orleans East as if they never existed and therefore didn't need to be rebuilt.

**Book case — The missing maps**: New Orleans East was never on the maps before Katrina. After Katrina, it was even easier to ignore — there was nothing left to map.

---

## Anti-Pattern 6: The Myth of "Natural" Disaster

**The problem**: Katrina is often described as a "natural disaster" — a hurricane that happened to hit a city. This framing obscures the human decisions that made the disaster so catastrophic: inadequate levees, delayed response, systemic neglect.

**Why it matters**: Calling Katrina a natural disaster absolves the people who failed. It was a disaster of race, class, and political neglect.

**Book case — The entire book**: The Yellow House exists to show that Katrina was not an act of God. It was the consequence of decades of decisions about who mattered and who didn't.
