# Voice, Application, and Why This Book Matters

This reference covers the broader significance of *The Yellow House* and what makes it a landmark work of nonfiction.

---

## Why the Yellow House Won the National Book Award

The 2019 National Book Award for Nonfiction went to *The Yellow House* because it does something rare: it tells a deeply specific story that resonates universally. The judges described it as "a book about love, loss, and the meaning of home."

**What makes it exceptional**:
- It personalizes a disaster that was often covered in statistics and headlines
- It gives voice to a neglected part of New Orleans
- It is both a family story and a political document
- The prose is extraordinary — sentence by sentence, it is a master class in literary nonfiction

---

## Applying the Lessons

Unlike the other books in this series, *The Yellow House* is not a how-to book. Its value is not in frameworks or techniques but in what it reveals about human experience.

**For memoir writers**:
- Study how Broom structures a narrative around a place, not a person
- Notice how she uses multiple voices to create a chorus
- Learn from her temporal layering — past and present coexisting

**For anyone interested in disaster and recovery**:
- Understand that "natural disasters" are never natural
- See how bureaucracy can be a second disaster
- Recognize that some neighborhoods are deemed worth saving and others are not

**For anyone who has lost a home**:
- The book validates the grief of losing a place
- It shows that the memory of a place can be as real as the place itself
- It demonstrates that writing can be a form of return

## The Yellow House in Context

**New Orleans East**: The neighborhood was built on drained cypress swamp land. It was developed in the 1960s as a suburban destination for Black families. At its peak, it was one of the largest concentrations of Black homeownership in the South. After Katrina, it was devastated.

**Hurricane Katrina (2005)**: The storm itself was not the worst-case scenario, but the levee failures were. 80% of New Orleans was flooded. 1,800 people died. The government response was widely condemned.

**New Orleans recovery**: The city that emerged after Katrina was smaller, whiter, and more expensive. Many displaced residents never returned. The rebuilding was uneven — tourist areas recovered quickly; residential neighborhoods struggled.

**Book case — The aftermath**: Broom documents the slow, painful aftermath with honesty. Some family members returned. Some didn't. The Yellow House was never rebuilt. But the story of it was written, and the act of writing became a form of rebuilding.

---

## The Radical Act of Paying Attention

Broom's ultimate achievement: she paid attention to a place that no one else was paying attention to. She mapped a place that had been unmapped. She wrote a house that had been erased.

**Book case — The final image**: The book ends not with the house, but with the act of memory. The Yellow House exists now as a story. It has been rebuilt in language if not in wood and nails.

---

## Who Should Read This Book

- Anyone interested in Hurricane Katrina beyond the headlines
- Writers and readers of literary nonfiction
- People who have lost a home or a place they loved
- Anyone interested in the intersection of race, class, and disaster
- New Orleanians, expats, and anyone who loves the city

## Related Works

- *The World Made Straight* by Jesmyn Ward — another memoir of place and loss in the Gulf South
- *Five Days at Memorial* by Sheri Fink — Katrina from the hospital perspective
- *Zeitoun* by Dave Eggers — another Katrina story from a personal perspective
- *The Poetics of Space* by Gaston Bachelard — a philosophical work about houses and memory that Broom explicitly references
