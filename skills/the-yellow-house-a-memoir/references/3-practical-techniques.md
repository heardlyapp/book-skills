# Literary Techniques: How Broom Builds the Memoir

This reference examines the specific craft techniques Sarah Broom uses in *The Yellow House*.

---

## Technique 1: The House as Character

The most striking literary choice: Broom writes about the Yellow House as if it were a living being. It has moods, sensations, memories. It "grieves." It "suffers."

**How she does it**: Personification applied not for a single passage but sustained across the entire book. The house is a body that ages, sags, and ultimately dies.

**Book case — The Grieving House**: Movement II is titled "The Grieving House" — the house itself is experiencing grief before anything has happened. It knows what's coming. This temporal displacement — the house grieving in advance — is a powerful narrative device.

---

## Technique 2: Choral Narration

The Katrina section is told through multiple voices — Carl, Michael, Ivory Mae, and others. Each sibling has their own chapter, their own perspective, their own version of survival.

**Effect**: The reader experiences the disaster not as a single narrative but as a splintering. There is no single "truth" of Katrina — there are as many truths as there were survivors.

**Book case — Multiple perspectives on the same event**: Carl's experience on the overpass, Michael's in the apartment, Ivory Mae's evacuation to Texas, Grandmother's bus ride to nowhere. Each is a fragment. Together, they create a mosaic of disaster.

---

## Technique 3: Maps as Metaphor

The book opens with epigraphs about maps from Kei Miller and Peter Turchi. Throughout, Broom returns to the idea of mapping — literal maps, mental maps, the maps that include and exclude.

**Effect**: Maps become a way of talking about power. Who decides what gets mapped? Who gets erased? The Avis map that cuts off New Orleans East is not an oversight — it's a political statement.

**Book case — The Avis map**: "On a detailed city map once given to me by Avis Rent a Car, the French Quarter has been shaded in light turquoise, magnified in a box at the bottom of the page. New Orleans East is cut off, a point beyond, a blank space on someone's mental map."

---

## Technique 4: The Specific as Universal

Broom never generalizes. She stays relentlessly specific — this house, this family, this street, this flood. The power comes from the accumulation of concrete details.

**Effect**: By being so specific, the book becomes universal. We understand the abstract (displacement, erasure, systemic neglect) through the concrete (the Yellow House, the Avis map, Carl's gold tooth).

**Book case — The blue porta potty**: The only structure remaining on the Wilson Avenue lot after Katrina is a bright blue porta potty with "CITY OF NEW ORLEANS" written on it. This single detail encapsulates the government's response: inadequate, bureaucratic, absurd.

---

## Technique 5: Temporal Layering

Broom moves freely between time periods within a single chapter or even a single paragraph. The past and present coexist in the Yellow House.

**Effect**: The house contains all times at once. 1961, 1985, 2005, 2019 — they all exist in the same space. This reflects how memory works: the past is never fully past.

**Book case — Carl on the lot**: When Broom describes Carl sitting on the empty lot in the present, she also describes what was there before — the house, the dining table, the sounds of family. The present and past layers are visible simultaneously.

---

## Technique 6: The Collective "We"

The book often shifts from first-person ("I") to first-person plural ("we"). This reflects Broom's understanding that her story is not just hers — it belongs to her family, her neighborhood, her city.

**Book case — The family as collective**: "We were twelve children in a four-room house." The "we" is not rhetorical. It carries the actual weight of twelve lives in a small space.
