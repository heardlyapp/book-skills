# Techniques: Emotion and Making Things Public

> Source: Contagious, Chapters 3-4

## Emotion Drives Sharing

High-arousal emotions (positive and negative) increase sharing. Low-arousal emotions suppress it.

> **Case: The "Most Emailed" Study** (Chapter 3): Berger analyzed the NYT's most emailed articles. The common thread: high-arousal emotion. Awe at a scientific discovery. Anger at an injustice. Laughter at a funny story. Sad stories (low arousal) rarely made the list.

> **Key takeaway:** To make something contagious, create high-arousal feelings. Excitement, awe, humor, anger, anxiety all work. Contentment and sadness do not.

## The Physiology of Arousal

Emotional arousal activates the nervous system. This activation drives action — including sharing.

> **Case: The Exercise Study** (Chapter 3): Students who exercised (physiologically aroused) shared more information. Those at rest did not. Berger shows that physiological arousal itself boosts sharing — whether from emotion or exercise.

> **Key takeaway:** Arousal is the mechanism. High-arousal emotions prime us to share. Create content that activates the body, not just the mind.

## Making the Private Public

People imitate what they see. If behavior is invisible, it won't spread.

> **Case: The Wristband at the Hospital** (Chapter 4): A hospital wanted to improve hand washing. They put "I washed my hands" wristbands on doctors. Suddenly, washing hands became visible — and everyone started doing it. The behavior became public.

> **Key takeaway:** Make desired behaviors observable. When people can see others doing something, they follow. Apple's white earbuds and Nike's Livestrong bracelet are classic examples.
