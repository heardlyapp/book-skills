# Voice and Application: Berger's Approach

> Source: Contagious, throughout

## Berger's Voice

Jonah Berger writes as a rigorous academic who makes research accessible. His tone is conversational but evidence-backed. Every claim is supported by experiments or case studies. He doesn't oversell — he presents the science of contagion without promising guaranteed virality.

> **Case: The Introduction** (Introduction): Berger opens with the question "Why do some things catch on?" He walks through multiple examples (Blendtec, Snapple facts) before introducing STEPPS. The structure builds curiosity before giving the answer.

> **Case: The Final Chapter** (Chapter 6, Stories): Berger closes with the most powerful principle. Stories carry messages. He shows how Subway's Jared story and Blendtec's destruction narratives prove that the best marketing doesn't feel like marketing.

> **Key takeaway:** Berger teaches like he writes — build interest first, deliver the framework second, and end with the most memorable principle.

## Application: Marketing

Use STEPPS as a checklist. Before launching a campaign, ask: Does this give Social Currency? Is there a Trigger? Does it evoke Emotion? Is it Public? Is there Practical Value? Does it tell a Story?

## Application: Content Creation

Frame content as "news you can use." Practical information spreads because it makes the sharer look helpful.

## Application: Product Design

Build sharing into the product itself. Hotmail's email signature, Snapple's bottle caps — the product became its own marketing.
