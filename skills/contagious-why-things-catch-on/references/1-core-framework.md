# Core Framework: The STEPPS Principles

> Source: Contagious, Introduction

## Jonah Berger's Research

Berger is a Wharton marketing professor who spent over a decade studying why things go viral. He analyzed the New York Times "Most Emailed" list (article after article, tracking patterns), conducted controlled experiments on word of mouth, and interviewed marketers behind viral campaigns.

> **Case: The Blendtec Blender** (Introduction): A blender company starts a YouTube series called "Will It Blend?" — blending iPhones, marbles, glow sticks, golf balls. The videos generate millions of views without paid media. Sales jump 700%. Berger argues this is not luck — it's the STEPPS framework in action.

> **Key takeaway:** Viral success follows predictable principles. Blendtec didn't get lucky; they engineered contagiousness.

## The STEPPS Framework

Six independent principles. Any combination increases contagiousness. The more principles applied, the more contagious the idea.

1. **Social Currency** — People share things that make them look good (smart, cool, in-the-know).
2. **Triggers** — Environmental cues prompt recall and discussion. Top of mind becomes tip of tongue.
3. **Emotion** — High-arousal feelings (awe, excitement, anger) drive sharing; low-arousal states (sadness, contentment) suppress it.
4. **Public** — Observable behavior is imitated. Make the private public.
5. **Practical Value** — Useful information spreads because people want to help others.
6. **Stories** — Narratives carry messages as Trojan horses. People tell stories, not sales pitches.

> **Case: The Snapple Facts** (Introduction): Snapple put interesting facts under bottle caps — "The average person has 100,000 hairs on their head." People collected and shared these facts. Snapple generated word of mouth without traditional advertising. The facts combined Social Currency (interesting), Triggers (every bottle), and Practical Value (usable knowledge).

> **Key takeaway:** Berger's research shows that word of mouth is 10x more effective than traditional advertising. It's targeted, credible, and self-reinforcing.
