# Anti-Patterns — How Science Goes Wrong and Why We Misunderstand It

> Source: A Short History of Nearly Everything, all chapters

## The Five Myths About Science

### 1. The Lone Genius Myth
**Pattern**: "Einstein/Nobel/Darwin discovered everything by themselves."
**Corrected**: Most discoveries are built on the work of dozens or hundreds of previous scientists. Newton: "If I have seen further, it is by standing on the shoulders of giants." The lone genius is a storytelling convenience, not the reality.

### 2. The "Science Is Boring" Myth
**Pattern**: "Science is about memorizing facts and formulas."
**Corrected**: Bryson's book proves the opposite. Science is about the most exciting stories: the origin of the universe, the evolution of life, the discovery of how the world works. The facts are the result — the stories are the path.

### 3. The "Science Is Finished" Myth
**Pattern**: "Scientists have figured everything out."
**Corrected**: Every answer raises new questions. We still do not know what dark matter is (85% of the universe's mass), what caused the Cambrian explosion, or how consciousness emerges from the brain.

### 4. The "Science Is Consensus" Myth
**Pattern**: "Scientists agree, so the debate is over."
**Corrected**: Science is not decided by consensus — it is decided by evidence. The consensus can be wrong (continental drift was rejected for decades). The evidence eventually wins.

### 5. The "Theory Means Guess" Myth
**Pattern**: "Evolution is just a theory."
**Corrected**: A scientific theory is not a guess. It is a well-tested explanation that makes predictions and has survived repeated attempts to disprove it.

> **Case: The Piltdown Man Hoax** (Chapter 4)
> In 1912, a fossil was discovered in England that appeared to be the "missing link" between apes and humans. For 40 years, it was accepted as genuine. In 1953, it was exposed as a hoax — someone had combined the jaw of an orangutan with the skull of a modern human. The Piltdown Man is a cautionary tale: it shows that scientists can be fooled, that wishful thinking can blind even experts, and that science eventually corrects itself.
> **Key takeaway**: The Piltdown Man hoax is not evidence that science is unreliable — it is evidence that science self-corrects. The hoax was exposed by scientists using better methods. Science is not about being infallible. It is about having a system that eventually finds the truth.

> **Case: The Rejection of Continental Drift** (Chapter 5)
> In 1912, Alfred Wegener proposed that the continents had once been joined together and had drifted apart. He had evidence: the coastlines of Africa and South America fit together; the same fossils were found on both continents. The scientific establishment ridiculed him. Continental drift was rejected for 50 years — until the discovery of plate tectonics proved Wegener was right. Wegener died before his theory was accepted.
> **Key takeaway**: The rejection of continental drift is one of the most famous examples of scientific resistance to a new idea. The establishment was wrong. Wegener was right. The lesson: evidence matters more than authority.
