# Evolution — The Story of Life

> Source: A Short History of Nearly Everything, Part V

## The Epic of Life

Life on Earth is 3.8 billion years old. For most of that time — 2.5 billion years — life consisted of single cells. Complex life is a recent innovation.

### The Timeline of Life

| Event | Time | What Happened |
|-------|------|---------------|
| First life | 3.8 BYA | Single cells appear |
| Photosynthesis | 2.5 BYA | Bacteria learn to use sunlight — oxygen fills the atmosphere |
| First complex cells | 1.5 BYA | Eukaryotes (cells with a nucleus) |
| Cambrian explosion | 540 MYA | Most of the major animal groups appear in a burst of evolution |
| Plants on land | 470 MYA | Life moves out of the oceans |
| Dinosaurs | 230-65 MYA | They ruled for 165 million years |
| First primates | 55 MYA | Our distant ancestors appear |
| Homo sapiens | 300,000 YA | Modern humans arrive |
| Civilization | 5,000 YA | Writing, cities, organized agriculture |

> **Case: The Cambrian Explosion** (Chapter 16)
> Around 540 million years ago, something remarkable happened: in a geological instant (20 million years), most of the major animal groups appeared in the fossil record. Before the Cambrian, life consisted of simple sponges and jellyfish. After the Cambrian, there were trilobites, mollusks, arthropods, and the ancestors of fish. The cause of the explosion is still debated: rising oxygen levels, the evolution of predation, the development of eyes. But the result is clear: this was the moment when the animal world took shape.
> **Key takeaway**: The Cambrian explosion is the most important event in the history of life since the origin of life itself. Why it happened so quickly is one of the greatest unanswered questions in science.

> **Case: The Burgess Shale** (Chapter 16)
> The Burgess Shale in British Columbia, Canada, is one of the most important fossil sites in the world. It preserves the soft body parts of Cambrian animals — details that are almost never seen in the fossil record. The fossils are so strange that early paleontologists thought they were from an entirely separate experiment in life. Some of the creatures do not fit into any known animal group. The Burgess Shale shows that evolution experimented wildly during the Cambrian — and most of those experiments went extinct.
> **Key takeaway**: The Burgess Shale reveals that evolution is not a straight line toward "better." It is a branching tree of experiments, most of which fail. We are not the inevitable outcome of evolution — we are one branch among many.
