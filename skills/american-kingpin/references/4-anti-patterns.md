# 4 — Corruption and the Dark Side

> Source: "American Kingpin" by Nick Bilton

## Anti-Pattern: The Corrupt Investigator

The Silk Road investigation had a character even more shocking than the criminal: DEA agent Carl Force, who was embedded in the investigation but secretly stealing from it.

> **Case: The Rogue Agent** (Part III — The Corruption): "DEA agent Carl Force was supposed to be investigating Silk Road. Instead, he became an informant, infiltrator, and thief. He opened his own Bitcoin accounts, extorted money from Ulbricht, and even posed as a Silk Road employee to solicit murder-for-hire." Bilton describes one of the most corrupt agents in modern law enforcement history.
> **Key takeaway**: The line between law enforcement and criminal activity can be terrifyingly thin. Force was not a villain who set out to betray his agency — he gradually crossed boundaries, one small step at a time, until he was as deep in the criminal world as Ulbricht himself.

## Anti-Pattern: Violence as a Business Tool

The marketplace that began as a libertarian ideal quickly attracted people who were willing to use violence to resolve disputes.

> **Case: The Murder-for-Hire Plots** (Part III — The Violence): "Someone on Silk Road was selling murder-for-hire services. Ulbricht was accused of paying for at least five murders, though there is debate about whether he actually intended them or was being manipulated by agents and criminals." Bilton presents the murkiest part of the story.
> **Key takeaway**: Whether or not Ulbricht intended the murders, the fact that a marketplace created to promote freedom became entangled with murder-for-hire plots shows how quickly the dark side of the dark web can escalate. The libertarian dream of a self-regulating free market collided with the reality that some people will use any tool for violence.
