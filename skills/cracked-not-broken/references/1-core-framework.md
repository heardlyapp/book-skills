# Core Framework — Cracked, Not Broken

## Introduction: The Four-Second Fall

On September 24, 2000, nineteen-year-old Kevin Hines jumped from the Golden Gate Bridge. The fall took four seconds. He traveled 220 feet at 75 miles per hour. "The most real thought my mind had in the weeks leading up to that moment" came in the instant he left the railing: "Oh God, what have I done? I don't want to die! God please save me."

More people die by suicide from the Golden Gate Bridge than any other manmade structure in the world. As of 2005, an estimated 1,200 souls had attempted suicide there. Fewer than 35 have survived. Kevin is one of them.

"I jumped when I heard voices in my head telling me that I must die, that I had no other option." He has bipolar 1 disorder with psychotic features. His brain convinced him there was no way to ease the pain except through death.

## Chapter 1: The Beginning — Abandonment and Adoption

Kevin's birth parents were drug addicts. He and his twin brother Jordache were found naked in a hotel room, abandoned, malnourished, fed only Kool-Aid and Coca-Cola. His brother died of bronchitis at less than a year old. "I have not gone one day of my life without thinking of my biological family."

Patrick and Debi Hines adopted him. Debi saw him at his foster home, unable to crawl due to severe malnourishment, tossing plastic horseshoes into the air. "She says that was the moment she fell in love." His last visit with his birth mother: she was drunk, called him Jordache (his dead brother's name), and said: "I'm going to kill myself, but you live, you live."

## Chapter 2: A Happy Childhood — The Lake, Superheroes, and Elizabeth

Kevin grew up in a loving home with holidays, a lake cabin, and superhero costumes. Halloween was his favorite — he dressed as Spider-Man, He-Man, GI Joe. His sister Elizabeth (Libby) was his best friend. She tied him to a tree and sprayed him with a hose — he laughed. "We were best friends as little kids, absolutely inseparable."

But Elizabeth struggled with bulimia and anorexia from age nine. She was teased for "chubby cheeks." By 29, her clothes hung like empty sacks from her skeletal frame. She was hospitalized, eventually recovered enough to come home. "Every day was a battle for Elizabeth. A battle that, if lost, could take her life."

## Chapter 3: Bullying and the First Voices

At a predominantly white Catholic school, Kevin was bullied relentlessly for his auburn hair, large ears and nose, and mixed-race appearance. He was called "red nigger" and "ginger." Students held him down and punched him. "That went on for all seven years I attended this school, without check."

He retreated into an inner world where he was a superhero. But the first warning signs appeared: "My brain was moving faster than a 'healthy' human brain should. I heard every word said by my teachers, family, classmates, and friends. Every word." Teachers thought he wasn't listening. He was listening too much.

## Chapter 5: The Jump

Kevin gave away his prized possessions — a $5,000 comic collection to his brother, a $400 CD collection to his friend. These are classic signs of suicidal planning. He took a bus to the Golden Gate Bridge, fought the voices in his head, and jumped. A sea lion kept him afloat in the frigid water. The Coast Guard rescued him.

He woke up in the hospital with twenty-three staples in his back from spinal surgery, shattered vertebrae, and a body cast. "I have never known control, but I do know it now. I control my life. It is mine."

## Chapter 6: The First Psych Ward

Kevin was transferred to a psychiatric ward — the first of seven over the next ten years ("luxurious hotel stays"). He met patients who cut themselves with plastic knives, a man who thought the FBI was coming for him, a woman who carved her pain into her own skin. "The patients without visitors were called 'The Forgotten.'"

He started reading everything he could about bipolar disorder. He followed his treatment plan: medication, therapy, exercise, social connection. "The routine I had learned in the ward worked. At last, for the first time in my life, I had learned how to take steps toward my own well-being."

## Chapter 7: Uncle Kevin

Kevin's namesake uncle was his anchor. A recovered alcoholic (30 years sober), former Teamster union leader, and lifelong humanitarian. "He saved countless lives across the Bay Area. He would find those living with alcoholism or they would find him." Uncle Kevin brought Kevin books about bipolar disorder, suicide prevention, and public speaking. He took him for soup and independent films. He never walked on eggshells. "Uncle Kevin showed me how to focus my good intentions."

## Later Years: Finding Purpose

Kevin became a professional public speaker and mental health advocate. He speaks worldwide about suicide prevention. He met his wife, got married, became a father. The book's title captures his philosophy: "I was Cracked... But will never be Broken." Recovery is not a straight line — he has experienced relapses — but he has learned the art of living mentally well.
