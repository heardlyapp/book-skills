# Techniques — Cracked, Not Broken

## How to Support Someone in Crisis (Based on Kevin Hines)

### Technique 1: Recognize the Warning Signs
Kevin gave away his comic book collection (worth $5,000) and his CD collection ($400) before his attempt. He made goodbyes. Be alert for: giving away prized possessions, sudden calm after depression, making final arrangements, saying goodbye. These are signs of suicidal planning, not recovery.

### Technique 2: Stay Present — Don't Walk on Eggshells
Uncle Kevin was the one person who never treated Kevin differently. He was "upfront, brusque, direct, and sincere." He didn't sanitize his language or avoid uncomfortable topics. He brought books about suicide and bipolar disorder — he didn't pretend the problem didn't exist. "No matter how worried he was about me, Kevin was the only one who never walked on eggshells around me."

### Technique 3: Provide Resources, Not Lectures
Uncle Kevin brought specific books: "How to Love Someone with Bipolar Disorder," "Bipolar for Dummies," "An Unquiet Mind," "Night Falls Fast." He didn't lecture — he equipped. "I no longer wanted to deny the reality of my condition. Instead, I wanted to learn as much as I could." The question: what can you give them that feeds their curiosity about their own recovery?

### Technique 4: Build a Recovery Routine
Kevin's psych ward routine: medication, therapy, exercise, social interaction. After discharge, he maintained this. "The routine I had learned in the ward worked. At last, for the first time in my life, I had learned how to take steps toward my own well-being." Recovery requires structure, not willpower.

### Technique 5: Find Purpose Through Helping Others
Kevin became a speaker because his story could save others. "I love helping people." His uncle saw speaking as Kevin's calling and brought him public speaking manuals. When you're in pain, helping someone else creates meaning. Purpose is the strongest antidepressant.

### Technique 6: The Bridge Rail Question
Kevin asks his audiences: "If we could change the bridge, should we?" The answer is obviously yes — and yet it took decades to install a suicide barrier on the Golden Gate Bridge (completed in 2024). The technique: when someone is in crisis, ask yourself what environmental changes could save them — not just what they need to change internally.

### Technique 7: Forgive Your Past Self
Kevin forgave his birth parents. He forgave himself for being blind to his own mental health decline. "I finally mustered enough compassion to forgive myself." Shame keeps people silent. Forgiveness opens the door to speaking out.
