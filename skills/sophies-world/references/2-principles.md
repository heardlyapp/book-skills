# Key Principles — The Examined Life, Wonder vs Habit, and Thinking for Yourself

## Core Principles

> **Case: The Pre-Socratics** (Chapters 4-6): The first philosophers believed the world was made of a single substance: water (Thales), air (Anaximenes), or an undefined "boundless" (Anaximander). Their answers were wrong. But that is not the point. The point is that they asked a new kind of question: instead of attributing natural phenomena to gods, they sought natural explanations. They invented reason. Sophie learns that it matters less what they believed than that they believed the world could be understood through thought.
> **Key takeaway**: The pre-Socratics teach us: you do not have to be right to be a philosopher. You just have to be willing to ask.

> **Case: Socrates** (Chapter 7): Socrates did not write anything. He walked around Athens asking questions. "What is justice?" "What is virtue?" "What is the good life?" He did not claim to know the answers. He claimed only to know that he did not know. The Oracle at Delphi said he was the wisest man in Athens. He interpreted this to mean: he was the only one who knew how little he knew. His method — the Socratic method — is not about giving answers but about asking questions that force the other person to examine their assumptions.
> **Key takeaway**: The wisest person knows they do not know. Question everything, especially your own certainties.

> **Case: Plato's Cave** (Chapter 9): Plato's allegory: people are chained in a cave, facing a wall. Behind them, a fire casts shadows of puppets on the wall. The prisoners believe the shadows are reality. One prisoner is freed. He turns around. He sees the fire and the puppets. He leaves the cave. He sees the sun — the source of all light and truth. He returns to the cave to tell the others. They do not believe him. They kill him. Plato's point: the world we perceive through our senses is only the shadow of a higher reality. Philosophy is the journey out of the cave into the light of truth.
> **Key takeaway**: Plato's cave is a map of the philosophical journey: from illusion to reality, from opinion to knowledge, from the shadow to the sun. It is a dangerous journey. But it is the only journey worth taking.
