# Techniques — The History of Philosophy as a Map, the Socratic Method, and Reading Philosophers

## Practical Methods

> **Case: Aristotle's Categories** (Chapter 10): Aristotle disagreed with Plato. He said the forms are not in a separate world. They are in the things themselves. He categorized everything. He classified the world. He said: to understand anything, you must ask four questions: What is it made of? What form does it take? What made it? What is it for? These are the four causes. Aristotle was the first biologist, the first logician, the first systematic thinker.
> **Key takeaway**: Aristotle's four causes are a tool for understanding anything. Ask: What is it? What is its purpose? What made it? What is its essence? These questions clarify everything.

> **Case: Descartes' Method** (Chapter 15): Descartes doubted everything. Could his senses be trusted? Could he be dreaming? Could an evil demon be deceiving him? He doubted until he found one thing he could not doubt: the fact that he was doubting. "Cogito ergo sum" — I think, therefore I am. From this single certainty, he rebuilt knowledge. Descartes' method: doubt everything until you find something you cannot doubt. Build from there.
> **Key takeaway**: Descartes' method is the most powerful tool for critical thinking. Doubt everything. Find what cannot be doubted. Rebuild from that foundation.

> **Case: Kant's Revolution** (Chapter 19): Kant said our minds are not blank slates. They have built-in structures that shape how we perceive reality. We do not see the world as it is. We see the world as our minds organize it. Time and space are not features of the world. They are the glasses we cannot take off. Kant's insight: we can never know the "thing in itself." We can only know the thing as it appears to us.
> **Key takeaway**: Kant teaches humility. You do not see reality directly. You see reality filtered through the structures of your mind. Be humble about what you think you know. Your knowledge is always partial.
