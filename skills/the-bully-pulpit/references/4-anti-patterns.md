## 6 Anti-Patterns of Reform

### 1. "One Person Can Change Everything"

Reform requires movement — multiple forces working together. TR could not have succeeded without the muckrakers, and the muckrakers could not have succeeded without TR.

### 2. "The Best Man Will Win"

TR and Taft were both good men. The better campaigner won the 1912 election — but only because the other two split the vote. Virtue is not victory.

> **Case: The 1912 Convention** (Chapter 16): TR won most of the Republican primaries, but Taft controlled the party apparatus. The convention gave Taft the nomination. TR walked out. The system defeated the popular will.
> **Key takeaway**: The rules matter. Taft won the nomination through party rules, not popular support. TR was the people's choice — but the people did not control the party.

### 3. "Investigative Journalism Always Wins"

Journalism exposes — but exposure alone does not create change. The muckrakers' work required political action to become reform.

### 4. "Friendship Is Stronger Than Politics"

TR and Taft's friendship could not survive political differences. Politics can destroy any personal relationship.

### 5. "A Leader Must Be Popular"

TR was popular. Taft was not. But Taft achieved significant reforms (more antitrust prosecutions than TR, the Mann-Elkins Act). Popularity is not the only measure of effectiveness.

### 6. "The Progressive Era Was a Coherent Movement"

The Progressives were a loose coalition of often contradictory forces: trust-busters and labor reformers, prohibitionists and suffragists, conservationists and urban reformers. They did not always agree.

> **Case: The Square Deal** (Chapter 7): TR's "Square Deal" was popular rhetoric but sometimes inconsistent in practice. He compromised with conservatives, accepted corporate donations, and made deals. The reformer was also a politician.
> **Key takeaway**: Even great reformers compromise. TR was not pure — he was effective.
