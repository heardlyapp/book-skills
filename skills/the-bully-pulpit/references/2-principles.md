## 7 Principles of Progressive Reform

### 1. Journalism Is the First Check on Power

Investigative journalism exposed the corruption that the political system would not address. The muckrakers were the first line of defense.

### 2. The Presidency Is What the President Makes It

TR transformed the presidency from a constitutional office to a moral platform. His energy, personality, and use of the bully pulpit defined what a president could be.

> **Case: The Coal Strike** (Chapter 5): In 1902, 140,000 coal miners went on strike. The strike threatened winter heating. TR intervened — not as a neutral mediator but as an active participant. He threatened to send the army to run the mines. Both sides agreed to arbitration. TR set a precedent: the president is responsible for the nation's welfare, not just its laws.
> **Key takeaway**: TR expanded presidential power by acting boldly in crisis. The coal strike established the president as a force in labor disputes.

### 3. Friendship and Politics Cannot Be Separated

TR and Taft were close friends. When political differences emerged, the friendship could not survive. Goodwin shows that personal relationships are always political at the highest level.

### 4. Movement Requires Multiple Forces

The Progressive Era succeeded because journalists, politicians, and civic activists worked together. None could have done it alone.

### 5. Division Defeats Reform

The Republican split in 1912 gave the White House to Wilson. The lesson: reformers must unite or lose to the opposition.

### 6. Temperament Determines Leadership

TR was aggressive, impatient, bold. Taft was cautious, judicial, consensus-seeking. Neither was wrong — but their temperaments determined what they could achieve.

> **Case: Taft's Reluctance** (Chapter 9): Taft did not want to be president. He wanted to be Chief Justice. He accepted the presidency because his wife, his brother, and TR pushed him. His lack of ambition for the office shaped his cautious approach.
> **Key takeaway**: A leader who does not want the job will not fight for it. Taft's reluctance was his defining weakness.

### 7. The Bully Pulpit Still Works

The concept of the presidency as a platform for reform is TR's most enduring legacy. Every president since has used the bully pulpit — some effectively, some not.
