## Practical Techniques

### 1. The Platform Assessment

Identify your "bully pulpit" — whatever platform you have to influence others. Whether it is a blog, a job, a community role, or a social media account — use it for something you believe in.

### 2. The Muckraking Method

To expose a problem: research thoroughly, document evidence, tell a story, connect individual cases to systemic issues. Tarbell's Standard Oil series is the model.

> **Case: Tarbell's Research** (Chapter 6): Ida Tarbell spent years researching Standard Oil. She pored over court records, interviewed former employees, and traced the company's methods. Her series ran for 19 installments. Her research was so thorough that Standard Oil did not sue — they could not disprove the facts.
> **Key takeaway**: Investigative journalism requires patience. Tarbell spent years on Standard Oil. The result was unassailable.

### 3. The Strategic Alliance

Find allies in different sectors. The Progressive Era's success came from journalists, politicians, and activists working together. Build coalitions across sectors.

### 4. The Temperament Match

Do not force yourself into a leadership role that does not fit your temperament. Taft would have been a great Chief Justice (he became one). He was a reluctant president. Know your strengths.

> **Case: Taft's True Calling** (Epilogue): After losing the 1912 election, Taft became Chief Justice of the Supreme Court in 1921. He loved the job. He is remembered as one of the great Chief Justices. He was never suited to the presidency.
> **Key takeaway**: The right role for the right person. Taft found his calling after losing the highest office.
