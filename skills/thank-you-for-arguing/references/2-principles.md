## 7 Principles of Rhetorical Persuasion

### 1. Ethos First

Before anyone will listen to your argument, they must trust you. Establish credibility before making your case.

### 2. Pathos Second

Emotion drives action. Logic justifies. Appeal to emotion first, then reinforce with logic.

> **Case: The Charity Appeal** (Chapter 8): A charity can say "12 million children are malnourished" (logos). Or they can show one child's face (pathos). The face moves people to act. The number makes them feel helpless.
> **Key takeaway**: One story moves people more than 1000 statistics. Pathos beats logos for action.

### 3. Concede to Control

Strategic concession disarms opponents and wins audiences. "You have a point" is not surrender — it is strategy.

### 4. Reframe the Debate

If you cannot win on the current terms, change them. The best argument is often not about the issue itself — it is about what the issue means.

### 5. Know Your Audience

Decorum — matching your style to your audience — is essential. What persuades a CEO will not persuade a teenager.

### 6. Use Figures of Speech

Chiasmus, rhetorical questions, and amplification make arguments memorable. Style is not decoration — it is persuasion.

### 7. Practice Cicero's Five Canons

Invention (find arguments), Arrangement (organize them), Style (choose words), Memory (know your material), Delivery (present effectively).

> **Case: The Classical Order** (Chapter 6): Heinrichs recovers Cicero's classical order for arguments: introduction, statement of facts, division, proof, refutation, conclusion. Most people skip the division step — where you state what you agree and disagree on. That step is where rhetoric lives.
> **Key takeaway**: Structure is persuasion. The order of your argument is as important as the content.
