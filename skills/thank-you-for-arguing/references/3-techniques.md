## Practical Techniques

### 1. The Concession Reframe

Step 1: Concede a point. "You are right about X." Step 2: Reframe. "But here is why Y matters more." Step 3: State your case.

### 2. The Rhetorical Question

Ask a question that leads the audience to your conclusion. "Who would want to go back to the way things were?" The audience answers mentally: "No one."

### 3. The Argument from Definition

Define the key term in a way that supports your case. "What does 'freedom' really mean?" Then define it your way.

> **Case: The Definition Debate** (Chapter 10): A debate about "marriage" was reframed as a debate about "equality." The reframe changed the outcome. The side that controlled the definition controlled the argument.
> **Key takeaway**: Whoever controls the definition of key terms controls the argument. Define first, debate second.

### 4. The Cicero Structure

1. Introduction — establish ethos
2. Statement of facts — neutral, credible
3. Division — what we agree on, what we disagree on
4. Proof — your case
5. Refutation — address objections
6. Conclusion — call to action

### 5. The Audience Switch

When stuck, change your imagined audience. Are you arguing with the person in front of you — or the people watching? The audience is who you need to persuade.

### 6. The Spin Doctor's Principle

After an event, control the narrative by framing: "What this means is..." The frame determines how the event is remembered.

> **Case: The Political Spin** (Chapter 12): After a loss, a politician says: "We did not lose. We laid the groundwork for future victory." The frame transforms defeat into a stepping stone.
> **Key takeaway**: Facts do not speak for themselves — you must frame them. The frame is the argument.
