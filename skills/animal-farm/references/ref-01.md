# Reference: The Allegory

## Overview
Animal Farm is an allegory of the Russian Revolution. Every character and event maps to historical figures and events.

## Case: Old Major
Old Major represents Marx and Lenin. He inspires the revolution with his vision of a free animal society. He dies before the revolution begins, leaving no plan for how to achieve it.

## Case: The Rebellion
The animals drive out Mr. Jones, representing the overthrow of the Tsar. The initial euphoria is real. The animals believe they are free.

## Case: The Pigs Take Over
The pigs, being the most intelligent, assume leadership. They claim to act for all animals. But they gradually take more privileges — milk, apples, beds, alcohol.

## Case: Napoleon vs. Snowball
Snowball represents Trotsky — brilliant, idealistic, and outmaneuvered. Napoleon represents Stalin — ruthless, manipulative, and patient. Napoleon uses his dogs to exile Snowball.

## Case: The Final Scene
The pigs walk on two legs, carry whips, and wear human clothes. The animals cannot tell pigs from humans. The revolution has produced exactly what it fought against.

## Case: The Universal Pattern
The allegory is not limited to Russia. Orwell shows a universal pattern: revolution -> leadership vacuum -> power seizure -> corruption -> new tyranny. It has happened throughout history.
The novel warns that the oppressed who become the oppressor are the most dangerous tyrants of all.
Orwell wrote Animal Farm during World War II, as a warning against idealizing the Soviet Union, which was then allied with Britain.
