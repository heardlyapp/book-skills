# Reference: Boxer and the Workers

## Overview
Boxer the carthorse represents the working class. He is loyal, strong, and exploited.

## Case: The Motto
Boxer's two mottos: "I will work harder" and "Napoleon is always right." He believes that if he gives more, the revolution will succeed. His loyalty is absolute.

## Case: The Collapse
Boxer works beyond his strength. He collapses while building the windmill. He has given everything to a cause that does not care about him.

## Case: The Betrayal
Napoleon promises to send Boxer to a hospital. Instead, the pigs sell him to the knacker. The animals pay for the glue. The most loyal comrade is traded for money.

## Case: The Squealer Justification
Squealer lies to the animals, telling them Boxer received the best possible care. The animals want to believe. They accept the lie.

## Case: The Pattern
Boxer's story repeats across history. The working class gives everything to revolutions that then exploit them. Loyalty is not rewarded. Power serves itself.

## Case: Benjamin's Warning
Benjamin, the cynical donkey, could have warned Boxer. He can read. He sees the betrayal coming. But he says nothing. He represents those who see the truth but choose silence.

## Case: The Monument
Napoleon gives a speech about Boxer's contribution. There is talk of a monument. Nothing materializes. The revolution uses the martyr's memory and discards it.
Boxer is the tragedy of the loyal worker. He gives everything and receives nothing. His story is the novel's most devastating indictment of exploitation.
