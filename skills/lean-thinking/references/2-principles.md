# Key Principles

> Source: *Lean Thinking* by James P. Womack & Daniel T. Jones, Parts I & II

## The Eight Types of Muda (Waste)

Taiichi Ohno identified the first seven; Womack & Jones added the eighth:

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| 1. Overproduction | Making more than the customer needs | Building inventory "just in case" |
| 2. Waiting | Idle time between process steps | Operators standing while machines run |
| 3. Transport | Moving products that don't need moving | Hauling parts across the factory floor |
| 4. Overprocessing | Doing more work than the customer values | Extra machining on hidden surfaces |
| 5. Inventory | Excess raw material, WIP, or finished goods | Warehouses full of unsold product |
| 6. Motion | Unnecessary movement of people | Walking to find tools or supplies |
| 7. Defects | Rework, scrap, inspection | Parts that need to be remade |
| 8. Underutilized human potential | Not tapping employee ideas | Ignoring operator improvement suggestions |

> **Case: Pratt & Whitney** (Introduction): When P&W walked its value stream, it found all eight types of muda simultaneously. A machining center that removed 90% of raw material (overprocessing and inventory), parts waiting 30 days for the next step (waiting), massive transport between facilities (transport), and engineers who knew how to fix it but had never been asked (underutilized human potential).

## Principles of the Lean Leap

From the conversion experiences of dozens of companies, Womack & Jones extract these guiding principles for any lean transformation:

### 1. Start with Customer-Defined Value

Before you improve anything, **ask your customers what they actually value**. Most companies skip this step and end up optimizing processes nobody cares about. Value is the North Star — everything else follows.

> **Case: Wiremold** (Chapter 1): Wiremold's previous product development was driven by engineering preferences (more features, more complexity). When they started asking customers, they discovered customers wanted simplicity, elegance, and fast installation. Wiremold simplified its entire product line and sales grew dramatically.

### 2. Walk the Actual Value Stream

Don't look at org charts, spreadsheets, or aggregated reports. **Go to the gemba (the actual place)** . Walk the physical flow of a single product. See what actually happens. The waste will jump out at you.

> **Case: Lantech** (Chapter 6): When Lantech's leadership walked the actual floor for the first time, they discovered their stretch-wrapping machines spent 95% of their time sitting in queues between departments. No spreadsheet had ever revealed this.

### 3. Organization by Product, Not by Function

Rearranging departments around products (cells, value streams) is the structural foundation of lean. Functional organization creates handoffs, waiting, and blame-shifting. Product organization creates flow, ownership, and accountability.

### 4. Make Problems Visible

Transparency is the engine of continuous improvement. **Surface every problem** so it can be fixed. Use visual controls (andon lights, kanban boards, performance boards) so anyone can see the current state in seconds.

### 5. Perfect Is a Direction, Not a Destination

You will never "arrive" at a perfect lean system. The goal is to create a **capability for continuous improvement** — a muscle that gets stronger over time. The Freudenberg-NOK example (991% productivity improvement over 3 years) shows that the improvement is infinite.

### 6. Lean Is for Every Industry

Lean thinking is not limited to automotive manufacturing. The principles apply equally to healthcare, software, construction, logistics, retail, and services. The specifics change; the principles don't.

## The Kaikaku Bonus

Organizations that make the initial radical leap (kaikaku) from batch-and-queue to continuous flow can expect:
- 2× labor productivity
- 90% reduction in throughput time
- 90% reduction in inventories
- 50% reduction in defects and errors
- 50% reduction in time-to-market
- Modest or negative capital investment (free up and sell old equipment)
