# Core Framework: The Five Principles of Lean Thinking

> Source: *Lean Thinking* by James P. Womack & Daniel T. Jones, Part I (Chapters 1–5)

## Overview

The book builds on the Toyota Production System and distills it into **five interconnected principles** that form a complete system. They must be applied in order:

1. **Value** → 2. **Value Stream** → 3. **Flow** → 4. **Pull** → 5. **Perfection**

You cannot skip steps. Each principle depends on the previous one.

### 1. Value

Value can **only** be defined by the **ultimate customer**. It is meaningful only when expressed as a specific product (good or service) that meets the customer's needs at a specific price at a specific time.

**Common distortions:**
- US firms: value defined by short-term financial metrics and shareholder demands
- German firms: value defined by engineers (too much complexity, too expensive)
- Japanese firms: value defined by where value is created (home-country employment bias)

> **Case: Pratt & Whitney** (Introduction): When P&W mapped its value stream for jet engine parts, it discovered that raw material (titanium/nickel) entering the plant was ten times the weight of the finished part. 90% of the material was being machined away — pure muda. The customer (an airline) wanted a lightweight, durable engine component at a competitive price, not a massive block of metal. Redefining value from the customer's perspective exposed the waste.

> **Case: Wiremold** (Chapter 1): When Wiremold's president reorganized product development in 1992, he asked customers to define value. They wanted simpler, more elegant wire guides that looked good in modern offices. Wiremold's engineers had been designing complex products they thought were superior. Once the customer-defined value was clear, Wiremold simplified its product line, hired an industrial designer for the first time, and sales surged.

### 2. The Value Stream

The value stream is the **set of all specific actions** required to bring a product from concept to launch (product development) and from order to delivery (production). Identifying the entire value stream exposes staggering amounts of muda.

Three types of actions emerge:
- **Value-creating steps** — actions the customer is willing to pay for
- **Type One Muda** — wasteful steps that cannot be immediately eliminated (e.g., regulatory inspections)
- **Type Two Muda** — pure waste that can and must be eliminated now

> **Case: Pratt & Whitney** (Chapter 2): P&W's value stream for jet engines involved 14+ companies (melter, forger, fabricator, parts manufacturer, fastener maker, subassembly, final assembly). Each had its own batch-and-queue system. Total lead time: months. Actual processing time: days. The rest was waiting, transport, and inventory. Value stream mapping showed that no single company could make the system lean alone — the entire chain needed to collaborate.

> **Case: Cola Can** (Chapter 4): Womack & Jones trace a single can of cola from the aluminum smelter, through rolling, can-making, filling, distribution, to the Tesco shelf. Total value stream steps: hundreds. Value-creating steps: a few seconds. The rest: waiting, transport, and inventory. The can spends most of its life sitting somewhere, not being drunk.

### 3. Flow

Once value is defined and the value stream mapped, the next step is to make the **value-creating steps flow continuously**. The enemy is batch-and-queue — organizing work by department and moving material in batches.

Taiichi Ohno realized that Henry Ford's continuous flow in final assembly could be extended to **small-lot production** by:
- Right-sizing machines (not bigger, but more flexible)
- Placing machines in process sequence (U-shaped cells)
- Cross-training workers to operate multiple processes
- Reducing changeover times to near zero

> **Case: Toyota** (Chapter 3): After WWII, Ohno faced the challenge of producing dozens or hundreds of copies of a product (not millions like Ford). He achieved continuous flow in low-volume production by learning to change dies in minutes instead of hours, arranging machines in sequence, and training workers to handle multiple process steps. The result: one-piece flow for small batches.

> **Case: Lantech** (Chapter 6): Lantech built stretch-wrapping machines in functional departments (cutting, welding, painting, assembly). A machine spent months moving between departments. By reorganizing into product-focused cells where all operations happened in sequence, Lantech cut throughput time from 16 weeks to 5 days and doubled productivity.

### 4. Pull

Pull means **no one upstream should produce a good or service until the customer downstream asks for it**. Instead of "pushing" products based on forecasts, you let actual demand trigger production.

Pull systems use **visual signals** (kanban cards, empty bins, electronic signals) to communicate demand upstream. The ideal: make-to-order with zero finished goods inventory.

> **Case: Tesco Supermarket** (Chapter 4): Tesco implemented a pull system for cola where the shelf (the last point in the value stream) signals the distribution center when to replenish. The distribution center signals the canner, which signals the can-maker, which signals the aluminum supplier. Each step pulls exactly what the next step consumed. The result: continuous replenishment with minimal inventory between every step.

> **Case: Porsche** (Chapter 9): Porsche applied lean thinking to transform from near-bankruptcy to profitability. By implementing pull systems throughout its supply chain and production, Porsche reduced inventory by millions of Deutsche marks and cut throughput time from weeks to days. The pull system also exposed quality problems immediately — no more hiding defective parts in inventory.

### 5. Perfection

The fifth principle is the most paradoxical: once you have value, value stream, flow, and pull working, **transparency makes perfection possible**. When everyone can see the entire value stream — subcontractors, suppliers, assemblers, distributors, customers — better ways to create value become obvious.

Lean transformation happens in two modes:
- **Kaikaku** (radical improvement): A breakthrough reconfiguration of the value stream. This is the initial leap.
- **Kaizen** (continuous improvement): Daily incremental improvements by the people doing the work.

> **Case: Freudenberg-NOK** (Chapter 5): FNGP conducted repeated kaizen events on the same part number over three years. The first event: 56% productivity gain. Three more events over the next three years: productivity improved by a staggering 991%. Each round found more muda. This disproves the myth of diminishing returns — in lean, you can always find more waste through transparency and fresh eyes.

> **Case: Automotive Glass Industry** (Chapter 5): Womack & Jones trace the auto glass value stream from silica float to final installation. With 25% material scrapped at the fabricator stage and enormous batch sizes at every step, the authors show how a full-stream kaikaku (all firms collaborating) could cut cost, lead time, and waste dramatically. Perfection requires the whole value stream to act as one enterprise.
