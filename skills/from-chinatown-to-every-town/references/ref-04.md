# Reference: Identity and Integration

## Overview
The second generation faces the challenge of navigating between Chinese and American identities. Educational achievement is the primary vehicle for upward mobility.

## Case: The Education Imperative
Chinese immigrant parents prioritize education above everything. The children are expected to excel in school and pursue professional careers — doctor, engineer, lawyer. This focus on education is the most effective path to closing the gap between immigrant and native-born success. Children of Chinese immigrants have among the highest educational attainment rates of any group in America.

## Case: Bilingual Identity
Children speak Chinese at home and English at school. Many lose fluency in Chinese as they grow up. They navigate between two cultural worlds. They may feel Chinese at home and American at school — or the opposite. The second generation creates hybrid identities that are neither fully Chinese nor fully American.

## Case: The Middleman Minority
Chinese immigrants often occupy an economic position between the white majority and other minority groups. They are business owners who serve a mostly non-Chinese customer base. This position can create tension: they face discrimination but are also perceived as "model minorities." Liang shows how this middleman role shapes the immigrant experience.

## Case: The Model Minority Myth
The "model minority" stereotype — that Asian Americans are successful, hardworking, and face no discrimination — is both true and false. It accurately reflects educational and economic achievement but obscures ongoing discrimination and the struggles of less-educated immigrants. Liang shows how the myth masks real challenges.
