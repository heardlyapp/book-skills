# Reference: Immigration Patterns

## Overview
Chinese immigration to the US has changed dramatically. Liang documents the shift from urban Chinatowns to rural and suburban America, driven by the Fuzhou diaspora.

## Case: The Fuzhou Wave
Since the 1990s, the Fuzhou region of Fujian province has been the primary source of Chinese immigrants. Unlike earlier waves from Hong Kong, Taiwan, and Guangdong, Fuzhou immigrants are more working-class and less educated. They come seeking economic opportunity. They work in Chinese restaurants across the country.

## Case: Chain Migration Networks
Chain migration is the mechanism. A pioneer immigrant arrives, works in a restaurant, saves money, and sponsors family members. The new arrivals do the same. The chain extends across towns and states. Liang shows how one restaurant job in a small town can lead to a community of hundreds over a generation.

## Case: The Role of Immigration Policy
US immigration policy shapes these patterns. Family-based immigration (visa categories for siblings, adult children) enables chain migration. The 1965 Immigration Act eliminated country quotas, creating the legal framework for large-scale Asian immigration. Liang argues that policy matters as much as individual motivation.

## Case: From Chinatowns to Everywhere
Traditional Chinatowns (NYC, San Francisco) still serve as gateways, but increasing numbers of Chinese immigrants bypass them entirely. New immigrants go directly to small towns or suburbs where a relative or community contact has established a restaurant. This is the "from Chinatown to every town" phenomenon.
