# Core Framework: What Feminism Means

> Source: We Should All Be Feminists, throughout

## The Core Argument

Feminism is the belief that women and men should have equal rights and opportunities. Adichie argues this should not be controversial. She defines a feminist as "a man or woman who says there's a problem with gender as it's today and we must fix it, we must do better."

> **Case: The Friend Who Changed** (throughout): A male friend told Adichie he became a feminist after his daughter was born. Before, he saw gender inequality as abstract. After having a daughter, he wanted her to have every opportunity a boy would. Personal connection changed his perspective.

> **Key takeaway**: Feminism is not complicated. It's the simple belief in equal rights and opportunities for all genders.

## The Problem with Gender Socialization

We teach girls to be likeable, modest, quiet, careful, and to dim their ambition. We teach boys to be strong, ambitious, loud, unemotional, and dominant. These lessons harm both sexes — limiting women's potential and men's emotional lives.

> **Case: The Primary School Friend** (throughout): Adichie describes a boy who was her best friend in primary school. He was gentle, creative, and sensitive. As they grew older, he was taught to suppress these qualities. By adulthood, he was a different person — closed off and emotionally distant. We socialize the sensitivity out of boys.

> **Key takeaway**: The problem is not biology — it's culture. And culture can change.
