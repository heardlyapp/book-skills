# Voice and Application: Implicit Bias, Race, and Inequality

> Source: The Broken Ladder, Chapter 7-8

## Implicit Bias Research

Keith Payne is the researcher who created the weapon perception task — the computerized test that measures how quickly people mistake harmless objects for guns when paired with black faces. His own results: he was biased even though he coded the test. Warning participants about the bias only made it worse.

> **Case: The Shooter Bias Experiment** (Chapter 7): Payne's own test. Photos of black or white faces flashed, followed by handguns or tools. Under time pressure, participants more often misidentified tools as guns when preceded by a black face. The bias appeared even in black participants. When warned about the bias, they tried harder — and became more biased.

> **Case: Devah Pager's Job Audit** (Chapter 7): Pairs of young men — one white, one black — applied for 350 jobs with identical résumés and scripts. White applicants were called back twice as often. Replicated in multiple cities, for apartments, cars, and mortgages.

> **Case: Florida Sentencing** (Chapter 7): Irene Blair's study of Florida prison mug shots. Inmates who looked "blacker" (as rated by independent judges) received 7-8 months longer sentences for identical crimes. Effect held regardless of actual race listed on the rap sheet.

## Application: Individual Awareness

Understanding implicit bias doesn't eliminate it, but it helps you build systems. Payne's advice: design choices that don't rely on snap judgments. Pre-commit to decisions. Create checklists for hiring.

## Application: Reducing Status Anxiety

The book's practical suggestions: subtract comparisons (unfollow wealth-flaunting accounts), add local perspective (your actual community is more equal than your Instagram feed), focus on absolute gains, build identity outside the ladder.

## Payne's Voice

The book is personal and academic. Payne opens with his own story of feeling poor in the school lunch line. He interweaves experimental psychology with memoir. His central message is not that inequality is unfair (though it is), but that understanding how it works can help you navigate it.
