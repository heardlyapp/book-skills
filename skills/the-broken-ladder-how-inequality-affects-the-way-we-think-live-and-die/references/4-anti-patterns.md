# Anti-Patterns: Common Traps

> Source: The Broken Ladder, Chapter 4, 6-7

## Zero-Sum Thinking

When inequality is high, people across the political spectrum become more likely to see the world as zero-sum: your gain is my loss. This drives polarization. Experiments show that making inequality salient increases support for both left-wing redistribution AND right-wing nationalism — depending on people's pre-existing values.

> **Case: Perceived Discrimination Gap** (Chapter 7): Whites increasingly believe anti-white bias is a bigger problem than anti-black bias — even as objective data show the racial wealth gap hasn't changed since the 1960s. Black families still earn 59 cents for every white dollar. White respondents saw discrimination as zero-sum: less against blacks meant more against them.

## System Justification

People have a powerful psychological need to believe the system is fair. This is why the poor sometimes oppose redistribution — acknowledging inequality as unfair would require believing the world is unjust, which is psychologically threatening.

> **Case: The Poor Voting Against Their Interests**: Low-income voters often support policies that benefit the wealthy, because they believe the system is meritocratic and that they or their children might one day be wealthy too.

## The Lake Wobegon Trap

Believing you are above average in everything prevents learning. The hospitalized accident victims who rated themselves as above-average drivers. The imprisoned felons who considered themselves more moral than average. The 94 percent of professors who said they were above-average teachers.

> **Key takeaway**: The belief that bias is always someone else's problem prevents us from addressing our own unexamined assumptions.
