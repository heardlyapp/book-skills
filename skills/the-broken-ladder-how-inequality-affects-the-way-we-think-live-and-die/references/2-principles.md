# Principles: How Inequality Changes Decision-Making

> Source: The Broken Ladder, Chapter 2-3

## Status-Seeking Behavior

When inequality is high, people become more motivated by status even when they don't realize it. They buy visible goods (cars, watches, handbags) over invisible ones (retirement savings, insurance). This is not about vanity — it's about signaling in a competitive environment.

> **Case: The BMW vs. Acura Study** (Chapter 3): When pay gaps within a company were made transparent, executives drove more expensive cars. The CEO's BMW was 70 percent more expensive when pay gaps were large vs. small. Status consumption increased with inequality even though salaries hadn't changed.

## Short-Term Focus

Inequality increases cortisol, the stress hormone. High cortisol impairs executive function in the prefrontal cortex, making impulse control harder. This creates a vicious cycle: feeling behind leads to bad decisions, which increase the gap.

> **Case: The Scrambled Sentences Experiment** (Chapter 2): Participants who unscrambled sentences about high-status words (prestigious, successful, wealthy) made riskier financial decisions in subsequent tasks than those who unscrambled neutral sentences. Just THINKING about status changed behavior.

> **Key takeaway**: When you feel like you're falling behind, your brain prioritizes immediate gratification over long-term planning. This looks like a character flaw but is actually a stress response.

## The Lake Wobegon Effect

Most people rate themselves above average in intelligence, driving, morality, and other traits. Convicted felons rated themselves above average in morality — except for "law abiding," where they rated themselves average. College professors: 94 percent rated themselves above average in teaching ability.

> **Key takeaway**: The desire to see ourselves as above average is so powerful it warps reality. This is not harmless — it makes us less able to learn from our mistakes.
