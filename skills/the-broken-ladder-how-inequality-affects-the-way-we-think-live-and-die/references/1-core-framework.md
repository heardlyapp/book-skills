# Core Framework: The Status Ladder

> Source: The Broken Ladder, Chapter 1-2

## The Status Ladder

The core tool of the book: a 10-rung ladder where the top represents the best-off people and the bottom represents the worst-off. When people place themselves on this ladder, only 20 percent of their position is explained by income, education, and job prestige. The other 80 percent is determined by social context — who they compare themselves to. This subjective position powerfully predicts health, happiness, decision-making, and lifespan.

> **Case: Lunch Lady Economics** (Chapter 1): Keith Payne learned he was "poor" when a new lunch lady asked him for $1.25 and he had no money. His family's income hadn't changed, but his perception had. He became silent at school, began noticing differences in shoes, haircuts, and accents. Nothing changed in the world — only his perspective. But that changed his future. The moment illustrates how subjective status can transform behavior more than objective poverty.

> **Case: Air Rage Study** (Chapter 1): Researchers DeCelles and Norton analyzed millions of flights. Air rage was four times higher in coach on planes with a first-class cabin vs. planes without one — equal to a 9.5-hour delay. When passengers boarded at the front (parading past first class), rage doubled vs. mid-plane boarding. Even the first-class passengers were affected: they were several times more likely to erupt in rage when seated near the rabble.

## Subjective Social Status (SSS)

The book's central finding: how you feel about your economic position matters more than where you objectively stand. Lower SSS predicts depression, anxiety, chronic pain, poor decision-making, belief in conspiracy theories, obesity, diabetes, heart problems, and shorter lifespan — regardless of actual income.

> **Key takeaway**: "I am not simply asserting that, if you are poor, then all of these things are more likely to happen to you. These things are more likely to happen to you if you feel poor, regardless of your actual income."

## Relative Deprivation

The gap between what you have and what others have — not what you have in absolute terms — drives satisfaction. Studies show that people would rather earn $50,000 when everyone else earns $25,000 than $100,000 when others earn $250,000. Your neighbor's new house literally shrinks yours in your mind.
