# Anti-Patterns

## 1. Cargo Cult Science

**The mistake**: Doing research that looks scientific but lacks rigorous testing.

**Fix**: The key is honest testing. If your conclusion is foreordained, it's not science.

> **Case: The Psychic Study** (Chapter 6): Feynman describes a study of psychic phenomena where researchers used loose methodology: not controlling for chance, not double-blinding, interpreting ambiguous results in their favor. The study looked scientific — but it was cargo cult science.
> **Key takeaway**: The form of science without the substance is worse than no science at all. Rigorous methodology is not optional.

## 2. Certainty Without Evidence

**The mistake**: Believing something is true because it feels right, because authority says so, or because everyone believes it.

**Fix**: Demand evidence. Accept that you might be wrong.

## 3. Protecting Your Ego Instead of Finding the Truth

**The mistake**: Defending your position because you're invested in it, not because it's correct.

**Fix**: The goal is not to be right — it's to find things out. Being wrong is part of the process.

## 4. Trusting Authority Over Observation

**The mistake**: Believing something because an expert said so, without checking the evidence.

**Fix**: Nature is the only authority that matters. Even Nobel laureates can be wrong.

> **Case: The Textbook Error** (Chapter 10): Feynman found an error in a physics textbook. When he pointed it out, the author defended it because "it had been taught that way for years." Feynman's response: "Science doesn't care how long something has been believed. It cares whether it's true."
> **Key takeaway**: Tradition is not evidence. The age of a belief has nothing to do with its truth.
