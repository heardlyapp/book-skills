# Core Framework: The Joy of Discovery

## The Central Thesis

"The pleasure of finding things out is the greatest reward of science. Not the Nobel Prize, not the prestige — the joy of discovering something new about how the world works."

> **Case: The Wobbling Plate** (Chapter 1): Feynman describes seeing a student toss a plate in the cafeteria. The plate wobbled and the Cornell medallion on it spun at a different rate than the wobble. Feynman became obsessed with figuring out the relationship. He derived equations — just for fun. This led to his Nobel Prize work in quantum electrodynamics.
> **Key takeaway**: Feynman's greatest discoveries came from playful curiosity, not from strategic planning. Follow the fun.

## Key Concepts

| Concept | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| Scientific Method | Observe, hypothesize, test, revise |
| Uncertainty | Scientific knowledge is always provisional |
| Honest Thinking | Not fooling yourself or others |
| Cargo Cult Science | Pseudoscience that mimics real science without substance |
| Nature Is the Judge | Theories must match observation |

> **Case: The O-Ring Demonstration** (Chapter 3): At the Challenger disaster hearings, Feynman famously dipped an O-ring in ice water to show it became brittle at low temperatures. This simple demonstration, done in front of TV cameras, revealed the cause of the disaster more clearly than thousands of pages of testimony.
> **Key takeaway**: The simplest explanation, when tested against reality, is often the most powerful. Feynman's genius was finding a one-minute experiment that cut through all the bureaucracy.
