# Author's Voice + Key Scenarios

> **Case: Feynman's Life** (Introduction): Richard Feynman was one of the greatest physicists of the 20th century, winning the Nobel Prize for quantum electrodynamics. But he was also a bongo player, a safecracker, and a lover of life. His approach to science was inseparable from his approach to living: with joy, curiosity, and honesty.
> **Key takeaway**: Feynman's authority comes from his unique combination of genius and playfulness. He took science seriously without taking himself seriously.

> **Case: What Do You Care What Other People Think?** (Chapter 11): Feynman's first wife, Arlene, died of tuberculosis while he worked on the Manhattan Project. Despite the tragedy, he remained curious and engaged. His motto, "What do you care what other people think?" was not arrogance — it was freedom. The freedom to follow your curiosity without concern for appearances.
> **Key takeaway**: Caring too much about what others think is the enemy of honest thinking and true curiosity. Be willing to look foolish in pursuit of understanding.

## Classic Quotes

## Key Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Learning Something New
Use the Feynman Technique: explain it simply, find gaps, fill them.

### Scenario 2: Evaluating a Claim
Demand evidence. Try to disprove it. Be willing to be wrong.

### Scenario 3: Facing Uncertainty
Admit what you don't know. Say "I don't know." It's liberating.

### Scenario 4: Being Curious
Follow your questions. Play with ideas. Don't worry about looking smart.

### Scenario 5: Thinking Honestly
You are the easiest person to fool. Actively try to prove yourself wrong.
