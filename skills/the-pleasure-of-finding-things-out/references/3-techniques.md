# Techniques: Thinking Like Feynman

## The Feynman Technique for Learning

1. Choose a concept you want to understand
2. Explain it as simply as possible, as if teaching a child
3. Identify gaps in your explanation — these are what you don't truly understand
4. Go back to the source material and fill the gaps
5. Repeat until you can explain it simply

> **Case: The Feynman Lectures** (Chapter 8): Feynman gave introductory physics lectures at Caltech that became famous for their clarity. He didn't use jargon or assumed knowledge. He started from first principles and built up. His approach: if you can't explain something simply, you don't understand it well enough.
> **Key takeaway**: The test of understanding is the ability to explain. If you can't explain it to a beginner, you haven't truly learned it.

## The Scientific Method in Practice

1. Observe something you don't understand
2. Formulate a hypothesis
3. Design an experiment that can falsify it
4. Run the experiment honestly
5. If the hypothesis is wrong, revise and repeat
6. If it survives testing, share it — but remain open to being wrong

## Feynman's Rules for Honest Thinking

1. You are the easiest person to fool
2. Try to prove yourself wrong before proving yourself right
3. Ask what evidence would change your mind
4. Be willing to say "I don't know"
5. Nature is the final judge — not authority, not popularity, not tradition

> **Case: The Rubber Band** (Chapter 9): Feynman played with rubber bands to understand why they heat up when stretched. He chewed on them, stretched them, measured temperatures. He looked foolish — but he was learning. "What do you care what other people think?" was his motto.
> **Key takeaway**: True curiosity is indifferent to appearances. Don't worry about looking smart. Worry about understanding.
