# Core Framework: The Extended Phenotype

> Source: The Extended Phenotype, Preface and Chapters 1-2

## The Central Concept

A gene's effects are not limited to the body it occupies. They extend outward into the environment. A spider's web is an extended phenotype of spider genes — as much a product of natural selection as the spider's legs.

> **Case: The Beaver Dam** (Chapter 1): A beaver's dam is not part of its body, but it is caused by beaver genes. Natural selection shaped dam-building because dams benefited beaver ancestors. The dam is an adaptation — an extended phenotype.

> **Case: The Caddis Fly Case** (Chapter 1): A caddis fly larva builds a protective case from pebbles and silk. The case is as much an adaptation as the larva's body. It is the extended phenotype of caddis fly genes — every bit as evolved as the larva itself.

> **Key takeaway**: Phenotypic effects of genes are not bounded by the organism's skin. Artifacts, structures, and environmental modifications are all biological adaptations.

## Replicator vs Vehicle

The fundamental unit of natural selection is the replicator (gene), not the vehicle (organism).

> **Case: The Gene-Centered Perspective** (Chapter 2): To understand adaptation, take the gene's view. Genes are replicators that survive or die based on the vehicles they build. Organisms are the vehicles, not the ultimate beneficiaries.

> **Key takeaway**: Shift your perspective from organism to gene. That's the key to understanding the extended phenotype concept.
