# Anti-Patterns: Misreading the Extended Phenotype

> Source: The Extended Phenotype, Chapters 9-11

## Confusing Extended Phenotype with Group Selection

The extended phenotype does NOT imply group selection. Genes are still the unit of selection.

> **Case: The Group Selection Fallacy** (Chapter 9): Some readers think extended phenotype means the environment is a "group" adaptation. Dawkins clarifies: extended phenotypes benefit the gene, not the group. The beaver dam benefits beaver genes, not the ecosystem.

> **Key takeaway**: Extended phenotype is gene-level thinking, not group-level. Don't confuse the two.

## Thinking Behavior Is Not Phenotype

Behavior is often treated as separate from "biology." Dawkins argues behavior is phenotype.

> **Case: The Bird's Song** (Chapter 10): A bird's song is behavior — but it's caused by genes and shaped by natural selection. It's an extended phenotype. The distinction between "behavior" and "biology" is artificial.

> **Key takeaway**: Behavior is phenotype. The extended phenotype includes all effects of genes, including behavior.

## Limiting Phenotype to the Body

The most persistent error: thinking phenotype = body. The extended phenotype concept challenges this directly.

> **Case: The Body Boundary** (Chapter 11): Why assume the organism's skin is the boundary of phenotype? A snail's shell is secreted by the body. A bird's nest is built by the body. The only difference is physical attachment.

> **Key takeaway**: The organism's skin is not a meaningful boundary for phenotypic effects. The extended phenotype has no fixed limit.
