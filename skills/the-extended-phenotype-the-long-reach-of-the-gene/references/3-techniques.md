# Techniques: Artifacts and Manipulation

> Source: The Extended Phenotype, Chapters 6-8

## Animal Artifacts

The most visible extended phenotypes. Structures built by animals are as much biological adaptations as their organs.

> **Case: The Spider's Web** (Chapter 6): A spider's orb web is an extended phenotype of spider genes. The web-building behavior, silk chemistry, and web architecture are all shaped by natural selection acting on spider genes.

> **Key takeaway**: Artifacts are adaptations. They must be explained by natural selection just like any other trait.

## Environmental Modification

Genes that build organisms that modify environments create extended phenotypes that can persist beyond the organism's life.

> **Case: The Termite Mound** (Chapter 7): Termite mounds are massive extended phenotypes of termite genes. They regulate temperature, humidity, and gas exchange. The mound is as much a termite adaptation as the termite's exoskeleton.

> **Key takeaway**: Environmental modifications — dams, mounds, burrows — are extended phenotypes that can last for generations.

## The Long Reach

How far can a gene's influence extend? Dawkins argues there's no theoretical limit — only practical ones.

> **Case: The Parasite's Extended Reach** (Chapter 8): A parasite that causes its host to be eaten by a predator is reaching across two organisms and one predation event. The gene's influence extends through multiple "handlings" to reach its goal.

> **Key takeaway**: Artifacts are not just tools. They are the extended phenotype of genes — as evolved as any organ.
