# Principles: The Gene's Eye View

> Source: The Extended Phenotype, Chapters 3-5

## The Organism as Vehicle

The intuitive view — organisms as fundamental units — is misleading. Organisms are vehicles genes build for replication.

> **Case: The Colonial Organism** (Chapter 3): Portuguese man o' war blurs individual vs colony. Each "individual" is actually a colony of specialized organisms. The gene's eye view cuts through: genes don't care about organism boundaries.

> **Key takeaway**: The organism is a vehicle, not a fundamental unit. Replicators (genes) are the units that matter.

## Adaptation for the Gene's Benefit

Adaptations are "for" something. Dawkins argues that something is the gene.

> **Case: The Altruism Puzzle** (Chapter 4): Animals sacrifice themselves for kin. The gene-centered view explains: altruism toward kin benefits copies of the same gene. Adaptation serves the gene, not the organism or the species.

> **Case: The Sterile Worker** (Chapter 4): Worker ants are sterile — they sacrifice reproduction for the queen. This makes no sense from the organism's perspective but perfect sense from the gene's: the worker carries copies of the queen's genes.

> **Key takeaway**: Altruism, sterility, and self-sacrifice are all explained by gene-level selection.

## Parasite Manipulation

The clearest extended phenotype. Parasite genes manipulate host behavior.

> **Case: The Fluke in the Snail** (Chapter 5): A fluke parasite causes its snail host to behave in ways that attract birds — the fluke's next host. The snail's behavior is an extended phenotype of fluke genes.

> **Key takeaway**: Parasite manipulation shows the extended phenotype in its most dramatic form: one genome controlling another organism's behavior.
