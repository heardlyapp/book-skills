# Techniques — Conscious Business

## How to Build a Conscious Organization (Based on Fred Kofman)

### Technique 1: The Responsibility Shift
When something goes wrong, ask: "What can I do about it now?" instead of "Whose fault is it?" Kofman's driving metaphor: someone cuts you off. You can blame them (past-oriented, gives you no power) or decide how to respond (future-oriented, gives you full power). Case: the Argentinean soccer team losing at halftime chose to take responsibility rather than blame — they won 3-0.

### Technique 2: The "I Could Be Wrong" Pause
Before asserting your view as truth, pause and say: "I could be wrong." This is ontological humility in action. Kofman: "I'm not saying 'I'm wrong and you're right.' I'm saying 'I could be wrong.' This opens a space for genuine inquiry." Case: In meetings, when someone insists their position is correct, asking "What would make it true?" shifts from debate to inquiry.

### Technique 3: Speaking in "I" Statements
Separate observations from judgments. "You're always late" is a judgment — and provokes defensiveness. "I noticed you arrived after the meeting started. I felt frustrated because I value starting on time" is an observation + feeling + value. "A thought is never a fact. A fact is a thought that many people agree on."

### Technique 4: The Commitment Protocol
When making a promise, be explicit about four elements: Who is committing? What exactly are they committing to do? By when? With what standard of satisfaction? Vague: "I'll get back to you." Precise: "I will send you the report by Friday at 5 PM with at least 90% of the data." When you can't keep a promise, renegotiate immediately.

### Technique 5: The Win-Win Exploration
When disagreeing, separate positions from interests. The Orange dispute: Two sisters both wanted the same orange. Their positions were identical ("I want the orange"). Their interests were different (one wanted juice, the other wanted peel for cake). Explore interests before proposing solutions. "Win-win requires genuine curiosity about what the other person truly needs."

### Technique 6: The Emotional Signal Check
When you feel a strong negative emotion, pause and ask: "What is this emotion telling me about my situation?" Kofman distinguishes primary emotions (signals about reality) from secondary emotions (signals about our reaction). Case: The manager who raged at a subordinate's mistake — the real issue wasn't the mistake, it was his own fear of looking bad to his boss.

### Technique 7: The Three Dimensions Audit
Assess any business issue across all three dimensions: It (task/results/technical), We (relationships/culture/trust), I (self/meaning/happiness). Kofman: "Most managers focus only on the It." Monthly, ask your team: How are our results? (It). How are our relationships? (We). How are you doing personally? (I).
