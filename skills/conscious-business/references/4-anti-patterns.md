# Anti-Patterns — Conscious Business

## The Central Error: "Business Is a Machine"

"Most managers — and investors — focus only on the It. Stripped of the human dimensions, business appears to be an unconscious activity." Marcus the research manager tried to treat people like molecules — predictable, controllable, formulaic. It failed completely.

### Mistake 1: "Blame is management"
Blame looks backward. Responsibility looks forward. "The moment you blame someone, you give away your power." A team that spent an hour blaming the accounting department for a reporting error is a team that hasn't done anything to fix the problem.

### Mistake 2: "My view is reality"
"Ontological arrogance" is treating your mental model as objective truth. Kofman's experience in Argentina: "The country was plastered with bumper stickers that read, 'Argentineans are human and righteous.' We didn't want it to be true." His own blindness to the death camp beneath the Navy School of Engineering is the ultimate cautionary tale.

### Mistake 3: "Compromise is the goal of negotiation"
The Orange dispute shows compromise is inferior. Both sisters settled for half when they could have had all of what they wanted. "Compromise is settling for less than either party could have. Win-win creates more value than either party expected."

### Mistake 4: "Vague commitments are polite"
"I'll get back to you" is not a commitment — it's an intention. Vague commitments are the #1 cause of coordination failure. "A promise is a declaration that creates a future." Without specific who/what/when/standard, a promise is meaningless.

### Mistake 5: "Emotions have no place in business"
"Emotions are not good or bad — they are signals." Repressing emotions is not emotional mastery — it's emotional denial. "The manager who raged at his subordinate wasn't expressing too much emotion — he was expressing the wrong emotion, at the wrong time, in the wrong way."

### Mistake 6: "Success is just about results"
"Success without human development is hollow." Kofman left his tenured position at MIT not because he failed — but because "success" at a prestigious university felt empty. "I had climbed the proverbial ladder, but when I reached the zenith of my academic career, I realized that I had placed it against the proverbial wrong wall."
