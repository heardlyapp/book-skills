> Source: Ordinary Men, Chapters 7-14

## Practical Techniques (from the book's analysis)

### 1. The Conformity Audit

When making a decision in a group, ask: "Am I doing this because it is right — or because I don't want to appear weak/different/disloyal?" The distinction is crucial.

### 2. The Refuser's Test

Identify the social cost of dissent in your environment. If refusing to participate in something wrong would cost you status or relationships, you are in a high-risk environment for ethical compromise.

> **Case: The Social Cost of Refusal** (Chapter 8): Men who refused to shoot at Józefów were not punished by their commanders. But their comrades refused to speak to them. They were mocked. One said: "They looked at me like I was a coward. I thought about it for days. In the next action, I shot." Social exclusion was unbearable.

### 3. The Escalation Awareness

Recognize that ethical boundaries erode gradually. The first compromise is the most important to resist — after that, the next becomes exponentially easier.

> **Case: The Jew Hunt** (Chapter 14): By late 1942, the battalion had moved from mass shootings to hunting individual Jews hiding in the forests. Men who had vomited at Józefów now hunted for sport. One man testified: "It became a game. We would track them, then shoot. I felt nothing." Desensitization was complete within months.
> **Key takeaway**: What is unthinkable today can become routine tomorrow. The only protection is to refuse the first step.

The strongest protection against ethical erosion is not a strong character in the abstract — it is the willingness to be the one person who says no, knowing the social cost.
