> Source: Ordinary Men, Chapters 1-18 — 7 principles of perpetrator psychology

## Principle 1: Ordinary People, Not Monsters

The men of Battalion 101 were not SS, not ideological Nazis. They were middle-aged working-class men from Hamburg. They had families. They had normal jobs. They became killers because of the situation they were placed in.

## Principle 2: Conformity Is the Strongest Motivator

The men killed because they did not want to let their comrades down. This is the central finding of the book. Peer pressure — not ideology, not hatred, not coercion — is what turned them into killers.

> **Case: The Man Who Wanted to Prove Himself** (Chapter 7): A 35-year-old policeman testified: "I remember thinking that I was being tested. I wanted to show that I was as tough as the others." He shot a woman and child at Józefów. Later he suffered nightmares for months. But the need to prove himself was stronger than his conscience.

## Principle 3: The First Killing Is the Hardest

Nearly every man described the first massacre as traumatic. Some vomited. Some drank heavily. But each subsequent killing was easier. Desensitization is rapid.

## Principle 4: The Choice Was Real

Trapp explicitly offered to excuse anyone who could not participate. Only 12 of 500 took the offer. The killers cannot claim they had no choice.

> **Case: The Man Who Refused Repeatedly** (Chapter 8): One man refused to shoot during every action. He was not punished — he was assigned to drive trucks. When asked why he refused, he said simply: "I couldn't do it." He was the exception, but his existence proves that refusal was possible.

## Principle 5: Ideology Was Secondary

Some men were motivated by anti-Semitism. Many were not. Most killed because killing was expected of them.

## Principle 6: The Killing Escalated

From the traumatic first massacre at Józefów, the battalion moved to systematic deportation, then to "Jew hunts" where men hunted individual Jews hiding in forests. Hunting became routine. Some men even enjoyed it.

## Principle 7: Aftermath Is Uncomfortable

Most of the killers returned to normal lives after the war. They became policemen again, retired, had grandchildren. Only a small number were ever prosecuted. The banality of evil is in the ordinariness of the killers' post-war lives.
