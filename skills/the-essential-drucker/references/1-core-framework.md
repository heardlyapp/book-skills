> Source: The Essential Drucker, Parts 1-3

# Core Framework: The Five Deadly Sins

## The Central Thesis

"Management is about making people capable of joint performance through common goals, shared values, right structure, and the training and development they need to perform." Drucker's entire framework is oriented toward making organizations effective in the marketplace.

> **Case: The Deadly Sin of Cost-Driven Pricing** (Part 2): Drucker describes companies that set prices based on their costs rather than what the customer is willing to pay. This guarantees a profit margin only if costs stay within budget. When costs rise, the company either must raise prices (losing customers) or accept lower margins. The correct approach: start with what the customer will pay and work backward to determine allowable costs.
> **Key takeaway**: Price-driven costing forces companies to innovate and control costs. Cost-driven pricing protects inefficiency and ignores the customer.

## The Five Deadly Sins

| Sin | Description | Fix |
|-----|-------------|-----|
| Deifying profit margins | Worshiping high margins invites competition | Price to attract customers, not maximize margins |
| Mispricing new products | Charging what the market will bear | Price for what creates a permanent market |
| Cost-driven pricing | Setting price based on costs | Start with customer's price, determine costs |
| Killing tomorrow's opportunity | Protecting yesterday's products | Shift resources to future opportunities |
| Feeding problems, starving opportunities | Resources flow to fixing what's wrong | Redirect resources to growth opportunities |

> **Case: IBM's Mistake** (Part 3): Drucker describes how IBM dominated the mainframe market by feeding yesterday's success while starving the emerging personal computer opportunity. The result: IBM missed the PC revolution that it had essentially invented. The sin was not lack of innovation — it was feeding yesterday at the expense of tomorrow.
> **Key takeaway**: The most dangerous moment for a successful company is when it's still succeeding on yesterday's formula. Resources must flow to tomorrow's opportunities, not yesterday's problems.
