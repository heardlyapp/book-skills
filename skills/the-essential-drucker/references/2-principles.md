# Key Principles

## 1. The Purpose of Business Is to Create a Customer

"Because the purpose of business is to create a customer, the business has two basic functions: marketing and innovation. Everything else is cost." This is Drucker's most fundamental principle. If you're not creating customers, you're not doing business.

## 2. Management by Objectives

"Objectives are needed in every area where performance and results directly affect the survival and prosperity of the business." MBO requires clear, measurable objectives that align every person in the organization toward common goals.

## 3. The Knowledge Worker Requires Different Management

"The knowledge worker cannot be supervised closely or in detail. They can only be helped." Drucker recognized that managing people who think requires autonomy, responsibility, and continuous learning — not command and control.

> **Case: The Effective Nurse** (Part 5): Drucker describes a hospital where nurses were spending 40% of their time on paperwork instead of patient care. The solution was not to tell nurses to work harder — it was to redesign the system so nurses could focus on their highest-value work. Management's job is to remove obstacles, not to supervise.
> **Key takeaway**: Management's highest function is to create the conditions for knowledge workers to do their best work. Micromanagement destroys knowledge worker productivity.

## 4. Focus on Opportunities, Not Problems

"Problem-solving prevents damage. Opportunity exploitation creates results." Drucker advocates that the best people and resources should be focused on opportunities, not problems. Feeding opportunities while starving problems is the key to growth.

## 5. Make Effective Decisions

"A decision is a judgment. It is a choice between alternatives. It is rarely a choice between right and wrong. It is at best a choice between almost right and probably wrong." The effective executive must make decisions based on principles.

> **Case: The Decision Process** (Part 6): Drucker outlines the decision process: 1) Define the problem 2) Specify the minimum requirements 3) Determine what is right 4) Build acceptance into the decision 5) Build feedback into the decision. Most decisions fail because they skip steps.
> **Key takeaway**: Decisions should be made systematically, not intuitively. A good process produces better decisions over time.
