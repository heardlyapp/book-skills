# Anti-Patterns

## 1. Cost-Driven Pricing

**The mistake**: Setting prices based on costs rather than what the customer will pay.

**Fix**: Start with the customer's price. Work backward to determine allowable costs.

## 2. Feeding Problems Instead of Opportunities

**The mistake**: Putting the best people on fixing problems rather than pursuing opportunities.

**Fix**: Assign your best resources to the biggest opportunities. Let problems solve themselves or assign them to less critical people.

> **Case: The Troubled Division** (Part 2): A company assigned its best executive to fix a troubled division. The division stabilized but never grew. Meanwhile, a high-growth opportunity was neglected. The company lost more from the missed opportunity than it gained from fixing the problem.
> **Key takeaway**: Opportunity exploitation creates more value than problem solving. Put your best people where the future is, not where the past went wrong.

> **Case: The Japanese Advantage** (Part 2): Drucker observed that Japanese automakers used price-driven costing — they started with what the customer could pay and designed cars to meet that price. American automakers used cost-driven pricing — set a target profit margin and priced accordingly. The Japanese approach produced better cars at lower prices.
>
> **Key takeaway**: Price-driven costing forces innovation. It compels you to find ways to deliver value at a price the customer will accept. Cost-driven pricing protects the status quo.

## 3. Mispricing New Products

**The mistake**: Charging high initial prices to capture maximum profit from early adopters.

**Fix**: Price to create a permanent market, not to maximize short-term profit. High prices invite competition.

## 4. Micromanaging Knowledge Workers

**The mistake**: Treating knowledge workers like manual workers — supervising, directing, controlling.

**Fix**: Give knowledge workers autonomy, clear objectives, and the resources they need. Then get out of their way.

## 5. Ignoring the Customer

**The mistake**: Defining the business by what the company produces rather than what the customer values.

**Fix**: The customer defines the business. Ask: "What does the customer value?" and build everything around that answer.
