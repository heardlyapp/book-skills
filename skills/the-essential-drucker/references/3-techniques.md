# Techniques: Drucker's Management Practices

## The Effective Executive's Five Practices

1. **Know your time** — Log it, manage it, consolidate it. Time is the most scarce resource.
2. **Focus on contribution** — Ask: "What can I contribute to this organization's results?"
3. **Build on strengths** — Your own, your team's, and your organization's. Don't focus on weaknesses.
4. **Set priorities** — Do first things first. Do one thing at a time. Eliminate the non-essential.
5. **Make effective decisions** — Follow the decision process systematically.

> **Case: The Executive's Time Log** (Part 7): An executive kept a time log for two weeks and discovered he spent 60% of his time on things that didn't matter. He eliminated those activities and freed up three hours a day for substantive work. The time log was the diagnostic tool.
> **Key takeaway**: You cannot manage your time until you know where it goes. A time log for two weeks is the most valuable management exercise you can do.

> **Case: The Focus on Strengths** (Part 8): A manager spent months trying to improve a team member's weakness. The improvement was marginal. Frustration was high. When Drucker suggested focusing on the team member's strengths instead, performance dramatically improved. The weakness was still there — but now it didn't matter.
>
> **Key takeaway**: Building on strengths produces disproportionate results. Fixing weaknesses produces marginal improvement at best. Put people where their strengths can be productive.

## The Innovation Audit

1. Look for unexpected successes and failures — they reveal opportunities
2. Find process needs — where is the weak link in your industry?
3. Watch for industry and market changes
4. Notice demographic changes
5. Pay attention to changes in perception
6. Seek new knowledge

## Setting Objectives

For each area of the business:
1. Define the objective clearly
2. Set measurable targets
3. Assign responsibility
4. Establish timelines
5. Review progress regularly
