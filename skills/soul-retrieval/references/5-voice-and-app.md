# 5 — Voice & App

> Source: *Soul Retrieval* by Sandra Ingerman.

## Ingerman's Voice

Ingerman writes as a teacher and practitioner — warm, direct, and deeply respectful of the shamanic tradition. She does not present herself as a guru. She presents as a student who learned from spirits and teachers, and who now shares what she has learned. The tone is gentle but authoritative: she has done this work thousands of times and knows what she is talking about, but she always credits the spirits, not herself.

> **Case: The Opening of the Journey Chapter** (Chapter 5): Ingerman invites the reader into a darkened room as a privileged observer. "Settle back now in your privileged role as a safe observer. Watch with interest and curiosity as each client comes to this room for a session." The entire chapter is written as shared witness — the reader is present as Ingerman travels, sees the Cave of Lost Children, negotiates with lost souls. The writing is cinematic and deeply empathetic.

> **Case: The Teacher's Instruction** (Introduction): When Ingerman journeyed to ask her teacher about writing this book, the teacher said only: "Write, and write from your heart." After three chapters, she journeyed again. The teacher said she was moving in the wrong direction — the book would frighten people. "This book must be a healing for every person who reads it, whether or not they ever decide to have a soul retrieval." This humility — being corrected by spirit — is central to Ingerman's voice.

## Five Scenarios

### 1. The Trauma Survivor

You have experienced abuse, accident, or major loss. You feel like a part of you never came back from that moment. Soul retrieval can find that part and bring it home. Work with a trained practitioner. The integration period matters as much as the retrieval itself.

### 2. The Therapy Graduate

You have done years of talk therapy, CBT, EMDR, or other modalities. You have made progress. But there is something that therapy couldn't reach — an emptiness, a lost feeling, a missing spark. Soul retrieval addresses the spiritual dimension of illness that conventional therapy does not.

### 3. The Healer/Practitioner

You want to learn shamanic journeying to help others. Ingerman's book provides the framework but is not a substitute for direct training. The Foundation for Shamanic Studies offers workshops worldwide. Ethical practice requires in-person training, not just reading.

### 4. The Person Experiencing Chronic Relationship Patterns

Your relationships keep failing in the same way. You attract unavailable partners, or you push away people who love you. Soul loss from early relationships — abandonment, betrayal, neglect — can create patterns that no amount of conscious effort seems to change.

### 5. The Seeker on a Spiritual Path

You feel called to a deeper understanding of your own soul. Soul retrieval is not only for people in crisis. It is for anyone who wants to feel more whole, more present, and more alive. Even without a dramatic trauma, small soul losses accumulate over a lifetime.
