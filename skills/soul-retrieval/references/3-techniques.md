# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *Soul Retrieval* by Sandra Ingerman.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The Tracking Method

When searching for a lost soul part in nonordinary reality: (1) Set a clear intention for what you seek. (2) Ask your power animal or teacher for guidance. (3) Look for personal markers — especially jewelry that matches what the client is wearing. The soul part at any age wears the same jewelry. (4) Notice the environment: Upper World (floating, space, light), Lower World (underground, caves, earth), or Middle World (ordinary places frozen in time).

> **Case: Susan's Silver Ring** (Chapter 5): When searching for Susan's lost part, Ingerman first asked to see her jewelry — a delicate silver ring on her right hand. In the journey, the 7-year-old soul part was hiding behind a rock in space. Before revealing herself, the child peeked out. Ingerman saw the silver ring. The matching marker confirmed she had found the right soul.
> **Key takeaway**: Personal markers — jewelry, tattoos, distinctive clothing — are tracking anchors across time and reality. They connect the present adult with the past soul part.

If the lost part refuses to return: (1) Don't force. Coercion creates harm. (2) Have the power animal remind the soul of what it loved. (3) Explain that the adult now lives in a different, safer reality. (4) Let the adult client (through the practitioner) express genuine need for the part. (5) Give the soul choice and respect.

### 3. The Return Ritual

After the journey: (1) Sit up and blow the retrieved essence into the client's chest and crown of the head. (2) Rattle around the client four times to seal the soul part into the body. (3) Say: "Welcome home." (4) Share the journey contents with care — plant seeds of hope, not fear. (5) Advise the client to be gentle with themselves for the next few weeks.

### 4. The Soul Remembering Technique

In Ingerman's later work, she developed soul remembering as a complement to soul retrieval: the practitioner journeys to a time before the person was born and sees their original essence — the beauty, gifts, and strengths they came into the world to express. This helps people remember who they truly are, not who they were told they are.

### 5. Daily Self-Integration Practice

Chapter 11 provides exercises: (1) Light a candle. (2) Ask the returned part: "What do you need today?" (3) Listen without judgment. (4) Act on what you hear where appropriate. This daily practice maintains the connection between adult and child parts.

> **Case: Ellen's Anger Revealed** (Chapter 5): During Ellen's retrieval, the 5-year-old soul part said something crucial: "Ellen thinks she has to be big and angry to protect me." The adult Ellen had never connected her chronic anger with the need to protect her inner child. The daily integration practice — asking the returned part what it needs — would reveal whether the child still feels unsafe and what would help.
> **Key takeaway**: The returned soul part often holds the key to understanding behaviors that therapy alone could not explain. Asking directly is the simplest and most powerful tool.
