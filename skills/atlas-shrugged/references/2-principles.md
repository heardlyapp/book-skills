> Source: Atlas Shrugged, Ayn Rand (1957).

# Key Principles — The Trader Principle, The Virtue of Selfishness, and the Morality of Capitalism

## Core Principles

> **Case: The Trader Principle** (Francisco d'Anconia's speech): Francisco d'Anconia, the playboy heir to a copper fortune, delivers a devastating analysis of money. "Money is the root of all good." He argues that money is not the root of evil but the symbol of the trader principle — the exchange of value for value. When money is honest, it represents the productive work of the person who earned it. When the state can create money out of nothing, money becomes a tool of plunder. Francisco's speech is the novel's most explicit defense of capitalism as a moral system.
> **Key takeaway**: Money earned through voluntary trade is moral. Money taken by force is not. The trader principle — value for value — is the only ethical basis for human interaction.

> **Case: The Looters and the Makers** (Throughout): Rand divides the characters into two groups: the makers (Dagny, Rearden, Galt, Francisco, D'Anconia) and the looters (James Taggart, Wesley Mouch, Dr. Ferris, the politicians). The makers create value. The looters redistribute it. The conflict is not between labor and capital. It is between the producers and the parasites. The looters always claim to act for the "public good." But the public good never includes the right of the makers to keep what they produce.
> **Key takeaway**: The fundamental conflict in any society is not between rich and poor. It is between those who produce and those who seize the products of others. This conflict exists in every organization and every relationship.

> **Case: The Sanction of the Victim** (Galt's Speech): Galt's most radical idea: the victims of the looters are complicit in their own destruction. They accept the moral code of the looters. They feel guilty for their success. They apologize for their competence. They wait for permission. The sanction of the victim is the psychological mechanism that enables tyranny. The looters cannot force the makers to work. But if the makers believe they OWE their work to others, they will work voluntarily — and hate themselves for it.
> **Key takeaway**: The worst thing the looters do is not taking your wealth. It is making you feel guilty for having it. The sanction of the victim is the most dangerous idea you can accept. Stop accepting it.
