> Source: Atlas Shrugged, Ayn Rand (1957).

# Anti-Patterns — Altruism, The Greater Good, and the Unearned

## The Central Fallacy

The most dangerous assumption: that self-sacrifice is the highest moral ideal. Rand calls this the morality of altruism — and argues it is the root of every evil in the modern world. Altruism teaches that your life belongs to others, that your needs are secondary, that your success is owed to those who have not earned it. The result: guilt in the productive, entitlement in the parasitic, and a society that punishes achievement.

> **Case: James Taggart** (Throughout): James Taggart, Dagny's brother and president of Taggart Transcontinental, is the novel's archetype of the looter. He does not produce. He cannot manage. He survives by manipulating, by claiming to act for the "public good," and by attacking those who actually produce. He feels entitled to his position despite his incompetence. He resents Dagny for being competent. He represents a world where the least capable rise to the top by claiming to serve others — and the most capable are ground down by guilt.
> **Key takeaway**: The James Taggarts of the world survive by making the productive feel guilty. Recognize this pattern. Refuse the guilt.

> **Case: The Directive 10-289** (Climax): The government issues Directive 10-289, which freezes the economy: no one can change jobs, start a business, or introduce new products without government permission. It is the logical conclusion of the morality of sacrifice. All individual rights are abolished in the name of the "public good." The directive is not portrayed as a sudden tyranny. It is the natural result of decades of accepting that the group comes before the individual.
> **Key takeaway**: Every sacrifice of individual rights to the "greater good" prepares the ground for the next sacrifice. There is no stopping point. The only defense is to refuse the premise that the group has rights over the individual.

> **Case: The Wet Nurse** (Rearden's frustration): Rearden is assigned a "wet nurse" — a government official whose job is to oversee his business. The wet nurse has no competence in steel manufacturing. He is there to ensure that Rearden complies with the public interest. Rearden, who built the business from nothing, must answer to a bureaucrat who has never produced anything. This is the anti-pattern in its purest form: the creator is treated as a servant of the parasite.
> **Key takeaway**: When you are told that you must submit to the oversight of people who know less than you, you are being looted. The words "public interest" are the knife used to cut your throat.
