> Source: Atlas Shrugged, Ayn Rand (1957). A philosophical novel about the strike of the men of the mind.

# Core Framework — Objectivism, The Strike, and the Three Axioms

## The Model

Objectivism rests on three axioms: existence exists, consciousness is awareness of existence, and A is A (the law of identity). From these, Rand derives the entire system: reason as the only valid means of knowledge, self-interest as the proper standard of ethics, and capitalism as the only moral political system.

> **Case: The Strike of the Men of the Mind** (Throughout): The central plot device of the novel. The men of the mind — the inventors, industrialists, scientists, and artists — begin to disappear. Each vanishes after being asked, one time too many, to sacrifice their values for the good of others. John Galt orchestrates the strike. He tells them: stop the motor of the world. Let the looters see what happens when the producers stop producing. The strike reveals the fundamental dependency: the parasites cannot survive without the creators. But the creators have been taught that they should feel guilty for creating. The strike is a refusal to accept that guilt.
> **Key takeaway**: The people who produce the most are often treated as if they owe their production to others. The strike is the ultimate answer: no one owes their mind to anyone.

> **Case: The Three Axioms** (John Galt's Speech): Galt's radio speech lays out the philosophical foundation. Existence exists — reality is real, independent of consciousness. Consciousness is the faculty of perceiving existence. A is A — things are what they are. These axioms cannot be proved because every proof depends on them. They are the foundation of all knowledge. Rand argues that accepting these axioms leads logically to reason, individual rights, and capitalism. Denying them leads to every form of tyranny.
> **Key takeaway**: Philosophy matters. The first principles you accept determine everything else. If you reject objective reality, you have no ground to stand on against anyone who wants to control you.

> **Case: The Engine That Failed** (Opening): The novel opens with the question "Who is John Galt?" It is asked by a bum on the streets of New York. The question has become a meaningless expression of despair, like saying "God knows." But as the novel progresses, the question becomes literal. The greatest inventor of the age has vanished. His motor — a revolutionary engine that could have transformed the world — sits useless in a scrap heap. The question is not rhetorical. Someone is feeding the best minds to the looters. The question demands an answer.
> **Key takeaway**: When the best people disappear and nobody asks why, civilization is dying. The question "Who is John Galt?" is the question of who is responsible for the collapse. The answer is: everyone who accepted the morality of self-sacrifice.
