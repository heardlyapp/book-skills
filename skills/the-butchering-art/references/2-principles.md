# Key Principles

## 1. Paradigm Shifts Require a New Way of Seeing

Victorian doctors could not accept germ theory because they could not see bacteria. Lister's challenge was to get people to believe in an invisible world.

## 2. Data Is the Most Powerful Weapon

Lister didn't argue — he published mortality statistics. His amputation death rate dropped from 45% to 15%. The numbers spoke for themselves.

## 3. Resistance Is Never Rational

The opposition was not based on evidence but on pride, authority, and fear of change. Admitting Lister was right meant admitting they had killed patients.

> **Case: The Edinburgh Opposition** (Chapter 7): Lister's Edinburgh colleagues refused to adopt antisepsis even after seeing his results. One surgeon licked his instruments to clean them. This was considered normal.
> **Key takeaway**: Resistance to innovation is rarely about evidence. It's about identity and the threat new ideas pose to established authority.

> **Case: The Carbolic Demonstrations** (Chapter 8): Lister traveled across Britain demonstrating his carbolic spray. At every hospital, his patients survived while conventional patients died. The demonstrations still failed to convince the establishment.
> **Key takeaway**: Even repeated undeniable demonstrations may not overcome institutional resistance.

## 4. Progress Requires Protection

Lister had a supportive wife, a secure university position, and Pasteur's backing. Semmelweis had none of these. Support systems matter.

## 5. Simple Solutions Are the Hardest to Sell

Washing hands. Cleaning instruments. Carbolic spray. Simple actions requiring a complete transformation in understanding disease.
