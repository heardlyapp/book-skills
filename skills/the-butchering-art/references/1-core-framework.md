# Core Framework: Revolution Through Antisepsis

## The Central Story

"Before Joseph Lister, surgery was the Butchering Art — a brutal, desperate gamble where the patient was more likely to die of infection than survive the operation." The book traces how Lister, building on Pasteur's germ theory, transformed surgery by introducing antiseptic principles.

> **Case: The Operating Theater** (Chapter 1): Victorian operating theaters were public spectacles. Surgeons operated in street clothes, often still covered in blood from the previous operation. Instruments were wiped clean but rarely sterilized. The audience of medical students watched from tiers, coughing and spreading disease. Patients died of "hospital gangrene" at alarming rates.
> **Key takeaway**: The conditions that Victorian doctors accepted as normal were actively killing their patients. They couldn't see the invisible causes of infection, so they didn't believe they existed.

## Key Figures and Concepts

| Figure | Contribution |
|--------|-------------|
| Joseph Lister | Applied Pasteur's germ theory to surgery, developed carbolic acid antisepsis |
| Louis Pasteur | Proved that microorganisms cause fermentation and disease (germ theory) |
| Ignaz Semmelweis | Discovered hand washing reduced childbed fever — destroyed by establishment resistance |
| Robert Koch | Identified specific bacteria that cause specific diseases |

> **Case: Semmelweis's Tragedy** (Chapter 3): Twenty years before Lister, Hungarian doctor Ignaz Semmelweis discovered that requiring doctors to wash their hands with chlorinated lime solution dramatically reduced childbed fever deaths. His colleagues rejected his findings. He was fired, humiliated, and eventually committed to an asylum where he died. His discovery was suppressed by the very people it could have saved.
> **Key takeaway**: Semmelweis's fate shows that resistance to new ideas is not rational. He was right. The evidence was clear. But the establishment destroyed him rather than accept that they had been killing patients.
