# Jordan's Counterterrorism Model

> Source: Black Flags, Chapters 2, 3, 15, 16

## The Jordan Approach

Jordan faced the same terrorist threat as the West — but its response was different. Jordan's intelligence service (GID) was effective because it understood the local context.

### Jordan's Tools

| Tool | How It Works |
|------|-------------|
| Human intelligence | Jordan had informants inside jihadist networks |
| Prison rehabilitation | Jordan tried to deradicalize prisoners (with mixed success) |
| Regional cooperation | Jordan worked with the US, Israel, and other Arab states |
| Understanding the ideology | The GID employed experts who understood jihadist theology |

> **Case: The Zarqawi Network** (Chapters 3, 15)
> Jordan's GID had penetrated Zarqawi's network early. They knew about his plans. They arrested his operatives. They warned the US about Zarqawi's capabilities. But the US did not listen. Zarqawi was dismissed as a "minor player" — a "two-bit thug" — by US intelligence. Jordan knew better. This is one of Warrick's most damning findings: the US had excellent intelligence from Jordan — and ignored it.
> **Key takeaway:** Good intelligence is useless if policy makers ignore it. Jordan told the US that Zarqawi was dangerous. The US disagreed. The US was wrong.

> **Case: The Prison at Al-Swaqa** (Chapter 2)
> Jordan's prison system was harsh. Inmates were held in desert camps with minimal amenities. But the real problem was that jihadist ideologues were imprisoned alongside common criminals. Zarqawi entered prison as a thug. He left as a jihadist. The prison system did not rehabilitate — it radicalized.
> **Key takeaway:** Jordan's prison system was a recruitment tool for extremists. The same pattern has been observed worldwide: prisons are where terrorists are made, not broken. Jordan has since reformed its prison system — but for Zarqawi, it was too late.
