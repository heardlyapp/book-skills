# The Iraq War — How the Invasion Created ISIS

> Source: Black Flags, Chapters 5, 6, 8, 10

## The US Invasion and Its Consequences

Warrick's central argument: the 2003 US invasion of Iraq was the single most important event in the creation of ISIS.

### What the US Did

| US Decision | Consequence |
|-------------|-------------|
| Dissolved the Iraqi army | 400,000 trained soldiers left unemployed and angry |
| De-Baathification | Removed all Baath Party members from government — 50,000 officials |
| Disbanded the security forces | Created a security vacuum |
| Installed a Shia-led government | Alienated the Sunni population |
| Failed to secure weapons depots | Thousands of tons of weapons fell into insurgent hands |

### The Sectarian Logic

Zarqawi's strategy was simple: attack Shia civilians to provoke a Shia retaliation against Sunnis, forcing Sunnis to join the insurgency. It worked. The sectarian civil war of 2005-2007 killed tens of thousands and created the conditions for ISIS's eventual rise.

> **Case: The Battle of Fallujah** (Chapter 8)
> In 2004, US forces fought two battles for Fallujah, a city in Anbar province. The first battle was incomplete — the US withdrew before securing the city. The second battle was devastating but drove insurgents into other areas. Fallujah became a symbol of resistance. Warrick shows that the US could never secure Anbar province because there were never enough troops, and the local population was alienated by US tactics.
> **Key takeaway:** The US never had a viable political strategy in Iraq. The military strategy was to kill insurgents, but without a political strategy to win over the population, killing insurgents was like mowing weeds — they grew back.

> **Case: The Awakening** (Chapter 10)
> In 2007, Sunni tribes in Anbar province turned against al-Qaeda in Iraq. The "Sunni Awakening" was the most successful counterinsurgency program of the war. The US paid Sunni tribes to fight AQI. It worked. Violence dropped dramatically. But the Awakening was never institutionalized. The Shia government in Baghdad refused to integrate the Sunni fighters into the security forces. When the US withdrew in 2011, the conditions that had created the insurgency returned — and ISIS was the result.
> **Key takeaway:** The Awakening worked — but only temporarily. The US failed to build a sustainable political settlement. Without a political solution, military success was temporary.
