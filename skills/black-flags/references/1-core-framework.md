# Zarqawi — The Founder of ISIS

> Source: Black Flags, Prologue, Chapters 1-4, 7, 13

## Core Framework

Abu Musab al-Zarqawi was the founder of what became ISIS. His story is essential for understanding the group.

### The Arc of Zarqawi

| Period | Years | What Happened |
|--------|-------|---------------|
| Early life | 1966-1989 | Born in Jordan. Street thug. Dropped out of school. Alcoholic and drug user. |
| Afghanistan | 1989-1993 | Went to fight in Afghanistan. Arrived too late — the war was ending. |
| Jordan prison | 1993-1999 | Imprisoned in Jordan's desert prison. Radicalized by jihadist ideologues. |
| Afghanistan again | 1999-2001 | Built a training camp. Met Bin Laden but refused to join al-Qaeda. |
| Iraq | 2002-2006 | Exploited the chaos of post-invasion Iraq. Built al-Qaeda in Iraq. |
| Death | 2006 | Killed by a US airstrike. His organization survived and eventually become ISIS. |

> **Case: The Jordan Prison Years** (Chapter 2)
> Zarqawi was imprisoned in Jordan's Al-Jafr and later Al-Swaqa prisons. In prison, he met Salafi jihadist ideologues who converted him from a common criminal to a committed militant. He learned the Quran, studied jihadist texts, and built a network of followers. Prison was not punishment — it was graduate school for terrorism.
> **Key takeaway:** Zarqawi's transformation in prison is one of the most important stories in modern terrorism. Prisons are the most effective recruitment tools for extremist groups. Incarceration without deradicalization is dangerous.

> **Case: The First Beheading** (Chapter 7)
> In 2004, Zarqawi's group captured American contractor Nick Berg and beheaded him. The video was posted online — one of the first jihadist beheading videos. It horrified the world and established Zarqawi's reputation for brutality. But it also served a strategic purpose: it deepened the sectarian divide in Iraq, forcing Sunnis to choose between supporting the Americans or supporting the insurgency.
> **Key takeaway:** Zarqawi's brutality was not random — it was strategic. Beheadings, suicide bombings, and attacks on Shia civilians were designed to provoke a sectarian war that would make Iraq ungovernable for the Americans.
