# The Roebling Family

> Source: The Great Bridge, Chapters 1-7, 14, 18, 21

## Three People, One Bridge

### The Roles

| Person | Role | Fate |
|--------|------|------|
| John Roebling (1806-1869) | Visionary, designer | Died of tetanus before construction began |
| Washington Roebling (1837-1926) | Chief engineer, builder | Disabled by caisson disease, led from bed |
| Emily Roebling (1843-1903) | Liaison, de facto chief | Learned engineering, managed the project |

> **Case: Washington's Bedroom Command** (Chapters 14, 18)
> After his severe case of caisson disease, Washington Roebling was a shadow of himself: emaciated, in constant pain, barely able to speak or write. Doctors said he would never work again. But he refused to give up the bridge. He set up a telescope in his bedroom window, aimed at the construction site. He wrote instructions on scraps of paper. Emily carried them to the site. He watched. He directed. He never stopped. For the next 11 years, he ran the largest construction project in America from his bed.
> **Key takeaway:** Washington Roebling's bedroom command is one of the greatest stories of determination in American history. He was physically broken — but his will was intact. The lesson: leadership is not about physical strength. It is about the refusal to quit.

> **Case: Emily Roebling, the Secret Engineer** (Chapters 18, 21)
> Emily was a young woman with no engineering background when her husband became disabled. She educated herself: she studied mathematics, cable construction, strength of materials. She became Washington's eyes, ears, and voice. She attended meetings. She argued with contractors. She made decisions. The men on the site came to respect her — and to fear her. When the bridge opened, she was the first to walk across. The speeches praised Washington. The workers knew: the bridge was also Emily's.
> **Key takeaway:** Emily Roebling is the book's most inspiring figure. She did what no one expected: a woman in 1870s America taught herself engineering and essentially ran the project. The lesson: capability is not about credentials. It is about showing up and learning what needs to be learned.
