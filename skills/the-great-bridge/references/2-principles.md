# Engineering the Impossible

> Source: The Great Bridge, Chapters 7, 8, 9, 10, 15, 19

## The Technology

### The Caisson

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| What | An upside-down box submerged in the river. Compressed air kept water out. |
| Depth | 78 feet below water on the Brooklyn side |
| Pressure | Equivalent to being 50 feet underwater |
| Risk | Workers emerging too fast got "the bends" — agonizing, sometimes fatal |

> **Case: Down in the Caisson** (Chapters 9-10)
> The caisson was a nightmare. Men worked in artificial light, in mud, under immense pressure. The air was thick, hot, and foul. When they came up too fast, nitrogen bubbles formed in their blood — the bends. Washington Roebling suffered a severe case; he was paralyzed, in agony, and never fully recovered. Workers died. But the caisson worked. It allowed men to dig 78 feet below the East River. The Brooklyn tower was built on bedrock.
> **Key takeaway:** The caisson was a marvel and a horror. It allowed the impossible to be built — but at a terrible human cost. The story of the caisson is the story of all great engineering: the line between innovation and cruelty is thin.

> **Case: The Spinning Machine** (Chapters 19-20)
> The suspension cables were made by spinning thousands of individual steel wires across the river, bundling them into cables, and wrapping them together. Roebling designed a spinning machine that traveled back and forth, laying the wire in perfect patterns. The process was hypnotic and terrifying: the wires sang in the wind, the machine clanked, the men balanced on catwalks 135 feet above the water. The wires were laid by hand, one by one, thousands of them, until the cables were 15 inches thick.
> **Key takeaway:** The spinning machine is pure McCullough: he makes the technical poetic. The building of the cables was not just engineering — it was a performance, a dance between men and machine, a testament to the beauty of human labor.
