# David McCullough's Voice & Application Scenarios

> Source: The Great Bridge, all chapters, Preface, Author's Note

## Authorial Voice

David McCullough writes as America's most beloved narrative historian. His voice is:

- **Sweeping and cinematic**: He makes history feel like a novel — or a movie.
- **Meticulously researched**: Every detail is sourced. He walked the bridge, read the letters, visited the archives.
- **Warm and humanizing**: He loves his subjects — their flaws as well as their strengths.
- **Inspiring without being sentimental**: The story is heroic — but the heroism is earned.

### Classic McCullough Quotes

- "The Brooklyn Bridge, a masterwork of granite and steel, stands today fulfilling its original purpose no less than ever."
- "He was a man of iron in an age of iron."
- "No American structure ever built has so inspired so many photographers and painters, songwriters and poets."
- "The bridge is a monument to the human spirit."
- "The Roeblings built the bridge. But the workers built the bridge too."

## Five Application Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Project Manager of a Massive Undertaking
You are responsible for something big, complex, and long-term.
**Lesson**: Washington Roebling's method: break it down. Trust your team. Watch everything. Never quit.

### Scenario 2: Person with a Disability or Health Challenge
You have been slowed or stopped by illness.
**Lesson**: Washington was paralyzed and in pain — and he built the Brooklyn Bridge from his bed. You do not need to be physically capable to lead.

### Scenario 3: Woman in a Male-Dominated Field
You are underestimated because of your gender.
**Lesson**: Emily Roebling. She was dismissed as "the chief engineer's wife." She taught herself engineering and ran the project. Let them underestimate you. Then show them.

### Scenario 4: Person Fighting Corruption
You see fraud and dishonesty in your organization.
**Lesson**: Washington Roebling faced wire fraud. He tested the wire. He found the problem. He fixed it — at enormous cost. Integrity is expensive. It is also necessary.

### Scenario 5: Anyone Who Loves New York
You love New York and want to understand it.
**Lesson**: The Brooklyn Bridge is the key to New York's modern identity. It connected Brooklyn to Manhattan. It made New York possible. McCullough's book is a love letter to the city.

> **Case: The Literature of the Bridge** (Preface)
> McCullough notes that no American structure has inspired so much art as the Brooklyn Bridge. Poets (Hart Crane, Walt Whitman), painters (Georgia O'Keeffe, Joseph Stella), photographers (Walker Evans), filmmakers — all have been moved by the bridge. The bridge is not just an engineering structure. It is a work of art — and a source of art.
> **Key takeaway:** McCullough's point: the best human creations are not just functional. They are beautiful. They inspire. The Brooklyn Bridge was built to carry traffic — but it also carries meaning, memory, and imagination.

> **Case: The People's Day** (Chapter 24)
> On opening day, the bridge belonged to the people. 150,000 walked across. They cheered. They wept. They knew they were part of something historic. McCullough captures the joy: "It was the people's bridge, and this was their day." For one day, class, wealth, and politics did not matter. Everyone was equal on the bridge.
> **Key takeaway:** The opening day is McCullough's final image: a bridge for everyone, a moment of collective pride. The lesson: the best things we build are not for ourselves — they are for everyone who comes after.
