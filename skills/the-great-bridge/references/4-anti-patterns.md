# Anti-Patterns — Corruption and Betrayal

> Source: The Great Bridge, Chapters 6, 12, 20

## The Five Errors

### 1. The Substitution of Inferior Materials
**Error**: "Use cheaper wire. No one will know."
**Exhibit**: J. Lloyd Haigh supplied defective steel wire. It was 50% weaker than specified.

### 2. The Arrogance of the Expert
**Error**: "I know better than anyone. I do not need help."
**Exhibit**: John Roebling refused medical treatment for his crushed foot. He died of tetanus.

### 3. Political Corruption as the Norm
**Error**: "This is how things are done in New York."
**Exhibit**: Tammany Hall, Boss Tweed, and the systematic corruption of the city government.

### 4. Sacrificing Safety for Speed
**Error**: "We need to go faster. The workers can handle it."
**Exhibit**: Workers were brought up from the caisson too quickly because it was more efficient. The bends were the result.

### 5. Taking Credit for Others' Work
**Error**: "I built this."
**Exhibit**: Politicians took credit for the bridge. Emily Roebling was invisible in the official record.

> **Case: The Wire Fraud** (Chapters 20, 22)
> In 1877, contractor J. Lloyd Haigh was supplying steel wire for the suspension cables. Washington began to suspect the wire was defective. He had samples tested: the wire broke at half the specified strength. Haigh had been fraudulently substituting inferior wire for months. Washington faced a terrible choice: rip out the wire and start over (cost: millions, time: years) or leave it and hope it held. He chose to replace it. The honest wire went in. The bridge stands to this day. Haigh went to prison.
> **Key takeaway:** The wire fraud could have doomed the bridge. If Washington had not been watching, if he had not tested, if he had looked away — the bridge might have collapsed. The lesson: quality cannot be assumed. It must be verified. Trust, but verify.

> **Case: Tammany Hall and the Bridge** (Chapters 6, 12)
> The Brooklyn Bridge was built during the era of Boss Tweed and Tammany Hall. Corruption was everywhere: inflated contracts, kickbacks, bribes. The bridge's Board of Trustees was packed with political appointees. Roebling had to fight constantly to keep the project honest. The constant battle against corruption was as exhausting as the engineering challenges.
> **Key takeaway:** The battle against corruption is never-ending. Every great public project faces it. The lesson: integrity is not automatic — it must be defended every day.
