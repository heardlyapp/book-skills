# The Brooklyn Bridge Story

> Source: The Great Bridge, Preface, Parts I-III, Epilogue

## Core Framework

### The Arc

| Phase | Key Events |
|-------|-----------|
| Vision (1850s-1867) | John Roebling designs the bridge. Proposes to span East River. Skepticism. |
| Death of the Visionary (1869) | Roebling's foot crushed by ferry. Dies of tetanus. Washington takes over. |
| The Caissons (1870-1872) | Digging under the river in compressed air. Workers get "the bends." Fires. |
| The Dark Years (1872-1877) | Washington disabled. Emily steps in. Wire fraud scandal. Financial crises. |
| Triumph (1877-1883) | Cables installed. Bridge completed. Opening day: 150,000 people cross. |

> **Case: John Roebling's Death** (Chapter 2)
> On June 28, 1869, John Roebling was standing on a ferry slip, taking measurements, when a ferry crushed his foot against a piling. The injury seemed minor. But Roebling refused proper treatment — he insisted on using his own methods (pouring water on the wound). He developed tetanus. He died 16 days later in agony. The greatest bridge designer of the age was killed by a minor injury and his own stubbornness.
> **Key takeaway:** Roebling's death is a warning about hubris. He was a genius who thought he knew better than the doctors. His refusal to accept help cost him his life — and nearly cost the bridge its visionary.

> **Case: Opening Day** (Chapter 24, Epilogue)
> On May 24, 1883, the Brooklyn Bridge opened. 150,000 people crossed it that day. Emily Roebling was the first to walk across, carrying a rooster as a symbol of victory. Washington watched from his bedroom window through a telescope. New York had never seen anything like it. The bridge was the tallest structure in the Western Hemisphere. It was a monument not just to engineering — but to the human spirit.
> **Key takeaway:** Opening day was Washington's triumph — and Emily's. The bridge was built by three people: one who died before it started, one who directed from his bed, and one who carried the burden in the world. The bridge is their monument.
