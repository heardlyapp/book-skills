# The History of Astronomy — From the Ancients to Galileo

> Source: Cosmos, Chapters 3, 4, 5

## The Slow Discovery of Our Place

### Key Figures

| Person | Contribution |
|--------|-------------|
| Eratosthenes | Measured the Earth (200 BCE) |
| Ptolemy | Geocentric model (150 CE) |
| Copernicus | Heliocentric model (1543) |
| Tycho Brahe | Precise observations (1570s) |
| Kepler | Laws of planetary motion (1609) |
| Galileo | Telescope observations, moons of Jupiter (1610) |

> **Case: Galileo's Heresy** (Chapter 4)
> Galileo Galilei pointed a telescope at the sky and discovered that Jupiter had moons — proving that not everything orbits the Earth. He found that Venus has phases like the Moon — proving that Venus orbits the Sun. He was tried by the Catholic Church and forced to recant. But the evidence could not be recanted. "And yet it moves," he is said to have muttered.
> **Key takeaway:** The truth does not require permission. Galileo's persecution by the church did not change the movements of the planets. Science advances one funeral at a time — but it advances.

> **Case: Kepler's Laws** (Chapter 3)
> Johannes Kepler, using Tycho Brahe's precise observations, discovered that the planets move in ellipses, not perfect circles. He also discovered that planets move faster when closer to the Sun. These laws (Kepler's three laws of planetary motion) were the first mathematical description of how the solar system actually works. They paved the way for Newton's theory of gravity.
> **Key takeaway:** Kepler's willingness to abandon the perfect circle — the most beautiful shape, theologically preferred — was a crucial step in science. Truth is more important than beauty. The data must always win.
