# The Universe and the Cosmic Calendar

> Source: Cosmos, Chapters 1, 2, 10

## Core Framework

Sagan's gift was making the scale of the cosmos comprehensible. The Cosmic Calendar is his most famous teaching tool.

### The Cosmic Calendar

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| January 1 | Big Bang |
| September 21 | Earth forms |
| September 25 | First life appears |
| November 1 | Sexual reproduction evolves |
| December 17 | Cambrian explosion |

| December 30 | Dinosaurs go extinct |
| December 31, 11:59 PM | Humans appear |

> **Case: Eratosthenes Measures the Earth** (Chapter 1)
> In 200 BCE, the Greek scholar Eratosthenes used a stick and a shadow to measure the Earth's circumference. He knew that at noon on the summer solstice, the Sun was directly overhead in Syene (Aswan), casting no shadow. In Alexandria, at the same moment, a stick cast a shadow of 7.2 degrees. Eratosthenes calculated the Earth's circumference as 250,000 stadia — approximately 40,000 km. He was only 1% off.
> **Key takeaway:** The Earth's size was known with remarkable accuracy 2,200 years ago. This shows that science does not always progress in a straight line — ancient knowledge can be lost and rediscovered.

> **Case: The Library of Alexandria** (Chapter 7)
> The Library of Alexandria was the greatest repository of knowledge in the ancient world. It contained hundreds of thousands of scrolls — the accumulated wisdom of the Mediterranean world. It was destroyed over several centuries by fires, neglect, and religious zealots. Sagan wrote: "No better example exists of the fragility of civilization." The loss of the Library set back human knowledge by centuries.
> **Key takeaway:** Knowledge is fragile. It must be actively preserved and transmitted. The burning of the Library of Alexandria is a warning: civilizations that do not protect their knowledge will lose it.
