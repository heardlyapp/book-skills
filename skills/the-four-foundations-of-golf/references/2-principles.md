# Key Principles

## 1. Your Expectations Are Probably Wrong

"Most golfers have no idea what score they should actually shoot. They compare themselves to their best round, not their average." Sherman recommends tracking 20 rounds to find your true scoring average.

## 2. Course Management Beats Swing Fixes

"The fastest way to lower your score is not to change your swing — it's to change your decisions." Aim away from trouble. Hit to your favorite yardage. Play the percentage shot.

## 3. Practice Should Be Specific

"Hitting 100 balls with the same club is not practice — it's exercise." Purposeful practice has a goal, a feedback mechanism, and a focus on weakness. Without these, you're just hitting balls.

> **Case: The Short Game Practice (Part III): A 14-handicap spent 80% of his practice time hitting full swings with his driver and irons. When he switched to spending 60% of his practice time on short game (inside 100 yards), his scores dropped by 5 strokes in three months.
> **Key takeaway**: Most golfers practice the wrong things. The scoring zone is inside 100 yards. That's where the strokes are saved — and that's where practice should focus.

## 4. The Mental Game Is Trainable

"Composure, focus, and resilience are not personality traits — they are skills that can be developed." A pre-shot routine, breathing techniques, and letting go of bad shots are all trainable skills.

## 5. Golf Is a Game of Misses

"The best players in the world miss the green 30% of the time. The difference is that they expect to miss and plan for it." Accepting that you will miss shots — and having a plan for when you do — transforms your mental approach.

> **Case: The Pro's Miss** (Part IV): Sherman observes that professionals don't try to hit perfect shots. They aim for the fat part of the green and accept a 30-foot putt. Amateurs aim at the pin and get frustrated when they miss in a bad spot.
> **Key takeaway**: Good players plan for their misses. Great players aim where the miss is still acceptable. The difference is not shot quality — it's decision quality.
