# Author's Voice + Key Scenarios

> **Case: Sherman's Golf Journey** (Introduction): Jon Sherman is the author of the Practical Golf blog. He's not a tour pro or a swing coach — he's an avid golfer who studied the game obsessively and wrote about what he learned. His credibility comes from his systematic approach.
> **Key takeaway**: You don't need to be a tour pro to have valuable insights about golf improvement. Sherman's systematic approach to the game helps any golfer improve.

> **Case: The 20-Round Experiment** (Part I): Sherman tracked 20 rounds to find his true scoring average. The results were humbling — he was worse than he thought. But that honest assessment was the foundation for real improvement.
> **Key takeaway**: Improvement starts with brutal honesty. Your true average is the starting point for getting better.

## Key Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Not Improving
Check your expectations first. Then your strategy. Then your practice. Then your mental game.

### Scenario 2: Poor Scores Despite Good Practice
Your practice may not be purposeful. Add goals, feedback, and course simulation.

### Scenario 3: Nerves on the Course
Develop a pre-shot routine. Use it every shot. The routine protects you from pressure.

### Scenario 4: Frustration
You're comparing yourself to your best rounds or to better players. Adjust your expectations.

### Scenario 5: Want to Lower Scores
Fix strategy first. It's the fastest path. Then practice. Then mental game.
