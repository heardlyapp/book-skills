# Voice and Application: Clausewitz's Approach

> Source: On War, throughout

## Clausewitz's Voice

Clausewitz writes as a philosopher of war. His style is dense, systematic, and dialectical — he advances arguments, critiques them, and refines them. On War was unfinished at his death; his wife edited and published it. The result is profound, sometimes contradictory, and never satisfied with easy answers.

> **Case: The Unfinished Manuscript** (Preface): Clausewitz died of cholera in 1830 before completing Book II's revision. His wife Marie published the manuscripts. This open, unfinished quality is why On War remains vital — it's not a closed system but a living inquiry.

> **Case: The Jena Experience** (throughout): Clausewitz witnessed the Prussian army's catastrophic defeat by Napoleon at Jena (1806). He was captured, later exchanged. This experience of total defeat shaped his understanding that war is fundamentally political, not merely military.

> **Key takeaway**: Clausewitz was not delivering final answers. He was teaching how to think about war. The questions matter more than his conclusions.

## Application: Business Strategy

Concentration of force = focus resources on the most important initiative. Center of gravity = identify the customer need that all else depends on.

## Application: Leadership

Friction and fog affect every organization. Clausewitz's advice — decide despite uncertainty, manage friction through experience — applies everywhere.

## Application: Policy

The primacy of political reason over military action is Clausewitz's most important lesson.

> **Key takeaway**: Clausewitz teaches strategic thinking — seeing the whole, identifying the decisive point, and acting despite uncertainty.
