# Principles: Friction, Fog, and the Role of Theory

> Source: On War, Book I, Chapters 3-8

## Friction

"Everything in war is simple, but the simplest thing is difficult." Friction is what makes real war different from war on paper.

> **Case: The Unmalleable Machine** (Book I, Ch 7): Clausewitz compares an army to a complex machine that breaks down in small ways constantly. Horses get sick, orders get lost, it rains, soldiers misunderstand. Theory can describe war perfectly — but the actual experience is shaped by countless small failures.

> **Key takeaway**: Friction cannot be eliminated. It is built into the nature of war. The commander's job is to manage it, not to dream of a frictionless ideal.

## The Fog of War

Uncertainty pervades military operations. Most intelligence is contradictory, false, or arrives too late.

> **Case: The Uncertain Report** (Book I, Ch 6): Reports arrive "constantly contradictory or false, still oftener uncertain, and at last always too late." The commander must act despite this fog. Waiting for certainty means never acting.

> **Case: The Man of Action** (Book I, Ch 3): Clausewitz argues that a certain "intellectual blindness" is necessary for decisive action. If commanders dwelled on every uncertainty, they would never move. Courage and determination fill the gap left by imperfect knowledge.

> **Key takeaway**: Fog cannot be eliminated. Decisive action with imperfect information beats perfect information that arrives too late.

## The Purpose of Theory

Theory educates judgment; it does not prescribe rules. Clausewitz rejected military "systems."

> **Key takeaway**: Friction and fog are not problems to be solved. They are conditions to be managed through experience and judgment.
