# Anti-Patterns: Misreading Clausewitz

> Source: On War, Books I-III

## "War Is Politics by Other Means" = Pro-War

The most famous misinterpretation. Clausewitz did not glorify war — he argued that policy must control war. When military action becomes autonomous from politics, it's a breakdown of the trinity.

> **Case: The Prussian Experience** (throughout): Clausewitz served in the Prussian army destroyed by Napoleon at Jena (1806). He knew war's horror personally. His argument that policy controls war was an attempt to prevent unlimited war — not to justify it.

> **Key takeaway**: "War is politics by other means" is a constraint, not a permission slip.

## Seeking Absolute Victory

Clausewitz distinguished absolute war (theoretical tendency) from real war (human reality). Mistaking one for the other leads to overreach.

> **Case: The Hitler/Molike Error** (Book I, Ch 1): Later German generals ignored the political constraint and pursued total military victory. The result was two world wars Germany could not win. They read Clausewitz but missed the political element.

> **Key takeaway**: The tendency toward extremes must be checked by political reason. Unconstrained war destroys both sides.

## Rigid Application of Principles

Clausewitz explicitly rejected systems and rules. His theory educates judgment. Using On War as a checklist is the opposite of his intent.

> **Key takeaway**: Clausewitz's purpose is to develop judgment, not to provide a manual. Applying his ideas requires understanding the specific context.
