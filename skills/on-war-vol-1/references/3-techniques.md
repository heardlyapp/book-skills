# Techniques: Strategy and the Center of Gravity

> Source: On War, Book III

## The Center of Gravity (Schwerpunkt)

The hub of enemy power. Concentrate all effort on destroying it. When the center of gravity falls, the entire enemy system collapses.

> **Case: The Enemy Army** (Book III, Ch 4): Clausewitz argues that in most wars the center of gravity is the enemy's army. Defeat the army and the state can't fight. But in popular uprisings, the center shifts to public opinion or the leader's personality.

> **Case: The Over-Extended General** (Book III, Ch 8): Clausewitz criticizes commanders who try to be strong everywhere. They end up strong nowhere. Better to accept risks on secondary fronts to concentrate overwhelming force at the decisive point.

> **Key takeaway**: The skill of strategy is not knowing principles — it's knowing which principle applies to the specific situation.

## Concentration of Force

The fundamental strategic principle: be strong at the decisive point.

> **Case: The Decisive Point** (Book III, Ch 8): Clausewitz argues that concentration requires sacrifice. You must accept weakness everywhere except where you plan to break through. The commander must have the nerve to accept those risks.

## Attack vs. Defense

Defense is stronger than attack. But only attack achieves positive results.

> **Case: The Waiting Army** (Book III, Ch 2): Clausewitz notes that defensive battles are easier to win — the defender chooses the ground, waits prepared, and fights from position. But the attacker, though facing greater difficulty, captures the prize.

> **Key takeaway**: Center of gravity, concentration, and the offense-defense relationship form the foundation of strategic thinking.
