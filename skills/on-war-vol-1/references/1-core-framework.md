# Core Framework: The Nature of War

> Source: On War, Book I, Chapter 1

## War: A Duel on a Larger Scale

Clausewitz defines war as "an act of violence intended to compel our opponent to fulfill our will." He begins with the simplest image — two wrestlers — and builds to a complete theory.

> **Case: The Absolute War Concept** (Book I, Ch 1): In theory, war tends toward the extreme. Each side must use maximum force because the opponent might. This creates a reciprocal dynamic pushing toward total war. In reality, political constraints limit this tendency — creating "real war" as distinct from "absolute war."

> **Key takeaway**: Clausewitz distinguishes "absolute war" (theoretical tendency to extremes) from "real war" (limited by politics, friction, and human factors). Understanding the gap is essential.

## War Is Politics by Other Means

The most famous thesis: war is not independent — it is an instrument of policy.

> **Case: The Political Objective** (Book I, Ch 2): The political objective determines the military objective. A war for limited aims needs different means than total conquest. The statesman and the general should be the same person — or at least work in complete harmony.

> **Key takeaway**: Never separate strategy from political purpose.

## The Paradoxical Trinity

War consists of three interacting tendencies: primordial violence (the people), chance and creativity (the commander), and reason (the government).

> **Case: The Trinity in Practice** (Book I, Ch 1): If one element dominates, war changes character. Revolutionary war emphasizes passion. Cabinet wars emphasize reason. Modern war (today) emphasizes the commander's creativity. The best strategy balances all three.

> **Key takeaway**: War is never a single thing. Its character depends on how the three tendencies interact in a specific case.
