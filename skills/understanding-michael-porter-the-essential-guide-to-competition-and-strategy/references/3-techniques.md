# Techniques: Fit and Continuity

> Source: Understanding Michael Porter, Chapters 6-7

## Fit Creates Sustainability

A competitor can copy any single activity. But they cannot easily copy a system of interlocking, mutually reinforcing activities. Fit makes the whole stronger than the sum of its parts. This is the ultimate source of sustainable competitive advantage.

> **Case: The Activity System Map** (Chapter 6): Porter introduces the activity system map — a diagram that shows how a company's activities reinforce each other. IKEA's flat-pack shipping reduces costs AND enables self-service pickup AND makes warehouse showroom format possible AND supports suburban locations. Each activity supports multiple others. Copying any single activity alone doesn't replicate the advantage.

> **Key takeaway**: Don't look for a single "secret" to advantage. Build a system of reinforcing activities. The system IS the advantage.

## Continuity Enables Improvement

Strategy takes time to execute. Frequent changes prevent deep learning and capability building.

> **Case: Japanese Auto Industry** (Chapter 7): Japanese automakers pursued continuous improvement (kaizen) within a consistent strategic direction for decades. Their advantage came from years of consistent effort, not from any single breakthrough.

## Three Types of Fit

Simple consistency (activities align with strategy), Second-order fit (activities reinforce each other), Third-order fit (optimization across the entire activity system).
