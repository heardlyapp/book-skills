# Anti-Patterns: Strategic Mistakes

> Source: Understanding Michael Porter, Chapters 2, 4, Epilogue

## Confusing Operational Effectiveness with Strategy

The most common mistake. Doing the same things as competitors but better — lower costs, faster delivery, higher quality — is not a strategy. It's operational effectiveness. Competitors will catch up.

> **Case: The Japanese Challenge** (Chapter 1): In the 1980s, Japanese manufacturers achieved enormous operational effectiveness gains — lower costs, higher quality, faster cycles. Western companies panicked and tried to copy them. Porter's insight: OE improvement is necessary, but it's not strategy. The companies that succeeded were those that combined OE with a unique strategic position.

> **Key takeaway**: Operational effectiveness raises the bar for everyone. It doesn't give you a durable advantage. Only strategy can do that.

## Competitive Convergence

When all competitors chase the same improvements, they become indistinguishable. They compete on price alone, destroying industry profitability.

> **Case: The Tire Industry** (Epilogue): All tire manufacturers adopted the same quality and efficiency standards. Eventually consumers couldn't tell them apart. Competition became purely price-based. Industry profitability collapsed.

## The Growth Trap

Growing for the sake of growth often undermines strategy. Adding products or customers that don't fit your strategic position forces trade-offs.

> **Key takeaway**: Growth is good — if it reinforces your strategy. If it dilutes your strategy, it's destructive.
