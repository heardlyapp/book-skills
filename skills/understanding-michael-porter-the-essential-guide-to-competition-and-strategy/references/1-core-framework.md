# Core Framework: The Five Forces

> Source: Understanding Michael Porter, Chapters 1-3

## The Five Forces Framework

Industry profitability is determined by five forces: Threat of Entry, Buyer Power, Supplier Power, Threat of Substitutes, and Industry Rivalry. Stronger forces = lower industry profits. Weaker forces = higher potential profits.

> **Case: The Airline Industry** (Chapter 2): Airlines are unattractive. High rivalry (many competitors), high buyer power (easy price comparison), high supplier power (unions, aircraft manufacturers), constant entry threat (low-cost carriers), substitutes (trains, video conferencing). Most airlines earn low returns despite billions in revenue.

> **Key takeaway**: The Five Forces reveal where profit will go. Don't compete in an unattractive industry without a clear advantage.

## The Value Chain

Every company performs activities: inbound logistics, operations, outbound logistics, marketing/sales, and service. These form the value chain. Competitive advantage comes from performing activities differently than competitors.

> **Case: IKEA's Value Chain** (Chapter 3): IKEA's value chain is uniquely configured: flat-pack furniture (reduces shipping costs), warehouse showroom (reduces retail costs), self-service (reduces labor costs), in-store childcare (increases visit time). Each activity is different from traditional furniture retailers. The system gives IKEA both lower costs and differentiated value.

> **Key takeaway**: Analyze your value chain. Where can you be different from competitors?
