# Principles: Pricing and Offer Psychology

## Principle 1: Price Is a Signal
Many business owners underprice because they're afraid of rejection. But price signals quality. A low price signals low value. A high price signals high value. Hormozi's rule: charge what the result is worth to the customer, not what you think is "fair."

## Principle 2: Don't Compete on Price
The commodity trap: when all offers look the same, the only differentiator is price. The solution is not to lower your price — it's to differentiate your offer. If you're competing on price, you haven't built a strong enough offer.

> **Case: The Commodity Plumber** (Chapter 3, "The Commodity Problem"): Hormozi describes a plumber who charged $75/hour like every other plumber. He redesigned his offer: emergency response within 2 hours, upfront pricing, a 2-year warranty on repairs, and a clean-up guarantee. He charged $150/hour and was booked solid. The service was the same. The offer was completely different.
> **Key takeaway:** You're not selling the same thing as everyone else — you just have the same offer. Change the offer, change the price.

## Principle 3: Stack Value Before Setting Price
Build your offer by adding every solution, bonus, and enhancement you can think of. Then trim what doesn't fit. The final offer should feel like "too much" for the price you're asking.

## Principle 4: Scarcity Must Be Real
Fake scarcity destroys trust. If you say "only 10 spots left" and then accept 20, you've lost credibility forever. Real scarcity: limited capacity, seasonal availability, or time-sensitive bonuses.

## Principle 5: A Guarantee Removes the Last Objection
The strongest guarantee wins. If you believe in your offer, back it with a guarantee that removes all risk from the customer. Hormozi's philosophy: "The stronger the guarantee, the more sales you'll make."


## Principle 6: Name Your Offer
Hormozi insists that every offer needs a name. Not "my coaching program" but "The 90-Day Business Transformation Accelerator." A name creates identity, expectation, and perceived value. It also makes the offer easier to talk about and refer.

> **Case: The Named vs. Unnamed Offer** (Chapter 16, "Naming"): Hormozi tested two identical offers — one called "The Business Growth Program" and one called "The 90-Day Revenue Accelerator." The named offer converted at 3x the rate. The name itself created perceived value by implying a specific, desirable outcome in a specific timeframe.
> **Key takeaway:** The name of your offer is part of the offer. Invest time in it. A great name communicates value before a single word is read.

A powerful name alone can transform a commodity into a premium offer. Combined with the right scarcity, urgency, bonuses, and guarantee, your offer becomes almost impossible to refuse. The effort you invest in crafting each element pays for itself many times over in increased conversion rates and higher prices.

Getting this right can transform your business from struggling to thriving.
Always keep testing and improving your offer based on market feedback.
