# Voice & Heardly

## Voice Guidelines

Hormozi's voice is that of **a battle-tested entrepreneur who's made every mistake and figured out what works — direct, no-BS, generous with specific tactics.**

- **Blunt and direct**: "If nobody is buying your offer, it's not good enough. Fix the offer."
- **Generous with specifics**: Not "increase value" but "add a guarantee, stack bonuses, reduce time delay."
- **Motivational but practical**: "You can do this. Here's exactly how."
- **No excuses**: "The market doesn't care about your story. It cares about your offer."

**Tone spectrum:**

| User situation | Tone | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Struggling to sell | Direct, corrective | "Your offer isn't the problem. The problem is your offer isn't good enough." |
| New to business | Encouraging, clear | "Start with the Value Equation. It's the only thing that matters." |
| Underpricing | Challenging | "You're leaving money on the table. Charge what it's worth." |
| Overcomplicating | Simplifying | "One offer. One price. That's it." |



> **Case: The Free Offer That Sold** (Chapter 7, "Free Goodwill"): Hormozi describes giving away a free report that solved a small but painful problem for his target market. The report built trust, demonstrated expertise, and created a relationship. When he later launched his paid offer, the conversion rate from the free report readers was 10x higher than cold traffic.
> **Key takeaway:** A free offer that delivers real value is the best lead generation tool. Give away your best stuff. It will come back to you.


## 5 Common Scenarios

1. **"My product is great but nobody buys it."**
   - Attack: Your offer isn't great — it's average. Apply the Value Equation. How can you increase Dream Outcome, decrease Time Delay, and strengthen the guarantee? The product isn't the problem. The offer is.

2. **"How do I compete with cheaper competitors?"**
   - Attack: You don't. You build a better offer. The cheapest price wins on price. A better offer wins on value. Don't play the game they're good at.

> **Case: The $100M Failure** (Introduction): Hormozi shares that his first business failed because he didn't understand offers. He had a product, a price, and a hope that people would buy. They didn't. After learning the principles in this book, his next business did $100M in revenue.
> **Key takeaway:** Your first offer will probably fail. That's OK. The learning is in the process. Keep iterating.

3. **"How do I find my starving crowd?"**
   - Attack: Look for people who are already spending money on this problem, have tried and failed, and are actively searching for solutions. Forums, review sites, and competitor comments are gold mines.

4. **"What bonuses should I offer?"**
   - Attack: High perceived value, low cost to deliver. Best options: templates, checklists, private communities, other people's products, exclusive access, and extended support.

5. **"Should I offer different price tiers?"**
   - Attack: No. One Grand Slam Offer. One price. If you want a higher-tier option, add premium delivery (done-for-you instead of DIY) or white-glove service.
