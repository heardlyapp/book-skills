# Techniques: Building the Grand Slam Offer

## Technique 1: The Problem Stack
List every problem your customer faces related to your area of expertise. Then create a solution for each problem. Stack them together into one offer. The more complete the solution, the more valuable it is.

**Example:** A weight loss coach's Problem Stack: can't stay motivated (accountability calls), doesn't know what to eat (meal plans), exercises wrong (workout programs), feels alone (community support), afraid of failing (guarantee).

## Technique 2: The Trim and Stack
After stacking solutions, review the list. Which ones truly belong? Which ones dilute the core offer? Trim the weak ones. What remains is your Grand Slam Offer — the package that would feel incomplete if anything were removed.

> **Case: The Grand Slam Offer** (Chapter 10, "Trim & Stack"): Hormozi describes creating a coaching offer step by step. He started with 20 potential components. After stacking and trimming, the final offer had 7: assessment call, weekly group coaching, private community, done-for-you templates, monthly one-on-one review, access to all past content, and a results guarantee. Each component increased the Dream Outcome or decreased Effort. The final price was 5x his original. Customers said: "This is too much value for the price."
> **Key takeaway:** Don't ask "Can I sell this for $X?" Ask "What would make this offer feel like a steal at $X?"

## Technique 3: Scarcity Stacking
Combine multiple scarcity drivers: limited time (price increases in 7 days), limited quantity (only 10 spots), and limited access (bonus that ends). Each scarcity layer increases urgency without feeling manipulative.

## Technique 4: Bonus Stacking
Bonuses should be high-perceived-value, low-cost-to-deliver. Best bonuses: other people's products (affiliate), your own digital products (courses, templates), and access (communities, calls).

## Technique 5: The Guarantee That Sells
The best guarantee removes the customer's specific fear. Not "satisfaction guaranteed" (vague) but "if you don't lose 10 pounds in 90 days, we'll coach you for free until you do" (specific, result-oriented).


## Technique 6: The Customer Interview
Before designing any offer, interview 10 people in your target market. Ask: What have you tried? What worked? What didn't? What nearly worked? What would you pay for a solution? The answers reveal your offer components.

> **Case: The Interview That Saved a Business** (Section I): Hormozi describes a business owner who was about to shut down. He interviewed 10 past customers and discovered they all valued the same thing: accountability. He redesigned his entire offer around accountability (daily check-ins, weekly calls, community). Revenue doubled. The insight came from listening, not guessing.
> **Key takeaway:** Your offer already exists in your customers' minds. Interview them to discover what they actually want.

The key to building a great offer is iteration. Your first offer will not be your best. Test, gather feedback, and refine continuously. The market will tell you what it values — if you listen.

Small iterations compound into massive improvements over time.
