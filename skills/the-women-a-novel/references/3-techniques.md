> Source: The Women, Kristin Hannah (2024).

# Techniques — How Frankie Survived and How She Healed

## Practical Methods

> **Case: The Friendship of the Women** (Parts Two-Four): Frankie's survival — in Vietnam and after — depends entirely on the women around her. In Vietnam, Barb and Ethel teach her to triage, to hold a dying soldier's hand, to sleep when she can. After Vietnam, Ethel is the one who refuses to let Frankie disappear into alcoholism and despair. She drags Frankie to her first VA appointment. She sits with her through the nightmares. The novel's message is clear: the women save each other because no one else will.
> **Key takeaway**: Do not try to heal alone. Find the people who have been where you have been. They are the only ones who will truly understand. Let them help you.

> **Case: The VA Hospital — First Steps** (Part Four): Years after the war, Frankie finally walks into a VA hospital. It is the hardest thing she has ever done — harder than Pleiku. The VA is overwhelmed, underfunded, crowded. But there, for the first time, she meets doctors and therapists who understand combat trauma. She joins a therapy group with other women veterans. She begins to name what happened to her. She begins to believe she might survive.
> **Key takeaway**: Asking for help is the hardest step. It was the hardest step for Frankie, and she had survived Pleiku. But it is the step that saves lives. You do not have to stay broken.

> **Case: The Wall** (Part Four): When the Vietnam Veterans Memorial is dedicated in 1982, Frankie travels to Washington, D.C. She stands before the black granite wall, touches the names. Finley is there. The nurses who died are there. She finally lets herself grieve — for her brother, for the soldiers she could not save, for the girl she used to be. The Wall becomes a place of reckoning. The names on the wall are not just the dead. They are the story that the country tried to forget.
> **Key takeaway**: Memorials are not for the dead. They are for the living. Find a place where you can touch the names of what you have lost. Let yourself grieve there. Then move forward.
