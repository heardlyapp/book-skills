# Anti-Patterns — The Expectations of Others, the Silence of Family, and the Denial of Trauma

## The Central Fallacy

The most dangerous assumption: that you can go through something terrible and then return to being the person you were before. Frankie's parents expect her to come home, put on a white dress, and pretend Vietnam never happened. But you cannot un-see what you have seen. The person who went to war does not come back. Someone else comes back, carrying everything they witnessed. The family that expects you to be who you were does not love you. They love the memory of who you used to be.

> **Case: The Mother Who Cannot See** (Part Three): Frankie's mother, Bette, is a study in denial. When Frankie returns from Vietnam, Bette refuses to acknowledge the war. She will not let Frankie talk about it. She throws away Frankie's uniform. She expects Frankie to put on a dress and attend society events. When Frankie falls apart, Bette interprets it as ingratitude. "After everything we've given you," she says. Bette cannot see that her daughter is drowning because to see it would require her to accept that the world is not safe and orderly.
> **Key takeaway**: Your family may not be able to see your pain. Not because they do not love you but because seeing it would require them to change their understanding of the world. You cannot wait for them to be ready. You have to save yourself.

> **Case: The Protester at the Airport** (Part Three): A woman spits on Frankie at the airport and calls her a baby killer. Frankie is wearing her uniform. She has just spent eighteen months saving lives in a field hospital. The protester does not know any of this. She sees the uniform and sees everything she hates about the war. Frankie is reduced to a symbol. She is not a person anymore. She is something to be against.
> **Key takeaway**: People will reduce you to symbols. They will project their anger, their fear, their confusion onto you. Their projections are not about you. But they still hurt. Find people who see you as a person, not a symbol.

> **Case: The Reunion That Did Not Happen** (Part Four): Frankie's old friends from Coronado try to reconnect with her. They are married now, with children and comfortable lives. They ask about Vietnam the way you ask about a vacation. "Was it scary?" they want to know. They are not equipped to hear the truth. Frankie realizes she cannot tell them. The gap between her experience and theirs is too wide. She stops trying. She lets them go.
> **Key takeaway**: After a transformative experience, you will outgrow some relationships. Not because anyone is bad. But because the gap between what you have seen and what they can understand is too wide. Let them go gently.
