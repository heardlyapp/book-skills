> Source: The Women, Kristin Hannah (2024).

# Key Principles — Homecoming, Invisible Wounds, and the Silence of the Country

## Core Principles

> **Case: The Spit-Stained Homecoming** (Part Three): When Frankie finally returns to the United States, she expects relief. Instead, she gets: protesters at the airport calling her a baby killer. A father who tells her to never wear her uniform in public. A mother who pretends she never went. Friends who change the subject when she tries to talk about what happened. The country has turned against the war and against everyone who served. Frankie learns: "The hardest part of war is not surviving the war. It is surviving the homecoming." She takes off her uniform. She stops talking about Vietnam. She tries to become the daughter her parents wanted. She fails.
> **Key takeaway**: Coming home is harder than going to war. The country that sent you may not want to hear what you have to say. Find the people who will listen. They are out there.

> **Case: The Invisible Wound** (Part Three): Frankie develops PTSD — though no one calls it that in 1969. She has nightmares. She drinks. She cannot concentrate. She pushes people away. Her parents think she is "moody" and "difficult." She is acting out, they say. What she is, is wounded. But there is no visible scar. The wound is inside. And no one has given her a way to talk about it. The military did not prepare her for coming home. The VA did not have programs for women. She is alone with her invisible wound.
> **Key takeaway**: The worst wounds are the ones no one can see. If you are carrying invisible wounds, do not expect the world to recognize them. You have to find your own way to heal.

> **Case: The Drinking** (Part Three): Frankie drinks to forget. She drinks to sleep. She drinks to stop the nightmares. The drinking works — for a while. Then it becomes its own problem. She loses jobs. She alienates friends. She hits bottom. The novel's honesty about Frankie's alcoholism is one of its most powerful threads. She does not recover quickly. She does not recover prettily. She recovers because Ethel — a woman she served with — refuses to let her drown. Ethel has been where Frankie is. "You cannot drink the war away," Ethel tells her. "I tried."
> **Key takeaway**: You cannot drink, drug, or work your way out of trauma. You have to go through it. And you cannot go through it alone.
