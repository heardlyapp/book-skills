> Source: The Women, Kristin Hannah (2024). A novel about women who served as nurses in Vietnam.

# Core Framework — Frankie's Journey from Debutante to Nurse to Survivor

## The Model

Frankie's journey follows three arcs: the idealist who volunteers, the nurse transformed by war, and the veteran fighting to survive in a country that does not want her.

> **Case: The Debutante's Rebellion** (Part One): Frankie McGrath is twenty years old, a graduate of St. Bernadette's Academy for Girls, attending her brother Finley's going-away party on Coronado Island. She is supposed to be a debutante — smiling, serene, proper. But when her brother leaves for Vietnam, she makes a decision that shocks everyone: she volunteers for the Army Nurse Corps. Her parents are horrified. Her mother cannot understand why her daughter would choose to go to a war zone when she could have a safe, comfortable life. Frankie goes anyway. It is the first time she has ever chosen herself over her family's expectations.
> **Key takeaway**: The bravest thing you can do is choose your own path, especially when everyone you love expects you to choose theirs.

> **Case: The ICU at Pleiku** (Part Two): Frankie arrives at the 71st Evacuation Hospital in Pleiku, deep in the central highlands of Vietnam. Nothing has prepared her for what she finds: a relentless stream of wounded young men, bodies broken in ways she never imagined, the constant sound of helicopters bringing more. She learns to triage, to amputate, to hold a hand while someone dies. Her hands shake. She throws up. She keeps going. The nurses work 18-hour shifts, seven days a week. They save who they can. They lose who they must. Frankie realizes: "I am not the same person who left Coronado. That girl is gone."
> **Key takeaway**: When you are in the middle of something that breaks you, keep going. You are not the person who started this journey. That person is gone. Someone stronger is being forged.

> **Case: Finley's Death** (Part Two): Frankie's brother Finley is killed in action. She learns about it from a chaplain in the middle of her shift. She does not stop working. She cannot stop — there are wounded to treat. Later, alone, she breaks down. Finley was the golden child, the one who could do no wrong. His death shatters the image of the perfect family she had been trying to protect. She stays in Vietnam despite her parents' pleas to come home. She stays because she is not ready to leave the war. She stays because the nurses at Pleiku are her family now.
> **Key takeaway**: Grief does not stop because you are needed. You keep working. You keep going. The tears come when they come. Let them.
