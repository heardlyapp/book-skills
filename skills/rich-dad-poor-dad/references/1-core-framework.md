## The Two Dads — The Core Contrast

> Introduction

The book's engine is the contrast between two father figures:

| Dimension | Poor Dad | Rich Dad |
|---|---|---|
| Education | Ph.D., highly educated | Never finished 8th grade |
| Money motto | "The love of money is the root of all evil" | "The lack of money is the root of all evil" |
| Advice on wealth | "Study hard, get a good job" | "Learn how to make money work for you" |
| Response to not affording | "I can't afford it" (statement — closes the mind) | "How can I afford it?" (question — opens the mind) |
| Financial outcome | Left bills to be paid | Left tens of millions to family and charity |

## The 6 Lessons (Chapters 1-6)

### Lesson 1: The Rich Don't Work for Money

**The Story** (Chapter 1): As kids, Mike and Robert worked for 10 cents/hour at Mike's dad's store. When Robert complained, Rich Dad didn't raise their pay — he cut it to zero and made them work for free, teaching them that working for a paycheck is the trap.

**Core idea:** The wealthy don't trade time for money. They build systems, own businesses, and invest — so money works for them while they do other things. The Rat Race is the cycle of working for a paycheck, using it to pay bills, and needing to work more.

### Lesson 2: Why Teach Financial Literacy?

**Core idea:** Financial literacy — the ability to understand the difference between an asset and a liability — is the single most important skill you can learn.

**Key distinction:**
- **Asset**: Puts money in your pocket (rental property, dividend stocks, a business that doesn't require your presence)
- **Liability**: Takes money out of your pocket (mortgage, car payment, credit card debt)

**The Middle Class Mistake:** Most people call their house an asset. In reality, a house is a liability because it takes money out every month (mortgage, taxes, maintenance). The rich buy assets first, then use the income from assets to buy luxuries.

### Lesson 3: Mind Your Own Business

**Core idea:** While keeping your day job, start building your **asset column** — investments, businesses, real estate — that generate passive income. Don't spend your entire life making someone else rich.

### Lesson 4: The History of Taxes and the Power of Corporations

**Core idea:** The rich use corporations as legal vehicles to:
1. Earn money
2. Deduct expenses before taxes
3. Pay the remainder at lower corporate tax rates

The employee earns → gets taxed → spends what's left. The corporation earns → deducts expenses → pays tax on the remainder.

### Lesson 5: The Rich Invent Money

**Core idea:** Financial intelligence allows you to see opportunities that others miss. Real wealth is created in the mind, not the bank account. The rich create money from nothing by spotting undervalued assets and adding value.

**The Story:** Kiyosaki's first real estate deal — a $10,000 down payment on a property that he sold for $80,000 six months later.

### Lesson 6: Work to Learn — Don't Work for Money

**Core idea:** Choose jobs based on the **skills they teach**, not the salary they pay. The most valuable skills: sales, marketing, leadership, understanding systems.

## Key Case Stories

### The Comic Book Library (Lesson 1)
Mike and Robert started their first business: renting comic books from a library in Mike's basement. They earned enough to pay themselves more than Rich Dad was paying them — learning that **owning a business** beats **working for someone**.

### The Assets vs Liabilities Diagram
The rich dad's simple diagram: arrows pointing toward you = asset. Arrows pointing away = liability. The middle class believes their house is their greatest asset, but its cash flow is negative.
