# Principles: How the State Operates

## Principle 1: Coercion Is the State's Defining Feature

All states use coercion. They tax. They conscript. They regulate under threat of punishment. This is not incidental. It is definitional. An institution that does not coerce is not a state.

## Principle 2: The State Prohibits Competition

The state prohibits anyone else from providing defense, dispute resolution, or governance services. If a private defense agency operated in a state's territory, it would be crushed. The state claims a monopoly on force and enforces it violently.

> **Case: Private Courts in Medieval Europe** (Chapter 3): Rothbard notes that medieval Europe had multiple competing legal systems: royal courts, church courts, merchant courts, and local customary law. People could choose which system to use. This competition improved the quality of justice. The modern state's monopoly on law is a relatively recent development.
> **Key takeaway:** Legal pluralism existed historically. The state's monopoly on law is not natural. It was created through conquest and centralization.

## Principle 3: Taxation Is Coerced Payment

If payment is voluntary, it is a donation. If payment is coerced under threat of violence, it is theft. Taxation is coerced. The state calls it taxes. Rothbard calls it theft.

## Principle 4: Ideology Legitimizes the State

The state depends on people believing it is legitimate. This belief is cultivated through education, media, religion, and nationalism. Without this ideological support, the state would have to rely entirely on force, which would be unsustainable.

## Principle 5: The State's Interests Conflict with Society

The state's interests are not aligned with society's interests. The state benefits from war, expansion, and increased taxation. Society benefits from peace, trade, and voluntary cooperation. This conflict is structural.

## Principle 6: War and the State Are Symbiotic

The state needs war to expand its power. War creates emergencies that justify increased taxation, conscription, and surveillance. Conversely, the state creates the conditions for war by monopolizing foreign policy. Rothbard argues that without states, large-scale warfare would be impossible.

## Principle 7: The State Undermines Morality

When the state commands people to do things they know are wrong (kill foreigners, pay for things they do not use), it corrupts their moral sense. People learn to obey authority rather than follow their conscience. This moral corruption is one of the state most insidious effects.

> **Case: The Resistance to Taxation** (Chapter 4): Rothbard examines historical resistance movements against taxation. The American Revolution began as a tax revolt. The Whiskey Rebellion was crushed by the new US government. The pattern is consistent: states demand taxes, people resist, states escalate force. Each cycle leaves the state more powerful than before.
> **Key takeaway:** Tax resistance is as old as taxation itself. States respond to resistance with force. The cycle of taxation, resistance, and repression is the pattern of state development.
The structure of the state is such that those who control it benefit from its power disproportionately. Rulers, politicians, bureaucrats, and their allies enjoy privileges that ordinary citizens do not. This is not a bug. It is a feature. The state is a machine for extracting resources from the producing class and distributing them to the ruling class.
