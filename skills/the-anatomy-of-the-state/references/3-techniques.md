# Techniques: Building a Voluntary Society

## Technique 1: Question Authority

The first step is to question the state's legitimacy. Ask: by what right does the state compel me? Why is this institution exempt from the rules it imposes on everyone else? Questioning authority undermines the ideological foundation of the state.

## Technique 2: Practice Voluntaryism

Live as voluntarily as possible. Use private alternatives to state services where they exist. Choose private schools, private arbitration, and private security. Every voluntary interaction is a small step away from state dependence.

> **Case: Private Communities** (Chapter 5): Rothbard discusses how private communities, residential associations, and contractual communities can provide governance services without a state. These communities operate through contract, not coercion. Residents agree to certain rules when they join. They can leave if they disagree. This is a model for a stateless society.
> **Key takeaway:** Contractual governance already exists. It works because it is based on consent. Scaling it up is a practical challenge, not a theoretical impossibility.

## Technique 3: Educate Yourself

The state's power depends on ignorance. People accept state authority because they do not know the alternatives. Learning about history, political philosophy, and economics undermines this ignorance. Education is the long-term solution.

## Technique 4: Engage in Peaceful Commerce

Economic exchange is a form of social cooperation that does not require the state. Every voluntary transaction is a demonstration that people can interact peacefully without coercion. Markets are training grounds for liberty.

## Technique 5: Withdraw Support

Where possible, withdraw support from state institutions. Use private alternatives. Reduce your tax footprint legally. Choose not to participate in state programs that are optional. Every act of withdrawal is an act of resistance.

## Technique 6: Build Parallel Institutions

The positive program is to build alternatives to state services. Private schools, private arbitration, private security, private insurance. These parallel institutions demonstrate that voluntary alternatives work. They also provide a safety net when state services fail.

> **Case: Private Lighthouses** (Chapter 5): Rothbard uses the example of lighthouses, often cited as a public good that only the state can provide. Historical research shows that many lighthouses in England were privately built and operated. Owners charged ships for the service. The market solved the supposed market failure. This is a counterexample to the claim that some services can only be provided by the state.
> **Key takeaway:** The public goods argument is weaker than it appears. Many alleged public goods have been provided privately throughout history.
The claim that the state is necessary for order is the most persistent myth in political philosophy. Rothbard counters that most of the order in society comes from voluntary cooperation, not from state coercion. The market, social norms, customary law, and private arbitration all produce order without centralized direction. The state contribution to order is often negative: it disrupts the spontaneous order that would otherwise emerge.
The role of the state in suppressing competition extends beyond force. The state uses regulation to create barriers to entry that protect established businesses. Licensing requirements, permits, and certifications all restrict competition. This is called consumer protection. Rothbard calls it protection of established interests from competition.
