# Anti-Patterns: Myths That Support the State

## ❌ The Social Contract Myth

The idea that citizens have consented to be governed. No one signed a contract. No one was asked. Consent is assumed, not actual. The social contract is a convenient fiction used to justify coercion.

> **Case: The Sniper** (Chapter 6): Rothbard uses a striking analogy. A sniper fires into a crowd and demands that the survivors pay him for his services. Those who pay are not consenting. They are being extorted. The state is no different. It threatens violence if you do not pay, and calls your submission consent.
> **Key takeaway:** Coerced compliance is not consent. The social contract argument confuses submission with agreement.

## ❌ The Anarchy Myth

The belief that without the state, society would descend into chaos and violence. Rothbard counters with historical examples of stateless societies that maintained order through customary law, private arbitration, and social norms.

## ❌ The Public Goods Myth

The argument that some services must be provided by the state because the market cannot produce them. Rothbard argues that almost all so-called public goods have been provided privately at some point: lighthouses, roads, defense, and dispute resolution.

## ❌ The Democracy Myth

The belief that democratic states are fundamentally different from authoritarian ones because we vote for our rulers. Rothbard argues that democracy changes the method of selection but not the nature of the institution. A democratic state still taxes, regulates, and coerces. The majority can be just as tyrannical as a monarch.

## ❌ The Great Man Myth

The belief that the state is necessary because great leaders are needed to guide society. Rothbard argues that great leaders are more likely to cause harm than good when they have coercive power.

> **Case: The Draft and Conscription** (Chapter 7): Rothbard argues that military conscription reveals the true nature of the state. When the state needs soldiers, it does not ask. It compels. Young men are forced to fight and die for causes they may not believe in. Conscription is slavery by another name. The fact that democracies also conscript shows that democracy does not change the fundamental nature of the state.
> **Key takeaway:** Conscription is the most naked form of state coercion. It reveals that the state ultimately depends on force, not persuasion.
The ideology of the state is reinforced by every institution of society. Schools teach patriotism. Media frames state actions as legitimate. Religion often sanctifies political authority. The cumulative effect is that most people never seriously question the state legitimacy. This is why the state can maintain power with relatively little overt force.
The key to understanding state power is to recognize that the state is not a neutral arbiter. It is a special interest group like any other, but with a unique tool: legalized coercion. The state uses this tool to benefit itself and its allies at the expense of society. Understanding this changes how you evaluate every government policy.
