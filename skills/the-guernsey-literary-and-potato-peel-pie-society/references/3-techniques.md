# 3. Techniques — The Epistolary Craft

> Source: "The Guernsey Literary and Potato Peel Pie Society" (2008). Techniques drawn from the authors' use of the letter form.

## Differentiating Voices Through Letters

Each character in the novel writes in a distinct voice. The reader can identify the writer without a signature. Shaffer and Barrows achieve this through:

- **Vocabulary**: Sidney uses publishing jargon, Dawsey uses simple rural language, Isola uses rambling colloquialisms.
- **Pacing**: Juliet's letters are quick and witty; Dawsey's are slow and considered.
- **What they reveal**: Some characters are confessional (Sidney), others are guarded (Amelia), others are unconscious about what they reveal (Isola).

> **Case: Kit's First Letter** (Part Two): Kit, the 6-year-old daughter of Elizabeth, writes her first letter to Juliet. "Dear Miss Ashton, I am writing to you because I want to thank you for my red wellingtons." The simplicity of this letter — the child's perspective, the focus on the gift — is devastating because the reader knows what Kit doesn't: her mother is dead.
> **Key takeaway**: A child's letter can carry more emotional weight than an adult's because the reader supplies what the child cannot.

## The Unreliable Epistolary Narrator

Letters are inherently unreliable — the writer shapes their story. The novel uses this unreliability to create mystery. Characters describe events from their perspective, and the reader must triangulate the truth.

> **Case: The Varying Accounts of Elizabeth** (Throughout): Each character describes Elizabeth McKenna differently. To Isola, she was vivacious. To Amelia, she was reckless. To Dawsey, she was everything. None of these accounts is false — but none is complete. The reader assembles Elizabeth from fragments.
> **Key takeaway**: In an epistolary novel, character is revealed not only by what they say about themselves but by how they describe others. Elizabeth emerges through the eyes of those who loved her.
