# Reference: Symbolism

## Overview
The novel uses powerful symbols to convey its themes: books, the accordion, bread, and colors.

## Case: Books as Symbols
Each book Liesel steals represents something different. "The Grave Digger's Handbook" is her connection to her brother and her first step toward literacy. "The Shoulder Shrug" represents rebellion against Nazi censorship. "The Word Shaker" is her connection to Max. Books become her record of love and loss.

## Case: The Accordion
Hans's accordion is the symbol of kindness and connection. The accordion belonged to a Jewish man, Erik Vandenburg, who saved Hans's life in WWI. Hans later hides Erik's son Max. The accordion connects past and present, kindness and obligation.

## Case: Bread
Bread appears throughout the novel as a symbol of sharing and humanity. Hans gives bread to a Jewish prisoner. Liesel gives bread to Max. The sharing of bread is an act of communion — a recognition of shared humanity in the face of cruelty.

## Case: The Basement
The basement under the Hubermanns' house is both a prison and a sanctuary. Max is confined there, yet it is where he creates his stories. The basement represents the hidden spaces where humanity survives in a dehumanized world.

## Case: Colors
Death's color motif — red (war, blood, passion), white (snow, death, purity), black (the swastika, mourning, darkness) — structures the narrative. The novel's three parts are named after the three colors Death sees on the day he first encounters Liesel. Colors provide Death's framework for understanding the emotional landscape of human lives.
