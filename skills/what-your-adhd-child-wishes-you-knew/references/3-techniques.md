# Techniques: Practical ADHD Parenting Tools

## Technique 1: The One-Instruction Rule
ADHD brains have limited working memory. Giving a multi-step instruction ("Go upstairs, brush your teeth, put on your pajamas, and pick out a book") guarantees failure. Instead: give ONE instruction at a time. Wait for completion. Then give the next.

**Why it works:** Each completed step provides a dopamine hit of accomplishment. Each incomplete step increases frustration and shame.

## Technique 2: The Visual Schedule
Create a visual chart for the morning and evening routines. Use pictures for younger kids, written lists for older kids. Place it where they'll see it. The visual schedule does the nagging so you don't have to.

> **Case: The Morning Board** (Chapter 11): A family was trapped in a cycle of morning yelling: "Get dressed!" "Eat breakfast!" "Brush your teeth!" "Where's your backpack!" Dr. Saline created a laminated morning board with magnets. Each task had a magnet that moved from "To Do" to "Done." Within a week, the child was moving the magnets independently. The visual removed the need for verbal reminders.
> **Key takeaway:** ADHD kids don't respond to verbal reminders after the first one. Their brains filter them out. Visual systems bypass this.

## Technique 3: The Homework Sandwich
Homework with break activities: 15 minutes of work → 5 minutes of movement break (jumping jacks, stretching, running in place) → 15 minutes of work. The movement break resets the ADHD brain's focus capacity.

**Key insight:** ADHD brains have a limited "focus battery." It depletes faster than neurotypical brains. Movement, hydration, and changes of scenery recharge it.

## Technique 4: The Before/After Framework
Structure tasks by pairing something the child wants with something they need: "First homework, then screen time." "First clean your room, then you can have a friend over." This creates a clear cause-effect relationship that ADHD brains can track.

## Technique 5: The Executive Functioning Toolbox
Build a physical toolbox for executive function challenges:
- **Time blindness:** Visual timer (Time Timer or sand timer) — not a digital clock
- **Forgetfulness:** A dedicated "launch pad" near the door for backpack, shoes, lunch
- **Overwhelm:** The "brain dump" notebook — write down everything swirling in their head
- **Task initiation:** The 2-minute rule — "Just do it for 2 minutes. If you want to stop after 2, you can."

> **Case: The Timer Solution** (Chapter 11): A 9-year-old with ADHD could spend hours "getting ready" for anything — paralyzed by the overwhelm of where to start. His parents introduced a visual timer set to 10 minutes. The rule: he had to get ready within the red zone. The visual timer (which showed time as a disappearing red disk) made time concrete. He started getting ready in 8 minutes.
> **Key takeaway:** ADHD brains struggle with abstract concepts like time. Make time visual, not numerical. A physical timer shows time passing in a way a digital clock can't.

## Technique 6: The School Collaboration Protocol
1. Meet with teachers before problems escalate.
2. Share what works at home (visuals, breaks, positive reinforcement).
3. Ask for one accommodation at a time, not a list.
4. Follow up with a brief email summarizing what was agreed.
5. Celebrate small wins with the teacher — gratitude goes a long way.
