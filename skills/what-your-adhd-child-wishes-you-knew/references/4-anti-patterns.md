# Anti-Patterns: What Hurts ADHD Kids

## ❌ Yelling and Shaming
Yelling triggers the ADHD child's fight-or-flight response. When the brain perceives threat (even from a parent's raised voice), the prefrontal cortex — already underdeveloped in ADHD — shuts down further. The child can't learn, process, or regulate when they're in survival mode. Yelling makes behavior worse.

> **Case: The Yelling Cycle** (Chapter 10): A father described his nightly pattern: he'd ask his son to do homework, his son would procrastinate, the father would yell, the son would cry, the homework would eventually get done, and everyone felt terrible. Dr. Saline asked: "What if you didn't yell?" The father tried it. When he stayed calm, his son was still slow — but the evening wasn't destroyed. Over time, the son's speed improved because he wasn't spending energy on emotional defense.
> **Key takeaway:** Yelling creates compliance (fear) but destroys cooperation (trust). ADHD kids need trust more than they need fear. Compliance without trust doesn't last.

## ❌ Using ADHD as an Excuse (on Either Side)
"The ADHD made me do it" is as harmful as "ADHD isn't real, just try harder." ADHD is an explanation, not an excuse. The child is still responsible for their actions. But they need support and tools to meet that responsibility, not punishment for having a different brain.

## ❌ Overprotection
ADHD kids need to learn resilience. Parents who constantly intervene to prevent failure — redoing homework, apologizing to teachers, solving social problems — prevent their child from developing coping skills. The goal is not a perfect childhood. It's a capable adult.

## ❌ Comparison to Neurotypical Siblings
"You're just as smart as your sister. Why can't you do what she does?" This comparison is devastating to ADHD kids. They already know they're different. They already feel inadequate. Comparison confirms their worst fear: that they're broken.

> **Case: The Sister Comparison** (Chapter 3): A mother told Dr. Saline that her ADHD son's neurotypical sister got A's with no effort, while he struggled for C's. She said to him: "You could do this if you just applied yourself like your sister." The son had stopped trying entirely. Why try when you believe you'll fail? Dr. Saline helped the mother reframe: "Let's celebrate YOUR progress. Not compared to anyone. Just your own."
> **Key takeaway:** Progress is personal. The only comparison that matters is the child against their own past self. Celebrate the 2-step improvement, even if they're still 10 steps behind their sibling.

## ❌ Expecting Neurotypical Timelines
ADHD brains mature 2-3 years slower in executive functioning areas. Expecting an 8-year-old to manage their time like a neurotypical 8-year-old is unrealistic. They're operating at a 5-6 year old's executive function level. Adjust your expectations to their developmental age, not their chronological age.

## ❌ Ignoring the Physical Environment
ADHD brains are highly sensitive to environmental stimuli. A cluttered room, a noisy kitchen, a bright screen - these create cognitive overload. Parents who insist their child just focus in a distracting environment are setting them up to fail. The fix: reduce the environmental demands before increasing the behavioral demands.
