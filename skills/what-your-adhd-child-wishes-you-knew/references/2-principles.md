# Principles: Understanding the ADHD Brain

## Principle 1: It's a Brain Difference, Not a Choice
The ADHD brain has structural and chemical differences that affect executive functioning: working memory, impulse control, emotional regulation, time perception, and task initiation. These are harder for the ADHD child, not because they're lazy or defiant, but because their brain processes these functions differently.

**Key insight:** If you expect an ADHD child to "just try harder," you're asking them to overcome a neurological difference through willpower. It doesn't work that way. You need to change the environment and the approach.

> **Case: The "Lazy" Student** (Chapter 2): A 12-year-old with ADHD was consistently failing to turn in homework — even though he completed it. His teachers called him lazy. His parents punished him. Dr. Saline discovered the problem: he did the homework, put it in his backpack, but then couldn't find it among the chaos. His backpack was a black hole. The solution wasn't motivation — it was a "homework folder" system with a visual checklist. Once the system was in place, he never missed an assignment.
> **Key takeaway:** When an ADHD child "fails" to do something, don't assume it's a motivation problem. Ask: "Is there a systems problem?" The system is nearly always the issue.

## Principle 2: Emotions Are on a Hair Trigger
The ADHD brain doesn't filter emotional input the way neurotypical brains do. Small frustrations feel catastrophic. Minor disappointments feel devastating. This isn't being dramatic — it's neurological. The emotional regulation centers of the brain are less developed in ADHD, so feelings hit harder and last longer.

## Principle 3: Connection Before Correction
Before you can teach, discipline, or instruct an ADHD child, you must first connect emotionally. A dysregulated child cannot learn. Their brain is in survival mode. The first step is always to regulate — through calm presence, validation, and empathy.

## Principle 4: Predictability Reduces Anxiety
ADHD kids live in a world that feels chaotic to them. Their own brains are chaotic. Predictable routines, clear expectations, and consistent responses reduce the cognitive load of navigating the world. Structure is not punishment — it's liberation.

> **Case: The Visual Schedule** (Chapter 11): A mother of a 7-year-old with ADHD was exhausted by the nightly struggle to get her son to bed. Dr. Saline suggested creating a visual schedule with pictures — bath, pajamas, teeth, story, lights out. Within three days, the child was following it independently. The visual schedule removed the need for verbal reminders (which he tuned out) and gave him a sense of control (he could see what came next).
> **Key takeaway:** ADHD kids often can't process verbal instructions delivered in sequence. Visual aids bypass the overwhelmed auditory processing and let them see what to do.

## Principle 5: Self-Care Is Not Selfish
Parents of ADHD kids are at high risk of burnout. The constant advocacy, the daily battles, the emotional intensity — it's exhausting. Taking time for yourself isn't abandoning your child. It's ensuring you have the capacity to be the parent they need. Self-control starts with you.
