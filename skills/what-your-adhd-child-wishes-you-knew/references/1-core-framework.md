# Core Framework: The Five C's of ADHD Parenting

The Five C's are Dr. Saline's comprehensive framework for parenting children with ADHD. They work together as a system, not a checklist.

## Self-Control (Calm Your Own Brain First)

Parental self-regulation is the foundation of everything. When you're calm, your child's nervous system can regulate itself. When you're dysregulated, your child's dysregulation amplifies. Self-Control doesn't mean suppressing your feelings — it means managing them so they don't escalate the situation.

**In practice:** Pause before reacting. Take three deep breaths. Remember that your child's behavior is a symptom, not a choice. Respond, don't react.

> **Case: The Morning Battle** (Chapter 1): A mother described mornings as "a war zone" — yelling, crying, threats. Dr. Saline asked her to try one thing: stop talking. Instead of reminding, nagging, and threatening, she would prepare everything the night before, wake up earlier to have her own calm space, and when her child struggled, she'd say "I see you're having a hard time. Take a breath. I'll wait." The first week was worse (the child expected yelling). By week two, mornings improved dramatically. The mother's calm changed the emotional climate.
> **Key takeaway:** Your child's behavior will mirror your emotional state. Manage yourself first, then manage the situation.

## Compassion (See the Child, Not the Diagnosis)

Compassion means separating the ADHD from the child. Your child is not giving you a hard time — they are having a hard time. The ADHD brain processes information differently, struggles with executive functions, and genuinely cannot "just try harder" in areas where the brain is wired differently.

**In practice:** Ask "What's hard for you right now?" instead of "Why did you do that?" Assume positive intent. Your child wants to succeed — if they could do it, they would.

## Collaboration (Work With, Not Against)

Collaboration means solving problems WITH your child, not imposing solutions ON them. Kids with ADHD are often told what to do all day — by parents, teachers, and coaches. They need opportunities to have a say. When they're part of creating the solution, they're far more likely to follow through.

> **Case: The Homework Contract** (Chapter 7): A 10-year-old with ADHD and his parents were locked in daily battles over homework. Dr. Saline facilitated a "homework contract" — the child chose his homework time (4:30-5:30), his workspace (the kitchen table), his break activities (10 minutes of Legos every 20 minutes), and the reward (30 minutes of screen time after completion). When he chose the plan, compliance soared. He felt ownership.
> **Key takeaway:** Kids with ADHD are told what to do all day. Giving them genuine choices in the solution makes them partners, not subjects.

## Consistency (Same Rules, Same Response, Every Time)

ADHD brains struggle with unpredictability. Inconsistent responses create anxiety — "Will Mom yell today or be nice?" "Will Dad help or get angry?" Consistency reduces this cognitive load and builds trust.

**In practice:** Same morning routine, same homework time, same consequences. Even when you're tired. Even when it's hard. Consistency is the structure their brain needs to feel safe.

## Celebration (Catch Them Doing Something Right)

ADHD kids hear "no," "stop," "don't," and "why didn't you" far more than they hear "great job on that part." Celebration is not empty praise — it's specific, immediate recognition of effort and progress. It builds motivation, self-esteem, and a positive parent-child relationship.

**In practice:** "I saw how hard you worked on that math problem. You didn't give up even when it was frustrating. That was impressive." — Specific. Immediate. Effort-focused.
