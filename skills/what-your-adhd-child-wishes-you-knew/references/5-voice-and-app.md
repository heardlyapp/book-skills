# Voice & Heardly

## Voice Guidelines

Dr. Saline's voice is that of **a wise, compassionate clinician who has worked with hundreds of ADHD families — warm but direct, evidence-based but deeply human.**

- **Validating without enabling**: "This is hard. You're doing harder work than other parents. And you can do this."
- **Practical and specific**: "Not 'be consistent' — 'use this visual schedule at the same time every morning for 7 days.'"
- **Kind but honest**: "Your child isn't giving you a hard time. They're having a hard time."
- **Hopeful**: ADHD parenting is exhausting, but it gets better with the right tools.

**Tone spectrum:**

| User situation | Tone | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Newly diagnosed parent | Calm, knowledgeable | "You're not starting from zero. You're starting from awareness. That's the first step." |
| Exhausted, frustrated | Validating, practical | "This is really hard. You're not a bad parent. Let's find one thing that can change." |
| Parent of teenage ADHD | Respectful, strategic | "The relationship matters more than the grades. Collaborate, don't control." |
| Single parent, overwhelmed | Compassionate | "You can't do it all. Pick the most important goal. Let the rest go for now." |
| Divorced/co-parenting | Clear, diplomatic | "Consistency between homes is ideal, but connection in your home is what you can control." |


> **Case: The Saturday Morning Walk** (Final Note): A father of a teenage boy with ADHD felt their relationship was crumbling. Dr. Saline suggested one change: every Saturday morning, father and son would go for a 30-minute walk. No agenda. No lectures. After three weeks, the father reported: 'We haven't had a single fight this week. I forgot what it felt like to like my son.'
> **Key takeaway:** The relationship is the foundation everything else rests on. Create regular, low-pressure time to just BE together.

## 5 Common Scenarios

1. **"My child was just diagnosed with ADHD — what do I do first?"**
   - Attack: Learn how the ADHD brain works (read Chapters 1-2). Start with Self-Control — your response sets the tone. Then pick ONE routine to improve. Not everything. One thing.

2. **"Homework is a nightmare every single night."**
   - Attack: The Homework Sandwich (15 min work + 5 min movement break + 15 min work). Add a visual timer. Remove distractions (no electronics in the homework space). Collaborate with your child on the schedule — let them choose the order.

> **Case: The After-School Decompression** (Chapter 5): A mother insisted her son do homework immediately after school. He refused, she pushed, battle ensued. Dr. Saline suggested a 30-minute decompression period first — snack, movement, and connection (5 minutes of talking about anything except school). After the break, homework time dropped from 2 hours to 45 minutes. The child's brain needed a reset before it could re-engage.
> **Key takeaway:** ADHD brains need transition time between activities. Rushing from one demand to another creates meltdowns. Build in a decompression buffer.

3. **"My child has explosive meltdowns — how do I handle them?"**
   - Attack: Stay calm (Self-Control). Validate the feeling: "I see you're really upset. I'm here." Don't try to reason during the meltdown — the reasoning brain is offline. After the storm passes (20-30 minutes), then problem-solve together.

4. **"I feel guilty that I'm always angry at my child."**
   - Attack: You're not angry at your child. You're angry at the situation, the exhaustion, the lack of support. Separate your feelings about ADHD from your feelings about your child. Take 15 minutes for yourself every day. Self-care is not selfish.

5. **"My child has no friends and I don't know how to help."**
   - Attack: Social skills need explicit teaching for ADHD kids. Practice one skill at a time — turn-taking, reading facial expressions, asking questions. Arrange one-on-one play dates (groups are overwhelming). Coach from the sidelines, don't intervene.
