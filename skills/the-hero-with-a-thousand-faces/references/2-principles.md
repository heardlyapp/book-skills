# The Hero's Journey — Complete Stages

This reference provides a detailed breakdown of all three stages of the monomyth, with key examples from world mythology as presented in Campbell's book.

## I. Departure (Five Sub-stages)

**The Call to Adventure** — The hero receives a summons. In fairy tales it may be a golden ball that falls into a well. In myths it may be a divine messenger. In daily life it may be an illness, a loss, a sudden opportunity that cannot be ignored.

**The Refusal of the Call** — "The folly of the flight from the god." When the hero refuses, the call does not go away. It turns into a problem. King Minos kept the white bull for himself; his reward was the Minotaur. The refusal transforms the adventure into a crisis.

**Supernatural Aid** — The protector figure appears once the commitment is made. Often a small, unexpected helper: an old woman, an animal, a child. In Hindu myth, the god Vishnu descends in animal incarnations to aid humanity. In European folktales, the helpful animal speaks to the hero at the moment of need.

**The Crossing of the First Threshold** — The hero must pass through a gate guarded by a threshold guardian. These guardians represent the hero's own fears. To cross is to declare: "I am willing to die to my old life."

**The Belly of the Whale** — The hero is swallowed. This image appears worldwide: Jonah in the whale, the Greek hero Heracles swallowed by a sea monster, the Polynesian hero Maui entering the body of the great fish. It is the passage into the realm of night, the womb of rebirth.

## II. Initiation (Six Sub-stages)

**The Road of Trials** — The hero faces a series of tests. Each test removes a layer of the old self. "The ordeal is the test the hero must undergo before he can receive the boon."

**Case: The Buddha's Road of Trials Under the Bo Tree.** When Gautama sat beneath the Tree of Enlightenment, the demon Mara attacked him with whirlwind, rocks, thunder, and flame. The missiles turned into flowers. Mara sent his daughters, Desire, Pining, and Lust — they did not distract him. Mara challenged his right to sit there. Gautama touched the ground, calling the Earth to witness. The hero's trials are not external. They are projections of what is inside him.

**Case: Psyche's Four Tasks.** In the myth of Eros and Psyche, the jealous Aphrodite gives Psyche four impossible tasks: sort a mountain of mixed grains in one night, fetch golden fleece from dangerous rams, fill a crystal flask from the waterfall of the River Styx, and retrieve a box of beauty from Persephone in the underworld. Each task requires a different kind of help — ants, a talking reed, an eagle, a tower. The road of trials teaches the hero that she cannot do it alone.

**The Meeting with the Goddess** — The hero encounters the feminine principle in its full power. This is not about romance — it is about the recognition of the life force that sustains all existence. In Hindu mythology, the goddess Shakti is the creative energy without which the god Shiva is inert.

**Woman as the Temptress** — The shadow side of the feminine. Not a misogynistic concept but a psychological one: the hero must also confront the seductive pull of regression, the desire to return to the comfort of the mother, to avoid the responsibility of adulthood.

**Case: The Sirens and Odysseus.** Odysseus stopped the ears of his crew with wax and had himself tied to the mast so he could hear the Sirens' song without being destroyed. The Sirens represent the temptation to abandon the journey for a beautiful death — the ultimate regression. Not all temptations are obviously bad. Some are beautiful.

**Atonement with the Father** — "The father is the initiating priest through whom the young being passes on into the larger world." This is not rebellion but reconciliation. The hero must recognize his own divine nature by understanding the father's authority.

**Case: Telemachus and Odysseus.** In Homer's Odyssey, Telemachus spends the entire epic searching for his father. When he finally finds him, the son does not fight the father — he fights beside him. Atonement is the recognition that the father's strength is the son's inheritance.

**Apotheosis** — The hero transcends the human. He becomes something more than he was. This is not about literal godhood but about the realization that the hero and the divine are not separate.

**The Ultimate Boon** — The hero achieves the goal. The treasure. The elixir. The knowledge. The freedom.

**Case: Prometheus Steals Fire.** Prometheus stole fire from the gods and gave it to humanity. Fire is culture, technology, civilization itself. He was punished — chained to a rock, his liver eaten by an eagle each day, regenerating each night. The boon comes at a cost. The cost does not cancel the boon.

## III. Return (Six Sub-stages)

**Refusal of the Return** — Having achieved enlightenment, the hero may not want to return to the ordinary world.

**Case: The Buddha's Refusal.** After his enlightenment, the Buddha sat in bliss for seven weeks. His first thought was to keep the teaching to himself: the truth was too profound for the world to understand. The god Brahma had to descend and persuade him to teach. Campbell notes that this is the "most difficult requirement of all" — the hero would rather stay in the light than bring light to the darkness.

**The Magic Flight** — If the hero has taken the boon without the permission of the powers that guard it, he must escape. This flight is often magical: the hero throws objects behind him that become obstacles (a mountain, a forest, a river).

**Rescue from Without** — Sometimes the hero cannot return on his own. He needs to be rescued. In fairy tales, the hero who falls under a spell must be awakened by a kiss.

**The Crossing of the Return Threshold** — The hero must bring the wisdom of the transcendent realm back into the ordinary world without being destroyed by either.

**Master of the Two Worlds** — The hero now lives in both realms. He can move between the mundane and the sacred, the ordinary and the transcendent.

**Freedom to Live** — The hero is no longer afraid of death. He has seen what lies beyond. He lives fully, without reservation.

**Case: Gilgamesh's Return.** After failing to achieve immortality, Gilgamesh returned to Uruk. He looked at the walls of his city — built by his own hands — and understood: the boon was not eternal life. The boon was the life he had already lived, the city he had built, the love he had shared with Enkidu. He became master of the two worlds.
