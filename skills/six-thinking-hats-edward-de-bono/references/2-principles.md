# Principles: White Hat and Red Hat

> Source: Six Thinking Hats, Chapters 4-16

## White Hat Thinking

Pure facts and information. Neutral, objective, no interpretation. De Bono distinguishes between "believed facts" (things people believe to be true) and "checked facts" (verified information). Both should be stated clearly so everyone knows the difference. Japanese-style input: in Japanese meetings, people present information first and only then discuss. This is pure White Hat discipline.

> **Case: The Doctor's Diagnosis** (Chapter 1): A doctor treating a child with a rash considers possible "boxes": sunburn, food allergy, measles. The doctor examines signs and symptoms and judges which box fits. That is traditional thinking at its best — but it only works in a stable world. In a changing world, the old boxes may not apply.

> **Key takeaway**: White Hat thinking is not about making judgments. It's about laying out information so that judgment can be based on facts.

## Red Hat Thinking

Emotions, feelings, intuition — without apology or justification. De Bono emphasizes that emotions are a crucial part of thinking and should not be hidden. The Red Hat gives permission to express feelings directly.

> **Case: Intuition in Decision-Making** (Chapter 12): De Bono argues that intuition can be based on complex pattern recognition that the conscious mind cannot articulate. A seasoned executive's "gut feeling" about a deal may be based on hundreds of subtle cues. The Red Hat makes this intuition explicit without requiring proof.

> **Key takeaway**: "I have a bad feeling about this" is a legitimate contribution under the Red Hat. No reasons needed. No defense required. The Red Hat makes emotions visible so they can be managed rather than hidden.
