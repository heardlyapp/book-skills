# Voice and Application: Green Hat and Blue Hat

> Source: Six Thinking Hats, Chapters 29-43

## Green Hat Creativity

The Green Hat is about creative thinking, lateral thinking, and alternatives. De Bono's key technique: "movement instead of judgment." Instead of judging whether an idea is good or bad, the Green Hat asks "where does this idea take us?" Provocation (using "po" — provocation operation) is used to deliberately generate ideas that are outside normal thinking.

> **Case: The Cambodian Villagers** (Preface): Simon Batchelor was on an aid mission in Cambodia teaching Khmer villagers to drill for water. He found it difficult to get them involved. He taught the Six Hats method from de Bono's book Teach Your Child How To Think. The villagers became so enthusiastic that they said learning to think was more important than drilling for water.

> **Case: Statoil Oil Rig** (Preface): A problem with an oil rig was costing $100,000 per day. A certified trainer introduced the Six Hats method. In twelve minutes, the problem was solved and the cost was eliminated.

## Blue Hat Process Control

The Blue Hat controls the thinking process itself. It sets the focus, designs the hat sequence, monitors progress, and summarizes conclusions. The Blue Hat is the "conductor's baton" for thinking.

> **Case: Jury Trial** (Preface): In one case, the jury took more than three hours to reach a decision. In a second case, one juror introduced the Six Hats method. A decision was reached in fifteen minutes. The Blue Hat designed the sequence that made the decision possible.

> **Case: 300 Civil Servants** (Preface): When 300 senior civil servants were introduced to the Six Hats method, thinking productivity increased by 493 percent. The Blue Hat's structuring of the thinking process was the key.

## De Bono's Voice

De Bono writes with the authority of someone who has spent decades studying thinking. He is direct, confident, and dismissive of traditional Western thinking's limitations. His central message: thinking is a skill that can be taught, learned, and improved — not a fixed trait.
