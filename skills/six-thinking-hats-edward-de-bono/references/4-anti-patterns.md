# Anti-Patterns: Common Mistakes

> Source: Six Thinking Hats, Chapters 29-36, Introduction

## Using Hats as Personality Labels

The most common mistake. People say "she's a black-hat thinker" or "he's a green-hat person." This destroys the entire system. The hats are directions, not descriptions. Everyone must be able to use every hat.

> **Case: The Misuse Example** (Chapter 3): De Bono reports that some groups assign one person to be the "black hat" for the entire meeting. This is the opposite of how the system should work. Everyone should wear the Black Hat at the same time, then everyone should switch to Yellow together.

> **Key takeaway**: If you use hats as labels, you're doing it wrong. The power comes from every person thinking in every direction.

## Argument Addiction

Western thinking tradition (Socrates, Plato, Aristotle) is built on argument and adversarial debate. This system is excellent for analysis but destructive for creativity and constructive design.

> **Case: Socrates' Dialogues** (Chapter 1): In 80 percent of the dialogues Socrates participated in (as written by Plato), there was no constructive outcome at all. Socrates saw his role as pointing out what was "wrong." De Bono's point: argument clarifies "what is" but never designs "what can be."

## The Australian Constitutional Convention (Chapter 1): De Bono told the story of the half-white, half-black car — witnesses in court contradict each other because they're both right but looking at different aspects. The convention chairperson used this story to illustrate why parallel thinking is needed.

> **Key takeaway**: Argument feels productive but often produces nothing. Focus on designing a way forward instead of proving who is right.
