# Core Framework: The Six Hats System

> Source: Six Thinking Hats, Chapters 1-3

## Parallel Thinking

The core innovation: instead of argument where people take opposite views, parallel thinking has everyone look in the same direction at the same time. The country house analogy: four people stand on four sides of a house, each arguing their view is correct. Using parallel thinking, they all walk to the front, then all to the side, then all to the back — each explores every perspective.

> **Case: ABB Project Teams** (Preface): A major multinational corporation used to spend 30 days on project team discussions. Using parallel thinking with the Six Hats method, the same discussions took 2 days. That's a 93 percent reduction in meeting time.

> **Case: IBM Research Lab** (Preface): A researcher reported that using the Six Hats method reduced meeting times to one quarter of what they had been. The structure forced focused thinking and eliminated wasted debate.

## The Six Hats

Each hat is a direction of thinking, not a description of a person. Everyone must use all six hats.

**White Hat** — Facts, figures, information. Neutral and objective. "What information do we have? What is missing? How do we get it?"

**Red Hat** — Emotions, feelings, intuition. No justification needed. "How do I feel about this right now? What does my gut say?"

**Black Hat** — Caution, critical judgment, risk assessment. "What could go wrong? Why won't this work? What are the dangers?"

**Yellow Hat** — Optimism, benefits, constructive thinking. "What are the advantages? Why is this valuable? How can we make it work?"

**Green Hat** — Creativity, alternatives, lateral thinking. "What new ideas can we generate? What other approaches exist? What if we tried something completely different?"

**Blue Hat** — Process control, meta-thinking, organizing. "What is our focus? What sequence of hats should we use? What have we concluded?"

## Key Rules

1. Hats are directions, not descriptions — never label people as "black hat thinkers"
2. Everyone uses each hat in sequence — you don't assign hats to individuals
3. Within a hat period, everyone stays in that mode
4. Short hat periods increase focus and productivity
