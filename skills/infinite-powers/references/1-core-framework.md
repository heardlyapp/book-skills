# Core Framework — Infinite Powers

## Introduction: The Language God Talks

Feynman to Herman Wouk: "You had better learn calculus. It's the language God talks." The universe is deeply mathematical. "Our universe obeys laws of nature that always turn out to be expressible in the language of calculus as sentences called differential equations." Strogatz is writing for interested non-mathematicians like Wouk — who tried and failed multiple times to learn calculus. "I've written Infinite Powers to make the greatest ideas and stories of calculus accessible to everyone."

## The Infinity Principle

"To shed light on any continuous shape, object, motion, process, or phenomenon — no matter how wild and complicated it may appear — reimagine it as an infinite series of simpler parts, analyze those, and then add the results back together to make sense of the original whole."

Calculus proceeds in two phases: cutting (differential calculus — infinitely fine subtraction) and rebuilding (integral calculus — infinite addition). "The golem of infinity" is the monster that needs to be controlled — infinity tends to slip out of control.

## Chapter 1: Archimedes — The Man Who Harnessed Infinity

Archimedes (~250 BCE) calculated the area under a parabola using the Infinity Principle. He filled the parabola with infinitely many triangles, then summed the geometric series. "This was the first integral calculus." He also calculated the volume of a sphere and the surface area of a sphere. Case: The Method of Exhaustion — filling a shape with known shapes, making them infinitely small and infinitely many, then adding them up. "Archimedes was the greatest mathematician of antiquity, and he came within a hair's breadth of inventing calculus."

## Chapter 2: Discovering the Laws of Motion (Kepler, Galileo)

Kepler's three laws of planetary motion — discovered through observation of Mars. "Kepler fell into a state of self-described 'sacred frenzy' when he found his laws." But Kepler couldn't explain why the planets moved the way they did — he didn't have calculus. Galileo measured ball rolling down ramps and discovered that distance increases as the square of time. "The mystery of motion required calculus."

## Chapter 4: The Dawn of Differential Calculus (Newton and Leibniz)

Newton (born Christmas Day 1642, premature, fatherless, abandoned by his mother at three) solved the holy grail: how to represent any curve as a sum of power series. "With these ingredients alone, he could cook up any curve he wanted." Then he discovered that "motion of any kind always unfolds one infinitesimal step at a time, steered by mathematical laws." Three laws of motion + law of gravity = all explained by differential equations.

**Newton vs. Leibniz:** Separate invention of calculus. Newton's "fluxions" vs Leibniz's "differentials." Leibniz's notation won because it was easier to use. "Newton was the greater genius. Leibniz was the better communicator."

## Chapter 6: Maxwell's Equations

James Clerk Maxwell translated Faraday's experimental laws into differential equations. "After some churning, the maw disgorged an equation that didn't make sense." He added a new term (the displacement current) and "this time it spat out a sensible result — a simple, elegant wave equation." Light was an electromagnetic wave. "Calculus not only predicted the existence of electromagnetic waves but also solved the age-old mystery of the nature of light."

## Chapter 7: Fourier's Heartbeat

Joseph Fourier found that any periodic function could be expressed as a sum of sine and cosine waves — Fourier series. This was controversial initially (infinite sums of smooth functions to represent discontinuous functions). Application: "Fourier analysis is used to compress digital music (MP3), to remove noise from recordings, to sharpen blurry images, and to detect tumors in medical scans."

## Chapter 8: Making Waves

Differential equations of wave propagation — from sound waves to quantum mechanics. "Everything vibrates." The wave equation, the heat equation, the Schrödinger equation.

## Chapter 10: The Future of Calculus

Dynamical systems, chaos theory, and the limits of calculus. Edward Lorenz discovered that tiny changes (butterfly effect) made long-term weather prediction impossible. "Calculus can handle simple systems, but the complexity of the real world may exceed its grasp." Yet calculus continues to find new applications — in epidemiology (HIV modeling), neuroscience, economics, and machine learning.
