# Key Principles — Infinite Powers

## I. The Infinity Principle

"Reimagine any continuous phenomenon as an infinite series of simpler parts, analyze those, and then add the results back together." This is calculus's single unifying idea. From Archimedes' parabola to Newton's mechanics to Maxwell's waves — same strategy. Case: Archimedes area of a parabola — filled with infinitely many triangles of known area, summed as a geometric series.

## II. Calculus Wants to Make Hard Problems Simple

"Calculus is utterly obsessed with simplicity." It breaks complicated problems down into infinitely many simpler parts. Cutting (differential calculus) then rebuilding (integral calculus). "The golem of infinity" — infinity is powerful but dangerous. It must be controlled. "The carefully controlled use of infinity is the secret to calculus and the source of its enormous predictive power."

## III. The Universe Runs on Differential Equations

"Our universe obeys laws of nature that always turn out to be expressible in the language of calculus as sentences called differential equations." Newton's laws, Maxwell's equations, Schrödinger's equation — all differential equations. Case: Newton discovered that planetary orbits, tides, and cannonball trajectories could all be described by a small set of differential equations.

## IV. Math Is Unreasonably Effective

Einstein: "The eternal mystery of the world is its comprehensibility." Wigner: "The miracle of the appropriateness of the language of mathematics for the formulation of the laws of physics is a wonderful gift which we neither understand nor deserve." Case: The Pythagoreans discovered that musical harmony is governed by the ratios of whole numbers — the first glimpse of this mystery.

## V. The Three Mysteries: Curves, Motion, Change

"Three mysteries above all have spurred calculus's development: the mystery of curves, the mystery of motion, and the mystery of change." Curves (Archimedes → integrals), motion (Galileo → Kepler → Newton → derivatives), change (Maxwell → Fourier → differential equations). "The solutions to these mysteries have reshaped civilization."

## VI. Newton and Leibniz: Rivals Who Changed the World

Newton (greater genius) discovered calculus first, kept it secret for decades. Leibniz (better communicator) published independently. His notation (dy/dx, ∫) was adopted widely. "Newton was the greater genius. Leibniz was the better communicator." The priority dispute poisoned Anglo-European mathematical relations for a century.

## VII. Calculus Has Limits — Chaos and Complexity

Edward Lorenz (1963) discovered the butterfly effect — tiny changes in initial conditions make long-term weather prediction impossible. "The behavior of such systems cannot be captured in closed-form formulas." Yet calculus remains essential. Case: HIV modeling uses differential equations to predict viral load and optimize drug therapy.
