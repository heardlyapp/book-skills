# Techniques — Infinite Powers

## How to Think Like a Calculus Master (Based on Steven Strogatz)

### Technique 1: The Infinity Principle in Practice
When facing any problem involving a continuous quantity (curve, motion, change), ask: "Can I split this into infinitely many simpler pieces?" Case: Archimedes splitting a parabola into triangles. The area of each triangle is easy. Summing them (as a geometric series) gives the total area. The principle applies to all continuous problems.

### Technique 2: The Zoom-In Insight
"If we zoom in closely enough on a circle (or anything curved and smooth), the portion under the microscope begins to look straight and flat." This insight — local linearity — is the foundation of differential calculus. Case: At the scale of infinitesimals, a changing speed becomes a constant speed. This is what makes derivatives work.

### Technique 3: The Secret Fountain Strategy
Strogatz describes differential equations as "secret fountains" that generate dynamic behavior from moment to moment. To understand any changing system, find its "secret fountain" — the differential equation that describes how it changes from one instant to the next. Case: Maxwell discovered that electricity and magnetism formed a fountain — each instant generates the next through their mutual interaction.

### Technique 4: The Power Series Bridge
Newton's method: represent any curve as a sum of power functions (x, x², x³...). "With these ingredients alone, he could cook up any curve he wanted." The technique: when stumped by a complicated function, expand it as an infinite series of simpler functions you can handle.

### Technique 5: The Fourier Decomposition
Fourier's insight: any periodic function can be expressed as a sum of sine and cosine waves. Case: Audio compression (MP3) works by decomposing a sound signal into its constituent frequencies using the Fourier transform, then discarding frequencies the human ear can't hear.

### Technique 6: The Calculus Simulation
For systems too complex for closed-form solutions (like weather or the HIV virus), use calculus-based simulation. Case: HIV modeling — differential equations of viral concentration are solved numerically by dividing time into infinitesimal steps and calculating the changes step by step.

### Technique 7: The Wouk Test
Feynman told Herman Wouk: "You had better learn calculus. It's the language God talks." When explaining any calculus concept, ask: "Would Herman Wouk understand this?" If not, find a better way. Strogatz's approach: "pictures, metaphors, and anecdotes."
