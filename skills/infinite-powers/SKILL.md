### Philosophy — 7 Rules to Remember

1. **The Infinity Principle.** "Reimagine any continuous phenomenon as an infinite series of simpler parts, analyze those, and then add the results back together." Cutting (differential) + Rebuilding (integral). Case: Archimedes's parabola — infinitely many triangles summed as geometric series.
2. **Calculus Wants to Make Hard Problems Simple.** It breaks complicated problems into infinitely many simpler pieces. But infinity is "the golem" — powerful and dangerous. It must be controlled.
3. **The Universe Runs on Differential Equations.** Newton's laws, Maxwell's equations, Schrödinger's equation — all expressible as differential equations. "It's the language God talks" (Feynman).
4. **Math Is Unreasonably Effective.** Einstein: "The eternal mystery of the world is its comprehensibility." The Pythagoreans discovered that musical harmony is governed by number ratios — the first glimpse.
5. **Three Mysteries Drove Calculus: Curves, Motion, Change.** Curves (Archimedes → integrals). Motion (Galileo → Kepler → Newton → derivatives). Change (Maxwell → Fourier → differential equations).
6. **Newton Was the Greater Genius; Leibniz Was the Better Communicator.** Newton discovered first but kept it secret. Leibniz published and had better notation. "Creation is intuitive; reason comes later."
7. **Calculus Has Limits — Chaos and Complexity.** Lorenz (1963): the butterfly effect. Some systems are inherently unpredictable even with calculus. Yet calculus remains essential — HIV modeling, neuroscience, machine learning.

### Core Framework Quick Reference

- **Who Strogatz Is:** Steven Strogatz, applied mathematician at Cornell University. Author of "The Joy of X" (New York Times series), "Sync," "Nonlinear Dynamics and Chaos." Known for making complex math accessible to general audiences.
- **The Book's Purpose:** Written for people like Herman Wouk — curious, intelligent non-mathematicians who want to understand calculus but were shut out by traditional textbooks. "It isn't necessary to learn how to do calculus to appreciate it."
- **The Infinity Principle:** The single unifying idea of calculus. "To shed light on any continuous phenomenon, reimagine it as an infinite series of simpler parts, analyze those, and then add the results back together." The two operations: differentiation (cutting) and integration (rebuilding).
- **The Journey:** 2,500 years from Archimedes (~250 BCE) to the present. Key milestones: Archimedes → Kepler/Galileo → Newton/Leibniz → Euler → Fourier → Maxwell → Einstein → Lorenz → HIV modeling.
- **The Three Mysteries:** Curves (geometry), motion (physics), change (dynamics). Each mystery drove the development of a new aspect of calculus.
- **The Modern Frontier:** Dynamical systems, chaos theory, and the use of calculus in medicine (HIV, cancer), neuroscience (neurons), and economics.
