# Anti-Patterns: Common Mistakes in Chasm Crossing

## Overview

Moore spends about a third of the book describing what goes wrong. The patterns are so consistent that you can diagnose a startup's trajectory by which anti-patterns they are manifesting.

## Anti-Pattern 1: The Crack Addict (Visionary Dependency)

**The mistake:** A startup gets its first 2-3 customers—all visionaries—and becomes addicted to the "easy" high-margin, low-volume deals. Each visionary deal is different, requiring custom features, custom pricing, and custom support. The company builds a reputation for flexibility but never for reliability.

**Why it's deadly:**
- Visionaries consume disproportionate resources (product management, engineering, support).
- Each visionary deal teaches the company to be good at one-off delivery, not repeatable sales.
- The sales team learns to sell to visionaries (long technical evaluations, bold promises) which is the exact wrong skill for selling to pragmatists.
- When the company runs out of visionary deals in their network, growth stalls.

**The fix:** Cap visionary deals early. Set a deadline: "We will sell to no more than 5 visionaries. After that, we are building a product, not custom solutions." Every account above 5 must represent a repeatable, non-customizable sale.

## Anti-Pattern 2: The Kitchen Sink (Whole Product Failure)

**The mistake:** The company ships the generic product and expects pragmatists to figure out the rest. "Our APIs are open. Third parties can build integrations. We have documentation."

**Why it's deadly:**
- Pragmatists do not do integrations. They buy solutions.
- Without a whole product, the pragmatist's deployment fails—not because your core tech is bad, but because the surrounding ecosystem (training, implementation, ongoing support) doesn't exist.
- Failed deployments create negative references that poison the market for years.

**The fix:** Before approaching a pragmatist, ensure the whole product exists. If that means hiring a consulting partner to build integrations, do it. If that means doing professional services yourself at negative margin, do it. You cannot cross the chasm with a partial product.

## Anti-Pattern 3: The Scattershot (No Beachhead)

**The mistake:** The company tries to sell to "the enterprise" or "SMB market" or "anyone with a pulse." Salespeople chase any deal they can find across multiple verticals, geographies, and use cases.

**Why it's deadly:**
- Dilution kills focus. Marketing can't be effective across 5 segments simultaneously.
- No word-of-mouth effect in any one segment.
- Product roadmap is pulled in 10 different directions by 10 different customer requirements.
- Competition beats you in every segment because you are not committed to any.

**The fix:** Pick one beachhead. Fire customers outside the beachhead. Say no to good deals that don't fit the target. The discipline of declining revenue is the hardest lesson for early-stage founders.

## Anti-Pattern 4: The Technology Tunnel Vision

**The mistake:** Founders believe that a superior product wins. They invest in R&D and assume that "if we build it, they will come."

**Why it's deadly:**
- The chasm is not a product problem. It's a market development problem.
- Superior technology has no market value if it isn't wrapped in a whole product that a pragmatist can easily deploy.
- Many technologically inferior products win because they deliver a better whole product.

**The fix:** Shift your mindset. You are no longer a technology company crossing the chasm. You are a market development company that happens to sell technology. The skills required are sales, marketing, positioning, and partnership—not engineering.

## Anti-Pattern 5: The Price-to-Ignore Dilemma

**The mistake:** Pricing is set too low (fearing the market won't pay) or too high (assuming the technology justifies premium pricing).

**The thin wedge problem:** When pricing is too low to fund direct sales, you are forced into indirect channels (distributors, resellers, online self-service). But indirect channels cannot sell a product that requires whole product assembly. The chasm crossing requires high-margin direct sales.

**The opposite problem:** When pricing is too high without a credible whole product to back it up, the sales cycle is consumed by value justification rather than value delivery.

**The fix:** Price for the whole product, not the generic. The price must fund direct sales, partner management, and ongoing support for the beachhead. If you can't charge enough to support this, either the beachhead is wrong or the whole product is incomplete.

## Anti-Pattern 6: The Wrong Buyer

**The mistake:** Selling to IT or engineering when the economic buyer is a line-of-business executive. Or selling to a departmental budget when the decision requires an executive committee.

**Why it's deadly:**
- IT buyers care about architecture, maintainability, and security—not business outcomes.
- Line-of-business buyers care about business outcomes—but may lack the budget or authority to approve purchases.
- If you are selling to the wrong person, no amount of value proposition improvement can fix it.

**The fix:** Map the actual decision process for your beachhead. Who controls the budget? Who signs the PO? What metrics do they care about? Target your positioning and sales pitch at the economic buyer.

## Case Study 1: Webvan (The Broad-Front Debacle)

Webvan raised $400M to build an online grocery delivery business. They spent massively on automated warehouses, same-day delivery infrastructure, and a full-service web platform across multiple cities simultaneously.

**The anti-patterns:** Scattershot (multiple cities at once), No Beachhead (no specific customer segment), Whole Product Failure (the logistics didn't work for the complex grocery supply chain), Technology Tunnel Vision (assumed technology would automate the hard problems of inventory management and perishable goods).

**Result:** Burned through $400M in 18 months. Filed for bankruptcy. One of the most famous startup failures in history.

## Case Study 2: Go Corporation (Pen Computing)

Go Corporation built the first pen-based operating system in the early 1990s. The technology was genuinely innovative and years ahead of its time.

**The anti-patterns:** Technology Tunnel Vision (assumed amazing tech would attract customers), Wrong Buyer (tried to sell to consumer electronics companies instead of enterprise verticals), No Beachhead (tried to sell to everyone from field sales to doctors to warehouse workers simultaneously).

**The fix that never happened:** They could have targeted a single vertical—e.g., insurance claims adjusters who had a clear pain point (filling out paper forms in the field with no typing skills). A whole product for that niche existed. Instead, they tried to sell a generic platform to multiple OEMs.

**Result:** Acquired in a fire sale. The lesson is taught in Moore's book as a cautionary tale.

## Summary: Anti-Pattern Quick Reference

| Anti-Pattern | Symptom | Root Cause | Fix |
|--------------|---------|------------|-----|
| Crack Addict | Revenue flat despite pipe | Chasing visionary deals | Cap visionaries, build for pragmatists |
| Kitchen Sink | Customers churn post-deployment | Missing whole product | Build partnerships, deliver complete solution |
| Scattershot | Sales chaos, no repeatability | No beachhead | Pick one niche, say "no" to everything else |
| Tech Tunnel Vision | Great tech, no revenue | Market development failure | Shift focus from product to market |
| Price-to-Ignore | Can't afford direct sales | Wrong pricing | Price for whole product, fund the sales model |
| Wrong Buyer | Long cycles, no closes | Wrong target persona | Map the actual decision and budget process |
