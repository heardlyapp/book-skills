# Techniques: Whole Product Model & Bowling Alley Strategy

## Overview

Two critical techniques for crossing the chasm: the Whole Product Model (what pragmatists actually buy vs. what you ship) and the Bowling Alley Strategy (how to expand from beachhead to mainstream dominance).

## The Whole Product Model

### Origin

Adapted from Theodore Levitt's "augmented product" concept and implemented by William Davidow. Moore applies it directly to the chasm-crossing problem.

### The Four Rings of the Whole Product

1. **Generic Product** (Core Technology)
   - What ships in the box. The software, hardware, or platform itself.
   - This is what Innovators buy and what your engineers are most excited about.
   - **Problem:** Pragmatists do not buy generic products. They buy solutions.

2. **Expected Product** (Minimum Viable Solution)
   - The generic product plus whatever is minimally required for a customer to achieve their buying objective.
   - For Salesforce: CRM software + zero-touch setup + web-based access + subscription billing.
   - **Who buys this:** Visionaries might—they accept minimal coverage because they plan to "fill in the gaps" themselves. Pragmatists won't.

3. **Augmented Product** (The Full Solution)
   - The expected product plus consulting, training, implementation services, integration partners, documentation, support, SLAs, and reference accounts.
   - This is what pragmatists actually buy. They want to see that the complete solution exists before they commit.
   - **Key insight:** Most of the augmented product may not be delivered by you. It comes from partners, resellers, systems integrators, and third-party vendors.
   - **The chasm-crossing challenge:** You must ensure the augmented product exists even if it costs more, requires more people, and gives you less margin. Dominate first, optimize later.

4. **Potential Product** (Room for Growth)
   - Everything the product could eventually become as new features, integrations, and ecosystem evolve.
   - **Role in chasm crossing:** Not immediately relevant. Focus on the augmented product for the beachhead. Let visionaries and long-term product management dream about potential.

### Pragmatist Buying Behavior

- Pragmatists evaluate **the whole solution**, not your specific technology.
- They will say: "Do you have a partner in our region who can implement this? Do you have case studies from companies our size? Can you guarantee uptime? Do you have 24/7 support?"
- If the answer to any of these is "we're working on it," the deal is dead.

### The Whole Product Compromise

Moore makes a painful point: **in the chasm, the whole product is often undeliverable by a startup alone.** You need partners, and partners want to see market traction before they commit. This is another catch-22 that must be solved by brute-force sales focus, founder-led delivery, and strategic partnerships built on vision and relationship rather than proven economics.

## Bowling Alley Strategy: Expansion After the Chasm

### Why Bowling, not Broadside

After crossing the chasm, founders feel enormous pressure to "go broad." Don't. The chasm is crossed narrowly; mainstream market penetration is also narrowly sequenced.

The bowling alley works like this:

- **Pin 1 (Beachhead):** Dominate your first niche. Achieve market leadership (30%+ share in the defined segment).
- **Pin 2 (Adjacent Niche):** Your Pin 1 customers become references for Pin 2. They share similar pain, decision process, or industry structure.
- **Pin 3 (Next Adjacent):** Your Pin 1 and Pin 2 customers together make an even stronger reference base.
- **Chain reaction:** Each new niche is easier to enter than the last, because your reference base compounds.

### Adjacency Rules

1. **Shared pain point.** Pin 2's problem should be recognizably similar to Pin 1's. If pharma regulatory affairs was Pin 1, chemicals regulatory affairs is Pin 2.
2. **Shared buying process.** If Pin 1 was sold through an industry trade show, Pin 2 should be reachable through a related show.
3. **Leverage existing distribution.** Don't invent a new channel for each pin. Extend the channel.
4. **Protect the beachhead.** Never reduce investment in Pin 1 while expanding to Pin 2. The beachhead is your cash cow, reputation base, and reference source.

### When to Move to the Next Pin

- When you have 30%+ market share in the beachhead.
- When sales cycle times have normalized (predictable, not heroic).
- When you have 3+ referenceable customers in the niche.
- When the beachhead is generating predictable cash flow.

## Case Study 1: VMware

VMware's x86 virtualization technology was a genuine breakthrough, but the mainstream market couldn't use it directly. The technology required new operational models for data center management.

**The beachhead:** VMware targeted **software test and development environments** — specifically systems administrators in enterprise IT who needed to test code on multiple OS configurations without buying dedicated hardware for each.

- **Compelling reason:** A single physical server could host 10-20 test environments. The ROI was immediate and measurable.
- **Whole product:** The hypervisor (generic) + tools to manage test environments (expected) + training and scripts for sysadmins (augmented).
- **Competition:** "Three servers worth of testing hardware for the price of one."

**Bowling alley expansion:**
1. Test/dev environments (beachhead)
2. Server consolidation (production)
3. Desktop virtualization
4. Cloud infrastructure (vSphere/vCloud)

**Result:** VMware grew from zero to $2B revenue in under 10 years and defined the virtualization category.

## Case Study 2: E.piphany (CRM Analytics)

E.piphany built powerful analytics for customer behavior prediction, but the product was complex to deploy and required data integration work.

**The beachhead:** E.piphany targeted **financial services marketing departments** — specifically customer retention teams at large banks and credit card companies.

- **Compelling reason:** "Your competitors are losing 30% of their best customers annually. We can tell you which ones are leaving and why, before they leave."
- **Whole product:** Analytics platform + pre-built banking data models + systems integrator partners (Accenture, Deloitte) for the custom integration work.
- **Bowling alley:** Banking marketing → insurance marketing → telco marketing → retail marketing.

**Result:** E.piphany went public in 1999 and reached a $4B market cap at its peak.

## The Crucial Distinction

The Whole Product Model and Bowling Alley Strategy work together:
- **Whole Product** solves the "how do we sell to pragmatists?" problem for a single niche.
- **Bowling Alley** solves the "how do we grow from one niche to the whole market?" problem after crossing the chasm.

You cannot do bowling alley effectively without first mastering the whole product for your beachhead. And you cannot refine the whole product without feedback from actual pragmatist customers in a single focused niche.

## Summary Checklist

| Step | Action | Success Criterion |
|------|--------|-------------------|
| 1 | Define the whole product for your beachhead market | All partners identified and committed |
| 2 | Sell whole product to beachhead customers | 3+ referenceable deployments |
| 3 | Achieve market leadership in beachhead | 30%+ share, predictable sales cycle |
| 4 | Select next adjacent pin | Shared pain, distribution, or process |
| 5 | Repeat whole product definition for new pin | Partners extend to new niche |
