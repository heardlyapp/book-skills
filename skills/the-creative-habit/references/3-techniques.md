# Techniques: Building Your Creative Practice

## Creating Your Ritual

1. Choose a consistent time — same time every day (morning works best)
2. Choose a consistent place — a specific space dedicated to creative work
3. Choose a consistent starting action — making tea, a stretch, a phrase you say
4. Do it every day for two weeks — by then, the ritual will trigger the creative state

> **Case: The Composer's Cue** (Chapter 10): A composer Tharp worked with always started his creative sessions by sharpening a pencil. Even though he composed on computer, he sharpened a pencil first. The physical act signaled his brain: time to compose.
> **Key takeaway**: The ritual doesn't have to make logical sense. It just has to be consistent. The brain learns the pattern and responds.

> **Case: The Dancer's Warm-Up** (Chapter 11): Tharp's dancers spend the first 30 minutes of every rehearsal doing the same warm-up routine. It's not just physical preparation — it's psychological. By the time the warm-up ends, every dancer is mentally in the room, focused and ready.
>
> **Key takeaway**: The warm-up is the ritual. It doesn't produce creative work directly, but it makes creative work possible. Never skip the preparation.

## Building Your Scaffolding

- Create a box for each project — physical or digital
- Fill it with everything related: research, notes, images, quotes
- Review the box before each creative session
- The box becomes your world — entering it is the creative act

## The Fear Inventory

1. Write down exactly what you're afraid of
2. Ask: "What's the worst that can happen?"
3. Ask: "Can I survive that?"
4. Ask: "What's the best that can happen?"
5. Start working despite the fear

## The Daily Practice

- Show up at the same time every day
- Do your ritual
- Work for a set minimum (30 minutes)
- Stop when you hit your limit — but don't skip the next day
- Track your streak — don't break it
