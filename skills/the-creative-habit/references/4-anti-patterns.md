# Anti-Patterns

## 1. Waiting for Inspiration

**The mistake**: Believing you can only create when "the muse" visits.

**Fix**: The muse rewards those who show up. Start working, and inspiration will follow.

> **Case: The Waiting Game** (Chapter 3): A composer Tharp knew spent years waiting for the right idea. He wrote nothing. Tharp's approach: start writing anything. Even bad ideas. The act of writing generates better ideas. Waiting generates nothing.
> **Key takeaway**: Waiting for inspiration is a luxury the professional cannot afford. Create first, judge later.

## 2. No Ritual

**The mistake**: Relying on willpower to start creating.

**Fix**: Willpower is unreliable. Build a ritual that makes starting automatic.

## 3. Ignoring Preparation

**The mistake**: Jumping into creative work without research or planning.

**Fix**: Scaffolding is not procrastination. It's an essential part of the creative process.

> **Case: The Box Method** (Chapter 2): Tharp's box method is the opposite of waiting. She fills a box with every piece of research she can find. When she opens the box, she's surrounded by the project. The box is the preparation that makes creation possible.
> **Key takeaway**: Preparation is not separate from creativity. It is creativity. The box is the bridge between research and creation.

## 4. Perfectionism

**The mistake**: Waiting until the work is perfect to show it.

**Fix**: The first draft is supposed to be bad. The second draft can be better. But you can't edit a blank page.

## 5. Inconsistency

**The mistake**: Creating in bursts (intense periods followed by long gaps).

**Fix**: Daily consistency, even in small amounts, produces more and better creative work than intermittent intensity.
