# 5 — Voice & App

> Source: *Bury My Heart at Wounded Knee* by Dee Brown.

## The Book's Voice

1. **Bitter clarity.** Brown does not pretend to be neutral. This is history from the victims' perspective, told in their words. "Americans who have always looked westward when reading about this period should read this book facing eastward."
2. **The dignity of defeat.** Every leader in the book is defeated. None is diminished. Little Crow's speech, Manuelito's refusal, Joseph's surrender — these are not records of shame but monuments of courage.
3. **The poetry of council talk.** Indian leaders spoke in images drawn from nature: "like snow before a summer sun," "like dogs in the Hot Moon," "like the locusts when they fly so thick." The translation preserved the metaphor.
4. **The cumulative weight.** Chapter by chapter, tribe by tribe, the pattern repeats. By the time you reach Wounded Knee, the reader understands: this was not a series of accidents. This was a design.

## Five Scenarios

### 1. The Community Being Displaced

The Long Walk pattern: forced relocation, broken promises, cultural destruction. Document everything. Refuse to sign under duress. Plan for return.

### 2. The Activist Facing Overwhelming Power

Little Crow's calculus: know the enemy's strength, choose to fight on your terms, lead even when the cause is hopeless.

### 3. The Negotiator with a More Powerful Party

Cycle of Broken Treaties check: audit every promise against past behavior. Insist on your own interpreter. Never accept "permanent" as real.

### 4. The Leader Whose People Are Divided

Little Crow's dilemma between the hot-headed young men and the cautious elders. The wise leader must make a choice that satisfies neither faction but serves the whole.

### 5. The Survivor Telling the Story

Big Eagle's newspaper interview. The council records. The oral histories preserved and passed down. Your story is your power.
