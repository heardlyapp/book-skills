# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *Bury My Heart at Wounded Knee* by Dee Brown — Chapters 1-19.

## The Four Frameworks

### Framework A: The Cycle of Broken Treaties (Chapters 1-2, 4-6, 12, 19)

The central pattern that repeats across every tribe and every decade: a treaty is signed (often under threat), land is ceded, the boundary is called "permanent," white settlers cross the boundary, the government fails to enforce its own law, the Indians resist, the military is called in to "restore order," a new treaty is forced through with smaller boundaries.

> **Case: The "Permanent Indian Frontier"** (Chapter 1): The 1834 Indian Trade and Intercourse Act declared all land west of the Mississippi River to be "Indian country" forever. White settlers were forbidden to enter. Within 25 years, the 95th meridian line was breached in both the north and south, Minnesota became a state extending far west of the line, and the "permanent" frontier had to be redrawn further west. Again. And again.
> **Key takeaway**: "Permanent" in a treaty means "until the other party wants something else." Plan accordingly.

### Framework B: The Hero's Impossible Choice (Chapters 3, 6, 13, 19)

Every leader in this book faces the same choice: resist and be destroyed, or surrender and be destroyed. Little Crow's speech is the definitive expression of this dilemma.

> **Case: Little Crow's Decision** (Chapter 3): After four young Santee men killed white settlers, Little Crow knew war was inevitable — either from the soldiers coming to punish them, or from his own warriors demanding action. He gave his famous speech: "You are like little children. You will die like the rabbits when the hungry wolves hunt them... But Ta-oya-te-duta is not a coward; he will die with you." He chose to lead a war he knew he would lose, because the alternative — watching his people be slaughtered without leadership — was worse.
> **Key takeaway**: Leaders sometimes face choices where both options lead to destruction. The only question is whether you die fighting or die hiding. Choose fighting.

### Framework C: The Long Walk as Policy (Chapters 2, 7, 10, 14)

Removal was not an accident — it was deliberate policy. The forced relocation of tribes from their homelands to "reservations" was a systematic program designed to break tribal power by destroying the people's connection to their land.

> **Case: The Navaho Long Walk** (Chapter 2): General Carleton's campaign to remove the Navahos from Canyon de Chelly to Bosque Redondo was conducted through scorched-earth tactics — burning crops, cutting down peach orchards, killing livestock. 8,000 Navahos were forced to march 300 miles. Over 200 died en route. At Bosque Redondo, the water was "black and brackish," the soil was "poor and cold," disease killed a quarter of the people. They were "like prairie dogs in burrows." After four years, the government admitted the experiment failed and let them return.
> **Key takeaway**: The goal of forced removal is not just geographic relocation — it is cultural destruction. A people disconnected from their land and their ancestors is a people made powerless.

### Framework D: The Ghost Dance and Wounded Knee (Chapters 18-19)

The final chapter is the most devastating: a peaceful religious movement (the Ghost Dance, promising renewal of the Indian world) was misinterpreted by authorities as preparation for war. The resulting massacre of 300 unarmed Lakota at Wounded Knee marked the symbolic end of Indian resistance.

> **Case: Wounded Knee** (Chapter 19): The 7th Cavalry was sent to disarm a band of Lakota at Wounded Knee Creek. Fighting broke out. The Hotchkiss guns on the hill opened fire on men, women, and children who were running away. 300 Lakota died, mostly women and children. Big Foot, their leader, died with them. A blizzard covered their bodies. When white searchers later gathered the dead for burial, they found children's bodies three miles from the massacre site, fleeing until the snow caught them.
> **Key takeaway**: The powerful will call a prayer a threat. They will call a peaceful movement a conspiracy. The massacre at Wounded Knee was not a battle — it was the execution of a culture.
