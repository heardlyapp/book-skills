# 4 — Anti-Patterns

> Source: *Bury My Heart at Wounded Knee* — Chapters 1-19.

## Common Errors

### 1. Treating "Permanent" and "Forever" as Real Commitments

"I believe these were the last acts of the Cherokees. They will never again rise up or assemble." This was said after one treaty. Within years, the Cherokee were reassembled on a Trail of Tears. The word "permanent" in a treaty with the powerful means "until circumstances change."

### 2. Trusting the Interpreter

Treaty councils routinely used interpreters chosen by the United States. The interpreters' translations were often distorted to favor the government's position. Never negotiate through your adversary's translator.

### 3. Believing the Enemy's Own Law Will Protect You

The Indian Trade and Intercourse Act declared Indian country inviolable. It was violated within years and never enforced against white settlers. A law that has no enforcement mechanism is a suggestion.

### 4. Fighting the Enemy's Battle

The Plains tribes were experts at mounted warfare in open country. The soldiers' strength was artillery, supply lines, and numbers. To fight on ground the enemy chooses with weapons the enemy has is to lose.

### 5. Surrendering When the Promise is "You Will Be Treated Fairly"

The Santees who surrendered to General Sibley after Wood Lake thought they would be treated as prisoners of war. Instead, 303 were sentenced to death. 38 were hanged. The rest were exiled. Surrender to a power that has broken every promise is not surrender — it is captivity.
