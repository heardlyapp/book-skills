# 2 — Key Principles

> Source: *Bury My Heart at Wounded Knee* — Chapters 1-19.

## The 7 Principles

### 1. Treaties Are Only as Strong as the Power Behind Them

Scarcely a single treaty with any Indian tribe was honored by the United States beyond the point where it became inconvenient. The "permanent Indian frontier" was breached within years. "As long as the waters shall flow" meant "as long as we don't need your land."

### 2. Know the Enemy's Strategy Before You Act

Little Crow warned his warriors: "The white men are like the locusts when they fly so thick that the whole sky is a snowstorm. You may kill one — ten — yes, as many as the leaves in the forest, and their brothers will not miss them." He knew the enemy's numbers and resources. His warriors did not. They paid for their ignorance with their lives.

### 3. Document Your Own Story

> **Case: Big Eagle's Account** (Chapter 3): Big Eagle, a Santee leader, gave a detailed account of the 1862 war to a Minnesota newspaper reporter in 1894. This interview preserved the Santee perspective for history. "The whites were always trying to make the Indians give up their life and live like white men. The Indians did not know how to do that, and did not want to anyway." Without such documentation, the only version of history would be the white man's.
> **Key takeaway**: The record of the powerful will always dominate. Speak while you can. Document while the events are fresh. Your version matters.

### 4. Culture Survives Through Practice, Not Permission

The Navahos returned to Canyon de Chelly after four years at Bosque Redondo. They rebuilt their hogans, replanted their peach trees, revived their ceremonies. The Ghost Dance spread across dozens of tribes in secret, transmitted by runners and visionaries. Culture persists where the state cannot reach.

### 5. Beware of False Friends

The Utes agreed to track Navahos for Kit Carson. The Winnebagos betrayed Black Hawk for twenty horses and a hundred dollars. Wabasha secretly wrote to Sibley offering to betray Little Crow. When you are under threat, your supposed allies may be bought.

### 6. The Buffalo Economy Lesson

The Plains tribes were destroyed when the buffalo were destroyed. Know what resource is essential to your way of life. Protect it above all else.

### 7. Survival Is the Ultimate Victory

The tribes were supposed to vanish. The government policy was extermination or assimilation. But the tribes did not vanish. "If the readers of this book should ever chance to see the poverty, the hopelessness, and the squalor of a modern Indian reservation," Brown writes at the end of his introduction, "they may find it possible to truly understand the reasons why." The wounds are still open. But the people are still here.
