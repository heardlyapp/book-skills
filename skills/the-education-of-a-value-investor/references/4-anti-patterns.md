# Anti-Patterns

## 1. Confusing Activity with Progress

**The mistake**: Believing that constant trading equals investing.

**Fix**: The best investors trade less, not more. Activity creates fees, taxes, and emotional mistakes.

## 2. Investing Outside Your Circle

**The mistake**: Buying stocks in industries you don't understand because they're popular.

**Fix**: If you can't explain the business in one paragraph, you don't understand it. Don't invest.

> **Case: The Dot-Com Lesson** (Chapter 4): Spier watched peers get rich on tech stocks during the dot-com boom. He didn't invest because he didn't understand them. When the bubble burst, his peers lost everything. He kept his capital.
> **Key takeaway**: The discipline to say "I don't understand" when everyone around you is getting rich is the hardest — and most valuable — skill.

## 3. Letting Short-Term Noise Drive Decisions

**The mistake**: Reacting to daily market fluctuations, news headlines, and quarterly earnings.

**Fix**: The stock market is a voting machine in the short term and a weighing machine in the long term. Ignore the voting. Focus on the weighing.

## 4. Neglecting Your Network

**The mistake**: Trying to make investment decisions in isolation.

**Fix**: Your thinking improves when you share it with principled people. Solitude in decision-making is overrated.

> **Case: The Isolation Trap** (Chapter 9): Before his transformation, Spier made investment decisions in isolation, surrounded by people who reinforced his worst instincts. His decisions improved dramatically when he joined a community of value investors.
> **Key takeaway**: The quality of your decisions is directly related to the quality of the people you surround yourself with. Choose your community carefully.

## 5. Impatience

**The mistake**: Expecting investments to work out in months instead of years.

**Fix**: The stock market transfers wealth from the impatient to the patient. Be patient.
