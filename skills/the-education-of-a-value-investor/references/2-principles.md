> Source: The Education of a Value Investor, Chapters 4-7

# Key Principles

## 1. Temperament Is Everything

"Investing is not a test of IQ. Many intelligent people make terrible investors because they lack emotional control." The most important quality for an investor is not intelligence but temperament — the ability to stay calm when others panic and skeptical when others celebrate.

## 2. Know Your Circle of Competence

"The single most important thing you can do as an investor is be honest with yourself about what you know and don't know." Spier emphasizes that the circle of competence is not about what you COULD learn — it's about what you genuinely understand right now.

> **Case: The Tech Stock Avoidance** (Chapter 5): Spier describes how he avoided investing in technology stocks during the dot-com boom because they were outside his circle of competence. He watched others get rich — and then lose everything when the bubble burst. His discipline preserved his capital.
> **Key takeaway**: Staying within your circle of competence means missing opportunities that turn out to be real. It also means avoiding disasters. The discipline to say "I don't understand this" is worth more than the ability to identify the next big thing.

## 3. Your Network Shapes Your Judgment

"The people you surround yourself with determine the quality of your thinking." Spier deliberately built a network of value investors who shared his principles. Their influence improved his decisions.

## 4. Process Over Outcome

"You cannot control whether an investment works out. You can only control the process by which you make the decision." A good process can produce a bad outcome. A bad process can produce a good outcome. Over time, process wins.

## 5. Read Constantly

"Buffett reads 500 pages a day. His knowledge compounds like his money." Spier emphasizes that reading deeply — not just financial news but history, psychology, and biography — builds the foundation for good investment judgment.

> **Case: The Reading Habit** (Chapter 7): Spier committed to reading 30 minutes a day from a physical book, away from screens. This habit, sustained over years, transformed his knowledge base and his decision-making. The compound effect of daily reading is enormous.
> **Key takeaway**: Reading is not leisure — it's the most important investment you can make. An hour of focused reading every day compounds into extraordinary knowledge over decades.
