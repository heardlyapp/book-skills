# Reference: Reentry Chapter 2 — Learning to Be Scrappy

> **Context:** SpaceX's acquisition and development of Launch Complex 40 (SLC-40) at Cape Canaveral, 2006-2009.

## The Capricorn One Rocket

In January 2009, SpaceX stood a Falcon 9 rocket upright on SLC-40 — but the rocket was largely fake. The second stage was hollow, the payload fairing was a mock-up that would crush the real rocket under aerodynamic loads, and it had almost no flight computers. Employees called it "Capricorn One" (a 1978 movie about a faked Mars mission). Musk hated the nickname and banned it.

## The SLC-40 Lease Fight

Musk wanted the historic Titan launch pad (SLC-40) at Cape Canaveral. Lockheed Martin and Boeing (forming United Launch Alliance) lobbied aggressively to block SpaceX. The key decision fell to Air Force General Susan Helms, a former NASA astronaut who had set the record for longest spacewalk. She saw Musk had "an executable plan, not a marketing pitch." She went up the chain to four-star General Kevin Chilton (also a former astronaut). After a tense meeting, Chilton backed her. Helms called Musk to tell him, then called ULA CEO Mike Gass, who said only: "I am very disappointed."

## Building a $20M Launch Site

SpaceX was told to build the entire launch site — pad, ground systems, processing hangar — for $20M. ULA had spent $375M on a comparable Delta IV pad. Boeing spent $500M on a pad at Vandenberg. The National Reconnaissance Office spent an estimated $2B on a nearby processing facility.

SpaceX's solutions:

- **Prefab hangar:** Ordered a steel building kit advertised by Dan Marino on Florida radio, costing under $1M
- **LOX ball:** Bought an abandoned 65-foot nitrogen sphere from Apollo-era Launch Complex 37 as scrap for $86,001. Bolted on custom support legs, sent two interns (Tyler Grinnell and Chris Wallden) down inside with winches to inspect and clean it. Total cost including certification: ~$200K (vs $3M new). Hundreds of launches later, it's still working
- **No budgets:** When new launch director Brian Mosdell presented a "master plan with a budget" to senior leadership, they laughed. "At SpaceX, we don't create budgets. We just go and execute."
- **Scrounged pressure vessels:** Two employees dubbed "Sanford and Son" prospected abandoned pads for scrap tanks

## Brian Mosdell's Story

Mosdell came from ULA, where he'd been chief launch conductor for Atlas and Delta rockets. He initially thought SpaceX was "a PowerPoint company" until he visited Hawthorne and saw $25M in flight hardware. When Musk interviewed him, the conversation focused on the near-failure of the Delta IV Heavy's maiden flight. The slow investigation-by-committee at ULA had driven Mosdell crazy. At SpaceX, he found the opposite.

## Rebar Incident

Musk refused to approve purchase orders for steel rebar in the hangar's concrete foundation, questioning whether it was needed. Rather than argue, Mosdell broke the purchase orders into smaller amounts below Musk's approval threshold. "Any civil or structural engineer knows that's ridiculous," he said. "So we just broke the purchase orders down into smaller amounts, below his approval level, and moved on."
