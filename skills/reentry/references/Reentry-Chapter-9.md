# Reference: Reentry Chapter 9 — F-Squared and the Amos-6 Disaster

> **Context:** September 1, 2016 — a Falcon 9 explodes on the launch pad during a fueling test, destroying a $200M Israeli communications satellite (Amos-6).

## The Explosion

During a routine static fire test at SLC-40, the Falcon 9 upper stage's oxygen tank burst. The explosion destroyed the rocket and the Amos-6 satellite payload. There were no injuries, but the pad was heavily damaged. For SpaceX, it was the worst failure since the company nearly died during the Falcon 1 years.

## Root Cause

The investigation (led by Hans Koenigsmann, SpaceX's VP of Mission Assurance, and involving NASA, the Air Force, and the FAA) traced the failure to an interaction between liquid oxygen (kept at −340°F) and helium in the composite overwrapped pressure vessels (COPVs) inside the upper stage oxygen tank. Solid oxygen had formed and ignited, causing a cascade failure.

The fix: redesign the COPV loading process. Instead of loading helium into cold COPVs and then filling with LOX (which could create solid oxygen), SpaceX would pre-load LOX, then pressurize with helium. This required 3 additional minutes of fueling — which meant crewed missions would need to fuel astronauts *before* loading propellant, rather than after. This became a key safety consideration for Crew Dragon.

## "F-Squared"

The chapter title references both Falcon 9 and a crude name engineers used for the failure ("F***ing Falcon"). The incident was a turning point for the company: it was the first time a rocket exploded on the ground during a non-launch operation, suggesting their processes had gaps they hadn't considered.

## Aftermath

SpaceX's Falcon 9 fleet was grounded for five months. Launch delays cascaded. NASA's Commercial Crew program (which paid SpaceX to develop crewed Dragon flights) faced renewed scrutiny. The company had to repair SLC-40 (sustaining $50M+ in damage) while also preparing LC-39A (the historic Apollo/Shuttle pad at Kennedy Space Center) for alternative launches.

Musk described the moment as the second-hardest in SpaceX's history, after the near-death Falcon 1 failure in 2008. But the redesigned COPV process ultimately made the Falcon 9 safer, and the rigorous investigation demonstrated SpaceX's commitment to transparency — which in turn helped win NASA's trust for crewed missions.

## Broader Implications

The Amos-6 failure highlighted a fundamental tension at SpaceX: speed vs. safety. The company's "test often, fail fast" culture had to coexist with the unforgiving reliability requirements of human spaceflight. The resolution — rigorous failure investigation, process redesign, and acceptance that some "optimizations" aren't worth the risk — became a template for how SpaceX approached later challenges like Starship development.
