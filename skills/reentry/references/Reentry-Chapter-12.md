# Reference: Reentry Chapter 12 — A Second Space Age

> **Context:** The culmination of SpaceX's first two decades: Crew Dragon, Falcon Heavy, Starship, and the broader transformation of the space industry.

## Crew Dragon: America Returns to Orbit

On May 30, 2020, SpaceX launched NASA astronauts Bob Behnken and Doug Hurley to the International Space Station aboard Crew Dragon. It was the first crewed launch from U.S. soil since the Space Shuttle retired in 2011, and the first time a private company had ever flown humans to orbit.

This was the payoff of a decade of work: Dragon cargo flights (starting 2012), the pad abort test, the uncrewed orbital test (Demo-1), the in-flight abort test, and finally Demo-2. NASA's Commercial Crew program was structured as a public-private partnership where SpaceX bore significant development risk — a model that proved dramatically cheaper and faster than traditional NASA procurement.

## Falcon Heavy

The Falcon Heavy (2018 debut) was the world's most powerful operational rocket — capable of lifting 64 metric tons to orbit. Musk used the maiden flight to launch his personal Tesla Roadster toward Mars orbit, with a spacesuited mannequin ("Starman") in the driver's seat. The two side boosters landed simultaneously in synchronized touchdowns — an image that became iconic.

## Starship: The Fabergé Egg

Starship development was different from anything SpaceX had done before. Instead of building a rocket in secret and unveiling it, Musk invited the world to watch. The prototype construction in Boca Chica, Texas (renamed "Starbase") was done outdoors, in full view of cameras and spectators.

The first full-size prototype — a shiny stainless steel vehicle — was called the "Fabergé egg" by some engineers, partly because it was too precious to fail and partly because of how it gleamed in the Texas sun. The earlier suborbital tests (SN8, SN9, SN10, SN11, SN15) were a roller coaster: spectacular explosions, near-misses, and finally a successful high-altitude flight and landing by SN15 in May 2021.

The first Starship/Super Heavy integrated flight test on April 20, 2023, was the largest rocket ever flown. It cleared the tower, climbed for about 90 seconds, then tumbled and self-destructed. Musk's reaction: "I thought the SpaceX team did amazing work. This is really one of the hardest technical projects that humanity's ever done."

## The Starlink Revolution

In parallel with Starship, SpaceX built and deployed the world's largest satellite constellation. Starlink now provides internet to millions of users globally, generating revenue that funds Mars ambitions. The rapid iteration of the Starlink satellites (from block v0.9 through v2 Mini) mirrors SpaceX's hardware development philosophy: "build, test, fix, repeat."

## Berger's Central Question

The book closes with an unflinching look at the tension at the heart of SpaceX's future: **Elon Musk is the reason SpaceX succeeded, and he may also be the reason it stumbles.** His increasingly political persona, his acquisition of Twitter/X, his global entanglements (Ukraine Starlink, China relations, and so on) create new risks for a company utterly dependent on government contracts. Berger asks: **"The biggest players sometimes make the biggest falls when confronted by fresh-faced upstarts. Essentially, that is the very story told here."**
