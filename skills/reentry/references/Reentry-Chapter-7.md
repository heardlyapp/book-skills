# Reference: Reentry Chapter 7 — Get the Barge Ready

> **Context:** The multi-year effort to land Falcon 9 first stages on a drone ship in the ocean, and the Easter 2014 rocket recovery attempt.

## The Easter Rocket Hunt

April 2014: Falcon 9's 9th launch. The first stage made a controlled descent into the Atlantic, but video cut out 8 seconds after splashdown. Elon Musk became obsessive about recovering the stage. Over Easter weekend, Musk, Gwynne Shotwell, and launch director Zach Dunn called hundreds of contacts trying to mobilize Navy P-3 Orion surveillance aircraft.

At 4 AM on Easter Sunday, Dunn was woken by a phone call: "All J-3s remain silent!" He found himself on a conference call with one-star generals from every military branch plus the Secretary of Defense representative. In his underwear, in a dark rented apartment, Dunn gave them a situational briefing on the rocket's landing. The P-3s were cleared, but a SpaceX pilot found the debris field first. The stage had broken apart.

Dunn: "Those sorts of moments were always so tough, but also the ones in which you really learn what you are capable of doing. It was a classic Elon moment. I learned about an even deeper gear when it comes to not giving up until it is over."

## The History of Reusability Attempts

- **Space Shuttle:** Built reusable orbiters and boosters, but required 21,000-tile inspections between flights. Cost per pound to orbit: ~$25,000 (1,000× NASA's 1969 projection)
- **DC-X (Delta Clipper):** One-third scale vertical takeoff/landing prototype built in 21 months by 100 people. First flight August 1993: 59 seconds. After three successful flights, funding was cut. NASA briefly revived it, achieving a 26-hour turnaround between flights. Then a landing strut failed, the LOX tank exploded, and the program was killed — partly because NASA insiders preferred the winged X-33 concept
- **X-33:** Lockheed's single-stage-to-orbit space plane. Canceled in 2001 after nearly $1B spent, unable to solve liquid hydrogen tank problems. By 2004, NASA abandoned low-cost reusability entirely

Musk founded SpaceX in 2002 — the year NASA gave up.

## The Drone Ship

The autonomous droneship "Just Read the Instructions" (named after a Culture ship from Iain M. Banks' novels) was essentially a floating landing pad with thrusters to maintain position. The early landing attempts were spectacular failures — rockets tipping over, exploding on impact, missing the barge entirely. Each failure was treated as data.

## Grasshopper and F9R Dev

Before attempting barge landings, SpaceX developed vertical takeoff/landing technology with "Grasshopper" — a 106-foot test vehicle that flew higher and higher over McGregor. Later replaced by F9R Dev. These tests proved the guidance and throttle control needed for precision landings.

The first successful Falcon 9 landing came in December 2015 (on land, at Cape Canaveral). The first droneship landing followed in April 2016. Both were years in the making and dozens of crashes.
