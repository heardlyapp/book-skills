# 3 — Decision and Execution Techniques

> Source: "Reentry: SpaceX, Elon Musk, and the Reusable Rockets that Launched a Second Space Age" by Eric Berger

## Technique 1: The Pivot Protocol (How to Change Direction Fast)

When Musk decided mid-development that Falcon 5 wasn't powerful enough, he didn't commission a six-month study. He called his VP at 1 AM and said "nine engines." The team scrapped months of work. The technique:

1. **Decide in real time** — Musk makes big decisions in the middle of the night. Not because he's impulsive, but because the data is clear to him and waiting wastes time.
2. **Communicate immediately** — The decision reaches the team within hours, not weeks.
3. **Absorb sunk cost** — Previous work is gone. Don't try to salvage a wrong architecture.
4. **The team executes** — Once the decision is made, no second-guessing. Thompson: "Five engines, nine engines, whatever. He was the boss."

> **Case: Falcon 5 → Falcon 9** (Chapter 1): Musk saw NASA's cargo requirements and knew Falcon 5 couldn't cut it. He called Thompson at home after midnight. The next morning the team grumbled — "WTF is he thinking?" — but they executed. The alternative (a single giant "Merlin 2" engine) would have cost hundreds of millions and years.
> **Key takeaway**: When you know a decision is right, don't wait for consensus. Decide and execute.

## Technique 2: The Silver Bullet Protocol (When to Ignore Safety Systems)

"Silver bullet" was an internal term for those rare moments when Musk ordered the team to bypass all abort sensors and "let it go." The protocol:

1. **Only invoked when the launch window is vanishing** — SpaceX had 2-3 day windows on Kwajalein; missing meant a month's wait.
2. **The person calling it accepts full responsibility** — Musk told Buzza: "I will take the responsibility to remove that conservatism. When I tell you to let it go, turn off all the aborts and let it go."
3. **Anne Chinnery wrote the procedure** — A formal override protocol existed, not just a reckless gamble.
4. **Not for routine use** — It was the exception, not the rule. Most tests ran with full abort systems.

> **Case: The Falcon 9 Test-Firing** (Chapter 1): During the first full-duration firing, engineers ran dry runs all night, finding a bug at 2 AM that would have opened every valve on the rocket. They fixed it, slept under desks for a few hours, and fired the next day. They did NOT invoke silver bullet — they fixed the problem.
> **Key takeaway**: Have a "break glass" protocol for emergencies, but fix the root cause first. Silver bullet is Plan Z.

## Technique 3: Green Lights to Malibu (The Deadline Framework)

Musk's schedules assumed "green lights all the way" — no traffic, no delays, everything goes perfectly. The technique:

1. **Set the impossible deadline publicly** — Musk told reporters the Falcon 9 would launch summer 2009. It launched summer 2010. Off by a year — but it actually launched.
2. **The team builds the real schedule underneath** — Engineers know the real timeline, but the public deadline creates urgency.
3. **Creative pressure** — When the December 2008 test-firing succeeded, the team had six weeks to get the rocket from McGregor to Cape Canaveral, ship it, and stand it up. They did it over Christmas.
4. **The cost is burnout** — This technique extracts maximum output but at human cost. 36-hour shifts, sleeping under desks, families sacrificed.

> **Case: The Christmas Miracle** (Chapter 2): After the November 2008 test-fire, SpaceX had six weeks to ship the Falcon 9 first stage from Texas to Florida, assemble it (on a concrete pad with no hangar yet), and stand it up for a January showcase. Engineers worked through Christmas. Roger Carlson texted Musk a picture from atop the 200-foot lightning tower.
> **Key takeaway**: A hard public deadline creates momentum that internal schedules can't match. But it costs personal life — plan for recovery cycles.

## Technique 4: Scavenge → Improvise → Execute (The Budget Playbook)

When you have 5% of the normal budget, you can't buy what you need. You find it.

1. **Scavenge**: Find abandoned, repurposable assets. The LOX ball from Apollo-era Launch Complex 37. The tripod test stand from Beal Aerospace. Scrap pressure vessels from defunct programs.
2. **Improvise**: The LOX ball needed support legs and certification. Interns went inside with winches. Total cost: ~$200K instead of $3M.
3. **Execute**: Once the improvised solution works, it becomes infrastructure. The $86K LOX ball has supported hundreds of launches.

> **Case: Brian Mosdell's Launch Site** (Chapter 2): Mosdell came from ULA, where he was chief launch conductor for Atlas and Delta. At SpaceX, he had to build a launch site for $20M — 5% of what ULA spent on a comparable pad. He bought a prefab building kit advertised by Dan Marino on Florida radio. He found a retired LOX ball at an abandoned launch complex. He hired a crane operator from a local rigging service. The pad still works 15+ years later.
> **Key takeaway**: Constraints force creativity. A 20X budget difference doesn't mean 20X worse results — it means you must find 20X smarter solutions.

## Technique 5: The Pathfinder Strategy (Ship the Mock-Up)

Before the real Falcon 9 could fly, SpaceX shipped what was essentially a mock-up — hollow second stage, fake fairing, no avionics. The purpose was to:

1. Test ground systems (hydraulics, fueling, transporter-erector)
2. Integrate with the launch pad infrastructure
3. Prove SpaceX could operate at Cape Canaveral
4. Build confidence with NASA and policymakers
5. Find issues before the real rocket arrived

> **Case: Capricorn One** (Chapter 2): The January 2009 Falcon 9 unveiling at SLC-40 was mostly theater, but it served a real purpose. The ground systems checked out. The transporter-erector worked perfectly. SpaceX demonstrated it could operate at America's oldest launch facility. "From a launch operations guy, I see the value of doing a pathfinder," Roger Carlson said.
> **Key takeaway**: A pathfinder doesn't need to be functional. It needs to test the critical interfaces. The real learning happens at the boundaries.
