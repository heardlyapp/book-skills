# Reference: Reentry Chapter 1 — A Violent Beast

> **Context:** The first full-duration test-firing of the Falcon 9 rocket, November 22, 2008, at SpaceX's McGregor, Texas test site.

## Key Scene

Tom Mueller, SpaceX's VP of Propulsion, ran out of the control bunker to witness the Falcon 9's nine engines fire together for 178 seconds. The sound was so immense it rattled windows 20 miles away; locals called 911 thinking World War III had begun or the Sun had exploded. The company had placed ads in the *McGregor Mirror* and posted warnings on the high school marquee, but nothing prepared Central Texas for the roar.

## The Merlin Engine

A single Merlin 1C engine (2008) produced 94,000 pounds of thrust by burning 350 pounds of propellant per second — more than a car uses driving NYC to Miami. The nozzle handled 10 megawatts of thermal energy through walls only 3 feet across. Clustering nine of these "violent beasts" into a 12-foot diameter base was a nightmare of thermal management, plumbing, and ignition timing.

## The 9-Engine Decision

Musk originally planned a "Falcon 5" rocket. In the middle of the night, making decisions as he often does, Musk called VP Chris Thompson and told him it would now be nine engines. Thompson and Mueller grumbled but got to work. The alternative — building a single huge "Merlin 2" engine — would have cost hundreds of millions and years.

## Silver Bullet

Musk sometimes demanded teams ignore abort sensors and "let it go" — called a "silver bullet" by launch engineers Tim Buzza and Anne Chinnery. The term came from Falcon 1 days on Kwajalein, where launch windows were 2-3 days and a missed window meant a month's wait. With the Falcon 9, engineers fixed a bug at 2 AM that would have opened every valve on the rocket instead of just the water valves. They slept under desks and fired the next day.

## Kevin Miller's Story

Miller, the 29-year-old Merlin engine lead, used a co-op at NASA but found "people who worked on things for twenty years and never saw anything fly." At SpaceX, he was responsible for nine Merlin engines and 18 TVC actuators. During countdown, he says he "more or less blacked out" from hyperfocus. His comparison: "At NASA people worked on things that got canceled. This was already my second rocket."

## The Tripod

The test stand in McGregor was a 135-foot concrete tripod left over from Beal Aerospace. Engineers worked on bomb-bay-style doors 100+ feet above ground that "rippled and dented" under their weight. A dog named Rockette ("dog not scared") became the culture mascot. The Stairway to Heaven led to a platform with no rails that swayed in the wind. New hires were pranked by veteran engineers into thinking hypergolic fuel had leaked.
