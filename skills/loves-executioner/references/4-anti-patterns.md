# Anti-Patterns — What Therapists (and Everyone) Get Wrong

> Source: Love's Executioner, Chapters 1, 4, 7, 9, Afterword

## The Five Errors

### 1. The Blank Screen
**Error**: The therapist should be a neutral, anonymous listener.
**Correction**: Yalom reveals himself. He shares his feelings, his confusion, his mistakes. This is not unprofessional — it is the foundation of authentic connection.

### 2. Avoiding Death Talk
**Error**: Therapists should not talk about death — it will upset patients.
**Correction**: Death is the elephant in the room. Ignoring it wastes the most powerful opportunity for growth.

### 3. The Quick Fix
**Error**: Therapy should relieve symptoms quickly.
**Correction**: Symptoms are signals of existential pain. Quick relief (medication, cognitive restructuring) without addressing the underlying givens is superficial.

### 4. The Professional Distance
**Error**: The therapist must maintain distance to be objective.
**Correction**: Yalom wrote: "It is the relationship that heals." Distance prevents healing.

### 5. The Pathologizing of Normal Pain
**Error**: Existential distress is a disorder to be treated.
**Correction**: Existence pain is not a disorder — it is part of being human. The goal is not to eliminate it but to respond to it with courage.

> **Case: Yalom's Own Countertransference** (Chapter 9)
> In "Therapeutic Monogamy," Yalom treats a therapist colleague who is attracted to her patient. Yalom realizes: he is attracted to her. He feels competitive with her patient. He is not neutral — he is a jealous man. He uses this self-awareness therapeutically, sharing some of it with her. The honesty deepens their work. His vulnerability becomes a tool.
> **Key takeaway:** Yalom's willingness to examine his own feelings — and occasionally share them — is the opposite of the "blank screen" model. The lesson: if you avoid your own emotions as a therapist, you are not helping. You are hiding.

> **Case: The Patient Who Did Not Change** (Afterword)
> Not every therapy succeeded. Some patients did not grow. Some left. One returned years later and said Yalom had helped after all — but she needed years to process it. Yalom's honesty about his failures is as important as his successes. Therapy is not magic. It is two people struggling together.
> **Key takeaway:** The best therapists fail. Yalom's willingness to include his failures in the book is a model of professional honesty. The lesson: do not pretend to be perfect. The patient knows you are not.
