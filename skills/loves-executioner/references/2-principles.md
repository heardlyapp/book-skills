> **Case: "If Rape Were Legal..." — Carlos** (Chapter 2)
> Carlos, a young man with lymphatic cancer, became compulsively promiscuous after his diagnosis. He told Yalom: "If rape were legal, I'd do it. I'd fuck every woman I see." Yalom did not judge. He explored: what is Carlos running from? Death. The sex was a talisman against mortality — proof he was still alive, still desired, still powerful. Yalom helped Carlos see that his promiscuity was not about pleasure — it was about terror. Carlos's last words: "Though the fact of death destroys us, the idea of death may save us."
> **Key takeaway:** Carlos's story is Yalom's most dramatic illustration of death anxiety driving behavior. The compulsive sexuality was not a "sex problem" — it was a death problem. When Carlos faced his mortality, he was able to live his remaining time with meaning.

> **Case: "The Wrong One Died" — Rose** (Chapter 3)
> Rose's daughter was killed in a car accident. Rose was consumed by grief — but she had two living sons she was neglecting. Her therapy was about facing the guilt of survival. "The wrong one died" was her unspoken thought: she wished it had been one of her sons, not her perfect daughter. Yalom helped her face this unbearable thought without judgment. Only by acknowledging the unacceptable could she release it.
> **Key takeaway:** Yalom's approach to grief is: do not reassure. Do not minimize. Face the truth. Rose needed to acknowledge her unacceptable feelings about her sons. The therapy worked because Yalom did not flinch. He stayed with her in the unbearable.
