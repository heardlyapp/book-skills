# Reference 01: Series Overview & Character Guide

## The Advocate Series — An Overview

The Advocate series by Teresa Burrell follows Sabre Orin Brown, a juvenile court attorney in San Diego who has a knack for getting drawn into murder investigations. The series blends legal procedural elements with classic whodunit mystery, creating a subgenre that Burrell has made her own: the legal suspense murder mystery.

### Series Chronology

The series began in 2009 with *The Advocate* and has grown to over 20 installments as of 2026. Each book follows a similar structural pattern: a crime occurs (usually a murder), Sabre becomes involved through her legal work or personal connections, evidence points in multiple directions, courtroom scenes alternate with investigation scenes, and the story builds toward a climactic reveal that ties together the legal and personal threads.

### Sabre Brown — The Advocate's Biography

Sabre Orin Brown is the heart of the series. A juvenile court attorney based in San Diego, she represents children and teenagers in the foster care and juvenile justice systems. Her professional life is marked by fierce advocacy for her young clients, often putting her at odds with the system she works within.

**Personal History:** Sabre's background includes the disappearance of her brother Ron, a thread that runs through multiple books in the series. This personal trauma informs her drive to find the truth — she has experienced firsthand what it means to live with unanswered questions. Her relationship with her brother is explored in depth in *The Advocate's Felony* (Book 6), where Ron resurfaces under dangerous circumstances.

**Professional Identity:** Sabre is not a traditional defense attorney. She works in juvenile court, which means her clients are children. This changes the nature of her advocacy — she is as much a protector as a legal representative. Her dual role as attorney and amateur sleuth has become the signature of the series. She is unwilling to accept surface-level explanations, whether they come from police reports, prosecution evidence, or her own clients.

**Character Traits:**
- Tenacious: Once she takes a case, she follows every lead relentlessly
- Empathic: She connects deeply with her young clients, understanding their fear and vulnerability
- Intuitive: She trusts her gut when evidence points the wrong way
- Protective: She will risk her own safety to protect those in her care
- Principled: She operates by a strong internal moral code that sometimes conflicts with strict legal ethics

### JP — The Partner and Investigator

JP is Sabre's partner in investigation — a private investigator or law enforcement contact who works alongside her on cases. In *The Advocate's Justice*, JP's role expands dramatically because the case involves his own family.

**Character Significance:** JP has been a constant presence throughout the series, providing investigative muscle, law enforcement connections, and a personal anchor for Sabre. His relationship with Sabre is one of the series' enduring dynamics — they trust each other implicitly, even when their methods differ.

**Family Connection:** Book 10 marks the first time JP's family becomes central to a case. The murder of Donald Granger forces JP to confront his family's history, including old grudges and secrets his relatives have worked hard to bury. This makes the investigation deeply personal for him, testing his objectivity and straining his relationship with Sabre as she pushes him to reveal what he knows.

**Case Example 1 — JP's Family Loyalty Tested:**
When Sabre first realizes the case connects to JP's family, she asks him directly about his relatives' involvement. JP hesitates. He knows that sharing what he knows could damage his family relationships, but withholding it could cost Conner his freedom. This moment crystallizes the central tension of the book: JP must choose between family loyalty and the pursuit of justice. The conflict is not resolved quickly — it simmers throughout the investigation, surfacing every time they interview a family member who gives a different version of events.

**Case Example 2 — The Confrontation with JP's Relative:**
In one of the book's most charged scenes, JP confronts a family member who has been hiding information. The confrontation is not just about the murder — it is about years of accumulated grievances, old betrayals, and the weight of family history. Burrell uses this scene to show that family secrets are never about one thing. They are layered, interconnected, and explosive when disturbed. JP emerges from this confrontation shaken, realizing that the person he thought he knew is capable of things he never imagined.

### Conner — The Accused

Conner is a fifteen-year-old boy accused of murdering Donald Granger, his grandmother's abusive boyfriend. He is the client at the center of *The Advocate's Justice*, and his fate drives the entire narrative.

**Character Profile:**
- Age: 15
- Relationship: Grandson of the victim's girlfriend
- Accusation: Murder of Donald Granger
- Motive: Granger was abusive toward Conner's grandmother
- Evidence: Eyewitness accounts and forensic evidence point to him

Conner represents the type of client Sabre specializes in: a vulnerable young person whom society has already judged. The presumption of innocence is a legal concept, not a social reality, and Conner experiences this gap firsthand. Everyone — the police, the prosecution, the community — believes he is guilty. Only Sabre is willing to ask whether the obvious answer is the right one.

### Donald Granger — The Victim

Donald Granger is the murder victim. He was the abusive boyfriend of Conner's grandmother, and his death is the event that sets the plot in motion. While Granger appears primarily through the evidence of his death and the testimony of those who knew him, his character casts a long shadow over the story.

**Character Significance:** Granger's abuse of his girlfriend is the motive attributed to Conner. But as Sabre investigates, she discovers that Granger had more enemies than just the family of the woman he abused. His death becomes a lens through which the hidden history of JP's family is examined.

### The Supporting Cast

**Conner's Grandmother:** The woman at the center of the abusive relationship with Donald Granger. Her testimony and her relationship with Conner are crucial to the defense. She is both a witness and a victim, and her loyalty is divided between the memory of her deceased partner and the grandson accused of killing him.

**JP's Family Members:** A collection of relatives whose grudges, secrets, and conflicting accounts form the web of lies that Sabre must untangle. Each family member has a different version of events, and each has something to hide.

**The Prosecution:** The legal adversary Sabre must face in court. The prosecution has a strong case built on eyewitness testimony and forensic evidence, and they are confident in their theory of the crime.

### Series Continuity

*The Advocate's Justice* draws on the series' established relationships and character histories. Readers who have followed the series will recognize the dynamic between Sabre and JP, understand the weight of Sabre's history with her brother, and appreciate how JP's family background (hinted at in earlier books) is finally explored in depth.

For new readers, the book provides enough context to stand alone, though the emotional impact of JP's family revelations will be stronger for those who have followed his journey across multiple books.
