# Techniques: Practical Logotherapy Methods

## Technique 1: The Socratic Dialogue
Logotherapy uses Socratic dialogue to help patients discover their own meaning. The therapist doesn't tell the patient what their meaning is. They ask questions that help the patient find it themselves. "What are you living for?" "What would you do if you knew the world would end tomorrow?"

## Technique 2: Dereflection
When a patient is hyper-focused on a problem or symptom, dereflection redirects attention away from the problem and toward meaning. The more you focus on happiness, the more it eludes you. Focus on meaning, and happiness follows.

> **Case: The Insomniac** (Part II): Frankl describes a patient who couldn't sleep because he was trying too hard to sleep. The more he tried, the more anxious he became, and the more the sleep eluded him. Frankl told him to stop trying to sleep and instead use the wakefulness for something meaningful — reading, writing, thinking. He fell asleep almost immediately.
> **Key takeaway:** Paradoxical intention: try to do the opposite of what you want, and the desired outcome occurs naturally. The harder you try to be happy, the more it escapes you.

## Technique 3: The Attitude Modulation
When a situation cannot be changed, the only option is to change your attitude toward it. This is not denial. It is the active choice to find meaning in unavoidable suffering. The question shifts from "Why is this happening to me?" to "What is this asking of me?"

## Technique 4: Meaning Audit
List the areas of your life where meaning could be discovered: work (what are you creating?), love (who do you care for?), attitude (how are you facing your challenges?). Identify which path to meaning is most available to you right now and pursue it actively.

## Technique 5: The Existential Analysis
Ask: "If my life were to end today, would I consider it meaningful?" If the answer is no, the follow-up question is: "What is missing?" Then pursue that missing element through work, love, or attitude change.

The existential analysis technique helps identify the gap between your current life and a meaningful one. If you were to write your life story today, would it be worth reading? If not, what chapter needs to change? This is not about regret. It is about recognizing that the next chapter is unwritten and you hold the pen.


> **Case: The Paradox of Happiness** (Part II): Frankl describes the "pleasure principle" as a modern myth. A young man came to Frankl obsessed with being happy. Every experience was judged by how much pleasure it provided. Nothing made him happy. Frankl told him: "Stop pursuing happiness. Pursue meaning. Happiness is a side effect."
> **Key takeaway:** The direct pursuit of happiness is self-defeating. Happiness is a byproduct of the pursuit of meaning.

The dereflection technique can be applied to any problem that gets worse when you focus on it. Anxiety, insomnia, performance pressure - all of these improve when you redirect attention toward meaning. Stop focusing on the symptom. Focus on what matters. The symptom subsides on its own.
