# Key Principles — Privatization, the Corporate Threat, and the Battle for Public Services

## Core Principles

> **Case: The Cherry-Picking Problem** (Chapter 4): Private carriers like FedEx and UPS want USPS's profitable package business without the obligation to serve unprofitable rural routes. They lobby for laws that restrict USPS's ability to compete on price while they take the most profitable segments. This is not competition. It is cherry-picking. The market, left to itself, would serve profitable urban and suburban routes and leave rural America with no service at all.
> **Key takeaway**: "Competition" from private carriers does not mean better service for everyone. It means better service for some — and no service for others. Universal service requires public provision.

> **Case: The Price of a Stamp** (Chapter 5): The price of a first-class stamp remains remarkably affordable. A stamp costs about $0.55 and delivers a letter anywhere in the US within 1-3 days. This is one of the greatest bargains in the developed world. The USPS is not overcharging. It is undercharging for universal service. And that is exactly why it is worth protecting.
> **Key takeaway**: A stamp is not just the price of delivery. It is the price of universal connectivity. At $0.55, it is a bargain.

> **Case: The Public Service vs Business Framing** (Foreword): Ralph Nader, who wrote the foreword, frames the debate: "The post office is not a business. It is a public service. When you judge a public service by business metrics, you always find it wanting. But the failure is not the service. It is the metric." This framing is the key to the entire book.
> **Key takeaway**: Do not let opponents define the terms of debate. The post office is not a business. It should not be judged by business metrics. Reframe the conversation.
