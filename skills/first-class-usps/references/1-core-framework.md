# Core Framework — The Manufactured Crisis and the Prefunding Mandate

## The Model

Shaw's framework: the USPS is a successful public service made to look like a failure by a deliberately burdensome law. The prefunding mandate is the engine of the manufactured crisis. Remove it, and the USPS is viable.

> **Case: The Prefunding Mandate** (Chapter 2): In 2006, Congress passed the Postal Accountability and Enhancement Act. Buried in the bill was a requirement: the USPS must prefund 75 years of retiree health benefits — in just 10 years. No other government agency or private company faces this requirement. The mandate cost USPS $5-6 billion per year. Before the mandate, USPS was self-sufficient. After, it reported massive losses. The mandate was not about fiscal responsibility. It was about creating the appearance of failure to justify privatization.
> **Key takeaway**: When you hear that the post office is losing money, ask: "Is that including the prefunding mandate?" Without it, the story changes completely.

> **Case: The Universal Service Obligation** (Chapter 1): The USPS is required to deliver mail to every address in America, six days a week, at the same price. This includes remote Alaskan villages, small islands, and rural routes where delivery costs far exceed the price of a stamp. Private carriers do not have this obligation. They can choose profitable routes and ignore the rest. This is why the USPS cannot be replaced by private carriers. It does something no private company would do.
> **Key takeaway**: Universal service is not inefficiency. It is the mission. The question is not whether the USPS can be more efficient. It is whether we want every American to have access to mail at the same price.

> **Case: The Package Revenue Paradox** (Chapter 3): While first-class mail volume has declined (email), package delivery has boomed (e-commerce). USPS actually delivers more packages than FedEx and UPS to residential addresses. Amazon relies on USPS for "last mile" delivery. This package revenue is growing. The story that mail is dying and USPS with it is incomplete. The mail is changing. USPS is adapting.
> **Key takeaway**: The USPS is not dying. It is evolving. Package delivery is growing. The question is whether Congress will let USPS adapt or will strangle it with mandates designed for a different era.
