# Techniques — Postal Banking and the Future of the USPS

## Practical Methods

> **Case: The Postal Savings System** (Chapter 6): From 1911 to 1967, the USPS operated a savings bank system. Americans could deposit money at their local post office and earn modest interest. The system was designed to serve immigrants and the working poor who did not trust or have access to private banks. At its peak, the system held $34 billion (in today's dollars). It was phased out not because it failed but because private banks lobbied for its elimination.
> **Key takeaway**: Postal banking is not a radical new idea. It worked for 56 years. It was killed by private interests who did not want the competition. It can be revived.

> **Case: The Unbanked Problem** (Chapter 7): Over 7 million US households are unbanked — no checking account, no savings account, no access to mainstream financial services. They rely on payday lenders and check-cashing services that charge predatory fees. Shaw argues that postal banking would give these households a safe, affordable alternative. Many countries — including Japan, France, and Switzerland — have successful postal banking systems.
> **Key takeaway**: Postal banking is a solution to a real problem: financial exclusion. Millions of Americans pay hundreds of dollars a year in predatory fees because they have no bank. The post office can change this.

> **Case: The Reform Agenda** (Conclusion): Shaw proposes a concrete reform agenda: repeal the prefunding mandate, allow USPS to offer banking services, invest in new sorting facilities and electric vehicles, protect six-day delivery, and remove the restrictions that prevent USPS from competing fairly with private carriers.
> **Key takeaway**: The solutions exist. They are not complicated. The obstacle is political will, not economic feasibility.
