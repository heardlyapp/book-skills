# Anti-Patterns — The Business Frame, the False Crisis, and the Efficiency Myth

## The Central Fallacy

The most dangerous assumption: that the USPS is a business that should be run like one. The business frame leads to every wrong conclusion — that the post office should cut service to save money, that rural routes should be eliminated, that private carriers are inherently more efficient. The USPS was never designed to be a business. It was designed to be a public service.

> **Case: The Sorting Machine Destruction** (Chapter 8): In 2020, Postmaster General Louis DeJoy ordered the removal of hundreds of high-speed mail sorting machines. The machines were dismantled without congressional approval. Critics said the goal was to slow mail delivery before the 2020 election. The USPS said it was a cost-cutting measure. Regardless of intent, the effect was slower mail. The destruction of functional equipment in the name of efficiency is the anti-pattern in its purest form.
> **Key takeaway**: When efficiency measures make the service worse, they are not efficiency measures. They are sabotage dressed up as management.

> **Case: The Rural Post Office Closure** (Chapter 9): The USPS has closed hundreds of rural post offices since 2012. Each closure saves a small amount of money. Each closure devastates a community. In many small towns, the post office is the last remaining public institution — the place where neighbors meet, where packages are sent, where community happens. The savings are tiny. The losses are enormous.
> **Key takeaway**: The cost of closing a rural post office is not measured in dollars. It is measured in community, connection, and belonging. These costs are invisible to spreadsheets.

> **Case: The Efficiency Trap** (Chapter 10): Shaw argues that "efficiency" is a loaded term. Efficient for whom? Cutting delivery from six days to five saves money but reduces service. Eliminating Saturday delivery saves money but hurts small businesses. Raising prices saves money but prices out poor Americans. There is no neutral "efficiency." Every efficiency measure is a choice about who loses.
> **Key takeaway**: When someone says "we need to make the post office more efficient," ask: "Efficient for what purpose? And who will bear the cost?"
