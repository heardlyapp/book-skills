# 3. Techniques — Network Analysis of Historical Data

> Source: "The Rise and Fall of Imperial China" by Yuhua Wang (2022). Methods drawn from the Introduction and Appendices.

## The Tomb Epitaph Method

Wang's most innovative technique is using tomb epitaphs (墓志铭) to reconstruct elite social networks. Over 10,000 tomb epitaphs from the Tang and Song dynasties were analyzed to map marriage patterns, kinship connections, and geographic dispersion.

**How it works:**
1. Tomb epitaphs record the deceased's family background, marriage connections, and official career.
2. By analyzing the geographic origins of marriage partners and kinship ties, researchers can map the geographic scope of elite networks.
3. Tang elites married across regions (star network). Song elites married locally (bowtie network).

> **Case: The Epitaph Data** (Chapters 3-4): Wang's dataset shows that during the Tang Dynasty, major officials' marriage networks spanned multiple provinces. By the Song, marriage networks had contracted to single prefectures. This shift — measurable through epitaph data — corresponds to the transition from star to bowtie network. The data is not anecdotal: it's systematic analysis of thousands of documents.
> **Key takeaway**: Historical social science can turn qualitative sources (tomb epitaphs) into quantitative data by systematically coding network relationships.

## The Bowtie Network Identification Method

Wang operationalizes the bowtie network concept through four measures:
1. **Geographic concentration**: The spatial distribution of an elite's social connections
2. **Network density**: How many elites are connected to each other
3. **Factionalization**: The degree of division among elite groups
4. **Centrality**: How much the ruler controls elite connections

## Measuring Fiscal Capacity

Wang uses tax revenue as a percentage of GDP as the primary measure of state strength. His dataset spans 1,300 years (618-1911) and includes:
- Per capita taxation levels
- Tax as share of GDP (estimated)
- Fiscal policy changes (by dynasty and ruler)
- Comparison with European states

The decline from Song (15% of GDP) to Qing (1%) is the most striking empirical finding.

> **Case: The Fiscal Decline Data** (Chapter 2): Wang shows that per capita taxation in China declined steadily from the Tang through the Qing. In the eleventh century, the Song state collected more taxes per person than any European state. By the nineteenth century, the Qing collected less than almost any. This decline correlates with the shift in elite social terrain from star to bowtie to ring.
> **Key takeaway**: Long-term fiscal data reveals structural changes that contemporary observers might not have noticed. Trends visible over centuries offer insights invisible in any single year.

## The Comparative Method

Wang's analysis is explicitly comparative, contrasting:
- China vs. Europe (different paths of state development)
- Different Chinese dynasties (Tang vs. Song vs. Ming vs. Qing)
- Different regions within China (north vs. south, core vs. periphery)

> **Case: China vs. England** (Chapter 9): Wang compares China's bowtie equilibrium with England's star network. England after 1066 had Norman aristocrats whose landholdings and marriage networks spanned the country. This star network created demand for a strong state — and produced the fiscal-military state that dominated the modern era. China's bowtie network, by contrast, produced a durable but weak state that was resilient but unable to compete with European powers in the nineteenth century.
> **Key takeaway**: Comparative analysis reveals that China's path was not a "failure" but a different equilibrium with its own logic. The star-bowtie-ring framework explains both paths without assuming European superiority.
