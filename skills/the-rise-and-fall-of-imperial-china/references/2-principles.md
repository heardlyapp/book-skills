# 2. Principles — The Logic of State Development

> Source: "The Rise and Fall of Imperial China" by Yuhua Wang (2022). Principles drawn from the Introduction (Chapter 1) and Conclusion (Chapter 9).

## The 7 Principles of State Development

### Principle 1: Networks, Not Culture, Determine State Capacity
The dominant explanation for China's political trajectory emphasizes culture (Confucianism) or ideology. Wang argues that the structure of elite social networks — not what people believed — determined state outcomes. When elite networks changed, state capacity changed, even though culture remained constant.

### Principle 2: The Compatibility Problem
State development is constrained by the "compatibility problem": the ruler wants security and revenue; elites want services and autonomy. These goals are only compatible under specific network configurations. Star networks align elite interests with state strength. Bowtie networks create a stable but low-capacity equilibrium.

> **Case: The Tang-Song Transition** (Chapters 3-4): Wang uses tomb epitaph data to show how elite social networks changed between the Tang and Song. In the Tang, elites married across regions, creating national kinship networks. In the Song, elites married locally. This shift — documented through thousands of tomb epitaphs — is the material basis for the transition from star to bowtie network.
> **Key takeaway**: Political change can be measured through social network data. Changes in marriage patterns, gift exchange, and kinship ties reveal structural transformations that precede political change.

### Principle 3: Strong States Require Cohesive Elites
Counterintuitively, a strong state requires strong societal actors, not weak ones. Cohesive elites can demand accountability from the ruler and have the incentive to fund the state. Weak, divided elites produce weak states.

### Principle 4: Fiscal Capacity Tells the Real Story
Tax rates are the most reliable single indicator of state strength. The decline in Chinese fiscal capacity from ~15% of GDP under the Song to ~1% under the Qing is not a technical failure — it's a political choice embedded in the bowtie network equilibrium.

### Principle 5: Private Order Is Not Free Order
When elites provide their own governance through clans and lineages, it's efficient locally but costly nationally. Private-order institutions compete with the state and resist incorporation.

> **Case: The Rise of Lineage Organizations** (Chapters 6-7): During the Ming and Qing, lineage organizations (clans) became powerful local institutions. They managed schools, resolved disputes, provided welfare, and organized militias. From the perspective of local communities, these were effective governance structures. From the perspective of the central state, they were rivals that consumed resources and loyalty that might otherwise have gone to the state.
> **Key takeaway**: Strong local institutions can weaken the central state, even if they serve local communities well. The relationship between civil society and state capacity is not always positive.

### Principle 6: Institutional Persistence Can Be Pathological
The bowtie equilibrium lasted from the Song through the Qing — nearly a thousand years. But institutional persistence is not the same as institutional health. The same structures that created stability in the Song became obstacles to adaptation in the Qing.

### Principle 7: State Failure Is a Process, Not an Event
The Qing Dynasty did not collapse suddenly. It underwent a centuries-long process of fiscal decline, elite defection, and institutional erosion. The Opium War, the Taiping Rebellion, and the 1911 Revolution were symptoms, not causes.
