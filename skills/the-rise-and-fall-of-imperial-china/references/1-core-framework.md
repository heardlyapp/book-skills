# 1. Core Framework — Elite Social Terrain

> Source: "The Rise and Fall of Imperial China" by Yuhua Wang (2022). Chapter 1: Elite Social Terrain and State Development.

## The Three Ideal Types

Wang's core contribution is the concept of "elite social terrain" — the network structure connecting central elites to local social groups. Three ideal types:

### Star Network (Oligarchy)
- Central elites are connected to geographically dispersed social groups.
- Elites are connected to each other through overlapping networks.
- Result: Strong state but short-lived rulers.
- Elites prefer a strong state (it serves their dispersed interests efficiently).
- Elites can check the ruler's power (they are cohesive and organized).
- Approximate example: England after Norman conquest.

### Bowtie Network (Partnership)
- Each central elite is connected to social groups in a confined geographic area.
- Elites are NOT connected to each other.
- Result: Durable but weak state.
- Elites prefer private-order institutions (clans, lineages) over the central state.
- Elites cannot check the ruler (they are divided), but the ruler also can't force them to pay taxes.
- Approximate example: Feudal France.
- **China's dominant equilibrium for most of its imperial history (Song through late Qing).**

### Ring Network (Warlordism)
- Central elites are not connected to any social groups — or to each other.
- Result: State failure.
- Elites have no incentive to support the central state.
- Local powerholders (warlords, chiefs) govern independently.
- Approximate example: Pre-colonial sub-Saharan Africa.
- **China's equilibrium in the late Qing, leading to the dynasty's collapse.**

> **Case: The Tang Dynasty Star Network** (Chapters 2-3): During the Tang Dynasty (618-907), the central elite was composed of aristocratic families connected to land and people across multiple regions. These elites had broad kinship networks that spanned the country. They used the central state to provide public goods across their dispersed holdings. The result: the Tang had a strong fiscal state (high taxation) and an effective bureaucracy. But the same elite cohesion meant that rulers could be checked — Tang emperors were frequently deposed or controlled by elite factions.
> **Key takeaway**: A star network produces strong states because elites demand state services for their dispersed interests, and their cohesion enables them to demand accountability.

> **Case: The Song Dynasty Bowtie** (Chapters 4-5): After the Tang collapse and the chaos of the Five Dynasties period (907-960), the Song Dynasty emerged with a fundamentally different elite structure. The old aristocratic families had been destroyed. The new elites were local gentry — scholars and landowners connected to their home regions, not the nation. These elites did not need a strong central state because they could provide governance locally through clan organizations. The result: the Song taxed less than the Tang, its military was weaker, but its rulers were exceptionally secure (Song emperors rarely faced deposition).
> **Key takeaway**: The bowtie network creates stability for rulers but weakens the state. This is the "strength-durability trade-off."
