# Key Principles — Separated

## I. The Policy Was Deliberate and Systematic

Family separation was not an accident or unintended consequence. "Zero tolerance" meant every illegal border crossing would be prosecuted. Since parents cannot take children to federal prison, separation was the necessary result. The March 2017 announcement, the El Paso pilot, the April 2018 memo — all led to a policy that Stephen Miller had been advocating for years. "The Trump administration's family separation policy was an avoidable catastrophe."

## II. The Government Had No Plan

"We don't have those numbers" was the response Soboroff heard repeatedly. The government had no system for tracking which child belonged to which parent. Children were moved between facilities without documentation. Parents were deported without their children. "The government's data was incomplete." As of 2020, nobody knows exactly how many families were separated.

## III. The Facilities Were Cages

Casa Padre was a former Walmart. Ursula BP Station had "pods" that looked like "animal kennels." Children slept on concrete floors. The government lawyer argued they didn't need blankets or toothbrushes. "I don't know any other way to describe it," Soboroff said on national TV.

## IV. The Trauma Is Permanent

The American Academy of Pediatrics says the practice will leave thousands of kids traumatized for life. A three-year-old climbed onto a table in court, asking for her mother. A mother in another case: "It hurts in my heart every day." Juan and José were separated for nearly five months — José slept on a concrete floor while his father searched for him across three states.

## V. The Legal System Eventually Acted

Judge Dana Sabraw's ruling: "The Government's action shocks the conscience." The ACLU's case, Ms. L v. ICE, forced the government to stop separating families and begin reunification. But reunification was slow, chaotic, and incomplete. Hundreds of children may never have been reunited with their parents.

## VI. The Media Mattered

Soboroff's report from Casa Padre went viral — tens of millions of impressions. Trump himself acknowledged it: "I didn't like the sight or the feeling of families being separated." By his own admission, the images on television contributed to ending the policy. "Without them, this story would not have received the attention it did."

## VII. The Full Truth Has Never Been Told

"Scarce few of their stories have been told. Most will never be." At least 5,556 children were separated. The actual number is almost certainly higher. The government's own watchdog (GAO) noted "it is unclear the extent to which Border Patrol has accurate records." The families who were quickly reunited are the lucky ones. There are children who were permanently orphaned.
