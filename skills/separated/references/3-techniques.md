# Techniques — Separated

## How to Understand the Family Separation Crisis (Based on Jacob Soboroff)

### Technique 1: The Timestamp Method
Soboroff tracks the policy through key dates — March 2017 (first public discussion), July 2017 (El Paso pilot), April 2018 (zero tolerance memo), June 2018 (executive order ending it). The technique: follow the policy timeline, not the media narrative. The government was separating families for over a year before the public noticed.

### Technique 2: The Numbers Trap
"I don't have those numbers" was the standard response. The technique: when an official says they can't provide data, that is itself the story. "We don't have those numbers" means the government has no system for tracking what it's doing.

### Technique 3: The Human Lens
Soboroff's approach: tell one family's story — Juan and José — to make 5,556 abstractions real. "They were forced apart from one another, unable to even say good-bye." The technique: start with one name, one face, one timeline. The statistics become real when attached to a child sleeping on a concrete floor.

### Technique 4: The Walgreens Notebook
Soboroff bought a cheap notebook before entering Casa Padre — no cameras allowed. Simple, direct, human observation. "kids everywhere / oreos / applesauce / smile at them — they 'feel like animals in a cage being looked at.'" The technique: when you can't bring a camera, bring a notebook. Write what you see. Trust the details.

### Technique 5: The Live Shot Test
"People in here are locked up in cages, essentially what look like animal kennels." Soboroff's description was plain, direct, and irrefutable. He didn't editorialize. He described what he saw. The technique: let the facts speak. "I don't know any other way to describe it" is sometimes the most powerful statement a journalist can make.

### Technique 6: The "Shocks the Conscience" Standard
Judge Sabraw's legal finding: "The Government's action shocks the conscience." Soboroff uses this as a moral benchmark. The technique: ask not just "is this legal?" but "does this shock the conscience?" The two are not always the same.

### Technique 7: Follow the Court
When political accountability fails, legal accountability remains. The ACLU's Ms. L v. ICE case was filed in February 2018 — before the public knew the scale of separations. The technique: watch the courts. That's where the truth often comes out first.
