# Core Framework — Separated

## Author's Note: The Little Blue Notebook

Jacob Soboroff, MSNBC correspondent, walked into Casa Padre — a former Walmart converted into a "shelter" holding 1,500 migrant boys — on June 13, 2018. He carried a little blue notebook bought at Walgreens. His first live report from outside the facility went viral. "This place is called a shelter, but effectively these kids are incarcerated." His reporting, by President Trump's own admission, contributed to ending systematic family separations.

## Prologue: Juan and José

Juan fled Guatemala after narco-traffickers threatened to kill his 12-year-old son, José. They traveled 2,000 miles north. At the Arizona border, they believed they'd reached safety. Within days, they were separated. José was taken. Juan was told nothing — no next steps, no timeline. They were separated for nearly five months. "The United States government treated us like animals, like dogs."

## Part 1: The Policy's Creation

### Chapter 1: Stephen Miller at the GOP Convention (April 2016)
Soboroff first encountered Stephen Miller at the Colorado Republican convention. "What a psycho," he thought. Miller screamed about "five-time deported illegal immigrants" and Kate Steinle. Trump's acceptance speech promised a border wall and the end of "catch-and-release."

### Chapter 2: "I Don't Have Those Numbers"
**The pilot program (El Paso, July 2017):** CBP's El Paso sector quietly began separating families to test whether it deterred crossings. The number was small but significant — a preview of what would come.

**"Zero tolerance" (April-May 2018):** Attorney General Jeff Sessions announced that every illegal border crossing would be prosecuted. Since parents could not take children into federal prison with them, the policy necessarily meant family separation. "If you cross the border without authorization, we will prosecute you."

### Chapter 3: The Implementation
As zero tolerance went into effect, the number of separated children exploded. The government had no plan for tracking families, no system for reunification. "We don't have those numbers" became a standard response from officials.

## Part 2: The Facilities

### Chapter 6: Casa Padre — The Walmart
A 250,000-square-foot former Walmart converted into a shelter for 1,500 boys. Soboroff's tour: "I brought the little blue notebook… Inside the five-by-ten-foot storage unit, surrounded by camping equipment and a baby-changing table, I sat on a stool and flipped open its tiny cover." His notes:
- "kids everywhere"
- "oreos / applesauce"
- "smile at them — they 'feel like animals in a cage being looked at'"

### Chapter 7: "They're Cages"
**Ursula Border Patrol Station (McAllen, TX):** Families were locked in "pods" — Soboroff's description: "People in here are locked up in cages, essentially what look like animal kennels. I don't know any other way to describe it." A Border Patrol agent confirmed: "Child could get moved even if parent back in a day."

**Sarah Fabian's testimony:** The government lawyer famously argued that migrant children didn't need blankets, toothbrushes, or soap. Soboroff tracked her numbers: "Fabian! Latest #'s / Executable 1,000!"

### Chapter 8: "No Way to Link"
The Trump administration had no centralized system to track separated families. Children were moved between facilities. Parents were deported without their children. The ACLU's lawsuit, Ms. L v. ICE, revealed that the government couldn't identify which children belonged to which parents.

## Part 3: The Legal Battle

### Chapter 9: "Shocks the Conscience"
Judge Dana Sabraw (San Diego) ordered the government to stop separating families and reunite them within 30 days. "The Government's action shocks the conscience." The case is still ongoing.

### Chapter 11: "It Hurts in My Heart"
Soboroff interviewed parents searching for their children across multiple states. One mother: "It hurts in my heart every day." Children as young as 18 months were placed in shelters hundreds or thousands of miles from their parents.

### Chapter 12: "We Know That He Is a Good Person"
Juan and José's reunification — nearly five months after separation. José had been sleeping on a concrete floor in a Border Patrol cell. Juan: "We know that he is a good person."

## Epilogue: "The Greatest Human-Rights Catastrophe of My Lifetime"

Total separated: at least 5,556 kids. The actual number is unknown — "the government's data was incomplete." The Trump administration never fully accounted for all the children. Hundreds may never have been reunited. As of 2020, the government was still fighting in court to avoid responsibility.
