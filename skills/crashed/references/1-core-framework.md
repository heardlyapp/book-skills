# Core Framework: The Interlocking Matrix and the Transatlantic Financial Crisis

> Source: *Crashed: How a Decade of Financial Crises Changed the World* by Adam Tooze, Introduction through Part II (Chapters 1-13)

## The Interlocking Matrix

Tooze's central analytical framework replaces the traditional "island model" of international economics — where national economies trade with one another and imbalances are measured in trade deficits and government budgets — with an "interlocking matrix" of corporate balance sheets. As he writes, borrowing from Hyun Song Shin, chief economist at the Bank for International Settlements: "We need to analyze the global economy not in terms of an 'island model' of international economic interaction — national economy to national economy — but through the 'interlocking matrix' of corporate balance sheets — bank to bank."

This shift is crucial because government deficits and current account imbalances are poor predictors of the force and speed with which modern financial crises strike. A modern global bank run moves far more money far more abruptly than any trade imbalance ever could.

## The Dollar Funding System

The second pillar of Tooze's framework is the dollar-based global financial system. The dollar is not just America's currency — it is the world's. European banks, Asian banks, and emerging market banks all borrowed in dollars to fund their balance sheets. When the crisis hit, they faced an acute dollar-funding shortage, not a glut. The Fed's response — establishing itself as liquidity provider of last resort to the global banking system through dollar swap lines with foreign central banks — was "perhaps the decisive innovation of the crisis."

Key elements:
- The Fed licensed a hand-picked group of core central banks (ECB, Bank of England, Bank of Japan, etc.) to issue dollar credits on demand
- Through these swap lines, trillions of dollars were pumped into the European banking system
- This contradicted the narrative of 2008 as an "all-American crisis" — half the liquidity support went to non-US banks, overwhelmingly in Europe

## The Wrong Crisis

Before 2008, the US policy elite feared a very different disaster. Figures like Robert Rubin, Larry Summers, and Peter Orszag (the Hamilton Project) warned of a dollar crisis triggered by China selling its US Treasury holdings. Niall Ferguson and Moritz Schularick coined "Chimerica" to describe the US-China economic complex; Summers warned of a "balance of financial terror."

But the actual crisis came from a mundane source: subprime mortgages in marginal American neighborhoods. The "wrong crisis" thesis is central: the disaster everyone prepared for didn't happen; the real crisis came from an overlooked, unglamorous corner of the financial system.

## The Fourfold Transformation of Housing Finance

Tooze traces how American housing finance became a destabilizing global force through four changes:

| Transformation | Description |
|---|---|
| Securitization | Mortgages were packaged into MBS and CMOs, tranched into risk tiers |
| Integration | Investment banks, commercial banks, and mortgage lenders vertically integrated the entire origination-to-distribution chain |
| New funding sources | Institutional cash pools (money market mutual funds, corporate treasuries) replaced bank deposits as the funding base |
| Internationalization | European banks became massive buyers of US MBS, creating transatlantic financial entanglement |

## The Eurozone as Aftershock

The eurozone crisis (2010-2012) was not a separate event about sovereign profligacy. Tooze argues it was "a massive aftershock of the earthquake in the North Atlantic financial system of 2008, working its way out with a time lag through the labyrinthine political framework of the EU." The common denominator across the eurozone crisis was not excessive public debt but "the dangerous fragility of an overleveraged financial system, excessively reliant on short-term market-based funding."

> **Case: Lehman Brothers' Failure** (Chapter 6): When Lehman was allowed to fail on September 15, 2008, the Fed and Treasury expected contained fallout. Instead, global money markets seized up completely. Tooze documents how this miscalculation revealed the Fed's fundamental misunderstanding of how deeply European banks were entangled in the US financial system. Lehman's collapse triggered a run on the entire transatlantic banking system.
> **Key takeaway**: The modern global bank run moves money faster and more destructively than any sovereign debt crisis.

> **Case: Greece 2010 — Extend and Pretend** (Chapter 14): When Greek debt became the flashpoint, European leaders constructed a narrative of sovereign profligacy. But as Tooze writes, senior EU officials now admit this "had no basis in economics." Greece was insolvent, but excessive public debt was not the common denominator of the wider eurozone crisis. By misdiagnosing a banking crisis as a fiscal crisis, Europe turned a manageable problem into a depression-level disaster.
> **Key takeaway**: Misdiagnosing a crisis doesn't just get the history wrong — it causes real human suffering on a massive scale.

## Key Concepts at a Glance

| Concept | Definition |
|---|---|
| Interlocking Matrix | The global financial system as a web of bank-to-bank balance sheets |
| Wrong Crisis | The disaster everyone prepared for (Sino-American dollar crisis) vs. what actually happened (subprime → transatlantic meltdown) |
| Originate-to-Distribute | Mortgage lending model where originators sell loans immediately, removing incentive to monitor quality |
| Dollar Swap Lines | Fed's mechanism to provide dollar liquidity to foreign central banks, making it global lender of last resort |
| Balance of Financial Terror | US-China economic interdependence where each side holds the other hostage through mutual financial exposure |
| Chimerica | Term by Ferguson & Schularick for the symbiotic US-China economic relationship |
| Sudden Stop | Abrupt reversal of cross-border capital flows that devastates banking systems |
| Doom Loop | Feedback cycle between sovereign debt and banking crisis — banks hold sovereign bonds, sovereigns bail out banks |
| Sovereign Debt Crisis | The eurozone's preferred (and misleading) framing — a political narrative, not an economic diagnosis |
| Systemically Important Financial Institutions (SIFI) | The ~20-30 global banks that dominate private credit creation |
