# Key Principles: How the Global Financial System Actually Works

> Source: *Crashed: How a Decade of Financial Crises Changed the World* by Adam Tooze, Parts I-IV

## 1. The Financial System Is Irreducibly Political

Tooze's foundational claim: "The foundations of the modern monetary system are irreducibly political." Money and credit are not natural market phenomena — they are constituted by political power, social convention, and law. At the apex of the monetary pyramid is fiat money, called into existence and sanctioned by states, with no "backing" other than its status as legal tender. This became literally true in 1971-1973 with the collapse of Bretton Woods. The response to the 2008 crisis confirmed this: the Fed didn't act as a rule-bound technocratic body but as an emergency executive authority, more akin to military operations or emergency medicine than law-bound governance.

## 2. Think in Balance Sheets, Not Trade Flows

The standard macroeconomic approach (inherited from Keynes) organizes the world economy around nation-states, national productive systems, and trade imbalances. But in an age of deep globalization, the real action is on corporate balance sheets. Tooze follows Hyun Song Shin in arguing for "macrofinance" — analyzing the global economy through the interlocking matrix of bank-to-bank balance sheets. A sudden stop in interbank credit moves far more money far more quickly than any shift in trade flows.

| Traditional View | Macrofinancial View |
|---|---|
| National economies trade with each other | Balance sheets interlock across borders |
| Trade deficits cause crises | Wholesale funding runs cause crises |
| Government debt is the risk | Private bank leverage is the risk |
| IMF supervises countries | Fed supports banks globally |

## 3. Dollar Supremacy Is an Active Achievement

The dollar's dominance is not automatic. It is maintained through active policy choices — most crucially, the Fed's willingness to act as global lender of last resort. When the crisis hit, "the United States engaged in a truly spectacular innovation. It established itself as liquidity provider of last resort to the global banking system." Through swap lines, the Fed provided dollars "to all comers in New York, whether banks were American or not." This revealed a deep structural truth: the global financial system depends on the Fed's dollar provision, but this dependency is barely acknowledged in public discourse.

## 4. The Transatlantic Entanglement

The crisis was not "American" vs. "European" — it was transatlantic. European banks were deeply entangled in the US mortgage market. When the crisis hit, the dollar-funding shortfall for European banks was measured in trillions. The idea of 2008 as an "all-American crisis" served everyone's political convenience: Europeans could shift blame across the Atlantic, Americans could frame it as a domestic story. But this narrative "obscures the reality of profound interconnection."

## 5. The Banking Oligarchy

The financial system is not made up of tiny competing firms. Tooze reveals: "The overwhelming majority of private credit creation is done by a tight-knit corporate oligarchy. At a global level twenty to thirty banks matter. Allowing for nationally significant banks, the number worldwide is perhaps a hundred big financial firms." This small group — the "systemically important financial institutions" (SIFI) — are both the key actors in the crisis and the recipients of the largest bailout in history. The "stark truth" is that the Fed's historic policy of global liquidity support funneled trillions to this coterie of banks, their shareholders, and their "outrageously remunerated senior staff."

> **Case: The Fed's Swap Lines to European Banks** (Chapter 8): In fall 2008, the Fed extended swap lines to the ECB, Bank of England, Swiss National Bank, and others. These weren't abstract — they specifically backed European banks that were starving for dollars. Tooze itemizes: "at least half the liquidity support [the Fed] provided went to banks not headquartered in the United States, but located overwhelmingly in Europe." This was not a bailout anyone wanted to discuss publicly.
> **Key takeaway**: The rescue of the global banking system was essentially a secret — barely discussed even in Fed chair Bernanke's memoirs.

> **Case: Europe's Corporate Decline** (Introduction): Tooze documents how the botched handling of the eurozone crisis led to the decline of European corporate power. "Since 2008, it is not just the rise of Asia that is shifting the global corporate hierarchy. It is the decline of Europe." Deutsche Bank, the last European investment bank with global standing, was reduced to being at the mercy of US authorities. This defeat of European capital was "historic."
> **Key takeaway**: The real cost of the mishandled crisis was the systematic weakening of European business, which ordinary citizens depend on for jobs and wages.

## 6. Crises Do Not End — They Mutate

A core principle throughout the book: financial crises don't resolve; they transform. The 2008 liquidity crisis became a 2009 recession, which became a 2010-2012 sovereign debt crisis, which became a 2013-2017 political crisis of the post-Cold War order. Trump's election, Brexit, the rise of populism across Europe — these are not separate phenomena. They are the same crisis in a new form. As Tooze writes, "What we face is not repetition but mutation and metastasis."

| Phase | Period | Form |
|---|---|---|
| Financial | 2007-2009 | Banking panic, credit freeze, bailouts |
| Economic | 2009-2012 | Recession, stimulus, austerity |
| Sovereign | 2010-2012 | Eurozone debt crisis, Greek implosion |
| Political | 2013-2017 | Populism, Trump, Brexit, EU fragmentation |
| Geopolitical | 2014-2017 | Ukraine, Russia confrontation, US-China rivalry |

## 7. The Neoliberal Proviso

The pre-crisis era (the "great moderation") was dominated by the idea of market discipline, independent central banks, and the rollback of state intervention. The crisis revealed this regime operated under a proviso: "In the event of a major financial crisis that threatened 'systemic' interests, it turned out that we live in an age not of limited but of big government." The question is not whether the state intervenes, but whom it saves — the answer in 2008 was "Wall Street first, Main Street later."
