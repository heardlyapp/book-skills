# Techniques: Crisis-Fighting Tools and the Machinery of Global Finance

> Source: *Crashed: How a Decade of Financial Crises Changed the World* by Adam Tooze, Parts II-IV

## 1. Dollar Swap Lines — The Fed's Most Powerful Weapon

The most important crisis-fighting tool Tooze identifies was nearly invisible to the public. The Federal Reserve's dollar swap lines were a network of bilateral agreements between the Fed and foreign central banks that allowed those banks to borrow dollars from the Fed and lend them to commercial banks in their jurisdictions.

**How it worked:**
1. A European bank needs dollars to pay off maturing debt or fund its dollar-denominated assets
2. It cannot borrow dollars in the frozen interbank market
3. The ECB borrows dollars from the Fed (via the swap line) and lends them to the bank
4. The bank survives the run

**Scale**: In October 2008, the Fed extended swap lines totaling hundreds of billions — eventually trillions — to the ECB, Bank of England, Swiss National Bank, Bank of Japan, and others. The ECB alone borrowed over $500 billion at the peak.

**Why it matters**: This was the decisive innovation. Without it, every major European bank would have faced dollar-funding insolvency, taking down the entire global banking system. But as Tooze notes, "in public discourse these actions have remained far below the radar."

## 2. Macroprudential Supervision — Identifying the Systemically Important

After the crisis, regulators developed techniques for identifying and monitoring "systemically important financial institutions" (SIFI). This is known as macroprudential supervision — one of "the major governmental innovations of the crisis and its aftermath."

**The framework:**
- Identify banks whose failure would threaten the entire financial system (not just their own depositors)
- Subject them to higher capital requirements, stress tests, and closer supervision
- Require "living wills" — plans for orderly resolution if they fail

**Tooze's assessment**: The SIFI framework was necessary but insufficient. It formalized the reality that 20-30 global banks dominate the system without addressing the fundamentally oligarchic structure of finance.

## 3. The Bailout Toolbox — Three Drills

Tooze documents three distinct bailout approaches used during the crisis:

| Method | Used by | How it worked |
|---|---|---|
| Direct injection | US TARP (Troubled Asset Relief Program) | Treasury bought equity in banks to recapitalize them |
| Liability guarantee | US FDIC, Ireland, UK | Government guaranteed bank debt to prevent runs |
| Asset purchase / "bad bank" | US (TALF), Switzerland (UBS) | Central bank bought toxic assets to remove them from bank balance sheets |

**The US approach**: Massive, fast, and relatively effective. "At least America's crisis management worked" — the US economy has grown continuously since 2009.

**The European approach**: Delayed, fragmented, and costly. "The eurozone, through willful policy choices, drove tens of millions of its citizens into the depths of a 1930s-style depression."

## 4. Quantitative Easing — The Sequel

QE was the second-wave tool after direct liquidity provision. The Fed, ECB, and Bank of Japan bought government bonds (and sometimes other assets) to inject money into the economy when short-term interest rates were already at zero.

**Tooze's critique**: QE was politically combustible. It appeared to benefit Wall Street more than Main Street. The Fed's asset purchases drove up stock prices while ordinary Americans lost their homes. This perception fueled the populist backlash that followed.

## 5. The ECB's "Whatever It Takes" — The Eurozone Stabilizer

In July 2012, ECB President Mario Draghi announced the bank would do "whatever it takes" to preserve the euro, followed by the Outright Monetary Transactions (OMT) program. Tooze calls this the "belated 'Americanization' of the ECB" — after years of insisting on rules-based discipline, the ECB finally embraced the Fed's approach of unconditional market intervention.

**Timeline of eurozone crisis escalation:**
- May 2010: First Greek bailout ($140 billion) + creation of European Financial Stability Facility
- Fall 2011: Crisis spreads to Italy and Spain; bond yields spike to unsustainable levels
- Summer 2012: Eurozone on brink of breakup; Draghi's "whatever it takes" speech stabilizes markets
- Result: The acute phase ended, but the damage to European economies and the EU's political legitimacy was permanent

> **Case: The Irish Bank Guarantee** (Chapter 7): On September 29, 2008, Ireland's government guaranteed the liabilities of its six biggest banks — a total of €440 billion, roughly twice Ireland's GDP. This was the first comprehensive bank debt guarantee of the crisis. It temporarily stabilized Irish banks but at enormous long-term cost: when the banks' losses materialized, the guarantee became a crushing sovereign debt burden, forcing Ireland into a bailout by 2010.
> **Key takeaway**: A liability guarantee can stop a bank run instantly, but if the underlying assets are truly toxic, the debt simply moves from private to public balance sheets.

> **Case: TARP — The Reluctant Recapitalization** (Chapter 7): Treasury Secretary Hank Paulson initially proposed TARP to buy toxic mortgage assets from banks. But when it became clear this wouldn't work, the US government forced the nine largest banks to accept equity injections — effectively a partial nationalization. Paulson famously called the bank CEOs to a meeting and told them they would participate, "voluntarily." Banks that refused would be called out publicly.
> **Key takeaway**: Crisis management requires coercion, not persuasion. The US government's willingness to force banks to accept help was crucial to stabilizing the system — a contrast with Europe's more consensual approach.

## 6. Stress Tests — The Confidence Restoration Tool

The US conducted "stress tests" in 2009 to evaluate whether major banks had enough capital to survive a severe recession. The results were published, restoring confidence by showing that even in a worst-case scenario, most banks would survive.

**European failure on stress tests**: When Europe finally conducted stress tests in 2010-2011, they were seen as too lenient and not credible. The contrast illustrates Tooze's point about European crisis management: "a shambles of conflicting visions."
