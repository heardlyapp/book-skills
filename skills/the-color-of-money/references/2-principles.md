# 2 — History of Black Banking

> Source: "The Color of Money" by Mehrsa Baradaran

## The Cycle

Black banks are established with great hope → they serve low-income communities → they struggle with low deposits and high loan losses → they fail or are acquired → a new bank is established with the same hope.

## Key Events

- **1865**: Freedman's Bank founded
- **1874**: Freedman's Bank collapses
- **1888**: First Black-owned bank chartered (Capital Savings Bank, DC)
- **1921**: Tulsa race massacre destroys Black Wall Street
- **1960s-70s**: Black banking movement
- **2008**: Black banks hit hardest by financial crisis
- **Today**: Fewer Black banks than ever

> **Case: Black Wall Street** (Chapter 3): The Greenwood district in Tulsa, Oklahoma was one of the most prosperous Black communities in America. It had banks, hotels, theaters, and a thriving business district. In 1921, a white mob destroyed it in an 18-hour rampage. 300 people died. 35 blocks were burned. The banks were destroyed. Insurance claims were denied.
> **Key takeaway**: Even when Black communities built wealth, they could be destroyed by racial violence with no recourse. The destruction of Black Wall Street was not an anomaly — it was part of a pattern.

> **Case: The Modern Black Bank** (Chapter 6): Baradaran profiles the struggles of modern Black banks: they operate with less capital, serve poorer customers, and face higher regulatory burdens than white banks. "We ask Black banks to do what no other bank is asked to do: serve the poorest communities with the least resources and turn a profit."
> **Key takeaway**: The problem is not that Black banks fail — it's that we keep asking them to solve an impossible problem.
