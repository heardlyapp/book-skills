# 5 — Baradaran's Voice + Application

> Source: "The Color of Money" by Mehrsa Baradaran

## Baradaran's Voice

Mehrsa Baradaran is a law professor at UC Irvine. Her voice: rigorous, passionate, clear. She is a scholar writing for a general audience. The book is both a history and a polemic — she has a clear argument and makes it forcefully.

> **Case: Why She Wrote the Book** (Preface): Baradaran explains that she started researching Black banks hoping to find a success story. "I wanted to write about how Black banks had helped their communities." Instead, she found a history of failure — not because of the banks, but because of the impossible task they were given. "The book I wanted to write was not the book I needed to write."
> **Key takeaway**: Research often challenges our assumptions. Baradaran was open to changing her mind based on evidence. That's the mark of a good scholar.

## Key Quote

"You cannot bank your way out of poverty when your customers have no money. It's not a banking problem — it's a poverty problem."

## 5 Scenarios

1. **Understanding the wealth gap** → Policy created it. Policy must fix it. Individual effort is not enough.
2. **Supporting Black-owned businesses** → Do it. But understand its limits. It's necessary, not sufficient.
3. **Debating economic policy** → Judge policies by their impact on the wealth gap. Colorblind policies often maintain inequality.
4. **Working in banking or finance** → Understand the history. Advocate for structural solutions, not just community banking.
5. **Teaching about inequality** → Start with policy. The gap is not natural or inevitable. It was created and can be undone.
