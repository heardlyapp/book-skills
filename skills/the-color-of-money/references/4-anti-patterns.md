# 4 — Anti-Patterns

> Source: "The Color of Money" by Mehrsa Baradaran

## Anti-Pattern 1: The Self-Help Myth

**The mistake:** Believing that individual effort, entrepreneurship, and "buy Black" can close a structural gap created by centuries of policy.

## Anti-Pattern 2: Bank-Dependent Solutions

**The mistake:** Expecting Black banks to solve the wealth gap when they operate with less capital and serve the poorest communities.

## Anti-Pattern 3: Colorblind Economics

**The mistake:** Assuming that if we just ignore race, the market will naturally produce equality. It won't. The market reproduces existing inequality.

> **Case: Booker T. Washington's Dream** (Chapter 2): Washington believed Black economic progress through entrepreneurship would lead to equality. "Cast down your bucket where you are." Baradaran shows this was inadequate: "Washington's vision assumed that hard work and thrift would be rewarded equally — but they were not. Black businesses were still subject to discrimination and violence."
> **Key takeaway**: Washington's strategy was sincere but insufficient. Economic progress without political and civil rights is fragile. It can be destroyed by a mob, a discriminatory policy, or a financial panic.

> **Case: The Post-2008 Failure** (Chapter 8): After the 2008 financial crisis, Black banks were hit hardest. "The same banks that had been promoted as the solution to the wealth gap were the most likely to fail." The crisis showed that Black banks could not survive a major economic downturn any better than their customers could.
> **Key takeaway**: Black banks are not magic. They are subject to the same economic forces as any bank — and because their customers have less wealth, they are more vulnerable. Systemic problems require systemic solutions.
