# 3 — Government Policy and Inequality

> Source: "The Color of Money" by Mehrsa Baradaran

## How Policy Created the Gap

**Redlining**: The FHA refused to insure mortgages in Black neighborhoods from the 1930s-1960s. This prevented Black families from buying homes — the primary way Americans build wealth.

**The New Deal**: Social Security, unemployment insurance, and labor protections excluded agricultural and domestic workers — most Black workers at the time.

**The GI Bill**: Provided education and housing benefits to veterans. But Black veterans were often denied by local administrators.

> **Case: The FHA Maps** (Chapter 4): The Federal Housing Administration created color-coded maps of American cities. Green areas were "best" for investment. Red areas were "hazardous" — and these were Black neighborhoods. "The maps were drawn with the explicit purpose of keeping Black families out of white neighborhoods and preventing Black families from getting mortgages."
> **Key takeaway**: The government didn't just allow discrimination — it designed it. The FHA maps were a blueprint for segregation and wealth inequality. The effects persist today.

> **Case: The GI Bill Exclusion** (Chapter 5): The GI Bill was supposed to help all veterans. But Black veterans were systematically denied its benefits by local administrators. "Less than 10% of Black veterans in Mississippi received GI Bill benefits. They were told loans were 'unavailable' or 'processing was delayed.'"
> **Key takeaway**: Even when federal policy was technically color-blind, local implementation was discriminatory. The gap between policy and practice matters.
