# Reference: Tesla's Vision for the Future

## Overview
Tesla's writings reveal a man who saw the 20th (and 21st) century with startling clarity. He predicted wireless communication, robotics, radar, the internet, and global information systems. He also proposed ideas that remain speculative — free energy, particle beam weapons, and interplanetary communication.

## Case: Teleautomation and the Robot Boat
In 1898, Tesla demonstrated a radio-controlled boat at Madison Square Garden. The boat was 4 feet long, powered by electric batteries, and controlled by a radio transmitter. Tesla called it "teleautomation." The crowd was amazed; the US Navy expressed interest but never adopted it. Tesla believed that teleautomation was the beginning of robotics, predicting that autonomous machines would replace human labor in dangerous and repetitive tasks. His patent for "Method of and Apparatus for Controlling Mechanism of Moving Vessels or Vehicles" (No. 613,809) laid the groundwork for drones, remote surgery, and autonomous vehicles.

## Case: The World System Vision
Tesla's most ambitious prediction was the "World System" — a global network of wireless transmission stations that would provide ubiquitous communication, news distribution, navigation, and power. He described a device that would "enable the bearer to hear anywhere as well as in his own home," "receive news from any part of the world," and "determine his position with reference to the meridians." This was a description of the smartphone, the internet, and GPS — in 1900. Tesla lacked the technology to build it, but he saw the future with uncanny precision.

## Case: Predictions About Robotics and AI
In his 1900 article "The Problem of Increasing Human Energy," Tesla predicted that robots would eventually perform most manual labor, that machines would be able to think and make complex decisions, and that humanity would be liberated from physical drudgery. "The future," he wrote, "will witness the invention and widespread use of automatic machines capable of accomplishing almost any kind of work." He also warned that humanity must use these machines wisely, because "the development of intelligence is the most important problem confronting humanity."
