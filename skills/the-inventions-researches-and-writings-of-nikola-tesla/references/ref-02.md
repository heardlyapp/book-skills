# Reference: AC Power & Electrical Inventions

## Overview
Tesla's AC polyphase system is arguably the most important engineering innovation of the late 19th century. It made long-distance power transmission feasible, enabling the electrification of the modern world.

## Case: The Induction Motor
The induction motor was Tesla's masterstroke. Unlike DC motors that required commutators and brushes (which sparked, wore out, and limited speed), the induction motor used rotating magnetic fields generated by AC. No physical connection between the stationary and rotating parts was needed. The motor was simpler, cheaper, more efficient, and required almost no maintenance. It was the first time a motor could operate without commutators — a breakthrough that Edison's team had declared impossible.

## Case: The Niagara Falls Project
The 1895 Niagara Falls AC hydroelectric plant was the world's first large-scale AC power station. Using Tesla's two-phase system and generators built by Westinghouse, it transmitted 15,000 horsepower 22 miles to Buffalo, New York. This proved beyond doubt that AC could deliver power economically over long distances. Within a decade, the AC system became the global standard for electrical power distribution. Niagara was not just a power plant — it was the demonstration that changed the world.

## Case: The Egg of Columbus
At the 1893 Chicago World's Fair, Tesla performed a demonstration that stunned visitors: a copper egg, standing on end, spinning rapidly on a rotating magnetic field — visible proof of the polyphase principle. This demonstration was part of the Westinghouse bid for the Niagara Falls contract. Tesla understood that a compelling visual demonstration was worth more than a thousand patent filings. The egg became the symbol of AC's victory over DC.
