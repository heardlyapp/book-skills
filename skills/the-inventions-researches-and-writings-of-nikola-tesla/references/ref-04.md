# Reference: X-rays, Resonance & Other Discoveries

## Overview
Beyond AC and radio, Tesla made pioneering contributions to multiple fields that are often forgotten. His work on X-rays, mechanical resonance, and fluid dynamics was years ahead of its time.

## Case: Tesla's X-ray Experiments
In 1894, Tesla began experimenting with high-voltage vacuum tubes. He produced what he called "shadowgraphs" — images showing the bones of his hand and the inside of his body. This was a year before Röntgen's official discovery of X-rays in 1895. Tesla also warned about the dangers of X-ray exposure, noting skin burns and eye damage from prolonged exposure. He developed safety protocols that were not adopted by other researchers, many of whom suffered severe radiation injuries. Tesla's work on X-rays was interrupted when his New York laboratory burned down in 1895, destroying his notes and apparatus.

## Case: The Resonance Experiments
Tesla was fascinated by mechanical resonance. In his New York laboratory, he built a small oscillating device that, when attached to a building's iron frame, caused the surrounding buildings to shake. According to his account, he had to stop the experiment with a sledgehammer when the police arrived, fearing an earthquake. The "Tesla earthquake machine" story has been exaggerated — it was a small oscillator, not a seismic weapon — but it demonstrates his understanding that even small forces, applied at the right frequency, could produce large effects.

## Case: The Tesla Turbine
In 1913, Tesla patented a bladeless turbine that used the boundary layer effect (fluid friction against a set of smooth disks) to drive a rotor. The design had no blades, no complex machining, and theoretically extremely high efficiency (Tesla claimed 95%+). The turbine was tested successfully at small scale but never commercialized, primarily because materials capable of withstanding the extreme stresses of large-scale operation did not exist at the time. Modern research has revived interest in Tesla turbines for niche applications where simplicity and reliability matter more than peak efficiency.
