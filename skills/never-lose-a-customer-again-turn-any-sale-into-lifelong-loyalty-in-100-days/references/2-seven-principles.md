# Seven Principles in Depth

## Overview

The 8-phase framework is built on seven foundational principles about human psychology, business structure, and customer experience design. Each principle is supported by real case studies from companies of all sizes and industries.

## Principle 1: Shift to H2H (Human-to-Human) Thinking

The classic B2B vs B2C distinction is a false dichotomy. All business is human-to-human. When you treat customers as humans with emotions, fears, and desires — not as "accounts" or "leads" — you design experiences that resonate.

**Why it works:** Humans make decisions emotionally and justify them rationally. The H2H approach meets customers where they actually are — in their emotional journey — rather than where the company's org chart says they should be.

**Case — Dr. McCann's dental practice (Aurora Modern Dentistry):** When Joey Coleman broke his tooth, he expected the usual terrible dental experience. Instead, the receptionist adjusted the schedule to see him immediately, offered online intake forms, remembered his name from his photo, and Dr. McCann gave him her personal cellphone number. The follow-up call two hours after the procedure checked on his pain level. Every interaction treated Colemen as a human, not a patient number. The result: a fan for life who referred five new patients.

**Case — Insurance company counter-example:** Coleman describes an insurance company where the salesperson promised personalized service and the account manager who took over after the sale had never even seen the file. The customer's expressed needs were ignored. Coleman concludes the company "became a case study of what not to do." The failure was treating the customer as a transaction, not a human.

## Principle 2: The First 100 Days Are Everything

The data is stark across industries. Banks lose 32 percent of new customers in the first year (50 percent of those in the first 100 days). Cellphone companies lose 21 percent in the first 100 days despite heavy penalties. SaaS companies lose 20 percent. Restaurants like Chuck E. Cheese lose 46 percent of new customers after a single visit. Auto repair shops lose 60-70 percent.

**Why it works:** The first 100 days are when the brain makes its "keep or cut" decision about a new relationship. If the experience is positive and the customer achieves success, the commitment deepens. If the customer feels neglected, doubt wins.

**Case — Banking customer defection:** A Corporate Executive Board report revealed that banks spend approximately $300 to acquire a new customer. The customer pays roughly $12 per month in maintenance fees. It takes 25 months to earn back the acquisition cost. Yet 32 percent leave before the 12-month mark, and 20 percent of defectors leave before their first transaction. The bank loses money on nearly a third of acquired customers.

**Case — 4Knines sales collapse:** When 4Knines turned off their auto-responder emails (which had been critical to the Advocate phase), monthly sales dropped from $260,000 to $74,000 — a 73 percent decline — in under a year. Turning the emails back on restored sales to $120,000 in two months and $175,000 in six months. The 100-day retention system was worth millions.

## Principle 3: Design for the Emotional Journey

Most businesses design customer experiences around their own operational needs, not the customer's emotional state. The result: friction, frustration, and churn.

**Why it works:** The customer's emotional journey follows a predictable arc: excitement (pre-purchase), doubt (immediately post-purchase), relief (first success), confidence (acclimation), pride (adoption), enthusiasm (advocacy). Designing for each emotional state creates a seamless experience.

**Case — Book in a Box emotional design:** After Coleman signed up to write his book, he experienced severe buyer's remorse. He stalled, rescheduled calls, and avoided the team. The CEO Tucker Max called personally, let Coleman express his doubts, normalized the feelings ("every author goes through this"), and persuaded him to continue. The company then built this emotional check-in into their standard process: a care package arrives a week after signing, the first call includes a direct conversation about emotions, and the publisher checks in regularly.

**Case — CD Baby's emotional insight:** Derek Sivers recognized that the standard order confirmation email actually increased customer anxiety — "Please let us know if it doesn't arrive" hinted at a pattern of non-delivery. He replaced it with a joyful, humorous narrative that completely reversed the emotional impact. The customer finished reading feeling happy and confident, not worried.

## Principle 4: Create Physical Artifacts

In an increasingly digital world, physical communication has more impact than ever. The empty mailbox with no competition cuts through the noise.

**Why it works:** Physical objects engage more senses. A handwritten note signals care. An unexpected package creates surprise. A birth certificate memorializes a moment. Digital communications are expected; physical ones are memorable.

**Case — Build-A-Bear birth certificate:** After a child assembles the stuffed animal, the store issues a personalized birth certificate showing the bear's name and "birth date." The clerk announces the adoption to the entire store. The paper certificate is a physical artifact the child keeps for years, reminding them of the experience.

**Case — Corporate Executive Board:** The think tank sent a physical FedEx package to prospects during evaluation. In a digital world, the unexpected arrival of a package signaled that CEB operated differently. The physicality of the experience differentiated them from every competitor.

## Principle 5: Remove Friction at Every Touchpoint

Friction is any point where the customer has to work harder than they expected. Every friction point increases the chance of defection.

**Why it works:** The brain is a "lazy piece of meat" that conserves energy. When a process requires too much effort, the brain looks for easier alternatives. Removing friction keeps the customer moving forward.

**Case — Dr. McCann online intake:** The dental practice offered mobile-friendly intake forms that asked for information only once. No paper, no clipboard, no chewed pen. Completion took six minutes. The receptionist recognized him by name from the electronic record. The entire check-in process was frictionless.

**Case — 23andMe kit:** The DNA kit is minimal — a tube, a simple visual instruction card, and a return envelope. There's no confusion about what to do. The instructions use diagrams, not paragraphs. The friction-free design gets the customer through the first step quickly.

## Principle 6: Co-Create the Experience with the Customer

Customers who participate in creating their experience are more committed to it. The more they invest, the more they value.

**Why it works:** The IKEA effect — people value things they helped create more than things they simply received. When a customer co-creates, their sense of ownership and commitment increases.

**Case — Build-A-Bear:** The child chooses the animal, the fur, the filling density, the outfit, the sound chip, and the heart. They physically stuff the animal. By the time the birth certificate is presented, the child has invested significant time and emotional energy. The bear isn't just a toy — it's their creation.

**Case — Harley-Davidson:** The motorcycle design process involves customer input. Owners customize their bikes extensively. Harley's H.O.G. (Harley Owners Group) creates a community where members organize rides, share stories, and even get tattoos of the logo. The co-creation of community identity makes the brand inseparable from the owner's identity.

## Principle 7: Time the Ask Thoughtfully

Most businesses ask for referrals, reviews, and testimonials too early — before the customer has experienced value. This damages the relationship and produces mediocre results.

**Why it works:** Asking at the right moment — after the customer has achieved their goal — makes the ask feel natural and earned. The customer is happy to help because they've already received value.

**Case — 4Knines review timing:** The company sends a review request exactly 30 days after purchase. This is when the customer has had enough time to use the seat cover and form an opinion. Before 30 days, the ask would be premature. After 60 days, the enthusiasm fades. The precisely timed ask yields a 24 percent response rate versus 8 percent industry average.

**Case — Dropbox referral timing:** Dropbox didn't ask for referrals when customers signed up. They waited until the customer had used their free 2GB of storage and hit the limit. At that moment, the need for more storage was real and urgent. The offer of 500MB per referral aligned perfectly with the customer's needs. The result: exponential growth.
