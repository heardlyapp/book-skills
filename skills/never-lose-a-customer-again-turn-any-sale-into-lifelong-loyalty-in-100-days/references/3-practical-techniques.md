# Practical Techniques

## Overview

Beyond the 8-phase framework, Coleman provides a toolkit of specific techniques for designing remarkable customer experiences. This file catalogs the most actionable techniques with book cases.

## The Six Communication Channels

Coleman identifies six communication channels available to every business. Most businesses use only two or three. The goal is to integrate all six across the 8 phases to create a multisensory customer journey.

### 1. In-Person
Face-to-face interactions create the strongest emotional connection. They build trust, allow reading of body language, and create shared experiences.

**Case — Domestic Violence Awareness:** Coleman tells a story of receiving a random hand-addressed envelope containing a message from a nonprofit: "We lost touch. Would you still like to receive updates?" The handwritten, in-person-seeming approach was more effective than any email campaign because it felt human.

**Case — San Francisco CrossFit:** New members receive an in-person welcome after their first week. Coaches learn names and goals. This personal connection reduces the dropout rate that plagues most fitness programs.

### 2. Email
Email is the most scalable channel. The key is personalization, timing, and personality.

**Case — CD Baby confirmation email:** The famed email replaced boring transactional language with a joyful, hilarious narrative that made customers smile. The technique: infuse personality into automated emails. The email itself — not any product feature — became a marketing tool through word of mouth.

**Case — 4Knines review request:** The email begins with "Can we ask a favor?" — a friendly, disarming opener. It estimates the time required ("about 3 minutes"). It shares the brand story ("we're a small, family-owned business"). It makes the ask clear and simple. Signed by the founders and their dogs. Result: 3x the industry average response rate.

### 3. Mail (Physical)
Physical mail cuts through digital noise. The empty mailbox has far less competition than the full inbox.

**Case — Corporate Executive Board:** FedEx packages sent to prospects during the Assess phase. The unexpected physical arrival gets immediate attention and signals that this company is different.

**Case — Book in a Box care package:** A box arrives a week after a new author signs, containing a handwritten note, celebratory treats, and a bottle of champagne. The physical package creates a moment of delight and reaffirms the decision.

### 4. Phone
Phone calls create immediacy and intimacy. Text messages get responded to in 90 seconds on average versus 90 minutes for email.

**Case — Dr. McCann follow-up call:** Two hours after the dental procedure, the office called to check pain levels. This unexpected call had enormous emotional impact because it showed genuine care. The receptionist asked follow-up questions about prescriptions and shared Dr. McCann's personal cellphone number.

**Case — Tucker Max call to Coleman:** When Coleman stalled in the Affirm phase, Max called unexpectedly to check in. The direct conversation about emotional state (fear, doubt, uncertainty) normalized the experience and kept Coleman engaged.

### 5. Video
Video creates emotional connection at scale. Informal, handheld video (shot on a phone) often outperforms polished productions.

**Case — Lady Gaga fan videos:** During the "Born This Way" era, Lady Gaga's team sent personalized video messages to top fans, thanking them for their support. The informal, one-to-many video felt personal and created intense loyalty. Fans reciprocated with even more promotion.

**Case — doTERRA:** The essential oils company trains distributors to send personalized video messages to new customers — thanking them for their purchase, explaining how to use the oils, and inviting them to a community event. The videos are shot on phones, not in studios, making them feel authentic.

### 6. Present (Gift)
Gifts create reciprocity and delight. The best gifts are personalized and meaningful — not branded merchandise or coupons.

**Case — Maverick1000:** Entrepreneurial community run by James Altucher sends personalized books, handwritten notes, and custom-curated gifts to members. Each gift reflects the individual member's interests and goals, not the community's branding needs.

**Case — Total Debt Freedom:** The financial coaching company sends personalized gifts that celebrate client milestones — paying off a credit card, closing a loan, reaching a savings goal. The gifts are timed to coincide with emotional peaks in the debt-free journey.

## The Emotion Mapping Technique

Coleman's most powerful diagnostic tool: map the customer's emotional state at each phase of the journey. Identify where emotions dip (buyer's remorse, setup frustration, post-accomplishment lull) and design experiences to lift them.

**How to do it:** Create a timeline from first contact to first anniversary. At each touchpoint, rate the customer's emotional state on a scale of 1-10. Look for dips below 5 — these are defection risks. Design interventions at each dip point.

**Case — Book in a Box journey mapping:** The company mapped the author's emotional journey and discovered a deep dip in the Affirm phase (weeks 1-3 after signing). They designed three interventions: (1) a care package arriving in week 1, (2) an emotional check-in on the first call, and (3) regular follow-up calls from the publisher. The interventions lifted the emotional valley and reduced cancellations.

**Case — Banking customer journey:** The Corporate Executive Board report showed that 20 percent of customers who leave banks do so before their first transaction. The emotional dip happens immediately after account opening — the tumbleweed zone. Most banks do nothing to address this.

## The Co-Creation Technique

Customers who participate in creating their experience are more committed. The technique: give customers meaningful choices and active roles in setup.

**How to do it:** Identify steps in your process where the customer can contribute. "Choose your welcome gift," "Set up your profile," "Name your account." Every act of participation increases commitment.

**Case — Build-A-Bear:** The entire business model is co-creation. The child selects every aspect of the bear and physically assembles it. By the end, the child has hours of emotional investment. The birth certificate ceremony reinforces the co-creation.

**Case — Apple setup:** Setting up a new iPhone asks the customer to choose preferences, set a passcode, configure Face ID. Each choice makes the phone feel like "mine" rather than "a generic device."

## The Milestone Celebration Technique

Milestones are moments when the customer achieves something meaningful. Celebrating these moments creates emotional peaks that customers remember and share.

**How to do it:** Identify the key milestones in your customer journey (first purchase, first success, first renewal, first referral). Design a celebration for each. It doesn't need to be expensive — a phone call, a personalized email, a small gift.

**Case — Yoko Co launch party:** The web design firm throws a small party or sends a gift when a client's website launches. The milestone celebration turns a routine project delivery into a memorable event.

**Case — Audible completion email:** When a member finishes an audiobook, Audible sends a congratulations email and offers the next credit. The simple acknowledgment of achievement keeps the customer engaged.

## The Preframing Technique

Show customers what their experience will be like before they commit. This reduces surprises and sets accurate expectations.

**How to do it:** In the Assess phase, give prospects a preview of the post-sale experience. Use video testimonials, sample onboarding materials, or trial periods.

**Case — Wealth Factory seminars:** Prospects attend in-person seminars before signing up. They experience the teaching style, see the materials, and meet other members. There's no gap between what's promised and what's delivered.

**Case — Notes from the Universe:** Mike Dooley's daily inspirational email service uses carefully designed intake questions during signup. The information gathered is later reflected back to the customer in personalized messages, proving that the company was listening from day one.
