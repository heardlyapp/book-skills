# Core Framework: The First 100 Days® — 8 Phases of the Customer Experience

## Overview

Joey Coleman's core framework is the **8 Phases of the Customer Experience**, designed around the critical first 100 days of the customer relationship. The framework is built on H2H (Human-to-Human) philosophy — the recognition that all business is ultimately about people connecting with people.

Coleman's research revealed that 20 to 70 percent of newly acquired customers leave within the first 100 days. The reason is not product quality or price — it's neglect. Companies spend heavily on acquisition and almost nothing on the post-sale experience. The 8-phase framework provides a structured approach to designing the emotional journey of every customer.

## Phase 1: Assess

The customer is evaluating whether to do business with you. This phase covers sales, marketing, and all pre-purchase interactions. Most businesses focus on closing the sale. What they miss: the Assess phase sets expectations for everything that follows.

**Key insight:** The salesperson must accurately communicate the customer's desires to the account management team. The promises made during sales must align with what is delivered after.

**Case — Corporate Executive Board (CEB):** CEB sent a physical FedEx package to prospects during evaluation. The arrival of an unexpected package signaled that this organization operated differently. The contents previewed the membership experience, preframing expectations. This small gesture differentiated CEB from every other B2B research firm.

**Case — Wealth Factory:** This financial education company uses in-person seminars as part of the Assess phase. Prospects attend a live event before signing up, giving them a taste of the community and teaching style. This pre-experience reduces buyer's remorse later because the customer knows exactly what they're getting.

## Phase 2: Admit

The customer admits they have a problem and believes you can solve it. They sign, they pay, they commit. This is the moment most businesses celebrate — and then go silent.

**Key insight:** The excitement gap is dangerous. The company is euphoric about the sale. The customer is already starting to doubt. This is the "tumbleweed zone" where communications drop off.

**Case — Build-A-Bear:** When a child finishes assembling a stuffed animal, the store presents a personalized birth certificate and announces the "adoption" to everyone in the store. This creates a physical artifact that memorializes the moment. The child's joy is reinforced by the store's visible celebration. The milk and cookies offered make the experience feel like a celebration.

## Phase 3: Affirm

Buyer's remorse floods in. The customer's brain releases chemicals of fear, doubt, and uncertainty: "Did I make the right choice?" "Is this really going to help?" "What if it fails?" This is a biological reflex, not a rational assessment.

**Key insight:** The most important thing you can do is offer ways for the customer to reaffirm their decision as quickly as possible. Positive, high-energy communications counter the chemically induced doubt.

**Case — Book in a Box (now Scribe Media):** After an author signs up, the team sends a care package with a handwritten note from the editor, celebratory treats, and a bottle of champagne with instructions not to open it until launch day. On the first call, the publisher initiates a direct conversation about emotional state: "How are you feeling? What are your doubts?" By naming the emotion, the team neutralizes its power.

**Case — CD Baby confirmation email:** Derek Sivers replaced a boring order confirmation with a hilarious narrative that went viral: "Your CD has been gently taken from our shelves with sterilized contamination-free gloves and placed onto a satin pillow. A team of 50 employees inspected your CD and polished it to make sure it was in the best possible condition before mailing." The email reaffirmed the customer's decision through humor and personality.

## Phase 4: Activate

Now the customer needs to get started. This is where most companies create friction: long forms, unclear instructions, waiting periods. The Activate phase should be fast, simple, and delightful.

**Key insight:** Co-create the experience with the customer. Give them a role in shaping their journey. The more they invest in setup, the more committed they become.

**Case — 23andMe:** The DNA testing company sends a simple kit with visual instructions. The process is intuitive. After the customer mails back the sample, regular updates track the lab's progress: "your DNA has been extracted," "your results are being analyzed." These updates keep the customer engaged during the waiting period.

**Case — Apple setup experience:** When a customer buys a new iPhone, Apple's setup process guides them through each step with minimal friction. The migration from the old phone is automated. The customer feels like they're being helped through a process, not dumped into one.

## Phase 5: Acclimate

The customer is getting comfortable. This phase is about helping them feel like they belong — that they made the right choice and are now part of a community or tribe.

**Key insight:** Acculturation happens when the customer starts to identify with your brand. This is accelerated by proactive check-ins, community events, and personalized touches.

**Case — San Francisco CrossFit:** New members receive a welcome call after their first week to check on progress and address concerns. The coaches learn each member's name and goals. The community events create a sense of belonging that makes leaving feel like leaving a family.

**Case — Delta Airlines:** After a customer's first flight in a new status tier, Delta sends a personalized welcome kit. The luggage tags, card holder, and welcome letter signal that the customer has entered a new relationship tier. The physical artifacts reinforce the emotional shift.

## Phase 6: Accomplish

The customer achieves their first meaningful success with your product or service. This is the turning point — the moment when abstract promises become tangible results.

**Key insight:** Celebrate the customer's success, not just the sale. The Accomplish phase is the best time to reinforce value because the customer can see results.

**Case — Audible:** After a new member finishes their first audiobook, Audible sends an email celebrating the achievement and reminding them that their next credit is available. The accomplishment (finishing a book) is acknowledged and the next step is offered.

**Case — Yoko Co (web design firm):** When a client's website launches, the agency provides a press release template, throws a small party, and sends a celebratory gift. The launch becomes a milestone the client can feel proud of, not just a project completion.

## Phase 7: Adopt

The customer integrates your solution into their identity. They start saying "we use [your product]" instead of "I'm trying [your product]." Adoption means your solution is now part of their routine.

**Key insight:** The deeper the adoption, the harder it is for competitors to dislodge you. Companies that reach the Adopt phase see dramatically lower churn.

**Case — Sephora Beauty Insider:** Sephora's loyalty program creates tiered status (Insider, VIB, Rouge) with corresponding benefits. Members receive birthday gifts, exclusive access, and personalized recommendations. The program integrates Sephora into the customer's beauty routine so deeply that switching to another retailer feels like starting over.

**Case — Harley-Davidson H.O.G.:** Harley-Davidson's owners group creates a community identity so strong that members get tattoos of the logo. The brand becomes part of who they are. This level of adoption makes the customer immune to competitor marketing.

## Phase 8: Advocate

The customer becomes a promoter. They refer, they review, they defend. This phase requires thoughtful timing — ask too early and you damage the relationship; ask too late and you miss the momentum.

**Key insight:** The best time to ask for advocacy is after the customer has accomplished their goal. Make it easy. Draft the testimonial. Time the ask automatically.

**Case — 4Knines:** The dog seat cover company sends a review request email exactly 30 days after purchase — when they know the customer has used the product enough to form an opinion. The email includes the founders' personal signatures with paw prints from their dogs. Twenty-four percent of customers respond versus the 8 percent industry average.

**Case — Dropbox referral program:** Customers who used their free 2GB of storage were offered 500MB of bonus space for every friend who joined. The timing matched the customer's moment of need. The result: 75,000 signups in 24 hours.
