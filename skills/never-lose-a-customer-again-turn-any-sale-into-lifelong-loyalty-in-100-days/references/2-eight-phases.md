# The 8 Phases in Depth

This reference expands each of the eight phases with specific book cases and implementation guidance.

---

## Phase 1: Assess — Cautious Optimism

**What's happening**: The customer evaluates options. They research, compare, and decide whether you can help.

**Coleman's insight**: Most businesses focus on quantity of leads, not quality of the assessment experience. Make evaluation easy and transparent.

**Book case — The Corporate Executive Board**: Banks spend $300 per acquisition but lose 32% of customers in year one. Much of this is because the Assess phase creates expectations that don't match reality. Solution: align marketing promises with actual experience.

**Book case — Dr. McCann**: The online intake form took six minutes, asked each question once, and allowed electronic signature. The contrast with the "ratty clipboard and chewed pen" approach was dramatic. She made assessment frictionless.

---

## Phase 2: Admit — Joy and Excitement

**What's happening**: The customer commits. They've admitted they need you and chosen you. This is the peak emotional moment.

**Coleman's insight**: Almost every business ignores this moment. They send a dry confirmation email and move on. The customer's excitement goes unmatched, starting the relationship on a flat note.

**Book case — Build-A-Bear**: When a child completes a bear, the entire store erupts in celebration. Bells ring, employees cheer, the bear receives a birth certificate. The purchase moment is a memory, not a transaction.

**Book case — CarMax**: The car-buying experience is designed to celebrate rather than stress. No haggling, no "talk to my manager" routine. The purchase is made joyful and simple.

---

## Phase 3: Affirm — Fear and Doubt

**What's happening**: Buyer's remorse sets in. The customer questions their decision. This is the most dangerous phase because it's invisible — the customer rarely tells you they're having doubts.

**Coleman's insight**: This is the most ignored phase in business. Companies spend millions on sales and marketing, then do nothing when the customer is most likely to leave. A simple reassurance can prevent defection.

**Book case — Zappos**: The 365-day return policy is the ultimate Phase 3 intervention. Customers know they can return anything, anytime, for any reason. This eliminates the fear of a bad decision.

**Book case — CD Baby**: Founder Derek Sivers designed a confirmation email that was so warm, funny, and personal that customers felt instantly reassured. The email became legendary — customers would forward it to friends, turning a Phase 3 touchpoint into marketing.

---

## Phase 4: Activate — Excited but Anxious

**What's happening**: The product arrives or the service starts. First real interaction.

**Coleman's insight**: Activation is where you set the trajectory for the entire relationship. A remarkable first use creates momentum; a disappointing one requires recovery that you may never achieve.

**Book case — Apple**: The unboxing experience is designed down to the millimeter. The box opens in a specific way. The product is presented perfectly. Even the smell is considered. Customers photograph the moment and share it.

**Book case — Casper Mattresses**: The mattress arrives in a compact box — a stunning reveal that creates a moment of pure delight. Customers film the unboxing. The surprise is so effective that it generates free social media marketing.

---

## Phase 5: Acclimate — Confusion and Frustration

**What's happening**: The customer learns how you operate. Your "obvious" processes are foreign to them.

**Coleman's insight**: The curse of knowledge is the biggest threat to Phase 5. You know your system inside out; the customer doesn't. Every moment of confusion is a churn risk.

**Book case — Domino's**: The pizza tracker revolutionized Phase 5. Instead of wondering "where is my pizza?," customers watched every step: ordered, prepared, baked, boxed, out for delivery. The confusion of waiting was replaced by the certainty of tracking.

**Book case — Delta Airlines**: The app proactively guides customers through every step of their airport journey. When a gate changes, the app notifies. When boarding starts, the app alerts. They anticipate confusion and address it before it happens.

---

## Phase 6: Accomplish — Happiness and Relief

**What's happening**: The customer achieves the result they wanted. This is the moment of truth.

**Coleman's insight**: Most businesses let this moment pass unnoticed. The customer achieves their goal and the business is silent. The opportunity to create an emotional highlight is lost.

**Book case — Audible**: When a customer finishes an audiobook, Audible sends a congratulatory message. "You finished [Book Title]! Great choice. Interested in another listen?" The acknowledgment makes the customer feel seen and celebrated.

**Book case — Online Trainer Academy**: When clients hit their first fitness milestone, the program celebrates with them. The acknowledgment reinforces the value and creates an emotional anchor that makes them more likely to continue.

---

## Phase 7: Adopt — Pride and Belonging

**What's happening**: The customer takes ownership. They identify with your brand. They're becoming "your people."

**Coleman's insight**: Adoption happens when the customer feels they belong to a tribe. You need to create insider status, shared language, and exclusive rewards.

**Book case — Harley-Davidson H.O.G.**: The Harley Owners Group is the ultimate adoption community. Exclusive rides, events, and merchandise. Owners wear the brand on their bodies, decorate their homes with it, and build their social identity around it.

**Book case — Sephora Beauty Insider**: Customers identify as "VIB" or "Rouge" members. It's a status symbol. Tiered rewards, birthday gifts, and exclusive events make customers feel like insiders. Leaving Sephora means leaving a community.

---

## Phase 8: Advocate — Enthusiasm and Generosity

**What's happening**: The customer becomes a raving fan. They refer, defend, and promote your brand without compensation.

**Coleman's insight**: Advocacy is the highest-value phase. One advocate is worth ten new customers. But advocacy must be earned — you can't buy it.

**Book case — Dropbox Referral Program**: Dropbox's genius was giving free storage to both referrer and referee. This turned every happy customer into a growth engine. From 100,000 to 4 million users in 15 months — almost entirely through advocacy.

**Book case — American Express**: The referral program creates a virtuous cycle. Existing cardholders earn points for referring friends, new cardholders get a welcome bonus. Both sides win, and AmEx grows exponentially.
