# Key Principles

## 1. Don't Criticize — Praise

"Criticism is futile because it puts a person on the defensive and makes them strive to justify themselves. Criticism is dangerous because it wounds a person's precious pride, hurts their sense of importance, and arouses resentment." Praise, by contrast, motivates change.

## 2. Live in Day-Tight Compartments

"The load of tomorrow, added to that of yesterday, carried today, makes the strongest falter." Carnegie's famous method: close the door on yesterday. Don't open it to tomorrow. Live today, in this day only.

## 3. Enthusiasm Is a Choice

"Act enthusiastic and you'll be enthusiastic." Carnegie argues that behavior shapes feelings as much as feelings shape behavior. By acting with energy and enthusiasm, you generate genuine enthusiasm.

> **Case: The Enthusiastic Salesman** (Part 2): A salesman who hated his job started forcing himself to act enthusiastic. He smiled, spoke with energy, and approached every call with genuine curiosity. Within a month, he not only sold more — he actually started enjoying his work. The enthusiasm became real.
> **Key takeaway**: Act the way you want to feel. Behavior change precedes feeling change.

## 4. Remember That People Crave Appreciation

"The deepest principle in human nature is the craving to be appreciated." Carnegie argues that this desire for genuine appreciation is a fundamental human need. Fulfilling it — sincerely — is the key to building relationships.

> **Case: The Appreciated Employee** (Part 1): A factory worker was doing mediocre work until his manager started noticing and praising what he did well — even small things. The worker's performance improved dramatically. He didn't change his skills; he changed because someone believed in him.
> **Key takeaway**: Appreciation is not flattery. It's honest recognition of genuine qualities. Everyone does something worthy of praise. Find it and express it.
