# Techniques: Practical Carnegie Tools

## The Praise Sandwich

1. Begin with sincere appreciation — find something specific to praise
2. Deliver the correction — frame it as "here's an even better way"
3. End with encouragement — "I know you can do this"

## The Worry Analysis Method

1. Ask: "What is the worst possible outcome?"
2. Accept that outcome mentally
3. Then ask: "What can I do to improve upon the worst?"
4. Take that action

## The Name Memory System

1. When introduced, repeat the name immediately: "Nice to meet you, [name]"
2. Use it naturally in conversation: "What do you think, [name]?"
3. Associate it with something distinctive about the person
4. Use it when saying goodbye

## The Enthusiasm Act

1. Smile before entering any interaction
2. Speak with vocal energy — slightly higher and faster than normal
3. Use open body language — uncrossed arms, forward lean
4. Find one genuinely interesting thing about every person you meet
5. Act as if you're genuinely glad to be wherever you are

> **Case: The Negative Employee Turnaround** (Part 3): A chronically negative employee was dragging down team morale. His manager didn't lecture him. Instead, she started acknowledging every positive contribution — even tiny ones. The negative employee began to shift. He didn't change overnight, but the consistent positive reinforcement eventually outweighed his negativity habit.
> **Key takeaway**: You can't argue someone out of negativity. But you can praise them into a more positive state. What you focus on grows.

> **Case: The Worry Cure** (Part 2): A business owner was paralyzed by worry about an economic downturn. Using Carnegie's Worry Analysis Method, he asked: "What's the worst that can happen?" Answer: bankruptcy. He accepted that possibility. Then he asked: "What can I do NOW?" He trimmed costs, diversifying his client base. The worry didn't disappear, but it became manageable.
>
> **Key takeaway**: Worry analysis works because it forces you from catastrophic thinking to practical action. Accepting the worst frees you to do your best.
