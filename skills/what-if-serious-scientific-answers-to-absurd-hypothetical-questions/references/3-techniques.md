## Practical Techniques

### 1. The Fermi Decomposition

Break any large question into pieces you can estimate. Each piece should be an order-of-magnitude guess. Multiply them. The result is your answer.

### 2. The Scale Conversion

Convert abstract numbers into concrete images. "1 exabyte" means nothing. "100,000 years of MP3s" means something.

> **Case: The Hair Dryer** (Chapter 5): Question: is it more dangerous to use a hair dryer in the shower or an AC unit in a pool? Munroe calculates: a hair dryer in water is bad (electrocution risk). An AC unit in a pool is worse — it would pump enough current to kill instantly. The intuitive answer (hair dryer) is wrong.
> **Key takeaway**: Converting the physics into concrete numbers — amps, resistance, voltage — reveals the truth that intuition hides.

### 3. The Sanity Check

After any calculation, ask: "Does this make sense?" If your answer says a baseball can destroy a city, it might be right — check your math.

### 4. The Power of "Let's Assume"

Start every estimation with "Let's assume X." This makes your assumptions explicit. If the answer is wrong, you can trace back to the assumption that caused it.

> **Case: The Ice Cream Melting** (Chapter 2): Question: if you hold an ice cream cone and it melts, does the amount you lose depend on how fast you lick it? Munroe assumes a simple model: heat transfer through the cone walls. The answer: yes — but the effect is smaller than you think.
> **Key takeaway**: Making assumptions explicit reveals the structure of the problem. Even a simple model beats no model.
