## Author's Voice

Randall Munroe writes as a friendly nerd having fun with science. His tone is informal, self-deprecating, and deeply informed. He uses stick figures, graphs, and simple language to explain complex physics. He is not afraid to say "I don't know" and estimate. The book feels like a conversation with someone who genuinely enjoys answering weird questions.

## Signature Phrases

- "Let's assume..." — the start of every estimation
- "What if?" — the question
- "Serious scientific answers" — the promise
- "Do not try this at home" — the disclaimer
- "For comparison..." — putting things in perspective
- "Fun fact" — digressions that are actually essential

## Application to 5 Scenarios

### Scenario 1: A student struggling with science
**Partial response**: "Read What If? It will remind you that science is fun. The book is not a textbook — but you will learn more physics than you expect because you are laughing while learning."

### Scenario 2: A teacher looking for engagement
**Partial response**: "Start each class with a What If? question. 'What would happen if the Earth stopped spinning?' — that question teaches angular momentum, inertia, and atmospheric physics."

### Scenario 3: Someone who thinks they hate math
**Partial response**: "This book might change your mind. The math here is not abstract — it is the math of baseball bombs and planetary-scale mole piles. Numbers are stories."

### Scenario 4: A professional needing quick estimates
**Partial response**: "Fermi estimation is a professional superpower. Learn to estimate within an order of magnitude. Most decisions do not need exact numbers — they need direction."

### Scenario 5: A curious person with weird questions
**Partial response**: "You have found your people. Munroe answers questions you did not know you had. And after reading this book, you will start asking your own absurd questions — which is the whole point."

> **Case: The Apollo 11 Velcro** (Chapter 1): Question: was Velcro invented by NASA for space travel? Answer: no — Velcro was invented in 1941 by George de Mestral. The myth persists because it is a good story. Munroe debunks it with a single fact.
> **Key takeaway**: Myths spread because they are satisfying. Science checks them against reality.

> **Case: The Birthday Paradox** (Chapter 0): In a room of 23 people, there is a 50% chance two share a birthday. In a room of 70, 99.9%. Most people guess it takes 183. The math is counterintuitive.
> **Key takeaway**: Probability is profoundly counterintuitive. Math reveals the truth intuition hides.
