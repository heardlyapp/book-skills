## 6 Anti-Patterns of Scientific Thinking

### 1. "I Don't Need Math — That's Intuition"

Intuition is wrong more often than people admit. Math corrects intuition. The relativistic baseball seems absurd — but the math shows it is real.

### 2. "Exact Numbers Are Required"

Waiting for exact numbers prevents action. An estimate is often good enough. Fermi would estimate before measuring.

> **Case: The Piano Tuners** (Disclaimer): The classic Fermi problem. How many piano tuners are in Chicago? The answer (~15) is within a factor of 2 of the real number (~20). Close enough for most purposes.
> **Key takeaway**: An estimate that is "right within a factor of 2" is often more useful than waiting a month for the exact number.

### 3. "Science Is Not Funny"

Some of the best science communication is funny. Humor does not reduce rigor — it increases engagement. Munroe's book proves it.

### 4. "The Question Must Be Practical"

The most useful insights come from the most impractical questions. Asking "what if the Earth stopped spinning" teaches you about the physics of rotation — which has real applications.

### 5. "I Know What's Dangerous"

Risk perception is broken. People fear flying but not driving. They fear nuclear power but not coal. Math fixes risk assessment.

### 6. "One Number Is Enough"

Never trust a single number. Always compare: "compared to what?" Munroe constantly converts numbers into comparisons — and the comparisons are where insight lives.


> **Case: The Nuclear Fuel Pool** (Chapter 6): Many people fear swimming in a pool of spent nuclear fuel rods. Munroe calculates: the water is highly radioactive — but it is also blue and transparent. The real danger is not the water — it is the sheer quantity of fuel rods that would be required to fill a swimming pool. The pool would need more uranium than exists on earth.
> **Key takeaway**: The visual of a "pool of nuclear fuel" is scary — but the math reveals that the scenario is physically impossible. Math kills fear.
