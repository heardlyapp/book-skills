## 7 Principles of Scientific Curiosity

### 1. Ask Ridiculous Questions

The best questions are the ones no one thought to ask. Absurdity is a feature, not a bug. Every great scientific insight started with someone asking "what if?"

### 2. Approximations Are Powerful

You do not need exact numbers. Orders of magnitude are often enough. Fermi estimation is a superpower for understanding the world.

> **Case: The Gmail Storage** (Chapter 3): Munroe calculates: how much data is in all of Gmail? He estimates: 300 million users, ~3 GB per user, ≈ 1 exabyte. Enough to store all of human written history. The answer is not exact — but it is close enough.
> **Key takeaway**: Approximations give you a ballpark. Ballparks are surprisingly useful.

### 3. Math Is the Language of Reality

The universe runs on math. When you apply numbers to a question, the universe answers. The answer may surprise you.

### 4. Visualize Scale

Humans are bad at understanding large numbers. Munroe helps by converting abstract numbers into concrete images — "enough to fill 27 swimming pools."

### 5. The Universe Is Weirder Than You Think

The truth is stranger than fiction. A baseball at 0.9c becomes a fusion bomb. A mole of moles becomes a planet. Reality is absurd — we just do not notice until we do the math.

> **Case: The Earth Stopping** (Chapter 9): If Earth stopped spinning, everything on the surface would continue at 1000+ mph eastward. Oceans would slosh into megatsunamis. The atmosphere would create winds far beyond hurricane force. The equator would become an uninhabitable ring of destruction.
> **Key takeaway**: We take the Earth's rotation for granted. Doing the math reveals how extreme our "normal" conditions are.

### 6. Risk Is Counterintuitive

What we fear is rarely what kills us. Munroe's scenarios reveal: a pool of nuclear fuel looks terrifying but is chemically just salt water. A baseball at relativistic speed looks harmless but is a bomb.

### 7. Science Is Joy

The book is fun because the author enjoys it. Scientific curiosity is not grim — it is play. The best learning happens when you are having fun.
