# 5 — Voice & App

> Source: *My Next Breath* by Jeremy Renner.

## Renner's Voice

Renner writes as an action star who never wanted to write a memoir. The book's opening line: "I did not want to write this book." The tone is raw, unpolished, and brutally honest — exactly what you would expect from a man who hates pretense and believes in action over words. He does not try to be a writer. He tries to tell the truth about what happened, how it felt, and what he learned. The prose is direct, profane when necessary, and surprisingly tender when he talks about his daughter.

> **Case: The "Not Today, Motherf***er" Prologue (Prologue):** Renner does not sanitize his last words before the leap. He leaves them as he shouted them. The rawness is the point — this is not a polished Hollywood story. This is a man who jumped to save his nephew and got crushed, and he is not going to pretty it up.

> **Case: Ava's School Essay (Dedication):** The book opens not with Renner's words but with a school essay written by his daughter Ava — about how proud she was when her dad got better after being run over by a snowcat. "I knew always since I was a little kid, he was the strongest, most powerful guy on earth. Not only is he strong, he is the sweetest, most funny guy alive." This framing — seeing Renner through his daughter's eyes — sets the emotional foundation for everything that follows.

## Five Scenarios

### 1. The Person in a Medical Crisis

You've been diagnosed with a serious illness or suffered a major injury. The road ahead is long. Renner's approach: don't focus on the full recovery. Focus on the next breath, the next treatment, the next hour. Set micro-milestones. Use your loved ones as fuel.

### 2. The Caregiver

Someone you love is going through a crisis. You feel helpless. Renner's insight: your presence matters more than you know. Alex holding Renner's arm did not fix anything — but it kept Renner going. Be present. Hold the arm.

### 3. The Person Who Has Survived Trauma

You lived through something that should have killed you. You feel survivor's guilt. You wonder why you made it. Renner's answer: the purpose of surviving is what you do next. Your recovery heals not just you but everyone who loves you.

### 4. The Perfectionist

You wait until everything is perfect before you act. Renner's entire life is a rebuke to this. His leap missed. His key fob was lost. His flight was missed. Things went wrong after wrong — and he kept going anyway. Action is messy. Do it anyway.

### 5. The Person Who Needs to Find Meaning in Disaster

Something terrible happened and you cannot make sense of it. Renner does not claim to know why. But he believes things happen for a reason. Not a divine reason — but a reason you create. The meaning is in what you do next.
