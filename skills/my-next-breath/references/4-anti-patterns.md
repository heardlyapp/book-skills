# 4 — Anti-Patterns

> Source: *My Next Breath* by Jeremy Renner.

## Common Errors

### 1. Waiting Until You Are Ready

**The trap:** "I need more time. I need to prepare. I need to be sure."

**Why it fails:** In a real crisis, there is no time to prepare. Renner's leap happened in milliseconds. Waiting until you feel ready is waiting forever. "I had one shot — a long shot, but a shot." He took it. He missed. But at least he tried.

> **Case: The Missed Flight (Chapter 1):** Renner arrived at the gate 15 minutes before takeoff. The crew had just closed the cabin door. "Sorry, sir, we can't open the door once it's closed. FAA regulations." He pleaded but could not board. The irony: he had waited too long to prepare for the trip and ended up driving — which led to the key-fob disaster and the snowcat incident. Sometimes waiting for readiness creates worse outcomes than acting imperfectly.
> **Key takeaway**: The window for action can close in seconds. When you see the opening, move.

> **Case: The Snowcat's Missing Handbrake (Chapter 2):** Renner admits he did not apply the handbrake on the snowcat before stepping off. "I have to live with not applying the hand brake on the snowcat." The omission was not malicious — it was a moment of inattention. But the consequence was catastrophic. Anti-pattern: small failures of preparation can cascade into disasters.
> **Key takeaway**: In high-risk situations, the small details matter. The handbrake was a two-second action he skipped. It cost him everything.

**The trap:** "I'm strong. I don't need help. I'll handle this myself."

**Why it fails:** Renner could not have survived without: his nephew holding his arm, neighbors calling 911, trauma surgeons, ICU nurses, physical therapists, and his entire family. Strength is not doing it alone. Strength is accepting help.

### 3. False Optimism Over Realism

**The trap:** "Stay positive! Think good thoughts! Everything will be fine!"

**Why it fails:** Renner specifically rejects this. False optimism sets you up to fail when reality hits. Realism — facing the worst honestly — allows you to prepare for it. "I'm not much of a fan of someone who is constantly looking on the bright side."

### 4. Focusing on the Mountain Instead of the Next Step

**The trap:** "I need to walk again. I need to recover fully. That's so far away. I can't do it."

**Why it fails:** Renner did not think about walking again while he lay on the ice. He thought about the next breath. The full recovery is overwhelming. The next step is manageable.

### 5. Carrying Guilt Alone

**The trap:** "This was my fault. I caused this. I should have been more careful."

**Why it fails:** Renner acknowledges responsibility — "I know I did something so bad to them" — but he does not let guilt stop him from recovering. Guilt is real. But it cannot be the final word. The only way to redeem the incident is to survive it and learn from it.
