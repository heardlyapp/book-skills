# Techniques — Media Literacy, Friendship Skills, and Creating Safe Spaces

## Practical Methods

> **Case: Media Literacy as Protection** (Chapter 9): Pipher argues that girls need to be taught to critically analyze media messages. She describes a therapy group where girls watched advertisements and discussed how they made them feel. One girl said: "They want me to hate my body so I will buy their products." This moment of insight was transformative. Once the girls could see the manipulation, they were less vulnerable to it.
> **Key takeaway**: Teach her to see through media manipulation. Watch ads together. Ask: "What are they trying to sell? How do they make you feel about yourself?" Critical thinking is the best defense.

> **Case: The Fence at the Top of the Hill** (Chapter 15): Pipher's most famous concept: "It's easier to put a fence at the top of the hill than an ambulance at the bottom." Prevention is better than crisis intervention. She recommends: limit media exposure, encourage physical activity, provide a safe home environment, keep communication open, and get to know her friends and their parents. The fence is built through consistent, everyday presence.
> **Key takeaway**: Do not wait for a crisis to take action. The best interventions are the small, daily acts of connection that prevent problems from developing in the first place.

> **Case: Creating a Safe School Environment** (Chapter 14): Pipher profiles schools that successfully support adolescent girls. Common features: teachers who know students personally, opportunities for girls to express themselves (writing, art, performance), zero tolerance for harassment, and programs that build leadership skills. One school had a "Girls' Group" that met weekly to discuss issues. The group was so popular it had a waiting list.
> **Key takeaway**: If your daughter's school is not supporting her, advocate for change. Girls need spaces where they can speak freely without judgment.
