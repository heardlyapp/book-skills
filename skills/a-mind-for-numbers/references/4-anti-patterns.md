# Anti-Patterns: Common Mistakes Learners Make

## 1. The Re-Reading Trap (Illusion of Competence)

**The mistake**: Re-reading textbooks, notes, and highlighted passages multiple times. Believing that familiarity = knowledge.

**Why it fails**: Reading is passive. It creates the illusion that you understand because the material feels familiar. "When you re-read a chapter, it's surprisingly easy to think you know the material when it's actually just becoming more familiar."

**Fix**: Close the book and recall. The feeling of "I don't know this" is the beginning of actual learning.

## 2. Highlighting and Underlining

**The mistake**: Spending study time marking text with highlighters instead of engaging with the content.

**Why it fails**: "Highlighting and underlining can fool you into thinking you're putting content into your memory when you're not." It feels productive but creates no neural chunk.

**Fix**: After reading a paragraph, write the key idea in your own words, then check. Or use recall: close the book and say it aloud.

> **Case: The Over-Highlighter** (Chapter 4): The book describes students who spend hours highlighting — the textbook ends up a rainbow of colors. But when tested, they can't recall a single concept without looking at the marks.
> **Key takeaway**: Highlighting is the illusion of studying. The only thing you're training is your hand.

## 3. Cramming (Massed Practice)

**The mistake**: Studying everything in one marathon session the night before a test.

**Why it fails**: "Cramming doesn't allow time for the neural patterns to consolidate." Without sleep and spacing, the information goes into short-term memory and disappears within days. You pass the test but retain nothing.

**Fix**: Space your practice over multiple days. "One hour today and one hour tomorrow beats two hours today."

## 4. Overconfidence from Blocked Practice

**The mistake**: Practicing 20 of the same type of problem consecutively. It feels easy because you're applying the same solution method repeatedly.

**Why it fails**: You're not learning to identify which method to use — you're just repeating a mechanical process. On a real test, when problems are mixed, you freeze.

**Fix**: Interleave. Mix problem types. It will feel harder and slower, but you'll develop the skill of recognizing which approach fits each problem.

## 5. Multitasking During Study

**The mistake**: Studying with social media, TV, or music with lyrics in the background.

**Why it fails**: "Every time you shift your attention from studying to something else, you use up part of your limited working memory, and part of your attention stays 'shadowed' on the interruption." Switching costs accumulate.

**Fix**: Pomodoro. 25 minutes of single-tasking, then a break where you can check everything.

## 6. Believing in Fixed Talent

**The mistake**: "I'm just not a math person." "I don't have a science brain."

**Why it fails**: "Many people believe that to be good at math and science, you need a special brain. The truth is, we are born with the ability to form new neural pathways and reshape our existing ones."

**Fix**: Growth mindset. Every hour of focused practice builds new neural connections. "Your brain is like a living creature — it grows and changes with use."

> **Case: The Late Bloomer** (Chapter 12): The book describes students who struggled with math throughout school but went on to become engineers and scientists later in life. Their brain literally rewired through consistent, focused practice.
> **Key takeaway**: Talent is built, not born. The students who succeed are not the "smart" ones — they're the ones who use effective learning techniques.
