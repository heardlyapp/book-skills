# Key Principles

## 1. Recall Is the Only True Test of Learning

The book identifies retrieval practice as the single most effective learning technique — and the most commonly neglected. "When you re-read a chapter, it's surprisingly easy to think you know the material — an 'illusion of competence' — when it's actually just becoming more familiar." The fix is simple: "When you have a book open, look away and see what you can recall."

**The mechanism**: Every time you retrieve a memory, you strengthen the neural pathway. Forgetting and recalling is better than perfect retention — the struggle to retrieve builds deeper encoding.

> **Case: The Illusion of Re-reading** (Preface): Research cited in the preface shows that college students' most common study strategy is repeated reading — "labor in vain." The material becomes fluent and familiar, creating the illusion of knowledge. When actually tested, students who re-read perform worse than those who practice recall.
> **Key takeaway**: If re-reading feels easy, you're not learning. Real learning feels effortful.

> **Case: Spacing over Cramming** (Chapter 8): The book presents the research: students who space their practice over several days dramatically outperform crammers on delayed tests. A student studying 1 hour a day for 5 days learns more than a student studying 5 hours in one day — even though total time is identical.
>
> **Key takeaway**: Time in the chair is not the measure of learning. Spaced effort with sleep between sessions produces durable neural change.

## 2. Procrastination Is a Zombie — Train It

"Procrastination is an easy habit to develop. The important thing to recognize is that it's a habit — one that can be changed." The book frames procrastination as a zombie: "When you let the zombie of procrastination take over, it's like you're putting a live rat on a pleasure-food-cocaine-marijuana-electronic-gaming treadmill."

**The Pomodoro technique** is the primary weapon: 25 minutes of focused attention, then a reward. "The tomato?" (Pomodoro means tomato in Italian — named for the tomato-shaped kitchen timer.)

**Why it works**: "The most important part of using the Pomodoro is that you're relaxing during that five-minute break." The break activates diffuse mode. The 25-minute constraint makes starting less painful.

## 3. Spaced Repetition Beats Cramming

"Spacing your learning over several days or even weeks is more effective than cramming it all into one day." The principle is grounded in how memory consolidation works — neural patterns need time (and sleep) to stabilize.

**The rule of thumb**: Revisit material 24 hours, then a few days, then a week, then a month later. Each retrieval strengthens the chunk and makes it more accessible.

## 4. Interleaving Builds Flexibility

"Once you have the basic idea of how to solve a particular kind of problem, mix up your problem types." Interleaving means practicing different types of problems in the same session, rather than doing all of one type before moving to the next.

**Why it's harder but better**: Blocked practice (doing 20 of the same problem type) feels productive but builds shallow knowledge. Interleaving feels frustrating but builds the ability to recognize which technique to use — the key to real problem-solving.

## 5. Sleep Is Part of the Learning Process

"When you sleep, your brain tidies up ideas and concepts you're thinking about and learning." Sleep consolidates neural patterns, prunes unnecessary connections, and strengthens the chunks you formed during the day.

**Practical rule**: Don't study late into the night before an exam. Sleep is more important than the last hour of cramming. "If you're tempted to pull an all-nighter, realize it's far better to get a good night's sleep."

## 6. Explain It Simply to Own It

The book channels the Feynman Technique: "If you can't explain an idea simply, you don't understand it well enough." When you force yourself to explain a concept in plain language without jargon, you reveal the gaps in your understanding.
