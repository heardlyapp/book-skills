> Source: The Outsiders, Chapters 3-5

# Key Principles

## 1. Capital Allocation Is the CEO's Primary Job

"Most CEOs delegate capital allocation to CFOs or investment bankers. The best ones make it their top priority."

## 2. Focus on Per-Share Value

"The only metric that matters is per-share intrinsic value growth. Revenue, earnings, and market share can all grow while destroying per-share value."

## 3. Be Patient and Contrarian

"The best opportunities come when everyone else is panicking. Patience gives you the cash to act when others can't."

> **Case: John Malone at TCI** (Chapter 4): Malone built TCI into a cable giant through a combination of leverage, tax-efficient deal structures, and relentless focus on per-share cash flow growth. He used TCI's stock as currency for acquisitions but always structured deals to protect per-share value. He was a master of using other people's money.
> **Key takeaway**: The best capital allocators use creative financial structures to create value. Malone's genius was finding ways to fund growth without destroying per-share value.

## 4. Decentralize Operations

"Hire great operating managers and give them autonomy. Your job is to allocate capital, not to run the daily business."

## 5. Know When to Do Nothing

"Sometimes the best investment is no investment. Holding cash is a legitimate strategy."

> **Case: Warren Buffett's Bet** (Chapter 5): In the late 1990s, Buffet held billions in cash while the tech boom raged. He was criticized for missing the party. Then the bubble burst. He bought companies at distressed prices. His patience was rewarded enormously.
> **Key takeaway**: The ability to resist chasing fads and hold cash during manias is a superpower. Buffett's returns came not from what he bought during the boom but from what he didn't buy.
