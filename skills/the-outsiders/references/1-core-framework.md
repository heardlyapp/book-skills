# Core Framework: The CEO as Capital Allocator

## The Central Thesis

"The CEO's most important job is capital allocation. Everything else — strategy, operations, culture — can be delegated. Capital allocation cannot."

> **Case: Tom Murphy at Capital Cities** (Chapter 1): Murphy ran a decentralized operation, giving local managers autonomy while he focused on buying and selling businesses. He bought wisely, often during downturns, and bought back shares aggressively when they were cheap. Over 23 years, Capital Cities returned 20x to shareholders.
> **Key takeaway**: Murphy's genius wasn't in operations — it was in allocation. He was an investor who happened to be a CEO.

## Key Concepts

| Concept | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| Capital Allocation | Deciding how to deploy company capital — invest, acquire, buy back, pay dividends, hold |
| Per-Share Value | The ultimate measure of CEO performance |
| Holding Cash | Waiting for better opportunities |
| Decentralization | Autonomy for operators, CEO focuses on strategy and allocation |
| Contrarian Timing | Acting when others are fearful or complacent |

> **Case: Henry Singleton at Teledyne** (Chapter 2): Singleton was one of the most aggressive share repurchasers in history. When his stock was undervalued, he bought it back aggressively. When it was fairly valued, he used stock for acquisitions. He understood that buying back undervalued stock created enormous value — and he had the nerve to act on it.
> **Key takeaway**: Buybacks are a powerful tool but require conviction. Singleton bought back 90% of Teledyne's shares — a move that looked crazy but created extraordinary value.
