# Anti-Patterns

## 1. Empire Building

**The mistake**: Growing the company's size (revenue, assets, headcount) regardless of per-share value.

**Fix**: Focus on per-share intrinsic value. Sometimes the best way to create value is to shrink.

> **Case: The Empire Builder** (Chapter 2): A CEO of a mid-tier industrial company made dozens of acquisitions, growing revenue from $500M to $5B. But per-share earnings barely increased. The acquisitions were funded by overvalued stock, diluting existing shareholders. The CEO got a bigger salary and more prestige. Shareholders got nothing.
> **Key takeaway**: Empire building destroys value. The CEO's incentives are often misaligned with shareholders'. The Outsiders CEOs resisted empire building.

## 2. Doing Something for the Sake of Doing Something

**The mistake**: Feeling pressure to "do something" — make an acquisition, launch a new product, expand.

**Fix**: Doing nothing is a valid strategy. If no opportunity meets your criteria, wait.

## 3. Following the Herd

**The mistake**: Making acquisitions because competitors are, or because bankers are pitching deals.

**Fix**: Tune out noise. Make decisions based on your own analysis and criteria.

> **Case: The Banker's Pitch** (Chapter 6): A CEO was pitched a "transformational" acquisition by an investment bank. The deal would increase earnings by 15% — but only because the bank's projections were optimistic. When the CEO ran the numbers himself, the deal destroyed value. He passed. The stock doubled over the next two years.
> **Key takeaway**: Investment bankers are salespeople, not fiduciaries for your company. Run your own numbers. Trust your own analysis.
