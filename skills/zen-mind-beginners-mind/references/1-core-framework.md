# Core Framework: Zazen — The Practice of Just Sitting

At the heart of Suzuki's teaching is zazen — Zen meditation. But zazen is not a technique for achieving something; it's a way of being that reveals what already is.

## The Posture

Suzuki's instructions for sitting are precise and simple:
1. Sit on a cushion (zafu) with legs crossed (full lotus, half lotus, or Burmese position)
2. Knees should touch the ground — this creates a stable tripod with your sitting bones
3. Spine straight — not rigid, but aligned — ears in line with shoulders, nose in line with navel
4. Hands in cosmic mudra — left hand on right, thumbs barely touching, forming an oval
5. Mouth closed, eyes open — gaze resting about three feet ahead, unfocused

The posture itself is the practice. When you sit correctly, your body is balanced and your mind naturally settles.

> **Case: The First Instruction** (Chapter "Posture"): Suzuki tells of a new student who asked for the "secret" of Zen. Suzuki replied: "There is no secret. Just sit. That's all." The student pressed: "But what should I DO when I sit?" Suzuki said: "Nothing. Just be present. Let your breath settle. The sitting itself is the teaching."
> **Key takeaway:** There is no technique within the technique. The sitting is complete in itself. Any attempt to "do" something extra is a distraction.

## The Breath

In zazen, follow the breath naturally — don't try to control it. Count your breaths (one to ten, then repeat) as an anchor. When thoughts come, don't follow them — return to the breath count. This is not a battle; it's a gentle return.

**Key insight:** The breath connects body and mind. When you're fully present with your breath, you're nowhere else. This moment is enough.

## Just Sitting (Shikantaza)

Suzuki's highest teaching: "just sitting" — zazen without object, without goal, without expectation. Not sitting to become enlightened. Not sitting to calm down. Just sitting because sitting is the expression of your true nature.

> **Case: The Student Who Tried Too Hard** (Chapter "Control"): A student complained to Suzuki that he was trying very hard to meditate but wasn't making progress. "When I sit, I try to control my mind, but it gets worse." Suzuki laughed: "Don't try to control. Don't try to stop. Just sit. The trying IS the problem. You're like someone who tries to smooth the water but only creates more ripples. Stop smoothing. Let the water settle on its own."
> **Key takeaway:** Effort in meditation is counterproductive. You can't force stillness. You can only create the conditions for stillness to arise naturally — and then get out of your own way.


## The Beginner's Mind
Suzuki's most famous concept: approach everything with "don't-know mind." The beginner doesn't think "this is how it should be" — they just observe what is. This is the heart of Zen practice: seeing directly, without the filter of past experience or future expectation.
