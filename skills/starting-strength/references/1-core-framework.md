# 1 — The Squat

> Source: "Starting Strength" by Mark Rippetoe

## Low-Bar Squat

The low-bar squat is the cornerstone of the Starting Strength method. Bar position: resting on the posterior deltoids, not the cervical spine. This allows more weight and uses the posterior chain.

## Key Points

- **Stance**: Heels shoulder-width apart. Toes pointed out 30 degrees.
- **Bar position**: Below the spine of the scapula. Elbows up. Chest down.
- **Descent**: Hips back first. Knees forward to track over toes. Back angle constant.
- **Depth**: Hip crease below the top of the knee. Below parallel.
- **Ascent**: Drive hips up. Keep the bar over the middle of the foot.

> **Case: The Hip Drive** (Chapter 2): Rippetoe emphasizes that the squat is a hip exercise, not a knee exercise. "The hamstrings are the prime movers of the squat. If you're not using your hamstrings, you're not squatting correctly." The cue: imagine pressing your lower back toward the ceiling on the way up.
> **Key takeaway**: Squat with your hips, not your knees. If your chest is leading on the way up, you're doing it wrong.

> **Case: The Breath** (Chapter 2): Rippetoe teaches the Valsalva maneuver. "Take a deep breath at the top. Hold it through the rep. Exhale only at the top. This creates intra-abdominal pressure that protects your spine." The breath is not optional — it's the belt you can't buy.
> **Key takeaway**: The breath is part of the lift. If you're exhaling on the way up, you're losing spinal stability. Hold your breath through each rep.
