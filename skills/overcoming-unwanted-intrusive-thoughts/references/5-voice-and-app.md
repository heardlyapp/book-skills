# 5 — Voice & App

> Source: *Overcoming Unwanted Intrusive Thoughts* by Sally Winston & Martin Seif.

## The Authors' Voice

Winston and Seif write as experienced clinicians who have worked with thousands of people suffering from intrusive thoughts. Their tone is compassionate, direct, and deflationary — they treat the thoughts seriously without being alarmist. They constantly remind the reader that the problem is not the thoughts but the relationship to them. The book is filled with "Helpful Fact" callouts that serve as anchors for the reader to return to when anxious.

> **Case: The "Not Alone, Not Broken" Opening (Introduction):** "Our first message is that you are not alone. There are millions of people who have thoughts just like yours. Good people have awful thoughts. Violent thoughts come from gentle people. Crazy thoughts occur for people who are not the least bit crazy." This normalization — repeated throughout — is the first and most important intervention.

> **Case: The Recipe Appendix (Appendix):** The book ends with a "Recipe for Unwanted Intrusive Thoughts" — a humorous list of what not to do (e.g., "Take the thought seriously, analyze it, seek reassurance, avoid triggers, check repeatedly"). The self-deprecating humor is intentional: it invites the reader to stop taking themselves and their thoughts so seriously.

## Five Scenarios

### 1. The New Parent with Harm Intrusions

You love your baby. But you have thoughts of dropping, shaking, or worse. You are terrified by these thoughts and afraid to tell anyone. Normal. Harm intrusions are one of the most common types — especially in new parents. They are not wishes. They are not commands. They are the brain's alarm system misfiring. The solution: stop fighting, stop reassuring, let them be background noise.

### 2. The Person with Religious/Scrupulosity Thoughts

You love your faith. But you have intrusive blasphemous thoughts — doubts, curses, sexual images during prayer. This is scrupulosity, a common form of OCD. The more important your faith is to you, the more distressing the thoughts are. Treatment: same principles apply. Do not neutralize. Do not pray for forgiveness for the thoughts. Let them be there. They are not sins.

### 3. The Person with Relationship OCD

You are in a loving relationship. But you have intrusive doubts: "Is this the right person? Do I love them enough? What if I am wrong?" The more you analyze, the more uncertain you become. The solution: accept "maybe, maybe not" and do not seek reassurance. Let the doubt be there while you stay in the relationship.

### 4. The Person with Health Anxiety

You have intrusive thoughts about having a serious disease despite medical reassurance. You check your body, research symptoms, seek tests. The reassurance never lasts. The pattern: doubt → check → temporary relief → more doubt. Break the cycle by stopping the checking and accepting uncertainty.

### 5. The Person Who Has Been Suffering in Silence

You have never told anyone the content of your intrusive thoughts. You are ashamed. You think you are the only one. You are not. The content does not matter. The mechanism is the same for everyone. Revealing the thoughts to a qualified therapist is the first step to recovery.
