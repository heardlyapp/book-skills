# 4 — Anti-Patterns

> Source: *Overcoming Unwanted Intrusive Thoughts* by Sally Winston & Martin Seif.

## Common Errors

### 1. Trying to Suppress the Thought

**The trap:** "I must push this thought out of my mind. I cannot let myself think this."

**Why it fails:** The ironic process: suppression requires monitoring for the forbidden thought — which keeps it active. Every attempt to suppress is a rehearsal. The thought returns stronger each time.

> **Case: The Elevator Obscenity (Chapter 1):** Someone who suddenly imagines shouting an obscenity in a quiet elevator and reacts with "I must never think that again!" has just guaranteed the thought will return. The attempt to suppress creates exactly what it tries to prevent.
> **Key takeaway**: Every suppression attempt is a rehearsal. Do not try to eliminate the thought — let it be there without engagement.

> **Case: The Cleaner's Poison Doubt (Chapter 2):** A house cleaner worried she might have poisoned a customer. She checked the counter once. Then again. Then she started avoiding certain products. The checking never resolved the doubt — it just escalated. She had to learn to accept "maybe I did, maybe I didn't" and stop checking entirely.
> **Key takeaway**: Checking is a False Comfort strategy that always backfires. The only way out is to stop checking.

**The trap:** "If I can just prove this thought is false, I will feel better."

**Why it fails:** Reassurance never ends. You answer one doubt, and another appears. Each time you seek reassurance, you train your brain that the doubt is worth taking seriously. The relief is temporary. The pattern is permanent — until you stop.

### 3. Analyzing the Thought's Meaning

**The trap:** "Why did I think that? What does it say about me? Maybe there is a deep psychological reason."

**Why it fails:** Analysis treats the thought as meaningful. The more you analyze, the more important the thought becomes. The insight is never satisfying — because there is nothing to find. The thought is noise.

### 4. Avoiding Triggers

**The trap:** "I'll just avoid situations that trigger the thought. Problem solved."

**Why it fails:** Avoidance narrows your life and does not resolve the underlying anxiety. The trigger is not the problem — the reaction to the trigger is. Avoiding trains avoidance. Facing trains resilience.

### 5. Expecting a Blank Mind

**The trap:** "I will be recovered when I never have these thoughts again."

**Why it fails:** Recovery is not the absence of intrusive thoughts. It is the absence of distress when they occur. Even recovered people have occasional intrusive thoughts — they just do not react to them. The goal is not a quiet mind. It is a peaceful relationship with a noisy mind.
