# The Brand Critique

> Source: No Logo, Introduction, Chapters 1-5, 15

## Core Framework

### The Rise of the Brand

| Era | Business Model |
|-----|---------------|
| Pre-1980s | Make products, advertise them |
| 1980s-1990s | Outsource production, brand everything |
| 2000s+ | The brand IS the business |

> **Case: Nike and the Swoosh (Chapters 1, 16)**
> Nike is the quintessential No Logo company. It owns no factories. It makes no shoes. It owns the swoosh — and the swoosh is worth billions. Nike's genius was realizing that the brand was more valuable than the product. By outsourcing production to low-wage countries, Nike could focus on what really mattered: the image. The swoosh became a symbol of cool, of athleticism, of rebellion. But behind the swoosh: workers in Indonesian factories earning $2/day, banned from forming unions.
> **Key takeaway:** Nike proved that the brand is the product. The physical shoe is almost incidental. The real value is in the logo. This insight explains the entire modern economy: we buy brands, not products.

> **Case: The Branding of Schools (Chapter 4)**
> Klein documents how corporate branding invaded schools: Channel One (ads in classrooms), Coke and Pepsi pouring rights contracts, corporate-sponsored curriculum. Schools became advertising platforms. Students became captive audiences. The logic: schools are underfunded, corporations fill the gap. The cost: children's education becomes a marketing opportunity.
> **Key takeaway:** The branding of schools is the most vivid example of the brand's expansion. Nothing is sacred — not even childhood education. The lesson: what is not defended as public space will be claimed as private property.
