# Anti-Patterns — Resistance

> Source: No Logo, Chapters 12-18

## The Five Errors of the Branded World

### 1. "Consumer Choice Is Freedom"
**Error**: "I am free because I can choose between 50 brands of sneakers."
**Correction**: Choosing between brands is not the same as choosing your life. Real freedom is deciding what is produced, how, and by whom.

### 2. "Globalization Is Inevitable"
**Error**: "You cannot stop the global economy."
**Correction**: Globalization was created by policy. Policy can be changed.

### 3. "There Is No Alternative"
**Error**: "Capitalism is the only system."
**Correction**: There are always alternatives. The market is one way to organize society. It is not the only way.

### 4. "Activism Doesn't Work"
**Error**: "Protesting changes nothing."
**Correction**: The brand boomerang is proof that activism works. Campaigns against Nike, Shell, and Gap won real changes.

### 5. "Corporations Are Democratic"
**Error**: "You can vote with your wallet."
**Correction**: Voting with your wallet is not democracy. Democracy requires one person, one vote. The market gives one dollar, one vote.

> **Case: Culture Jamming (Chapter 12)**
> Culture jamming is the practice of subverting corporate advertising. Adbusters magazine is the most famous example. Culture jammers alter billboards, create parodies of ads, and expose the gap between brand image and reality. The goal is not to destroy advertising — it is to reclaim the space that advertising has taken. Culture jamming is the symbolic version of reclaiming public space.
> **Key takeaway:** Culture jamming works because it uses the brand's own tools against it. A billboard is an occupying force in public space. Culture jammers are the resistance.

> **Case: The Battle of Seattle (Chapter 14)**
> In November 1999, 50,000 activists shut down the World Trade Organization meeting in Seattle. It was a coalition of labor unions, environmentalists, students, and anarchists. They stopped global trade negotiations. The city was under martial law for days. The protests changed the debate: after Seattle, no one could pretend that globalization was uncontroversial. The movement had arrived.
> **Key takeaway:** Seattle 1999 is the book's climax. It proved that the anti-globalization movement was real, diverse, and powerful. The lesson: when people come together across differences, they can stop the global elite.
