# The Brand Expands

> Source: No Logo, Chapters 2, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8

## Corporate Colonization of Culture

### Where Brands Go

| Space | Before | After |
|-------|--------|-------|
| Schools | Public education | Channel One, Coke contracts |
| Museums | Cultural institutions | Corporate-sponsored exhibits |
| Cities | Public squares | Branded districts, sponsored parks |
| Music | Youth culture | Sponsored tours, branded bands |

> **Case: The Mall as Town Square (Chapter 6)**
> Klein argues that the shopping mall replaced the town square as the center of public life. But the mall is not public — it is private property. You can be ejected for wearing the wrong shirt. You cannot protest. You cannot organize. The mall is a branded space that looks public but is private. The illusion of public space is the final triumph of branding: we think we are in a public square when we are in a corporate-controlled environment.
> **Key takeaway:** The mall is the perfect symbol of the branded world: it looks like public space, feels like public space, but is entirely private. Your rights end at the mall entrance. The lesson: the privatization of public space is stealthy — and total.

> **Case: Mergers and Synergy (Chapter 7)**
> The 1990s merger mania created media conglomerates that controlled every part of the cultural pipeline: movies, music, TV, books, theme parks, merchandise. Disney owned ABC, ESPN, theme parks, and a trillion branded products. Time Warner owned movies, magazines, CNN. The result: fewer voices, more synergy, less diversity. The same brand could be cross-promoted across every platform.
> **Key takeaway:** Media consolidation is the economic foundation of the branded world. When a few companies control all the channels, they can brand everything. The opposite of synergy is independence. The lesson: media diversity is not just cultural — it is political.
