# The Global Supply Chain

> Source: No Logo, Chapters 9, 10, 11

## The Human Cost

### The Supply Chain

| Stage | What Happens |
|-------|-------------|
| Raw materials | Sourced from cheapest supplier |
| Manufacturing | Outsourced to export processing zones |
| Assembly | Done by young women earning minimum wage |
| Branding | Logo added, image created |
| Retail | Sold in branded stores for 10x cost |

> **Case: The Discarded Factory (Chapter 9)**
> Klein visits export processing zones in Indonesia and the Philippines. Factories that produce brand-name goods: Nike, Gap, Reebok. Workers are young women, mostly from rural areas. They work 12-hour days, 6 days a week, for less than $2/day. They sleep in dormitories. They are not allowed to form unions. If they protest, they are fired. The factories are clean. The workers' conditions are not.
> **Key takeaway:** The global supply chain is designed for invisibility. The brand is visible everywhere. The factory is hidden. Klein's reporting makes it visible again. The lesson: what you cannot see is the most important part of the product.

> **Case: Nike's Response to the Sweatshop Exposé (Chapter 16)**
> In the 1990s, Nike was exposed for sweatshop labor. The company's initial response: denial. Then they tried to deflect. But the exposés kept coming. Finally, Nike was forced to change. They raised wages at some factories. They allowed monitoring. They published supplier lists. The brand boomerang had worked: Nike's swoosh was so valuable that the company could not afford the bad publicity. The brand's power became the activists' leverage.
> **Key takeaway:** The brand boomerang is No Logo's most important strategic insight. The same brand power that companies use to sell products makes them vulnerable to consumer pressure. The swoosh is a weapon that can be turned against Nike.
