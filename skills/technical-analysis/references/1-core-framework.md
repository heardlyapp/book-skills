# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *Technical Analysis of the Financial Markets* by John J. Murphy — Chapters 1-10, 17.

## The Three Frameworks

### Framework A: The Trend Structure (Chapters 1, 2, 4)

Technical analysis starts with identifying the trend. Markets move in three directions (up, down, sideways) and three timeframes (primary, secondary, minor). Dow Theory provides the foundation: trends persist until a confirmed reversal. Support and resistance levels define the boundaries of the trend.

> **Case: The Head and Shoulders Bottom Tactics** (Chapter 5): Murphy describes how experienced traders enter a head and shoulders bottom during the formation of the right shoulder — before the neckline breakout. They watch for: (1) a 50-66% retracement of the rally from head to right shoulder low, (2) the right shoulder reaching the same level as the left shoulder, and (3) the breaking of a short-term downtrendline from the head. Aggressive entry at these levels yields better risk/reward than waiting for the neckline breakout.

> **Case: The Failed Head and Shoulders Warning** (Chapter 5): When a head and shoulders pattern breaks the neckline but prices then recross back above it (at a top) or below it (at a bottom), the pattern has failed. Murphy calls this a "failed head and shoulders" — a powerful warning that the initial analysis was wrong. "One of the keys to survival in the financial markets is to keep trading losses small and to exit a losing trade as quickly as possible."

### Framework B: Reversal and Continuation Patterns (Chapters 5, 6)

Price patterns fall into two groups: reversal patterns (head and shoulders, double tops/bottoms, triple tops/bottoms, saucers, spikes) and continuation patterns (triangles, flags, pennants, wedges, rectangles). Reversals take longer to build and signal trend changes. Continuations are shorter pauses before the trend resumes.

> **Case: The Double Top on Nasdaq** (Chapter 5): Murphy shows that the Nasdaq Composite Index formed a double bottom near the 1470 level before turning higher, confirmed by a break of the downtrendline. The time between the two troughs (about 2 months) gave the pattern its significance. Volume picked up on the upside breakout — the essential confirming factor.

### Framework C: Oscillators and Their Use (Chapters 9, 10)

Moving averages smooth price data to identify trend direction. Oscillators (RSI, MACD, Stochastic, CCI) measure momentum and identify overbought/oversold conditions. Murphy emphasizes that oscillators are most useful in trading ranges and less helpful in strong trending markets (where they can remain overbought/oversold for extended periods).

> **Case: RSI and MACD Practical Application** (Chapter 10): Murphy presents the 14-period RSI with the 70/30 lines as standard. He stresses that MACD (12/26/9) is most useful as a crossover system (signal line cross = entry) and for divergences (price makes higher high but MACD makes lower high = bearish divergence). These tools should never be used in isolation — they work best when the primary trend has already been identified.
