# 2 — Key Principles

> Source: *Technical Analysis of the Financial Markets* by John J. Murphy.

## The 7 Principles

### 1. The Trend Is Your Friend

Identify the primary trend before making any trade. An uptrend is defined by higher highs and higher lows. A downtrend is defined by lower highs and lower lows. Trade in the direction of the primary trend. Fighting the trend is the most expensive mistake.

> **Case: Dow Theory's Three Trends** (Chapter 2): Dow Theory identifies three trends: primary (lasting months to years), secondary (3 weeks to months, 33-66% retracement of the primary), and minor (less than 3 weeks, noise). Murphy stresses that the minor trend can easily fool traders — always step back to the primary trend first.

### 2. Volume Confirms Price

A price breakout without volume is suspect. Heavy volume gives credibility to a new move. Declining volume during a rally suggests the trend is losing steam. Volume spikes at bottoms (selling climax) and declines at tops (distribution).

### 3. No Pattern Is Infallible

Murphy states this explicitly: chart patterns work most of the time, but not always. The failed head and shoulders pattern proves that even the most reliable patterns can be wrong. The key is to recognize failure quickly and exit.

### 4. The Longer the Pattern, the Bigger the Move

A two-year saucer bottom on a weekly chart is far more significant than a two-week triangle on a daily chart. Time duration determines the potential magnitude of the breakout. Murphy warns against premature pattern diagnosis — wait for the pattern to complete.

### 5. Intermarket Forces Are Interconnected

> **Case: The Bond-Stock Link** (Chapter 17): Murphy shows that bond prices lead stock prices. Falling bond yields (rising bond prices) are bullish for stocks. Rising bond yields are bearish. The dollar is linked to commodities: a rising dollar puts pressure on commodity prices; a falling dollar lifts them. Understanding these intermarket relationships provides a broader context for individual trades.

### 6. Money Management Trumps Entry Signals

The best entry signal is useless without proper money management. Risk 1-2% per trade. Maintain at least a 3:1 reward-to-risk ratio. Know exactly where your stop loss will be before entering.

### 7. Filter Reduces Whipsaws

Use time and price filters to reduce false breakouts. A two-day penetration rule (close beyond the level for two consecutive days) or a percentage filter (1% or 3% beyond) can save money. These filters don't eliminate false signals — they reduce them.
