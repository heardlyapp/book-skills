# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *Technical Analysis of the Financial Markets* by John J. Murphy.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The Trendline Drawing Protocol

Draw a trendline connecting at least two reaction lows (for an uptrend) or two reaction highs (for a downtrend). A valid trendline requires at least three touches to be trusted. The fan principle: when the first trendline is broken, draw a second, less steep line. When the second is broken, draw a third. Breaking the third trendline signals a major reversal.

> **Case: The Fan Principle** (Chapter 4): Murphy describes how multiple trendline breaks form a fan pattern. When the third trendline is broken, the original trend is likely over. The fan shows the trend decelerating before it reverses. This is one of the most reliable signals in technical analysis.

### 2. Measuring Price Objectives

For head and shoulders: measure the vertical distance from the head to the neckline. Project that distance from the breakout point. For double tops/bottoms: measure the height of the pattern (peak to trough) and project from the breakout. For triangles: measure the widest part of the triangle and project from the breakout.

### 3. The Oscillator Divergence Signal

When price makes a new high but RSI (or MACD) makes a lower high = bearish divergence. When price makes a new low but RSI makes a higher low = bullish divergence. Divergence is one of the most powerful signals in technical analysis — but it needs trend confirmation.

### 4. Moving Average Crossover Strategy

The 50-day crossing above the 200-day moving average (Golden Cross) is a long-term bullish signal. The 50-day crossing below the 200-day (Death Cross) is bearish. Shorter crossovers (10/30, 20/50) are more frequent but less reliable.

### 5. Bollinger Band Squeeze

When Bollinger Bands contract (narrow width relative to recent history), volatility is low and a breakout is likely. The direction of the breakout is unknown — wait for price to break the band before entering.

> **Case: Bollinger Band Width** (Chapter 9): Murphy explains that Bollinger Band width measures volatility. When bands are unusually narrow, a significant move is coming. John Bollinger himself recommends watching for "The Squeeze."
> **Key takeaway**: Low volatility periods are not boring — they are preparation for movement. Watch for the squeeze and be ready to trade the breakout.

> **Case: The 50/200 Day Moving Average System** (Chapter 9): Murphy demonstrates that the 50-day moving average serves as the intermediate trend barometer while the 200-day signals the long-term trend. When prices cross the 50-day, it's a short-term signal. When the 50-day crosses the 200-day, it's a major trend change. The system is simple but powerful.
