# 5 — Voice & App

> Source: *Technical Analysis of the Financial Markets* by John J. Murphy.

## Murphy's Voice

Murphy writes as a teacher — clear, methodical, and precise. This is a textbook, not a trading memoir. There are no stories of big wins or dramatic losses. Murphy presents each concept systematically: definition, rationale, construction, application, and caveats. He uses the word "usually" constantly, emphasizing that technical analysis is about probabilities, not certainties.

> **Case: The Consistent Warning Language** (Throughout): Murphy repeatedly uses phrases like "usually indicates," "most of the time," "not always," and "there are exceptions." This is central to his teaching philosophy: technical analysis is a toolkit for tilting probabilities in your favor, not a crystal ball. The honest acknowledgment that patterns fail is what separates Murphy's book from less rigorous works.

> **Case: The Practical Application Focus** (Chapters 5, 10, 16): Murphy doesn't just describe patterns — he shows how to trade them. His head and shoulders bottom tactics section (Chapter 5) provides three specific early entry techniques. His money management chapter (Chapter 16) gives specific numbers: risk 1-2%, 3:1 reward-to-risk ratio, scale out of positions at 50% and 100% of target. This practical guidance makes the theory actionable.

## Five Scenarios

### 1. The New Trader

Start with Murphy's core: identify the primary trend (daily chart, 200-day moving average), learn support and resistance (most basic and most useful), master the head and shoulders pattern (most reliable reversal), and implement a 2% risk rule (money management is more important than entry).

### 2. The Swing Trader

Focus on intermediate-term trends. Use 50-day MA for trend direction, RSI (14) for overbought/oversold, and volume to confirm breakouts. Trade continuation patterns (triangles, flags) in the direction of the primary trend.

### 3. The Long-Term Investor

Use weekly charts and 200-day MA for the primary trend. Watch for Golden Cross / Death Cross signals. Use intermarket analysis to understand sector rotation. Bollinger Bands on weekly charts can identify extreme valuations.

### 4. The Market Analyst

Combine intermarket analysis (Chapter 17) with Dow Theory. Use the advance-decline line and new highs-new lows to measure market breadth. The top-down approach: analyze global markets first, then sectors, then individual stocks.

### 5. The System Trader

Murphy's Chapter 15 on computers and trading systems provides the foundation. Use a combination of trend-following indicators (moving averages) and oscillators (MACD). Backtest before trading live. Paper trade for at least 3 months before committing real capital.
