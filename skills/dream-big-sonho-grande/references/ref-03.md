# Reference: The Anheuser-Busch Takeover

## Overview
InBev's $52 billion hostile takeover of Anheuser-Busch in 2008 was the defining deal of 3G's history. It transformed the global beer industry and made InBev the world's largest brewer.

## Case: The Hostile Bid
InBev approached Anheuser-Busch with a $46 billion offer in June 2008. Anheuser-Busch rejected it as inadequate. InBev responded with a hostile bid — taking the offer directly to shareholders. The Anheuser-Busch board faced a choice: negotiate or fight. They negotiated, accepting a $52 billion offer.

## Case: The Cost-Cutting Machine
After the deal closed, InBev moved quickly. They replaced the CEO, eliminated the corporate jet fleet, stopped sponsoring the Clydesdale promotional team, and cut thousands of jobs. ZBB was implemented across the company. The changes were deeply unpopular with employees and the St. Louis community. But they worked: InBev's operating margins increased dramatically.

## Case: The Global Beer Empire
The Anheuser-Busch acquisition was the culmination of a decade of beer industry consolidation. InBev had formed in 2004 from the merger of Interbrew and AmBev. After acquiring Anheuser-Busch, AB InBev controlled Budweiser, Stella Artois, Beck's, Corona, and hundreds of other brands. It became the dominant force in the global beer market.

## Case: The Cultural Clash
The takeover was also a clash of corporate cultures. Anheuser-Busch was a 150-year-old American institution with a family culture. InBev was a Brazilian-led company with a performance-at-all-costs ethos. The transition was painful for employees and the local community. But from a business perspective, the combination was immensely successful.
