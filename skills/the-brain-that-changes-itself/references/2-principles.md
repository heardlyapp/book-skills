# Key Principles — The Brain That Changes Itself

## I. The Brain Is Not Hardwired

"The brain can change its own structure and function through thought and activity." The old machine metaphor is wrong. The brain is plastic — changeable, malleable, modifiable. Case: Bach-y-Rita's tongue sensor — the brain rewired itself to "see" through the tongue.

## II. Neuroplasticity Works Both Ways

"The plastic paradox: plasticity has the power to produce more flexible but also more rigid behaviors." Your brain can become locked in painful patterns (OCD, chronic pain, bad habits) just as easily as it can heal. Case: Phantom limb pain — the brain creates a rigid, painful body map that persists despite the limb being gone.

## III. Mental Activity Changes Physical Structure

"Thinking, learning, and acting can turn our genes on or off, shaping our brain anatomy." Every thought leaves a physical trace. Case: London taxi drivers who memorize "The Knowledge" develop larger hippocampi.

## IV. Use It or Lose It

Brain functions that are not used weaken. Functions that are practiced strengthen. Case: CI therapy — forcing use of a paralyzed limb prevents "learned non-use."

## V. There Is No Age Limit

"The common wisdom was that after childhood the brain only declined." Neuroplasticity operates throughout life. Case: Stroke patients in their 80s recovered movement through CI therapy.

## VI. Imagination Is a Form of Simulation

"Thinking and doing use the same brain circuits." Mental practice strengthens neural pathways almost as effectively as physical practice. Case: Imagining playing piano activates the same motor cortex as actually playing.

## VII. Brain Change Requires Focused Attention

"Plastic change occurs only when the brain is paying close attention." Passive exposure is not enough. Case: Schwartz's OCD treatment required patients to actively relabel their thoughts, not just passively observe them.
