# Techniques · Money Biases · Decision Frameworks

> Source: *The Art of Thinking Clearly*, chapters on financial and decision biases

---

## Sunk Cost Fallacy (Ch. 6)

We continue investing because we've already invested. "I've already spent $100 on this concert ticket, so I should go even though I'm sick." **Fix:** Ask: "Knowing what I know now, would I make the same decision?" If not, walk away. The past is irrelevant.

## Endowment Effect (Ch. 17)

We value what we own more than what we don't. We demand more to sell something than we'd pay to buy it. **Fix:** Ask: "If I didn't own this, would I buy it?" If not, sell it.

## Anchoring (Ch. 8)

The first number we see influences all subsequent judgments. A $5000 watch makes a $500 watch seem cheap. A high asking price makes the counteroffer seem reasonable. **Fix:** Never negotiate without knowing your independent valuation first.

## Framing (Ch. 28)

How a choice is presented changes our decision. "90% survival rate" sounds better than "10% mortality rate" — even though they're the same. **Fix:** Restate the same information in different frames. If your decision changes, the frame is influencing you.

## Self-Serving Bias (Ch. 32)

We attribute success to our own skill and failure to external circumstances. **Fix:** Track your predictions and decisions. When you're wrong, look for what YOU did wrong.
