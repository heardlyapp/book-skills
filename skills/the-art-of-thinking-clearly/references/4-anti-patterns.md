# Anti-Patterns · The Most Dangerous Biases

> Source: *The Art of Thinking Clearly*, selected chapters

---

## 1. Confirmation Bias (Ch. 13)
We look for evidence that confirms our beliefs and ignore evidence that contradicts them. This is the mother of all biases because it keeps us locked in error.

**Fix:** Actively seek disconfirming evidence. Read opinions you disagree with. Try to prove yourself wrong.

## 2. Narrative Fallacy (Ch. 27)
We prefer stories to statistics. A vivid anecdote about one person outweighs a spreadsheet of data. This is why marketing works and why we make bad decisions based on compelling stories.

**Fix:** Ask "Is this one story, or is this the general pattern?" Don't be seduced by a good narrative.

## 3. Recency Bias
We give more weight to recent events. The last stock market performance colors our view of the next. The last meal colors our view of the restaurant.

**Fix:** Look at the long-term trend, not the last data point.

## 4. Action Bias (Ch. 35)
We feel the need to do something, even when doing nothing is better. In investing, trading is often worse than holding. In management, changing things is often worse than letting them run.

**Fix:** Ask "What happens if I do nothing?" If the answer is acceptable, consider inaction.

## 5. Commitment and Consistency Bias (Ch. 45)
Once we commit to a course of action (publicly or privately), we tend to stick with it even when it's wrong, to appear consistent.

**Fix:** Make it easy to change your mind. "Strong opinions, loosely held."
