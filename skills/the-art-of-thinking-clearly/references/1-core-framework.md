# Core Framework · Social Biases · Overconfidence Biases

> Source: *The Art of Thinking Clearly*, chapters on social and overconfidence biases

---

## Social Biases

**Social Proof (Ch. 3)**: We copy the behavior of others in uncertain situations. If everyone is buying a stock, it must be good. If everyone is running one direction, we follow. **Fix:** In situations of uncertainty, ask "What is the actual evidence?" not "What is everyone else doing?"

**Authority Bias (Ch. 10)**: We over-trust experts, even when they're wrong. A CEO's opinion isn't more valid than the data. **Fix:** Evaluate the argument, not the person making it.

**Contrast Effect (Ch. 7)**: We perceive things relative to what's around them. A $1000 suit seems reasonable after looking at $5000 suits, but expensive after $200 suits. **Fix:** Always evaluate options in isolation against an absolute standard.

**Herd Mentality**: The tendency to follow the crowd even when the crowd is clearly wrong. In financial bubbles, this is the dominant bias.

---

## Overconfidence Biases

**Overconfidence Effect (Ch. 4)**: 93% of American drivers consider themselves above average. We systematically overestimate our knowledge, skills, and predictions. **Fix:** Assume your confidence is inflated by 20%. Adjust accordingly.

**Hindsight Bias (Ch. 5)**: After an event, we believe we predicted it. "I knew the dot-com bubble would burst" — but you didn't. **Fix:** Before an event, write down your prediction. After it happens, compare.

**Planning Fallacy (Ch. 25)**: We underestimate how long tasks will take, even when we have experience. **Fix:** Use reference class forecasting — how long did similar projects actually take?
