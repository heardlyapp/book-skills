# 5 — Bikman's Voice + 5 Application Scenarios

> Source: "Why We Get Sick" by Benjamin Bikman

## Bikman's Voice

Benjamin Bikman is a biomedical scientist who studies insulin resistance at Brigham Young University. His voice: rigorous, accessible, passionate. He presents complex metabolic science in clear terms without oversimplifying.

> **Case: The Book's Opening** (Prologue): "We are sick. Worldwide, we are struggling with diseases that were once rare. Heart disease, Alzheimer's, cancer, diabetes — they are rising at alarming rates. But what if these seemingly different diseases share a single root cause? What if the epidemic of chronic disease is actually one epidemic with many faces?" Bikman sets up the central question of the book.
> **Key takeaway**: Bikman's framing is that chronic disease is not inevitable or random — it has a common root. This is an optimistic message: if you address the root, you address all the branches.

> **Case: The Call to Action** (Chapter on Solutions): "You have more control over your metabolic health than you think. Your diet, your activity, your sleep, your stress — these are not minor factors. They are the primary determinants of your insulin sensitivity. The science is clear. The question is whether we will act on it." Bikman ends with a practical, empowering message.
> **Key takeaway**: Bikman is not fatalistic. He believes that metabolic health is largely within our control. The science provides the knowledge; the rest is up to us.

## Key Quote

"Insulin resistance is not a disease you have — it is a state your body is in. And states can change."

## 5 Application Scenarios

1. **Worried about heart disease** → Check your TG/HDL ratio. If over 2, address insulin resistance before chasing LDL.
2. **Concerned about Alzheimer's risk** → Consider a low-carb or ketogenic diet to provide ketones for brain fuel.
3. **Diagnosed with PCOS or fertility issues** → Address insulin resistance through diet and lifestyle before pursuing hormonal treatments.
4. **Dieting without results** → Focus on reducing insulin, not just calories. Intermittent fasting and carb restriction lower insulin directly.
5. **Trying to understand why chronic diseases run in families** → It's not just genetics. Shared dietary and lifestyle patterns transmit metabolic dysfunction across generations.
