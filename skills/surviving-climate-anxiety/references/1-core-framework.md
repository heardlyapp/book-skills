# Core Framework: The ACORN Protocol

Doherty's central framework for climate resilience is the ACORN Protocol — five steps for coping with climate anxiety that can be applied in any order, repeated as needed.

## A — Acknowledge
The first step: acknowledge that climate anxiety is real, valid, and expected. You are not broken for feeling distressed about the climate crisis. This is a rational response to a genuine threat. Suppressing or minimizing these feelings only makes them stronger.

**Key insight:** "The climate crisis is personal." It affects your sense of safety, your future, your values, your identity. Acknowledging that is the foundation of coping.

## C — Cope
Develop specific strategies for managing climate distress: limit news consumption, practice grounding techniques, maintain routines, and create boundaries around climate discussions. Coping isn't about fixing the problem — it's about staying functional while you work on it.

> **Case: The News Junkie** (Chapter 1, "Thinking"): Doherty describes a patient who checked climate news obsessively — multiple times an hour, every day. He felt informed but was paralyzed by fear. The intervention: limit news to 30 minutes per day, at a set time (not before bed). Within a week, his anxiety dropped significantly. He was still informed. He was no longer consumed.
> **Key takeaway:** Information consumption must be managed like any other resource. More information doesn't mean more action — it often means more paralysis.

## O — Orient
Orient yourself to what you can actually control. Climate change is vast and global. Your sphere of influence is local and specific. Orient your energy toward the intersection of what matters to you and what you can actually affect.

## R — Relate
Connect with others who share your concerns. Climate anxiety thrives in isolation. Community is the antidote. Join a group, talk to friends, find your people. Shared burden is lighter.

## N — Nurture
Take care of yourself. Rest, nature, joy, art, relationships — these are not distractions from climate action. They are the foundation that makes sustained action possible. Nurturing yourself is part of the solution.

> **Case: The Activist's Burnout** (Chapter 4, "Adapting"): A young climate activist came to Doherty exhausted. She had been organizing protests, attending every meeting, and burning out fast. The ACORN protocol helped her see that she was skipping "Nurture." She added one simple practice: a weekly walk in the woods with no agenda. Her anxiety decreased and her effectiveness increased.
> **Key takeaway:** Rest is not retreat. Taking care of yourself makes your climate work sustainable. Burnout helps no one.


## The Thinking-Feeling Loop
Doherty identifies a common pattern: people either overthink climate change (analyzing, researching, planning) or overfeel it (anxiety, grief, despair). The goal is balance. Thinking without feeling leads to detachment. Feeling without thinking leads to overwhelm. The practice: notice when you're in one mode and gently invite the other.
This balance is not achieved once — it's a daily practice. Notice which mode you're in and gently invite the other.
