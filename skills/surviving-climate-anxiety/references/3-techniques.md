# Techniques: Practical Climate Resilience Tools

## Technique 1: The Information Diet
Set clear boundaries around climate news consumption. Doherty recommends: check climate news once per day at most, for no more than 30 minutes. Use a timer. No climate content within one hour of bedtime. After consuming bad news, balance with a positive climate story or a concrete action step.

## Technique 2: The Sphere of Influence Map
Draw three concentric circles:
1. **Inner circle** — What you can directly control (your consumption, your career, your voice)
2. **Middle circle** — What you can influence (your community, your workplace, your local politics)
3. **Outer circle** — What concerns you but you can't control (national policy, global emissions)

Put your energy in the inner and middle circles. Acknowledge the outer circle but don't let it consume you.

> **Case: The Paralysis of Scale** (Chapter 8, "Family"): A mother was paralyzed by the scale of climate change. "What's the point of recycling when corporations are polluting the planet?" Doherty helped her map her sphere of influence. She couldn't control corporations, but she could teach her children about nature, join a local environmental group, and advocate for better recycling policies in her town. Small actions, done consistently, restored her sense of agency.
> **Key takeaway:** When the problem feels too big, shrink your focus to what you can actually affect. Agency is the antidote to helplessness.

## Technique 3: The Climate Emotions Journal
Write for 10 minutes about your climate feelings. No editing. No judgment. Just let it out. Doherty's research shows that naming and expressing climate emotions reduces their intensity. Writing also reveals patterns — what triggers your anxiety, what restores your hope, what actions feel meaningful.

## Technique 4: The Nature Prescription
Schedule regular, intentional time in natural spaces. Not exercise. Not errands. Just being in nature. Doherty recommends: 2 hours per week minimum. Leave your phone behind. Notice what you see, hear, and smell. Reconnection is medicine.

## Technique 5: The Two-Worlds Practice
Doherty's framework for living with climate reality: "We must learn to live in two worlds." The world that is burning (staying informed, taking action) AND the world worth saving (finding joy, loving people, appreciating beauty). Both are real. Both demand your attention. Master both.

> **Case: The Grief and Joy** (Chapter 12, "Place"): A climate activist described feeling guilty whenever she felt happy. "How can I enjoy a beautiful sunset when the world is burning?" Doherty reframed: "If you can't enjoy the sunset, you've lost the thing you're trying to protect. Joy is not a betrayal of the cause — it IS the cause. What's the point of saving a world you can't enjoy?"
> **Key takeaway:** Joy is not a distraction from climate work. It's the entire point of it. Protect your capacity for joy as fiercely as you protect the planet.


## Technique 6: The Climate Companion Practice
Find one person you can talk to about climate anxiety without judgment. Schedule regular check-ins. Not to solve problems — to share the burden. Doherty found that patients with a climate companion reported significantly lower anxiety and higher resilience than those who processed alone.
