# Principles: Navigating Climate Emotions

## Principle 1: Climate Anxiety Is Rational, Not Pathological
The first thing Doherty establishes: feeling anxious about climate change is not a mental health disorder. It's a healthy response to a real threat. The problem isn't the anxiety — it's what we do with it. Suppressed anxiety leads to numbness. Unprocessed anxiety leads to paralysis. Processed anxiety becomes fuel for action.

## Principle 2: You Must Feel It to Heal It
Avoidance is the most common response to climate distress. We scroll past the news. We change the subject. We distract ourselves. But avoided feelings don't disappear — they accumulate. Doherty's approach is to create space for difficult feelings: grief for what's being lost, anger at those responsible, fear of what's coming. Processed feelings lose their power to control us.

> **Case: The Climate Refugee's Daughter** (Chapter 7, "Nature"): Doherty shares the story of a young woman whose family had been displaced by wildfires. She never talked about it — she "moved on." But the displaced grief showed up as anxiety attacks that seemed unrelated. Through therapy, she connected her current anxiety to the unprocessed loss of her childhood home. Naming it didn't erase the grief, but it stopped controlling her.
> **Key takeaway:** Unprocessed climate grief doesn't disappear — it resurfaces in other forms. Naming it is the first step to containing it.

## Principle 3: Connection Is the Antidote to Despair
Climate despair thrives in isolation. When you alone carry the weight of the world's problems, it crushes you. When you share that weight with others, it becomes bearable. Doherty emphasizes that community is not a nice-to-have in climate work — it's a psychological necessity.

## Principle 4: Your Values Are Your Compass
Climate anxiety often comes from a values conflict: you value the natural world, but your lifestyle contributes to its destruction. Resolving this conflict isn't about achieving perfect alignment — it's about moving in the direction of your values, one step at a time, without shame for the steps you haven't taken yet.

## Principle 5: Nature Heals
Reconnecting with the natural world is one of the most effective treatments for climate anxiety. Paradoxically, the very thing causing the distress is also the cure. Time in nature reduces cortisol, improves mood, and restores perspective. More importantly, it reminds you what you're fighting for.

> **Case: The Urban Planner** (Chapter 5, "Meaning"): A city planner working on climate adaptation found herself increasingly hopeless. The problems were too big, the solutions too slow. Doherty prescribed "two hours in a park, no phone, once a week." She resisted — "I don't have time for that." When she finally did it, she realized: "I forgot what I was trying to protect. The data had replaced the trees in my mind."
> **Key takeaway:** Nature connection is not optional for climate workers. It's the reminder of what you're fighting for. Don't let the abstraction of data replace the reality of the natural world.
