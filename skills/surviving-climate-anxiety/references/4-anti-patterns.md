# Anti-Patterns: What NOT to Do

## ❌ Doomscrolling and Information Overload
The most common trap: consuming ever more climate information, believing that knowledge alone will lead to action. It doesn't. Excessive consumption of bad news leads to paralysis, not mobilization. You can know everything about the climate crisis and do nothing. Information is not action.

## ❌ Guilt and Self-Blame
"I should be doing more." "My carbon footprint is too high." "I flew last year." Individual guilt is not productive. It consumes emotional energy without producing meaningful change. Doherty's approach: acknowledge your impact, but focus your energy on collective action and systemic change. Personal perfection is neither achievable nor necessary.

> **Case: The Guilty Recycler** (Chapter 2, "Feeling"): A patient was consumed by guilt about her recycling habits. She spent hours sorting waste, researching packaging, and feeling bad when she couldn't recycle something. Her carbon footprint from recycling was a fraction of her footprint from air travel, but she felt guilty about recycling and didn't think about flying at all. Doherty helped her redirect her guilt energy toward a more impactful action: advocating for better recycling infrastructure in her building.
> **Key takeaway:** Guilt is a compass, not a fuel source. Use it to identify where to act, then let it go. Don't marinate in it.

## ❌ Burnout from Over-Action
The other extreme: doing too much, too fast, without rest. The climate activist who attends every meeting, protests every weekend, and never rests isn't helping the cause — they're building toward a crash. Sustainable action means pacing yourself for the long haul.

## ❌ Isolation and Withdrawal
When climate anxiety becomes overwhelming, the instinct is to withdraw. Stop reading the news. Stop talking about it. Stop engaging. This provides short-term relief but long-term harm. Withdrawal leads to numbness, not peace. The goal is sustainable engagement, not disengagement.

## ❌ Toxic Positivity
"It'll be fine." "Technology will save us." "Don't worry, be happy." Forced optimism in the face of climate reality is not helpful. It invalidates legitimate fear and grief. What people need is permission to feel difficult emotions AND hope for the possibility of meaningful action — not dismissal of either.

> **Case: The Toxic Optimist** (Chapter 11, "Despair"): A patient described being told by a friend to "just think positive" about climate change. The advice made her feel worse — her fear was being dismissed. Doherty worked with her to develop "tragic optimism" — the ability to hold both the reality of loss and the possibility of meaning-making simultaneously. She could grieve AND act.
> **Key takeaway:** Neither despair nor forced positivity is the answer. The middle path is "tragic optimism" — acknowledging the worst while working toward the best.


## ❌ Ignoring Your Body
Climate anxiety is not just in your head — it lives in your body: tight chest, shallow breath, tense shoulders. Ignoring these physical symptoms makes them worse. The fix: body-based practices — yoga, walking, stretching, breathwork. Calm the body, and the mind follows.
