# Anti-Patterns: What the Industrial Age Taught Us Wrong

> Source: Icarus Deception, Parts One & Two

## Compliance Over Creativity

The industrial age trained us to follow instructions, keep our heads down, and fit in. This was valuable for the factory but is the opposite of what the connection economy rewards.

> **Case: The Resume Trap** (Part One): You were told to build a resume — collect credentials, punch tickets, get gold stars. But a resume is a record of compliance, not creativity. It proves you can follow instructions, not that you can make a difference.

> **Key takeaway**: Your resume proves you can follow rules. Your portfolio proves you can make art.

## Safety as the Goal

Seeking safety — the steady job, the predictable path, the known route — is the modern equivalent of Icarus flying too low. It seems safe but is actually the most dangerous choice.

> **Case: The Golden Handcuffs** (Part One): The high-paying job with great benefits that you hate. Godin argues this is the Icarus Deception's trap — you think you're being safe when you're actually drowning. The real risk is not the leap — it's staying.

## Perfectionism as an Excuse

Waiting until your work is perfect before sharing it is a form of hiding. Perfectionism is fear wearing a mask.

> **Case: The Unfinished Masterpiece** (Part Five): Artists who spend years polishing a single piece but never share it. Godin says: ship it. The world doesn't need perfect art — it needs YOUR art, even if rough.

> **Key takeaway**: "Better than perfect" is a lie. Ship. Share. Iterate.
