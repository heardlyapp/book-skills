# Anti-Patterns: What Went Wrong in Modern Education

## 1. Education Without a Telos (Purpose)

**The mistake**: Modern education has no agreed-upon purpose. Is it for workforce preparation? Social justice? Self-esteem? Test scores? Without a clear purpose, education becomes a series of competing fads.

**Fix**: Start with the question: "What kind of person do we want to graduate?" Then design everything backward from that answer.

## 2. The Myth of Neutrality

**The mistake**: Believing education can be "value-free" or "just the facts." Every curriculum teaches values — either intentionally or by default.

**Fix**: Acknowledge that all education is formative. The question is not "should schools teach values?" but "which values are they teaching?"

## 3. Replacing Virtue with Social Utility

**The mistake**: Measuring education only by economic outcomes — college admissions, job placement, salary. Losing the idea that education should make people better, not just richer.

**Fix**: Ask: "Is my child becoming wiser and more virtuous?" alongside "Is my child achieving?"

> **Case: The Progressive Heist** (Chapters 4-5): The book traces how John Dewey and the progressive movement transformed American education from classical formation to social engineering. Dewey explicitly rejected the classical model of education as "transmission of the past" and replaced it with education for social efficiency. The book calls this "the unauthorized takeover of American education."
> **Key takeaway**: The shift from classical to progressive education wasn't accidental — it was a deliberate philosophical change about what education is FOR.

> **Case: John Dewey's Vision** (Chapter 4): The book traces how John Dewey explicitly argued that education should not transmit the past but should prepare children for "social efficiency" in a changing democratic society. Dewey wrote that "the past is a great resource for the future" — but progressives who followed him used his ideas to discard the past entirely.
>
> **Key takeaway**: A philosophical shift in education has concrete consequences. Dewey's ideas, filtered through generations of education schools, changed what happens in classrooms every day.

## 4. The Loss of Wonder

**The mistake**: Standardized testing, data-driven instruction, and checklist curricula kill curiosity. Children learn to perform, not to wonder.

**Fix**: Protect unstructured time. Ask "what did you wonder about today?" instead of "what did you learn?" Model curiosity yourself.

## 5. Ignoring the Home Culture

**The mistake**: Parents abdicating educational responsibility to schools. Assuming that 6 hours of school can overcome 18 hours of home culture.

**Fix**: Recognize that the home is the primary educational environment. What you talk about at dinner, what you read, what you value — this forms your child more powerfully than any curriculum.
