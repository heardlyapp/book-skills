# Techniques: Practicing Classical Education at Home

## The Four Towers Checklist for Schools

When evaluating any school — public, private, or homeschool — ask these questions:

- **Reason**: Do students learn logic? Are they taught to identify fallacies? Do they read primary sources and learn to evaluate arguments?
- **Virtue**: Does the school talk about character? Is moral formation part of the curriculum? Are students praised for integrity, not just achievement?
- **Wonder**: Do students seem curious? Is there time for exploration? Does the curriculum inspire awe or just cover standards?
- **Beauty**: Is the environment beautiful? Is art, music, and poetry valued? Are students taught to appreciate excellence?

## The Trivium at Home: Age-Appropriate Practices

### Grammar Stage (ages 5-10)
- Read aloud from the best books (myths, fairy tales, Bible stories, classic children's literature)
- Memorize and recite poetry, scripture, and historical speeches
- Practice Latin or another language through song and chant
- Explore nature with wonder — describe, draw, name what you see

### Logic Stage (ages 11-14)
- Introduce formal logic — the Socratic method is your best tool
- Discuss the "why" behind everything: "Why is this true? How do we know?"
- Debate current events using the classical framework
- Read primary sources, not just textbooks

### Rhetoric Stage (ages 15-18)
- Write and deliver persuasive speeches
- Defend a thesis in formal debate
- Study the Great Books — read, discuss, and write about them
- Practice finding your own voice while respecting the tradition

## The Socratic Method at the Dinner Table

"The Socratic method is not about winning arguments — it's about pursuing truth." The book recommends:

1. Ask open-ended questions: "What do you think?" "Why?"
2. Follow up: "What evidence do you have?" "What would someone who disagrees say?"
3. Don't rush to give the answer — let the child discover it through reasoning
4. Model intellectual humility: "I don't know — let's find out together"

## Building a Paideia-Rich Home

- Fill your home with good books, art, and music
- Have regular family discussions about ideas, not just logistics
- Celebrate learning as a family value
- Limit screens — they are the enemy of wonder
- Cultivate silence and solitude — the conditions for deep thought

> **Case: The Socratic Classroom** (Chapter 10): A teacher asks a classroom of 12-year-olds: "Was Brutus right to betray Caesar?" Students must take a position, defend it, and respond to counterarguments. This is the Logic stage in action — not waiting until high school to teach critical thinking.
>
> **Key takeaway**: Critical thinking is not a skill reserved for advanced students. The Logic stage (ages 11-14) is the ideal time to introduce reasoned debate.

> **Case: The Memory Recitation** (Chapter 10): In a classical first-grade classroom, a 7-year-old stands and recites a memorized poem from memory. This isn't test prep — it's training the mind to absorb beautiful language. The Grammar stage is the "sponge" stage, and classical education fills it with the best content.
