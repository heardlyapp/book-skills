# Principles for Creators

## I. Quantity Leads to Quality

The most important principle in the book. Make more work, and the work will get better.

**Case: The Pottery Class.** Students graded on quantity produced more and better pots than students graded on quality. The quantity students learned from their mistakes. The quality students were afraid to make mistakes.

**Case: The Photographer's Contact Sheet.** A photographer's best images emerge from many attempts. The first 20 shots are warm-up. The 100th shot is where the magic happens.

## II. The Gap Is Permanent

The difference between what you envision and what you can execute is not a problem to be solved. It is the condition of being an artist.

**Case: The Disappointed Painter.** Every painter has felt the disappointment of finishing a painting and seeing that it does not match the vision. The authors: this is not failure. It is the engine of growth.

**Case: The Writer's First Draft.** Every writer knows that the first draft is terrible. The authors: the first draft is supposed to be terrible. The purpose of the first draft is to exist.

## III. External Validation Is Unreliable

The opinions of others — critics, awards, sales — are not a reliable guide to the value of your work.

**Case: The Van Gogh Story.** Van Gogh sold one painting in his lifetime. The authors: if external validation mattered, Van Gogh would have stopped painting.

**Case: The Rejection Letter.** Every artist has received rejection. The authors: rejections are not judgments on your work. They are evidence that you are doing the work.
