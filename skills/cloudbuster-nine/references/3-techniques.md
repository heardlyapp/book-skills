# The Navy Pre-Flight Training

> Source: Cloudbuster Nine, Parts III, IV

## The Training Regimen

### A Day in Pre-Flight

| Time | Activity |
|------|----------|
| 5:00 AM | Reveille, physical training |
| 6:00 | Obstacle course |
| 8:00 | Academic classes (navigation, engineering) |
| 12:00 | Lunch |
| 2:00 | Flight training or sports |
| Evening | Study, drill |
| 9:00 | Lights out |

> **Case: The Right Stuff Culture (Part IV)**
> The Pre-Flight School cultivated what Tom Wolfe later called "the right stuff" — the mentality of elite performers. Trainees were pushed to their limits physically and mentally. Those who could not handle it were washed out. The program was designed to identify who had the right stuff — and who did not. Baseball players like Ted Williams had already proven they could perform under pressure. The pre-flight program tested whether they could do it in a cockpit.
> **Key takeaway:** The right stuff is not about talent — it is about character. The pre-flight program tested: can you keep going when you are exhausted? Can you think clearly under stress? Can you function as part of a team? These are the same qualities that make a great athlete — and a great pilot.

> **Case: The Survival Training (Part IV)**
> Trainees learned how to survive if shot down: parachute landing, jungle survival, water survival, evasion and escape. They were dropped in the North Carolina woods with minimal gear and told to find their way back. They learned to read maps, find water, and avoid capture. This was serious preparation for the reality of war — not just training for parades.
> **Key takeaway:** The survival training shows that the Pre-Flight program was not a game. The men who went through it were being prepared for the worst. Baseball was a small part of a comprehensive training program designed to keep them alive in combat.
