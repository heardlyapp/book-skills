# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *The Demon-Haunted World* by Carl Sagan — Chapters 10-14.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The Baloney Detection Audit

**When evaluating any claim, run the 9 tools:**
1. Can it be independently confirmed?
2. Is debate encouraged or suppressed?
3. Who is the authority, and why should we trust them?
4. What alternative explanations exist?
5. Are you attached to one explanation?
6. Can the claim be quantified?
7. Is every link in the argument solid?
8. Is the simpler explanation being ignored?
9. Can the claim be falsified?

### 2. The Dragon Test

**When someone makes an untestable claim:** "What evidence would convince you that you're wrong?" If the answer is "nothing," you are dealing with faith, not science. You can respect the faith without accepting it as evidence.

### 3. The New Age Exercise

**When someone offers an anecdote as proof:** "Anecdotes are unverifiable. They are frequently offered as evidence, but they are almost impossible to check. No matter how sincere or certain someone is, anecdotes alone do not constitute evidence."

> **Case: The Psychic Cold-Reading (Chapter 4)**: Cold readers make vague, high-probability statements that apply to almost anyone: "You have a lost loved one who wants to communicate." "You are under stress at work." "You have a health concern that worries you." These work because people remember the hits and forget the misses. Controlled experiments show that cold reading works no better than chance.
> **Key takeaway**: Vague, probable statements are not evidence of supernatural power.

> **Case: Mr. Buckley's Education** (Chapter 1): Sagan's cab driver was intelligent, curious, and scientifically illiterate. He knew all about Atlantis, channeling, and the prophecies of Nostradamus — but nothing about DNA, quantum mechanics, or the raising of the Himalayas. "Our cultural motifs, our educational system, our communications media had failed this man." The information he was fed was pseudoscience. No one had ever taught him how to distinguish it from real science.
> **Key takeaway**: The reason people believe pseudoscience is not stupidity — it's that they have never been taught how to tell the difference. Education is the primary prevention.

### 4. The "It Could Be True" Reframe

**When someone says "It could be true, you can't prove it's not":** "Yes, it could be true. But many things 'could be true.' Without evidence, we have no reason to believe one over another. I could claim there is a china teapot in orbit around Mars. It 'could' be true. But do you believe me?"

### 5. The Error Bar Check

**When evaluating any statistical claim:** "What is the margin of error? How confident are the researchers? Has this been replicated?" Any claim presented without an estimate of its uncertainty is incomplete.
