# 4 — Anti-Patterns

> Source: *The Demon-Haunted World* by Carl Sagan — Chapters 1, 7, 12, 13.

## Common Errors

### 1. The "It Works for Me" Fallacy

Anecdotes are not data. Personal testimony is the weakest form of evidence. "Miracles are attested, but what if they're instead some mix of charlatanry, unfamiliar states of consciousness, and mental illness?"

> **Case: The Touched Photograph** (Chapter 5): The faithful bring Sagan a photograph that "proves" the Virgin Mary appeared. Sagan asks: where's the negative? The photographs were taken by strangers who sold them. The more one investigates, the more prosaic the explanation becomes.
> **Key takeaway**: An impressive story is never as strong as verifiable evidence.

### 2. The "Argument from Ignorance" Trap

"Science can't explain this, so it must be supernatural." Gaps in our knowledge do not constitute evidence for any particular explanation. "A God of the Gaps is assigned responsibility for what we do not yet understand."

### 3. The Conspiracy Shield

When evidence contradicts a claim, the pseudoscientist shifts to: "They're suppressing the truth." This makes the claim unfalsifiable. Sagan's response: "I reject the notion that science is by its nature secretive."

> **Case: The NASA Cover-Up Accusation** (Chapter 3): When Mars Observer failed before entering Mars orbit, conspiracy theorists claimed NASA faked the failure to study the "Face" without releasing images to the public. No evidence. No mechanism. Just conspiracy. "There was even a handful of pickets outside the gates of the Jet Propulsion Laboratory."
> **Key takeaway**: Conspiracy is the last refuge of the pseudoscientist. Real science makes its data available.

### 4. The "You Can't Prove It Wrong" Dodge

Claims that cannot be tested are not scientific. "The hypothesis that is not falsifiable is not a scientific hypothesis at all."

### 5. The "Because I Want It to Be True" Error

We believe what we want to believe. "Pseudoscience speaks to powerful emotional needs that science often leaves unfulfilled." The antidote is intellectual honesty about our own biases.
