# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *The Demon-Haunted World* by Carl Sagan — Chapters 1, 2, 7, 10, 12.

## The Three Frameworks

### Framework A: The Baloney Detection Kit (Chapter 12)

Sagan's most celebrated contribution to critical thinking: nine tools for distinguishing science from pseudoscience.

1. **Independent confirmation** — "Whenever possible, there must be independent confirmation of the facts."
2. **Encourage debate** — "Encourage substantive debate on the evidence by knowledgeable proponents of all points of view."
3. **Authority is not evidence** — "Arguments from authority carry little weight — authorities have made too many mistakes."
4. **Spin more than one hypothesis** — If you have only one explanation for a phenomenon, you are inevitably committed to it.
5. **Don't get attached to your hypothesis** — "When you become attached to a hypothesis, it's very hard to give it up, even when contradictory evidence arrives."
6. **Quantify** — "If you can't measure it, you can't evaluate it."
7. **The chain must be complete** — "Every link in the chain of argument must work — if there is one weak link, the argument fails."
8. **Ockham's Razor** — "If there are two explanations that explain the data equally well, choose the simpler one."
9. **Falsifiability** — "If the hypothesis can be tested and potentially shown to be false, is it falsifiable?"

> **Case: The Invisible Dragon** (Chapter 10): Sagan imagines someone claiming there is a dragon in his garage. When you go to look: "It's an invisible dragon." When you try to feel it: "It's incorporeal." When you sprinkle flour to find footprints: "It floats." Each attempt to test the claim is met with a revision that makes the claim untestable. Sagan's question: "What's the difference between an invisible, incorporeal, floating dragon and no dragon at all?" The claim that cannot be falsified is not science.
> **Key takeaway**: When a claim adapts to every attempt to disprove it, it has ceased to be a testable hypothesis. It has become an article of faith.

### Framework B: Science vs. Pseudoscience (Chapters 1, 2, 7)

The core distinction: science is open to falsification and self-correcting. Pseudoscience avoids falsification, is secretive and defensive, and claims conspiracy when challenged.

| Feature | Science | Pseudoscience |
|---|---|---|
| Falsifiability | Actively seeks to test hypotheses | Avoids tests that could disprove |
| Peer review | Open to expert criticism | Claims conspiracy when criticized |
| Error bars | Acknowledges uncertainty | Presents certainty |
| Evidence | Demands repeatable, public evidence | Relies on anecdote and testimony |
| Progress | Builds and corrects over time | Stays stuck, repeats same claims |

> **Case: The Face on Mars** (Chapter 3): The Cydonia "face" on Mars was widely touted as evidence of an ancient Martian civilization. Upon closer examination with higher-resolution images, it was clearly a natural mesa — a complex geological formation shaped by wind and mudflows. The face was a trick of light, shadow, and our hard-wired tendency to see human faces in random patterns. The pseudoscientific interpretation failed because it was not falsifiable — it could always be defended by claiming "NASA is covering up the truth."
> **Key takeaway**: When a claim is protected by conspiracy theories (the evidence against it is "suppressed"), it has left the realm of science. True scientific claims welcome scrutiny.

### Framework C: The Marriage of Skepticism and Wonder (Preface, Chapter 2)

Sagan's unique contribution was to show that science is not cold reason — it is also the source of profound spiritual wonder. The very act of understanding the universe is itself a form of awe.

> **Case: The Starstuff Insight** (Preface, Chapter 1): Sagan's most famous idea: "Except for hydrogen, all the atoms that make each of us up — the iron in our blood, the calcium in our bones, the carbon in our brains — were manufactured in red giant stars thousands of light-years away in space and billions of years ago in time. We are starstuff."
> **Key takeaway**: The most profound awe comes from understanding reality as it actually is, not from the comforting stories we tell ourselves about it.
