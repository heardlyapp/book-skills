# Core Framework: Purpose Is Everything

> Source: The Art of Gathering, Introduction and Chapter 1

## The Purpose Problem

Most gatherings fail before they start because the host hasn't defined a clear, specific purpose. "Let's get together" is not a purpose — it's a placeholder.

> **Case: The Category Confusion** (Chapter 1): Parker distinguishes "category" (dinner party, meeting, wedding) from "purpose." The category is just the form. The purpose is the specific reason. A dinner party's category is "dinner" — but its purpose might be "to welcome a new neighbor to the building."

> **Key takeaway**: Never confuse category with purpose. Category tells you the form; purpose tells you why it matters.

## A Disputable Purpose

The best purposes are disputable — someone could disagree. If your purpose is so bland no one could argue, it doesn't work.

> **Case: The Company Offsite** (Chapter 1): "Team building" is generic. "We need to figure out why our best people keep leaving" is specific, urgent, and disputable. These two purposes produce completely different gatherings — different guest lists, agendas, formats, and outcomes.

> **Case: The Birthday Party** (Chapter 1): "Celebrate my birthday" is basic. "Express gratitude to the people who shaped this year" produces a different experience entirely.

> **Key takeaway**: A bold, specific, disputable purpose is the foundation of every great gathering. It guides everything.

## Displacement Activity (Category Is)

Parker's tactic of having guests do something together that's different from the gathering's main purpose — cooking before a business meeting, for example.
