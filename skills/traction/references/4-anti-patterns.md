## Anti-Pattern 1: The Vision in Your Head

The most common anti-pattern: the leader has a clear vision in their own mind but has never written it down or shared it. They assume everyone knows where the company is going. Nobody does. The result: misaligned priorities, wasted energy, and frustration on all sides.

> **Case: The Six Different Visions** (Chapter 2): A CEO asked his six direct reports to independently write down the company's core values and core focus. He got six different answers. One person thought the core focus was "growth." Another thought it was "profitability." Another thought it was "customer satisfaction." All were wrong — because the CEO had never explicitly stated it.
> **Key takeaway**: If it's in your head, it doesn't exist. Leadership is the discipline of making the invisible visible.

## Anti-Pattern 2: The Wrong Person in the Wrong Seat

Companies tolerate misalignment because it's painful to admit. A great person who's in the wrong role costs the company more than a weak person in the right role. They're doing work they're not suited for, creating frustration for themselves and everyone around them.

> **Case: The Miserable CFO** (Chapter 4): A CFO was technically brilliant — had the numbers down cold. But he hated managing people, and his team was a mess. The People Analyzer revealed that he had high "Gets It" and "Capacity" but low "Wants It" for the management part. He was in the wrong seat. They restructured to let him focus on analysis and hired a controller for the team management. He became happy and productive.
> **Key takeaway**: The right person in the wrong seat is still a wrong seat problem. Find the seat that fits them — or help them find a new company.

## Anti-Pattern 3: Opinion-Based Performance Reviews

Without data, performance conversations become personal. "I think you're doing a good job" vs. "I think you're not" — these are opinions, not assessments. The Scorecard removes opinion from the equation.

> **Case: The Sales Manager Debate** (Chapter 5): A leadership team spent 30 minutes arguing about whether the sales manager was performing well. One member thought she was great. Another thought she was failing. They had no data. When they finally put a Scorecard together, the numbers showed she was above target on revenue but below on new customer acquisition. The data turned a 30-minute argument into a 5-minute problem-solving session.
> **Key takeaway**: When people argue about performance, they're not arguing about facts — they're arguing about perceptions. Data ends the argument.

## Anti-Pattern 4: The Same Problem, Different Meeting

Many companies spend months (or years) discussing the same issue without solving it. The issue gets raised, everyone has an opinion, someone says "we should fix that," and the meeting ends. Next meeting: same issue. The IDS process breaks this cycle.

> **Case: The Customer Service Black Hole** (Chapter 6): A company had the same issue on the Issues List for 14 months: "Customer response time is too slow." Every monthly meeting, they discussed it. Every month, no solution. Using IDS, they identified the root cause in 15 minutes (no standard SLA, no escalation process). They implemented a solution in one week. The issue disappeared.
> **Key takeaway**: An unsolved issue is an accepted issue. If you've discussed it three times without solving it, you've decided to live with it.

## Anti-Pattern 5: The Annual Panic Cycle

Companies that don't use quarterly rocks tend to operate in an annual panic cycle: January planning (ambitious), March (already behind), June (panic), September (desperation), December (white-knuckle finish). Then repeat. Quarterly rocks create a cadence of realistic progress.

> **Case: The December Sprint** (Chapter 9): A company consistently hit 70% of annual goals by November, then frantic December sprint. Every year, the same pattern. When they switched to quarterly rocks, they realized the problem: annual goals were too abstract. By the third quarter, they had forgotten the annual plan. With quarterly rocks, each 90 days had clear priorities. They hit 90% of annual goals by October, without a fire drill.
> **Key takeaway**: The annual plan is the direction. Quarterly rocks are the engine. If you only look at the plan once a year, you'll drift.

## Anti-Pattern 6: The Indispensable Founder

The business that depends on the founder to make all decisions has an artificial ceiling — the founder's time and attention. This is the most expensive anti-pattern because it prevents the business from ever becoming an asset.

> **Case: The 24/7 CEO** (Chapter 12): A founder was receiving an average of 50 decision requests per day. Every question came to him. He couldn't sleep without checking email. His team was competent but had been trained that the founder made all decisions. The Accountability Chart and Level 10 meetings reversed this. Decision rights were defined. The founder's inbox went from 50 requests per day to 5. The business grew 30% in the next year.
> **Key takeaway**: If you are the bottleneck, the business has a growth ceiling at your personal capacity. The only way to grow the business is to grow past yourself — by putting systems in place.
