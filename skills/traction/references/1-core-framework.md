## The Entrepreneurial Operating System (EOS)

### Component 1: Vision

The foundation of EOS. If the leadership team doesn't share a clear, simple vision, the organization has no direction. The V/TO (Vision/Traction Organizer) is a one-page document that answers eight questions:

1. What are your core values? (the timeless principles that define your culture)
2. What is your core focus? (what you do and don't do — your niche)
3. What is your 10-year target? (a BHAG that inspires)
4. What is your marketing strategy? (the promise you make to customers)
5. What is your 3-year picture? (what the business looks like at that milestone)
6. What is your 1-year plan? (the annual priorities, measurables, and budget)
7. What are your quarterly rocks? (3-7 most important priorities for the next 90 days)
8. What are your issues? (the obstacles between now and the vision)

> **Case: The Vision/Traction Organizer** (Chapters 1-2): Wickman introduces the V/TO as the central tool. Most leadership teams lack clarity on even the first question (core values). They've never had the conversation. The V/TO forces it. When one manufacturing company finally defined its core values, it realized it had been hiring people who violated those values for years. Within six months, the culture had transformed.
> **Key takeaway**: If you can't write your vision on one page, you don't have a vision yet.

### Component 2: People

"Get the right people in the right seats." The right people share the company's core values. The right seat matches their strengths to the role. The People Analyzer tool rates every person on: Gets It (do they understand the role?), Wants It (do they want the role?), Capacity (do they have the capacity to grow into the role?), and Core Values fit.

> **Case: The People Analyzer** (Chapter 4): Wickman describes a CEO who used the People Analyzer and realized his VP of Sales had been in the wrong seat for three years. The VP was a great person (shared core values), but didn't have the capacity for strategic selling. The CEO moved him to an account management role and hired a hunter. Revenue grew 40% that year. The VP was happier too.
> **Key takeaway**: The wrong person in the wrong seat wastes everyone's time. Wrong person right seat = fix the seat. Right person wrong seat = fix the seat or move them.

### Component 3: Data

A business without data is flying blind. The Scorecard tracks 5-15 leading indicators (not lagging financial reports) every week. Every leadership team member has a number they own. Red/yellow/green status makes performance visible at a glance.

> **Case: The Scorecard Meeting** (Chapter 5): Wickman shows how a weekly 15-minute review of the Scorecard replaces hours of debate. When a CEO saw that "new customer acquisition" had been red for six weeks, the issue was finally discussed — not as a blame session, but as a problem to solve.
> **Key takeaway**: The Scorecard replaces opinion with data. When the numbers are visible, the problems become obvious.

### Component 4: Issues

The IDS process (Identify → Discuss → Solve) is the mechanism for dealing with problems. Any issue is brought to the weekly meeting. It gets ten minutes of IDS time. If it can't be solved in ten minutes, it gets assigned to a team for further work. The key: an issue is not solved until a solution is implemented with a due date.

> **Case: The Issues List** (Chapter 6): Wickman advises every leadership team to keep a running Issues List. In one company, the same "customer service response time" issue had been discussed in every meeting for a year. Using IDS, they solved it in 20 minutes: implement a standard SLA, assign a backup person, measure it weekly.
> **Key takeaway**: Most issues don't get solved because they're discussed but never assigned. IDS forces closure.

### Component 5: Process

Document your core processes. If you can't document them, you can't systematize them. EOS identifies the 6-10 core processes that make or break the business. Document them at the 80% level — good enough to be repeatable, not perfect.

> **Case: Core Processes** (Chapter 7): Wickman tells the story of a service company whose quality was inconsistent. They documented their six core processes (sales, onboarding, service delivery, billing, support, hiring). Every new employee followed the same system. Quality became predictable. The owner was finally able to take a vacation.
> **Key takeaway**: Document processes at 80%. Perfection is the enemy of consistency.

### Component 6: Traction

The execution component. Rocks (3-7 quarterly priorities, 90-day sprints) create accountability. The Level 10 weekly meeting creates the rhythm. Annual planning sessions set the trajectory. Quarterly pivots adjust the course.

> **Case: The Level 10 Meeting** (Chapter 8): Most management meetings are a waste of time. The Level 10 agenda (Segue → Scorecard → Rock Review → Headlines → IDS → Conclude) transforms them. The first 60 minutes are tightly structured. The last 30 minutes are deep IDS work. One CEO said: "Before Level 10, our meetings were two hours of frustration. Now they're 90 minutes of progress."
> **Key takeaway**: The meeting structure IS the management system. If the meeting is broken, the business is broken.
