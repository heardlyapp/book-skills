## 1. The V/TO (Vision/Traction Organizer)

**The tool**: A one-page template with 8 questions. Print it, fill it out with your leadership team, and review it quarterly.

**How to do it**: Schedule an annual planning session (offsite, 1-2 days). Go through each question in order. Core Values first — they guide everything else. Don't move past a question until the team agrees.

> **Case: The Core Values Debate** (Chapter 2): One leadership team spent 6 hours debating core values. They couldn't agree on three words. The debate revealed a fundamental disagreement about the company's identity. Rather than rushing, they resolved it. Six months later, they said it was the most important conversation they'd ever had.
> **Key takeaway**: The V/TO is not a form to fill out. It's a conversation to have. The value is in the debate, not the document.

## 2. The People Analyzer

**The tool**: A simple matrix: list every person in the organization. Rate them on Gets It (1-3), Wants It (1-3), Capacity (1-3), and Core Values (check/cross). Total score. Anyone with a cross in Core Values is the wrong person — regardless of score.

**How to do it**: Leadership team rates each person together. Don't do it alone. The group conversation is essential. Be honest. People Analyzer reveals what you already know but haven't said.

> **Case: The Uncomfortable Rating** (Chapter 4): A CEO had to rate his COO — a longtime friend — as "doesn't get it" and "lack of capacity." It was painful. But keeping the COO in the wrong seat was hurting the company and the COO's confidence. They moved him to a role that fit his strengths. Both the company and the friendship survived.
> **Key takeaway**: The People Analyzer does not tell you to fire people. It tells you to put them in the right seat or help them find a new company.

## 3. The Level 10 Meeting Agenda

**The tool**: A 90-minute weekly meeting with a fixed agenda:

| Time | Section | What happens |
|------|---------|-------------|
| 5 min | Segue | Good news from each person — builds connection |
| 5 min | Scorecard | Review 5-15 numbers. Red/yellow/green. 15 seconds each. |
| 5 min | Rock Review | Each person reports: On track or off track? |
| 5 min | Headlines | Major news, cascading info. Announcements only. |
| 60 min | IDS | Identify-Discuss-Solve. 10 min per issue. |
| 10 min | Conclude | Recap to-dos. Rate meeting 1-10. What to improve. |

> **Case: The 90-Minute Miracle** (Chapter 8): One CEO said: "We used to have two-hour meetings that decided nothing. Now we have 90-minute meetings that decide everything. The Segue alone — just sharing good news for five minutes — changed the culture. We started liking each other more."
> **Key takeaway**: The structure is the solution. Don't skip any section. Each one serves a purpose.

## 4. The Rock Setting Process

**The tool**: Each quarter, every leadership team member sets 3-7 Rocks. A Rock is a 90-day priority that, if achieved, moves the company meaningfully toward its annual goals. Rocks are SMART: Specific, Measurable, Assignable, Realistic, Time-related.

**How to do it**: Annual plan → break into 4 quarterly groups → each person picks 3-7 rocks for their function → team reviews and commits → check weekly in the Level 10 meeting.

> **Case: The 90-Day Toggle** (Chapter 9): A company had a 10-year target but no idea how to get there. They broke it into 90-day chunks. Each quarter, they asked: "What three rocks would most move us toward our 10-year target?" After 3 years of quarterly rocks, they were 70% of the way to the 10-year target.
> **Key takeaway**: A 10-year goal is a direction, not a plan. Quarterly rocks are the plan. Stack enough quarters, and you reach the goal.

## 5. The Delegate and Elevate Exercise

**The tool**: List your 10 most important activities. Rank them by importance. For each one, ask: "Can someone else do this?" If yes, delegate it. If no, that's your core value-add — elevate it (spend more time there).

**How to do it**: Write your list. Be brutally honest. If you're doing tasks that someone else could do, you're not leading — you're doing. Delegate them this week.

> **Case: The CEO Who Loved Operations** (Chapter 10): A CEO loved diving into operational details — it's where she felt most useful. But her team was waiting for decisions she should have been making. The Delegate and Elevate exercise showed her that 80% of her time was spent on tasks that a good COO could handle. She hired a COO and spent her time on strategy and fundraising. Revenue doubled.
> **Key takeaway**: If you're the best person in the company at everything, you've failed at building a team. Your job is to find people better than you at most things.
