## Principle 1: Clarity Is Everything

The single biggest cause of business failure is not competition or market — it's confusion. Leadership teams don't share a common understanding of where the company is going. Employees don't know what's expected. Wickman's solution: write every part of the vision down. The V/TO makes it impossible to be confused.

> **Case: The Invisible Vision** (Chapters 1-2): Wickman describes a CEO who thought everyone knew the vision. When he asked his team to write down the company's core values independently, he got six different answers. The vision was in his head, not in the organization. The V/TO surfaced the gaps and created alignment. The CEO said: "I thought we were aligned. We were aligned to six different visions."
> **Key takeaway**: If you haven't written it down, your team doesn't know it. Vision is not what you think — it's what you've documented.

## Principle 2: Right People First, Strategy Second

The right people who share your core values will figure out the strategy. The wrong people, no matter how talented, will undermine the culture. Wickman's rule: hire slow, fire fast. Use the People Analyzer. Don't compromise on values.

> **Case: The Toxic Sales Star** (Chapter 4): A company had a top salesperson who consistently violated core values — he lied to customers, bullied colleagues, and blamed others. Leadership hesitated because he produced 30% of revenue. When they finally fired him, revenue dipped briefly, then grew 50% because the culture improved and retention increased.
> **Key takeaway**: A high performer who violates core values is destroying more value than they create. Culture is what you tolerate.

## Principle 3: Data Eliminates Drama

When performance conversations are based on opinion, they become personal and emotional. When they're based on objective data (the Scorecard), they become productive. The Scorecard gives the team a shared reality.

> **Case: The Firefighting CEO** (Chapter 5): A CEO spent 80% of his time putting out fires. He had no data on where the fires were coming from. When he implemented a Scorecard tracking 10 leading indicators, he discovered that 60% of his fires came from one department that was understaffed. The solution: hire two more people for that department. Firefighting dropped by 70%.
> **Key takeaway**: If you're always fighting fires, you're not managing data. The Scorecard is the smoke detector.

## Principle 4: IDS Until It's Solved

The most common problem in business: issues get discussed but never solved. The IDS process forces closure. Identify the real issue. Discuss it with all perspectives. Solve it with a specific action, owner, and due date.

> **Case: The Recurring Customer Complaint** (Chapter 6): A company had received 47 complaints about a specific billing error over six months. Each month, the team discussed it. Each month, it wasn't solved. Using IDS, they identified the root cause: a manual data entry step that could be automated. The fix took two hours and the complaints stopped.
> **Key takeaway**: If a problem keeps coming back, it hasn't been solved — it's only been discussed. IDS forces the decision.

## Principle 5: 90-Day Sprints Create Traction

Annual goals are too abstract. Daily tasks are too tactical. The 90-day rock is the sweet spot: long enough to accomplish something meaningful, short enough to maintain focus. Each person commits to 3-7 rocks per quarter. At the end of 90 days, you either did them or you didn't.

> **Case: The Quarterly Pivot** (Chapter 9): A software company was 18 months behind on a major product launch. The team was overwhelmed. They broke the launch into 90-day rocks. The first rock: ship the minimum viable product. The second rock: get 10 paying customers. The third rock: raise prices. Within 9 months, they were profitable. The annual panic was replaced by quarterly momentum.
> **Key takeaway**: A year is too long to focus. A week is too short. 90 days is the execution sweet spot.

## Principle 6: The Meeting Is the Operating System

Most leadership teams hate their meetings. That's not a meeting problem — it's a management problem. The Level 10 meeting is the EOS operating system in action. If the meeting is working, the business is working.

> **Case: The Meeting Transformation** (Chapter 8): A leadership team that had been meeting for three hours every Monday (and hating it) switched to the Level 10 format. First week: they covered everything in 90 minutes. By the fourth week: they were solving issues that had been pending for months. One member said: "I didn't know a meeting could feel productive."
> **Key takeaway**: Bad meetings are not inevitable. The agenda is the operating system. Change the agenda, change the meeting.

## Principle 7: The Business Must Run Without the Owner

If the business depends on the owner to make every decision, the business has no value. EOS is designed to create a self-managing organization. The Accountability Chart defines who owns what. The meeting rhythm creates accountability. The scorecard provides visibility.

> **Case: The Owner Who Couldn't Let Go** (Chapter 12): A founder had built a $10M company but couldn't take a week off without the business stalling. His team wouldn't make decisions without him. EOS gave him the Accountability Chart, the Level 10 meetings, and the Scorecard. Within six months, he took his first two-week vacation. The team didn't call him once.
> **Key takeaway**: If you can't leave for two weeks, you don't own a business — it owns you.
