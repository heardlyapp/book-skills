## 6 Anti-Patterns of Nutritional Confusion

### 1. "It's All About Willpower"

Willpower is not the issue — information is. Most people do not know which foods are most protective. The Daily Dozen removes the guesswork.

### 2. "Healthy Food Is Expensive"

Beans, rice, oats, and seasonal vegetables are among the cheapest foods available. The most expensive items in the average grocery cart are meat, cheese, and processed foods.

> **Case: The Cost Comparison** (Introduction): Greger compares the cost of a plant-based diet to a standard American diet. Beans at $1-2 per can provide more protein per dollar than beef at $5-8 per pound. A plant-based diet is actually cheaper.
> **Key takeaway**: The "expensive health food" myth is maintained by the marketing departments of processed food companies, not by the data.

### 3. "Protein Is the Priority"

Most Americans get far more protein than they need. Excess protein — especially animal protein — is linked to increased risk of chronic disease. Plant protein from beans and grains provides ample protein without the risks.

### 4. "All Fats Are Bad"

Healthy fats from nuts, seeds, and avocados are essential. The problem is not fat — it is the type of fat. Saturated and trans fats (from animal products and processed foods) are harmful; unsaturated fats (from plants) are beneficial.

### 5. "You Need Meat for Iron"

Plant sources of iron (beans, lentils, spinach, fortified grains) provide adequate iron. The absorption of plant iron is enhanced by consuming vitamin C (citrus, bell peppers) with iron-rich meals.

### 6. "Cooking Destroys Nutrients"

Cooking can increase the bioavailability of some nutrients (lycopene in tomatoes, beta-carotene in carrots) while reducing others. The solution: eat a mix of raw and cooked vegetables.

> **Case: The Mediterranean Paradox** (Introduction): Greger notes that the Mediterranean diet is often cited as proof that olive oil is healthy. But the most protective version of the Mediterranean diet (the one studied in clinical trials) includes ample vegetables, legumes, whole grains — and limited amounts of olive oil. The benefits come from the plants, not the oil.
> **Key takeaway**: Even healthy-sounding dietary patterns must be examined critically. The Mediterranean diet works because of the plants, not despite them.
