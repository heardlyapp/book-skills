## 7 Principles of Plant-Based Nutrition

### 1. Add, Don't Remove

Greger's approach is additive: focus on adding healthy foods rather than removing unhealthy ones. When you fill up on plants, there is less room for processed foods.

### 2. Variety Is Medicine

Different plants contain different protective compounds. Eating a rainbow of vegetables ensures you get a broad range of phytochemicals.

### 3. Beans Are the Foundation

Beans are the single most health-promoting food group. They are high in protein, fiber, and resistant starch. They improve cholesterol, blood sugar, and gut health.

> **Case: The Bean Study** (Introduction): Greger cites research showing that people who eat beans live significantly longer than those who don't. For every 20g of legumes consumed daily (about 3/4 cup of cooked beans), the risk of premature death decreases by approximately 8%.
> **Key takeaway**: Beans are not just a side dish — they should be a daily staple. They are the most cost-effective health food available.

### 4. Flaxseeds Are a Superfood

One tablespoon of ground flaxseed daily provides omega-3s (ALA), fiber, and lignans — a compound that may reduce cancer risk. Flax must be ground to be digested.

### 5. Cruciferous Vegetables Are Critical

Broccoli, kale, cabbage, Brussels sprouts — these vegetables contain sulforaphane, which has been shown to activate detoxification enzymes and potentially slow cancer growth.

### 6. Berries for the Brain

Berries (especially blueberries and strawberries) are rich in flavonoids that protect cognitive function and may delay age-related memory decline.

### 7. The Supplement Is B12

B12 is the only nutrient that cannot be obtained adequately from a varied plant-based diet. A supplement is essential. Greger recommends 2000 mcg once per week.

> **Case: The Turmeric and Black Pepper** (Spices section): Greger explains that turmeric is a powerful anti-inflammatory — but it requires black pepper to be absorbed. The piperine in black pepper increases curcumin absorption by 2000%. This is why so many turmeric recipes include black pepper.
> **Key takeaway**: The science of nutrition is not just about which foods to eat — it is also about how to combine them for maximum benefit.
