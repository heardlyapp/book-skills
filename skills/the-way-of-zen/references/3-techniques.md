# Practical Zen — Techniques

Watts' book is theoretical but his subject is deeply practical. These are the techniques of Zen as described in The Way of Zen.

## Technique 1: Zazen — Just Sitting

The core practice of Zen. Zazen is not concentration, not contemplation, not trance. It is simply sitting — fully present, aware, without trying to achieve anything.

**How to do it:** Sit in a comfortable but upright posture. Focus on the breath — not by controlling it, but by feeling it. When thoughts arise, do not fight them. Do not follow them. Let them come and go like clouds passing through the sky. Just sit.

**Case: The Soto School.** The Soto school of Zen, founded by Dogen, teaches shikantaza — "just sitting." No koan. No object of concentration. Just sitting. Dogen said: "To study Zen is to study the self. To study the self is to forget the self. To forget the self is to be enlightened by all things."

**Case: The Bamboo Analogy.** To sit in zazen is to be like a bamboo — empty inside, flexible, rooted, and open to the wind. The bamboo does nothing, yet it grows and bends and sways. It is not trying to be a bamboo. It just is.

## Technique 2: The Koan — Breaking the Mind Open

A koan is a question that cannot be answered by logic. Its purpose is not to produce an answer but to exhaust the intellect, creating an opening for direct realization.

**How to do it:** Take a koan. Sit with it. Do not try to solve it. Do not try to understand it. Let it gnaw at you. When you find yourself constructing logical answers, throw them away. Keep returning to the koan. Eventually, the mind will give up — and in that giving up, the answer appears.

**Case: Joshu's Dog.** The classic koan: "Does a dog have Buddha-nature?" Master Joshu answered: "Mu" (no). The answer seems to contradict Buddhist doctrine (all beings have Buddha-nature). The student is stuck. There is no logical way through. The only way out is through direct experience.

**Case: The Sound of One Hand.** Master Hakuin's most famous koan. A hand has two sides. The sound of one hand clapping is not a sound — it is a question. The koan forces the student to go beyond the dualistic thinking that divides experience into subject and object, self and other, sound and silence.

## Technique 3: The Direct Point — No Words

Zen famously claims to be "a special transmission outside the scriptures, not relying on words or letters, directly pointing to the mind."

**How to do it:** Do not look for answers in books. Do not look for answers from teachers. Look directly at your own experience. What is thinking, before it becomes thoughts? What is awareness, before you name it?

**Case: The Flower Sermon.** The Buddha once held up a flower before his disciples. Only Mahakasyapa smiled. The Buddha said: "I have the treasury of the true Dharma-eye, the wondrous mind of nirvana, the formless form, the subtle teaching that does not rely on words." This is considered the first transmission of Zen.

**Case: Bodhidharma's Response.** When the Emperor of China asked Bodhidharma what merit he had earned by building temples, Bodhidharma said: "No merit." When the Emperor asked who Bodhidharma was, he said: "I don't know." The direct point does not explain. It cuts through.

## Technique 4: The Peripheral Mind

Watts encourages the development of "peripheral" or intuitive knowing — the kind of knowing that a skilled musician uses to play without reading every note, that a dancer uses to move without planning each step.

**How to do it:** In any activity, notice the difference between doing it deliberately (thinking about each step) and doing it spontaneously (letting it happen). Practice letting go of control in small ways. Trust the body. Trust the moment.

**Case: The Jazz Musician.** Watts uses the analogy of jazz — a form of music that requires years of discipline to play with apparent spontaneity. The jazz musician does not plan each note. He listens, responds, and trusts the flow. This is wu-wei in action.

**Case: The Night Driver.** Most people have had the experience of driving home on a familiar road and arriving without remembering the journey. The body knew what to do. The conscious mind was elsewhere. This natural capacity for non-thinking action can be cultivated and extended beyond mundane activities.

## Technique 5: The Art of Everyday Zen

Zen is not separate from daily life. The practice is to bring the same presence, attention, and non-striving to every activity — washing dishes, writing an email, walking to the store.

**How to do it:** Do one thing at a time. When you are washing dishes, just wash dishes. Do not think about what you will do next. Do not listen to music. Do not plan. Just wash dishes. When you are walking, just walk. When you are listening, just listen.

**Case: The Tea Master.** The most famous Zen art is the tea ceremony. Sen no Rikyu, the greatest tea master, elevated a simple activity into a profound practice. Every movement was deliberate. The host and guest were fully present to each other. The tea was not the point — the moment was.

**Case: The Gardener.** The Zen garden at Ryoanji in Kyoto consists of fifteen rocks arranged on a bed of raked gravel. There is no water, no plants, no change. The garden is not about beauty — it is about emptiness. The monks rake the gravel as a form of meditation.
