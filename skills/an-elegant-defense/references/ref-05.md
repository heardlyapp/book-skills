# Reference: Immune Aging

## Overview
The immune system changes dramatically with age. Understanding immunosenescence is essential for understanding why older adults are vulnerable to infections and how to support immune health in aging.

## Case: Thymus Involution
The thymus — the organ where T cells mature — begins to shrink after puberty. By age 40, most of the thymus has been replaced by fat. By age 70, T cell production is a fraction of what it was at 20. This is thymus involution, the primary driver of immune aging. The decline in naive T cells means older adults respond poorly to new pathogens and new vaccines.

## Case: Inflammaging
Inflammaging is the chronic low-grade inflammation that accompanies aging. It is driven by accumulated cellular damage, dysfunctional immune cells that produce inflammatory signals, and the loss of regulatory mechanisms. Inflammaging contributes to age-related diseases: heart disease, dementia, frailty, and cancer. It is the immune system's contribution to the aging process.

## Case: Vaccination in Older Adults
Older adults respond more poorly to vaccines because their immune system produces fewer memory cells. This is why vaccines for older adults often require higher doses (flu vaccine) or specialized formulations (shingles vaccine with an adjuvant). The development of vaccines that work in older adults is a priority for public health.

## Case: Reversing Immune Aging
Research is exploring ways to reverse immune aging: restoring thymus function (growth hormone, IL-7), eliminating senescent cells (senolytics), and improving nutrition and exercise. While we cannot stop immune aging, we can slow it through lifestyle measures that reduce chronic inflammation and support immune function.
