# Reference: Autoimmune Disease

## Overview
Autoimmune disease occurs when the immune system loses the ability to distinguish self from non-self and attacks the body's own tissues. There are over 80 known autoimmune diseases, affecting 5-10% of the population.

## Case: The Breakdown of Self-Tolerance
Normally, the immune system is "tolerant" of self — it does not attack the body's own cells. Tolerance is established during T cell development (the thymus eliminates self-reactive T cells) and maintained by regulatory T cells. Autoimmune disease occurs when this tolerance breaks down. The trigger may be genetic, environmental (infection, toxin), or a combination.

## Case: Molecular Mimicry
One theory of autoimmunity is molecular mimicry: a pathogen has a protein that looks like a self-protein. The immune system attacks the pathogen and then, because the self-protein looks similar, attacks the body's own tissue. This is thought to be the mechanism behind some forms of rheumatoid arthritis and multiple sclerosis.

## Case: The Autoimmune Epidemic
Autoimmune disease is increasing in developed countries. The hygiene hypothesis suggests that reduced exposure to microbes in childhood leaves the immune system under-trained and prone to attacking self. Other factors include changes in diet, environmental toxins, and chronic stress. Richtel profiles patients whose autoimmune disease dramatically changed their lives.

## Case: The Treatment Challenge
Autoimmune disease is treated by suppressing the immune system — which makes patients vulnerable to infection. Biologic drugs that target specific immune molecules (TNF-alpha inhibitors, IL-6 inhibitors) are more targeted than older immunosuppressants. The goal is to suppress the autoimmune attack without destroying the immune system's ability to fight infection. It is a difficult balancing act.
