# Reference: Immunotherapy

## Overview
Immunotherapy is one of the most exciting developments in modern medicine. By harnessing the immune system to fight cancer, it has transformed the treatment of certain cancers and holds promise for many more.

## Case: Checkpoint Inhibitors
Cancer cells can evade the immune system by activating "checkpoint" molecules that tell T cells to stand down. Checkpoint inhibitors block these molecules, "releasing the brakes" on the immune system. Drugs targeting PD-1 (pembrolizumab, Keytruda) and CTLA-4 (ipilimumab, Yervoy) have been remarkably effective against melanoma, lung cancer, and other cancers. The 2018 Nobel Prize was awarded to James Allison and Tasuku Honjo for discovering these mechanisms.

## Case: CAR-T Cell Therapy
CAR-T cell therapy takes the patient's own T cells, engineers them to recognize the patient's cancer, grows them in the lab, and infuses them back into the patient. It is like creating a living drug. CAR-T has been dramatically effective against certain blood cancers (leukemia, lymphoma). The challenge is engineering T cells to recognize solid tumors. This is the cutting edge of immunotherapy research.

## Case: The Cancer-Immune Cycle
Richtel describes the cancer-immune cycle: cancer cells produce abnormal proteins (neoantigens), the immune system recognizes them, and T cells kill the cancer cells. But cancer evolves to evade the immune system. Immunotherapy works by blocking the evasion mechanisms. The success of immunotherapy depends on the cancer's mutation burden — more mutations mean more targets for the immune system.
