# Reference: The Immune System Explained

## Overview
The immune system is a complex network of cells, tissues, and organs that protects the body from infection. It has two main branches: innate and adaptive immunity.

## Case: The Innate System
The innate immune system is the first responder. It includes physical barriers (skin, mucus), chemical defenses (stomach acid, antimicrobial proteins), and cells that recognize general patterns of pathogens (macrophages, neutrophils, natural killer cells). The innate system responds within hours, but it has no memory — it treats every infection as if it were the first. It is the body's rapid response team.

## Case: The Adaptive System
The adaptive immune system is slower but more precise. It recognizes specific pathogens through receptors on T cells and B cells. The first exposure takes days to mount a response. But the system creates memory cells — long-lived cells that remember the pathogen and can respond within hours on re-exposure. This is why vaccines work. The adaptive system is the body's special forces.

## Case: The Three Friends
Richtel introduces the immune system through the story of three patients with different immune challenges: a man with HIV (immune deficiency), a woman with rheumatoid arthritis (autoimmunity), and a woman whose immune system cured her cancer (immunotherapy). Their stories illustrate the immune system's power, its failures, and its potential.

## Case: The Lymphatic System
The lymphatic system is the immune system's highway. Lymph nodes filter lymph fluid and house T cells and B cells. When you feel swollen lymph nodes during an infection, that is your immune system at work — cells proliferating to fight the pathogen.
