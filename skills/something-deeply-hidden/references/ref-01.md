# Reference: Many-Worlds Quantum Mechanics

## Overview
The many-worlds interpretation (MWI), originally proposed by Hugh Everett III in 1957, is the simplest interpretation of quantum mechanics: it takes the Schrodinger equation literally and denies that wavefunction collapse is a real process.

## Case: Everett's Doctoral Thesis
Hugh Everett III wrote his Ph.D. thesis at Princeton in 1957 under John Wheeler. The thesis, titled "The Theory of the Universal Wavefunction," argued that the wavefunction never collapses and that all possibilities are realized. Wheeler was enthusiastic but advised Everett to soften his language because the physics community was not ready. The thesis was published in a revised, toned-down version that attracted almost no attention. Everett left academic physics and spent the rest of his career as a defense analyst. His interpretation was rediscovered decades later.

## Case: Schrodinger's Cat in Many-Worlds
The famous thought experiment: a cat in a box with a radioactive trigger. In Copenhagen, the cat is both alive and dead until observed. In many-worlds, the cat is alive in one branch and dead in another. Both cats are real. The observer who opens the box splits into an observer who finds a live cat and an observer who finds a dead cat. There is no single "real" outcome. The puzzle of Schrodinger's cat is only a puzzle if you believe there must be one outcome.

## Case: The Quantum Suicide Thought Experiment
A quantum version of Schrodinger's cat: a gun fires if a quantum event occurs. In many-worlds, the observer survives in the branches where the gun does not fire. From the observer's first-person perspective, the gun never fires — they always survive. This thought experiment highlights the asymmetry of branches: all branches are equally real, but an observer's experience is confined to a single branch trajectory.
