# Reference: Quantum Information

## Overview
Information theory has reshaped our understanding of quantum mechanics. Quantum information is fundamentally different from classical information — it cannot be copied, it can be teleported, and it reveals deep connections between quantum physics and gravity.

## Case: Quantum Teleportation
Quantum teleportation does not transport matter but transfers quantum states between particles at a distance. It works by creating an entangled pair (Alice and Bob share one particle each), then Alice performs a measurement on her particle and the particle she wants to teleport. The measurement destroys the original state but transfers it instantly to Bob's particle. The catch: Bob cannot read the state until Alice sends him a classical message (two bits) telling him what measurement she performed. This requires communication at or below the speed of light. Teleportation does not violate relativity.

## Case: Bell's Theorem and Nonlocality
Bell's theorem (1964) proved that no local hidden variable theory can reproduce all predictions of quantum mechanics. Experiments confirm that entangled particles can be correlated in ways that are impossible under any local realistic theory. Many-worlds resolves the tension with relativity differently: the correlations exist because the two particles share the same branch of the wavefunction. The "spooky action at a distance" is not action at all — it is just the entanglement structure of the wavefunction.

## Case: Quantum Computing
The qubit (quantum bit) is the fundamental unit of quantum information. Unlike a classical bit (0 or 1), a qubit can exist in a superposition of both. Quantum computers exploit this to process information in ways that classical computers cannot. Shor's algorithm (factoring large numbers) and Grover's algorithm (searching unsorted databases) would be exponentially faster on a quantum computer. Carroll explains that quantum computing is possible precisely because many-worlds is true — the computation happens across branches.
