# Reference: Decoherence

## Overview
Decoherence is the key physical process that explains why macroscopic objects appear classical. It does not solve the measurement problem by itself (something still needs to select which branch we experience), but it explains why interference between branches becomes unobservable.

## Case: The Double-Slit Experiment
The classic double-slit experiment: electrons fired through two slits produce an interference pattern on a screen. When a detector is placed at the slits to determine which slit each electron goes through, the interference disappears. Standard explanation: observation collapses the wavefunction. Many-worlds explanation: the detector becomes entangled with the electron, creating a record of which slit it went through. The two branches (electron went through left slit / electron went through right slit) are now decohered — they can no longer interfere. The detector didn't collapse anything; it entangled itself with the electron.

## Case: Quantum Eraser Experiments
In quantum eraser experiments, the which-path information is destroyed after the electron hits the screen, and the interference pattern reappears. This seems to suggest retrocausality. In many-worlds, the explanation is straightforward: the branches where which-path information is available never interfere; the branches where the information was erased can interfere. The experiment does not "erase" the past; it merely selects a subset of branches that can interfere with each other.

## Case: Decoherence Timescales
Decoherence is not a slow process. A macroscopic superposition (like a dust particle in two locations) decoheres in about 10^-31 seconds — far faster than any human measurement. This is why the classical world appears classical: not because quantum mechanics breaks down for large objects, but because the entanglement with the environment happens so fast that no experiment can detect the superposition.
