# Reference: The Interpretations Debate

## Overview
Quantum mechanics is an extraordinarily successful theory, but physicists disagree about what it means. Carroll's book is a sustained argument that the many-worlds interpretation is the most natural and that alternatives introduce unnecessary complications.

## Case: The Copenhagen Interpretation
The Copenhagen interpretation (Bohr, Heisenberg) states that the wavefunction is not real — it is a tool for calculating probabilities, and "measurement" is a special process that lies outside the quantum formalism. The wavefunction collapses when measured. Carroll's criticism: Copenhagen is not a consistent interpretation but a collection of philosophical positions that avoid the measurement problem rather than solving it. It provides no criterion for what constitutes a measurement or when collapse occurs.

## Case: Bohmian Mechanics
David Bohm's pilot-wave theory posits that particles have definite positions at all times and are guided by the wavefunction. It reproduces all quantum predictions and solves the measurement problem but at a cost: it is explicitly nonlocal (faster-than-light influence) and requires a separate "guiding equation" alongside the Schrodinger equation. Carroll's criticism: Bohmian mechanics adds extra structure (particle positions) that is never needed for predictions. It works but is less parsimonious than many-worlds.

## Case: Objective Collapse Theories (GRW)
Ghirardi-Rimini-Weber (GRW) theory modifies the Schrodinger equation to include spontaneous collapse. Each particle has a tiny probability of collapsing spontaneously. Macroscopic objects collapse almost instantly because they contain many particles. Carroll's criticism: GRW modifies the Schrodinger equation in a way that has not been detected, adds an extra parameter (the collapse rate), and does not solve the problem of which branch is selected.
