# Reference: Spacetime and Quantum Gravity

## Overview
One of the deepest insights of modern theoretical physics is that spacetime may not be fundamental. Carroll argues that space and time emerge from the quantum wavefunction, and that understanding this emergence is the key to quantum gravity.

## Case: The Holographic Principle
The holographic principle states that the amount of information that can be stored in a region of space is proportional to the surface area of the region, not its volume. This is deeply counterintuitive — it suggests that space is not what it seems. The most concrete realization is the AdS/CFT correspondence: a theory of gravity in anti-de Sitter space is mathematically equivalent to a quantum field theory on the boundary of that space. Carroll explores this as evidence that spacetime is emergent.

## Case: Entanglement and Spacetime Geometry
Recent theoretical work (Van Raamsdonk, Maldacena, Susskind) has shown that entanglement is directly related to the geometry of spacetime. If you remove entanglement between two regions, the spacetime between them pinches off. This suggests that spacetime itself is a manifestation of quantum entanglement. ER = EPR (Einstein-Rosen bridges are equivalent to Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen entanglement) is the provocative formulation.

## Case: The Arrow of Time
The second law of thermodynamics (entropy increases) is not a fundamental law of physics. The fundamental laws (including the Schrodinger equation) are time-reversible. So why does the past have lower entropy than the future? Carroll argues that the arrow of time is a consequence of the universe's low-entropy initial condition (the Big Bang). The Past Hypothesis — that the universe began in a very special low-entropy state — explains why entropy increases. This is not a separate law but a boundary condition.
