# 5 — Voice & App

> Source: *I'm Still Here* by Austin Channing Brown.

## Brown's Voice

Brown writes as a Black woman, a Christian, a professional, and an advocate — without compartmentalizing these identities. The voice is direct, warm, and unapologetic. She does not perform respectability. She tells the truth about her experience — including her anger, her exhaustion, and her joy. The book is structured as a series of essays, each exploring a different dimension of navigating whiteness. Brown's power is in naming experiences that many Black readers will recognize immediately and many white readers will encounter for the first time.

> **Case: The Opening Line (Chapter 1):** "White people can be exhausting." Brown opens with a sentence that announces exactly what the book is about. She does not soften it. She does not qualify it. She names the experience. For Black readers, this is recognition. For white readers, it is an invitation — or a warning.

> **Case: The "Still Here" Meaning (Chapter 12):** Brown explains the title: "I'm Still Here" is not a plea for white people to accept her. It is a statement of persistence. "We are still here. Not because of white generosity or progress. We are here because we have refused to leave." The framing shifts the story from "how far we have come" to "we have survived this long despite everything."

## Five Scenarios

### 1. The Black Professional in a Predominantly White Workplace

You are the only Black person in leadership. You are expected to be grateful for the opportunity. You are told your perspective is valued — until it makes white colleagues uncomfortable. Brown's advice: set boundaries, find community outside the workplace, and do not confuse the organization's diversity rhetoric with genuine commitment to change.

### 2. The Black Student at a Predominantly White School

You are navigating a curriculum that does not reflect you, teachers who do not see you, and classmates who do not understand you. Brown's experience: find your Tiffani — a friend who affirms your Blackness. Find your anchor. Do not let the institution define your worth.

### 3. The White Person Who Wants to Be a Better Ally

You have read the book and recognize yourself in "nice white people." Brown's message: good intentions are not enough. Do the work of examining your own complicity. Do not expect gratitude for basic decency. Listen without defensiveness.

### 4. The Person of Faith Questioning Their Tradition

You grew up in a white church and are discovering that there is another way to experience faith. Brown's Black church experience offers a model: faith that is liberating, communal, and unafraid of naming injustice.

### 5. The Activist

You are fighting for racial justice and feeling burned out. Brown's message: joy is not a distraction. Community is not a retreat. The Spades games and the church choir are as essential to the movement as the protests.
