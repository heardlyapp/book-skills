# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *I'm Still Here* by Austin Channing Brown — Chapters 1-14.

## The Three Frameworks

### Framework A: The Name as Armor (Chapter 1)

Brown's parents named her Austin — a white man's name — strategically. They wanted her resume to make it past the initial screening, wanted her to have a chance to "make it to the interview." The strategy worked, but it came with constant micro-labor: correcting assumptions, proving her existence, explaining herself. The library scene at age seven — where the librarian could not believe the name on the card belonged to a little Black girl — crystallized everything.

> **Case: The Library Card (Chapter 1):** At seven years old, Brown brought her stack of books to the library counter. The librarian scanned her card, raised an eyebrow, and asked: "Is this your card?" Three times. "Are you sure this is your card?" Brown realized: this was not about her literacy. It was about her name not matching the body standing in front of the librarian. "That's why the librarian hadn't believed me. She didn't know a name like Austin could be stretched wide enough to cloak a little Black girl."

> **Case: The Fourth Grade Slur (Chapter 2):** A white boy named Zach stood in front of Brown in the lunch line and called her the n-word. "Everything stopped." Brown's response was immediate and fierce — she does not remember exactly what she said, but she knows she was not silent, and Zach never tried it again. This was her first direct encounter with the hatred her parents had been preparing her for — and her first experience of righteous anger.

### Framework B: The In-Between Space (Chapters 2, 3)

Brown grew up in predominantly white schools but also spent summers in Cleveland's Black neighborhood. She was called "Oreo" by Black kids and subjected to racist slurs by white ones. She was "too white for Black people, too black for white people." The friend Tiffani became her bridge — teaching her that Blackness is expansive, not monolithic. "Because she believed in my Blackness, I could, too."

> **Case: The Tootsie Roll Summer (Chapter 2):** Brown spent summers in Cleveland with her mother after the divorce. She was thrown into an all-Black world where she did not know the music, the dances, or the slang. She feigned being sick to avoid exposure. She lip-synced SWV songs she had never heard. Then she met Tiffani, who taught her the dances, vouched for her, and taught her that Blackness could look nerdy or cool — it was all valid. "It was freedom."

### Framework C: Black Joy as Resistance (Chapters 2, 8, 12)

Brown's discovery of the Black church was transformative — not because the theology was different, but because Jesus looked like her. The Spades games, the choir, the peppermint-dealing church mothers, the preaching that sounded like the present day — these were not escapes from reality. They were affirmations of a life that whiteness cannot define or diminish.

> **Case: The Black Church (Chapter 2):** At ten years old, Brown walked into a Black Baptist church for the first time. "I fell in love with the soaring voices and the songs that moved us to tears and then chased the blues away. I fell in love with a Jesus who saw the poor and sick and hurting, a Jesus who loved and reveled in our Blackness." This experience shaped her understanding that faith could be liberating, not constraining.
