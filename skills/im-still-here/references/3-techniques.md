# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *I'm Still Here* by Austin Channing Brown.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The Name/Reality Gap Identification

Brown's experience — the librarian not believing the name belonged to her — is a microcosm of a larger pattern: the world's expectations of who you are based on race do not match your reality. Identify the gaps between how the world reads you and who you actually are. Those gaps are where exhaustion lives. Name them.

> **Case: The Library Card (Chapter 1):** Brown learned at age seven that her name — carefully chosen by her parents as armor — would constantly require explanation. Every time a stranger expected a white man and got a Black woman, she had to do the work of correcting the expectation. This is the micro-labor of race.
> **Key takeaway**: The gap between how the world reads you and who you are is a source of exhaustion. Identify those gaps so you can name — and stop taking responsibility for — the labor they require.

> **Case: The Justified Anger Refusal (Chapter 9):** Brown writes that when she hears of another Black person killed by police, she does not need to calibrate her voice to be "respectable." "My anger is not a tool I need to put away. It is a signal that something is wrong." The technique: recognize that the demand for you to be calm is a tactic of control, not a requirement of justice.
> **Key takeaway**: Your anger is legitimate. You do not need to perform palatable emotions to be heard.

When a white person reacts defensively to a discussion of racism, recognize that their reaction is about their own discomfort, not about you. Brown distinguishes between "white people who don't know they are white" and "nice white people" — both are exhausting, but understanding the dynamic helps you stop taking responsibility for their feelings.

### 3. The Bridge Friend Practice

Find the Tiffani — the person who can help you reconnect with the parts of your identity that white spaces have eroded. For Brown, Tiffani was the friend who taught her that Blackness could look any way she wanted it to. "Because she believed in my Blackness, I could, too."

### 4. The Boundary Statement

When asked to educate, explain, or soften your message: "I'm not available to explain that right now." Brown does not say you should never educate — but you should protect your energy. You are not an unpaid diversity trainer.

### 5. The Joy Audit

Regularly ask: where am I experiencing joy that has nothing to do with whiteness? For Brown, it was the Black church, playing Spades, the sound of SWV. Joy is not a luxury. It is fuel for the fight. If you do not have a source of Black joy in your life, find one.
