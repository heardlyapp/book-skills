# 4 — Anti-Patterns

> Source: *I'm Still Here* by Austin Channing Brown.

## Common Errors

### 1. Believing That Being Nice Enough Will Protect You

**The trap:** "If I am respectful, patient, and kind, white people will treat me well."

**Why it fails:** Brown's parents gave her a white name to protect her. She was still called a nigger in fourth grade. She still faced suspicion in stores. Respectability is not a shield against racism — because the problem is not Black behavior. It is white perception.

> **Case: The Store Lesson (Chapter 2):** Brown's father taught her never to touch her pockets after handling store merchandise, because "someone might notice and assume you are trying to steal." Her mother taught her never to open a product in a store without the receipt in hand. These lessons were about survival, not about any actual wrongdoing. "It's funny how in these little life lessons, I always knew that 'someone' was white people."
> **Key takeaway**: Respectability does not protect you. The lessons Black parents teach their children about survival are not about changing behavior — they are about managing white perception.

> **Case: The Diversity Job (Chapter 5):** Brown took a job at an organization that touted its commitment to racial justice. In practice, she was expected to be grateful for the opportunity, to educate her colleagues, and to never make them uncomfortable. When she raised concerns about how the organization's practices harmed Black communities, she was told she was "too focused on race."
> **Key takeaway**: An organization's stated values mean nothing if its actual culture punishes the people who take those values seriously.

**The trap:** "If we can all share the same classrooms, that means we are equal."

**Why it fails:** Brown's mother challenged this directly at the family dinner table: "Segregation didn't have to be followed with integration. Surely relegating us to the back of the bus could have stopped without us having to give up all the businesses that died because we started going to white folks." Integration brought access — but it also cost Black institutions.

### 3. The "Not All White People" Distraction

**The trap:** "But not all white people are like that. I myself am a good white person."

**Why it fails:** The "not all white people" response centers white feelings in a conversation about Black experience. Brown addresses this through the "nice white people" chapter — focusing on individual innocence distracts from systemic analysis.

### 4. Believing the Goal Is to Be Colorblind

**The trap:** "I don't see color. I treat everyone the same."

**Why it fails:** Brown critiques this as a refusal to see the reality of racism. Colorblindness is not a solution — it is a way of avoiding the problem. The goal is not to stop seeing race. It is to stop allowing race to determine outcomes.

### 5. Performing Diversity Without Changing Power

**The trap:** "We have a diversity statement. We hired a DEI consultant. We are an antiracist organization."

**Why it fails:** Brown's experience working for organizations that claimed to be antiracist taught her that diversity rhetoric without structural change is performance. The real question: who holds power, and are they willing to share it?
