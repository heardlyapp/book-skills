# 2 — Key Principles

> Source: *I'm Still Here* by Austin Channing Brown.

## The 7 Principles

### 1. White People Who Don't Know They Are White Are the Most Exhausting

Brown opens with this because whiteness as the invisible norm is the root of the exhaustion. When white people see themselves as "just people" and everyone else as "racial," the burden of explaining, educating, and proving falls entirely on people of color.

> **Case: The Nice White People (Chapter 7):** Brown dedicates a chapter to "nice white people" — those who believe their good intentions exempt them from examining their complicity. They are the ones who say "I don't see color," who cry when confronted about racism, who expect gratitude for their allyship. They are often harder to deal with than overt racists because they believe they are on the right side.

### 2. Black Dignity Is Not Conditional

You do not need to be respectable, non-threatening, or exceptional to deserve dignity. Brown refuses the premise that Black people must earn the right to be treated humanely.

### 3. Code-Switching Is Survival — But It Is Also Loss

Brown's parents gave her a white-sounding name to help her "make it to the interview." It worked. But the cost was navigating between two worlds, never fully at home in either. Code-switching allows you to survive in white spaces, but it fragments your identity.

### 4. Blackness Is Expansive

> **Case: Tiffani's Gift (Chapter 2):** Tiffani taught Brown that Blackness can look like anything — nerdy or cool, loud or quiet, a Spades player or a bookworm. "Black is not monolithic. Black is expansive, and I didn't need the approval of whiteness in order to feel good in my skin; there was no whiteness available to offer an opinion."

### 5. Black Joy Is Resistance

The Spades games, the church choir, the line dances — these are not escapes from the fight. They are what make the fight sustainable. Joy is not a distraction from justice. It is the evidence that a full life is possible even under oppression.

### 6. Anger at Injustice Is Righteous

Brown refuses the demand that Black people respond to racism with calm, measured, "respectable" anger. "When I hear of yet another Black person killed by police, I do not have to use my most measured, calm, respectable voice." The question is not whether to be angry — it is what you do with the anger.

### 7. "I'm Still Here" Is a Declaration

The title is not a plea for acceptance. It is a statement of persistence. Brown is still here — not because whiteness has been kind to her, but because she has refused to leave. "We're still here" is a collective statement of survival, presence, and resistance.
