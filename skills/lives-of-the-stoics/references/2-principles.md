# Key Principles — Lives of the Stoics

## I. Philosophy Is About Action, Not Words

"The only reason to study philosophy is to become a better person." The Stoics were "pen-and-ink philosophers" in reverse — they didn't just write, they lived. Case: Cato the Younger wrote almost nothing, but his ironclad stand against Caesar taught more about Stoicism than any essay. "Cato was the one Stoic who never compromised."

## II. Adversity Is the Path

Zeno's shipwreck — losing everything — was the best thing that ever happened to him. "I made a prosperous voyage when I suffered shipwreck." Case: Epictetus was born a slave, crippled by his master, and became one of the most influential philosophers in history. "Some things are up to us, and some things are not."

## III. The Four Virtues Are Inseparable

Courage, Temperance, Justice, Wisdom. "These traits are inseparable but yet distinct and different from one another" (Zeno). Each Stoic embodied them differently: Cato had courage, Seneca struggled with temperance, Marcus had wisdom, Musonius Rufus had justice.

## IV. Death Is Not to Be Feared

Every Stoic faced death with dignity. Cato stabbed himself after reading Plato. Seneca's veins were opened on Nero's orders — he dictated philosophy as he bled. Chrysippus died laughing. Zeno held his breath. "To philosophize is to learn how to die" (Cicero).

## V. You Can Change Your Circumstances Through Character

Cleanthes arrived in Athens with nothing, worked as a water-carrier, and became head of the Stoa. Epictetus was a slave who became a teacher of emperors. Musonius Rufus was exiled repeatedly and continued teaching. "Well-being is realized by small steps, but is truly no small thing" (Zeno).

## VI. Contradictions Should Be Examined, Not Ignored

Seneca preached simplicity while accumulating immense wealth. He acknowledged this contradiction — he wasn't perfect. "The Stoics instruct us wisely not only in how to live, but in how to face the scariest part of life: the end." Case: Seneca's compromises in Nero's court are instructive failures.

## VII. We Are All Citizens of the World

Zeno refused Athenian citizenship — he believed in cosmopolitanism. "Consider all men to be of one community and one polity." This idea — that we belong to humanity first, not to any country — is one of Stoicism's most radical and enduring contributions.
