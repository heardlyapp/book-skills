# Techniques: The Search and the Hermit's Way

> Source: Road to Heaven, Chapters 8-12

## Porter's Expedition Method

The book follows a specific approach to finding the unfindable: begin with official channels (they deny existence), follow rumors, ask locals, hike into the mountains, follow instinct.

> **Case: The Buddhist Association Deputy Director** (Chapter 1): Chou Shao-liang told Porter there were no more hermits and called the search dangerous. But the abbot, Ching-hui, gave a faint smile. Porter learned to read what officials said vs. what they implied.

> **Case: The Old Monk at Wutaishan** (Chapter 2): When Porter repeated his question about hermits, one old monk said: "Of course there are still hermits in China." Then: "But when you meet them, you won't know them. You won't find them, unless they want to be found."

## The Hermit's Way of Life

A typical hermit's routine: rise before dawn, chant sutras or meditate, tend a garden of tea and vegetables, gather firewood, sleep when the sun sets. No possessions beyond what can be carried. No contact with the outside world except when necessary.

> **Case: The Taoist Adept** (Chapter 9): A Taoist living in a cave who had not spoken in seven years. He communicated through gestures and notes. His cave had a small shrine, a bed of straw, and a single window looking out over the valley.

## The Road to Heaven

The Chinese name for the Chungnan Mountains means "road to heaven." For Porter, the journey itself became the message: you don't find hermits by planning. You find them by walking.
