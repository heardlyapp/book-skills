# Core Framework: China's Hermit Tradition

> Source: Road to Heaven, Chapters 1-3

## The Five-Thousand-Year Tradition

Chinese records of hermits go back to the Yellow Emperor, Huang-ti (2700-2600 BC), who learned from two hermits how to conquer his enemies and prolong his life. Since then, hermits have been a constant presence in Chinese civilization.

> **Case: Hsu-yu Refuses the Kingdom** (Chapter 1): When Emperor Yao tried to give Hsu-yu the kingdom, the hermit replied: "When the sun or moon is shining, what use is lighting a torch? When the rain is falling, what use is watering crops?" He washed out his ears to rid them of the offer. This story was recorded in the Kaoshihchuan and repeated for two thousand years as the ideal of virtue over power.

> **Case: Shun's Failed Offer** (Chapter 1): Shun tried to give the kingdom to Shan-chuan, who replied: "In winter, I wear skins. In summer, I wear hemp. When the sun rises, I get up. When it sets, I rest. I'm free to do what I want." He disappeared into the mountains and was never heard from again.

## Respect, Not Rejection

Unlike Western traditions where hermits were often outcasts, Chinese hermits were the most respected members of society. They were consulted by rulers, admired by poets, and their simple lives were seen as the highest form of cultivation.

> **Key takeaway**: The Chinese hermit tradition is based on admiration, not pity. "Distant and insignificant, they were the most respected men and women in the world's oldest society."

## The Search

In 1989, Bill Porter (Red Pine) set out to find if the hermit tradition had survived the Cultural Revolution. Most officials told him no. An old monk told him: "Of course there are still hermits in China. But when you meet them, you won't know them."
