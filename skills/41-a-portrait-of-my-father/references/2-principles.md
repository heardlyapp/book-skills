# Principles of a Life Well Lived

## I. Lead with Humility

George H.W. Bush was a war hero, a successful oilman, a diplomat, the head of the CIA, Vice President, and President of the United States. He was also famously uncomfortable with the word "I."

**Case: The Grammatical Avoidance.** Colleagues noticed that Bush would find ways to start sentences without using the first person. Instead of "I think we should do X," he would say "It seems that doing X would be advisable." He deflected praise and absorbed blame. His son writes: "He would never say 'I won the Cold War.' He would say 'I was fortunate to serve at a time when freedom prevailed.'"

**Case: The 1988 Convention Speech.** When accepting the Republican nomination, Bush could have talked about his own accomplishments. Instead, he gave a speech about "a kinder, gentler nation" — a vision, not a résumé.

## II. Face Adversity Head-On

Bush's life was marked by profound loss — but he never let it define him.

**Case: Robin's Death.** In 1953, Bush's 3-year-old daughter Robin was diagnosed with leukemia. The family moved to New York for treatment. Bush flew from Texas every weekend to be with her. She died that October. His son writes: "He never got over the loss, but he never let it consume him." Bush later said: "I learned that you can survive tragedy. You can go on."

**Case: The Loss of the Presidency.** The 1992 defeat was devastating. Bush had been one of the most popular presidents in history — a 90% approval rating after the Gulf War — and then he lost to Bill Clinton. Ross Perot's third-party campaign split the vote. Bush never blamed Perot. He never blamed anyone. He simply moved on.

## III. Build Bridges, Don't Burn Them

Bush believed that personal relationships were the most important currency in public life.

**Case: The Kennedy Partnership.** Despite deep political differences, Bush worked closely with Senator Ted Kennedy on the Americans with Disabilities Act. The bill passed with overwhelming bipartisan support. Bush later said: "Ted Kennedy and I disagreed on many things, but we agreed that discrimination against disabled Americans was wrong."

**Case: The Gorbachev Friendship.** Bush and Soviet leader Mikhail Gorbachev developed a relationship based on mutual respect. When the Soviet Union collapsed, Bush did not gloat. He helped manage the transition with dignity. The peaceful end of the Cold War was not a victory lap — it was a joint achievement.

## IV. Never Stop Growing

Bush lived to 94. He never stopped learning, adapting, or taking on new challenges.

**Case: The Parachute Jump at 90.** After jump number eight at age 90, Bush was asked why he kept doing it. He said: "Just because you're old doesn't mean you can't have fun." He also started skydiving in his 70s. The point was not the jump — it was the refusal to accept limitations.

**Case: The Post-Presidential Work.** After leaving office, Bush wrote books, gave speeches, and raised money for charity. He worked with Bill Clinton to raise funds for Hurricane Katrina and the 2004 tsunami. He did not retire. He refocused.

## V. Write Things Down

One of the most consistent habits of George H.W. Bush's life was writing. He wrote thank-you notes, letters, and diary entries.

**Case: The Thank-You Note Habit.** Bush wrote notes constantly. After every meeting, every speech, every interview. He wrote to people he agreed with and people he disagreed with. When asked about it, he said: "It doesn't cost anything to be polite."

**Case: The Diary.** Bush kept a daily diary throughout his presidency. He used it to process events, record conversations, and capture his thoughts. The diaries became a historical record — but more importantly, they were a discipline. Writing forced him to reflect.
