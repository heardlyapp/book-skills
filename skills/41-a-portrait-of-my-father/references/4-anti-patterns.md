# The Mistakes and Their Lessons — Anti-Patterns

George H.W. Bush was a good man who made mistakes. His son's book does not hide them.

## Anti-Pattern 1: "Read My Lips" — The Promise That Broke Him

In his 1988 convention speech, Bush made a famous pledge: "Read my lips: no new taxes." He broke that pledge in 1990 to reach a budget deal with Congress. The broken promise cost him the 1992 election and defined his presidency in the minds of many voters.

**Case: The Budget Deal.** Bush agreed to a tax increase as part of a deficit-reduction package with Congressional Democrats. He believed it was the responsible thing to do — the deficit was growing, and a deal was necessary. But his base never forgave him. The lesson: promises matter. Even when breaking a promise is the right thing to do, the political cost can be fatal.

**Case: The 1992 Primary Challenge.** Pat Buchanan challenged Bush in the 1992 Republican primaries, fueled by anger over the broken tax pledge. Bush won the nomination but was weakened. The challenge reflected a deeper problem: Bush had violated the trust of his own supporters.

## Anti-Pattern 2: The Distance of the Patrician

Bush came from privilege. His father was a US Senator and a Wall Street banker. He attended Phillips Academy and Yale. Sometimes this background created a distance between him and the average American.

**Case: The Supermarket Scanner. During the 1992 campaign, Bush famously appeared amazed by a supermarket barcode scanner. The image — a President who did not know how supermarkets worked — became a symbol of his detachment from ordinary life. The perception was unfair, but it stuck.

**Case: The "Message: I Care" Button. At the 1992 Houston convention, Bush wore a button that said "Message: I Care." The gesture was intended to show empathy but came across as manufactured. The lesson: empathy cannot be performed. It must be felt.

## Anti-Pattern 3: The Failure to Tell His Own Story

Bush was uncomfortable talking about himself. This was admirable in personal terms but damaging in political terms. He refused to explain his own accomplishments, assuming that the results would speak for themselves.

**Case: The Gulf War Victory.** After driving Saddam Hussein out of Kuwait, Bush could have held a victory parade. He did not. He could have given speeches about the triumph. He did not. He simply stated that the mission was accomplished and moved on. The lack of narrative allowed others to define what the war meant.

**Case: The Cold War End. Bush presided over the peaceful end of the Cold War — the collapse of the Soviet Union, the fall of the Berlin Wall, the reunification of Germany. He managed this transition with skill and restraint. But he did not tell the story of how he did it. As a result, Ronald Reagan received more credit for ending the Cold War than Bush did.

## Anti-Pattern 4: The Stiff Public Image

In private, Bush was warm, funny, and self-deprecating. In public, he could appear stiff, awkward, and out of touch.

**Case: The 1988 Debate with Dukakis. Bush's performance in the first presidential debate was stiff and wooden. He seemed uncomfortable on stage. His son writes: "My father was not a natural performer. He was a natural connector — but only one-on-one or in small groups."

**Case: The "Prudent" Language. Bush's speaking style was cautious, measured, and averse to bold rhetoric. He did not give rousing speeches. He gave reasoned statements. In the television age, this was a liability. The lesson: in a media democracy, the ability to communicate your message is as important as the message itself.

## Anti-Pattern 5: Letting Loyalty Blind

Bush was deeply loyal to his staff and friends. This loyalty sometimes prevented him from making necessary changes.

**Case: The 1992 Campaign Team. Bush kept his campaign team largely intact from 1988. They had won once. But the political landscape had changed. His son implies that a changing of the guard might have produced a more effective campaign.

**Case: The Iran-Contra Pardons. Bush pardoned several figures involved in the Iran-Contra affair during his presidency. The pardons were controversial. They were seen as protecting friends rather than serving justice. The lesson: loyalty must be balanced with judgment.
