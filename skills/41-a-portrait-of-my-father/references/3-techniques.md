# Practical Techniques from 41

George H.W. Bush's life offers specific, repeatable techniques for effective leadership and personal fulfillment.

## Technique 1: The Thank-You Note as Relationship Builder

Bush's most famous habit. He wrote thank-you notes by hand, constantly, to anyone who helped him or even met with him.

**How to do it:** When someone does something for you — even a small thing — write them a handwritten note within 24 hours. Be specific about what you are thanking them for. The note is not about you. It is about making the other person feel seen.

**Case: The Meeting Follow-Up.** After every meeting, Bush would write a thank-you note to the person he had just met. The note would reference something specific they had discussed. The recipient felt valued. Over a lifetime, thousands of these notes created a network of goodwill that served Bush in every role.

**Case: The Letter to His Son.** When George W. Bush was running for president, his father wrote him letters of encouragement. They were not strategic advice. They were personal: "I love you. I'm proud of you. I'm praying for you." The technique of the personal note works for family as much as for colleagues.

## Technique 2: The Principle of Full Commitment

Bush never did anything halfway. Whether he was sweeping a warehouse, running the CIA, or planning a birthday party, he gave it his full attention.

**How to do it:** When you commit to a task, commit to it completely. Do not hold back energy for other things. The task in front of you is the only thing that matters right now. Sweep the floor better than it has ever been swept.

**Case: The CIA Turnaround.** Bush took over the CIA when it was reeling from Watergate-era investigations. He restored morale by visiting every division, learning every job, and showing respect for the career officers. He did not treat the assignment as a stepping stone — he treated it as his only job.

**Case: The Christmas Party.** When Bush volunteered to mix drinks at his company's Christmas party, he did it with abandon. He was rolled home in the back of a pickup truck. His son says: "Typical Bush — when he committed to doing something, he did it all out."

## Technique 3: The Art of Strategic Disappearance

Bush understood the value of withdrawing from the spotlight. After losing the 1992 election, he did not hold press conferences or give interviews about what went wrong. He simply left Washington and returned to private life.

**How to do it:** When you have suffered a defeat or finished a chapter, withdraw. Do not explain. Do not defend. Do not complain. Let your silence speak. Use the time to reflect, recharge, and decide what comes next.

**Case: The Vanishing Act After 1992.** Bush left Washington on Inauguration Day 1993 and did not look back. He did not second-guess his successor. He did not write a tell-all book about his White House years. He did not build a presidential library as a platform for continuing political influence. He simply went home.

**Case: The Clinton Friendship.** Years later, Bush developed a genuine friendship with Bill Clinton — the man who defeated him. They worked together on humanitarian projects. The friendship was possible because Bush had processed his defeat privately, not publicly.

## Technique 4: The Power of Physical Activity

Bush was an athlete — baseball, tennis, golf, running, speedboats, parachuting. He believed that physical activity cleared the mind and strengthened the spirit.

**How to do it:** Make physical activity a non-negotiable part of your routine. It does not have to be competitive — it just has to be vigorous enough to clear your mind.

**Case: The Morning Run.** Even during his presidency, Bush ran almost every day. He was famous for his speedboat "Fidelity." He played tennis into his 80s. His son notes: "He was always in motion. He believed that a healthy body supported a healthy mind."

**Case: The 85th Birthday Jump.** When Bush decided to make a parachute jump for his 85th birthday, his family worried. His son initially questioned it. But Bush insisted. He trained for it. He did it. The jump was not just about thrill — it was a statement that physical vitality does not end at a certain age.

## Technique 5: The Daily Discipline of Gratitude

Bush's thank-you notes were part of a larger discipline of gratitude. He did not take people for granted.

**How to do it:** End each day by identifying one person you are grateful for and writing them a note. The discipline forces you to focus on what you have received, not what you lack.

**Case: The Birthday Calls.** Bush made a habit of calling friends on their birthdays. He did not delegate this to staff. He remembered the dates. He made the call himself. The small effort produced enormous loyalty.

**Case: The Wedding Toast.** At his son's wedding, Bush gave a toast that was not about himself — it was about the couple, the families, and the joy of the moment. He did not need to be the center of attention. He made the bride and groom the center.
