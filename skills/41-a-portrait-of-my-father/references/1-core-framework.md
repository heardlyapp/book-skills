## The Code: Four Pillars of the Bush Ethos

George W. Bush identifies the principles his father lived by through stories, not statements.

**Pillar 1: Do Your Best.** Whatever task George H.W. Bush was assigned — from sweeping an oil warehouse to managing the CIA — he did it with full commitment.

**Case: The Odessa Warehouse.** When Bush was learning the oil business, he was assigned to sweep the warehouse. His son writes: "If George Bush was assigned to sweep the warehouse, the manager would find the cleanest floor he'd ever seen. If he had to paint rigs, he would come in Saturday morning to slap on an extra coat so that the job got done right."

**Case: The CIA Directorship.** Bush took the CIA job at a time when the agency was demoralized by Watergate-era investigations. He was told it would destroy his political career. He took it anyway, restored morale, and served without seeking credit. His son notes: "He did not ask whether the job would help him politically. He asked whether he could make a difference."

**Pillar 2: Never Complain.** Bush's mother instilled in him: "Don't be arrogant. Never complain." He never did — not when his 3-year-old daughter died of leukemia, not when he lost the presidency.

**Case: The 1992 Defeat.** Bush received the news of his re-election loss with dignity. There was no blame, no excuses. He simply said the people had spoken and it was time to move on. His son writes: "Self-pity is not in George Bush's DNA."

**Case: The Fake Onion.** When Bush's friend Earle Craig bit into a rubber onion placed in his martini as a prank, the group laughed. Bush never took himself too seriously. He was as willing to be the butt of a joke as to tell one.

**Pillar 3: Build Relationships.** Bush believed that personal relationships were the foundation of everything — business, diplomacy, family, and friendship.

**Case: The Thank-You Notes.** Bush wrote thousands of thank-you notes. After meetings, after interviews, after receiving gifts. His son writes: "He wrote notes to people he had just met, to colleagues, to friends, to family. It was not a political strategy. It was who he was."

**Case: The Cross-Aisle Friendships.** As President, Bush developed genuine friendships with Democratic leaders, including Senator Ted Kennedy. Together they passed the Americans with Disabilities Act and the Clean Air Act Amendments. The relationships were not transactional — they were personal.

**Pillar 4: Love Your Family Unconditionally.** The most important pillar. Bush was a devoted husband to Barbara for 73 years and a devoted father to his six children.

**Case: The Ring.** Bush wore a ring engraved with the names of his children. He wore it every day. When his son asked about it, he simply showed the names. He did not need to say more.

**Case: The Parachute Jump.** At 90, Bush made a parachute jump. His son initially worried it would tire him out for his birthday party. Bush insisted. The jump was not about thrill-seeking — it was about proving that age does not define you. His son understood: "Because it will make him feel younger."
