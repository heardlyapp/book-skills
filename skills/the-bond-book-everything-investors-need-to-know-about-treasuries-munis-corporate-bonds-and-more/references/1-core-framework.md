# Core Framework: Bond Basics

> Source: The Bond Book, Chapters 1-2

## What Is a Bond?

A bond is a loan. You lend money to a borrower who pays periodic interest (coupon) and returns principal at maturity. The basic unit is $1,000 par value.

> **Case: The State of New Jersey Issuance** (Chapter 1): New Jersey needs $500 million and hires an investment bank as underwriter. The bank buys the bonds and resells them to institutions and individuals. The indenture specifies payment terms, call features, and funding sources (taxes).

> **Key takeaway**: Always read the prospectus. Know the issuer, coupon, maturity date, and call features before buying.

## How Bonds Are Issued and Traded

Primary market = buying at issue from underwriter. Secondary market = buying from another investor. Treasuries enjoy active secondary markets; many corporates and munis have thin markets.

> **Case: The Inactive Market** (Chapter 1): Once bonds "go away" to investors, the market can become inactive. Selling may be possible but at high commission costs. This is why liquidity matters.

## Key Terminology

Par value ($1,000), coupon (interest rate), maturity date, indenture (legal contract), prospectus (summary document), call feature (issuer's right to redeem early), credit rating (default risk assessment), yield (return earned).

## Bond Types

Treasuries (federal government), Municipals (state/local government, tax-exempt), Corporates (companies), Agency (government-sponsored), Mortgage-Backed (pools of home loans), Zeros (no coupon, sold at discount).
