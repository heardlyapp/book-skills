# Anti-Patterns: Bond Investing Mistakes

> Source: The Bond Book, Chapters 8, 12-13

## Ignoring Call Features

Callable bonds can be redeemed by the issuer when interest rates fall. The bondholder is forced to reinvest at lower rates. This is the most common costly mistake for individual bond investors.

> **Case: The Called Bond** (Chapter 8): An investor buys a corporate bond at 5% yield. Two years later, rates fall to 3% and the bond is called. The investor gets principal back but must reinvest at much lower rates. The call schedule was in the prospectus — but the investor didn't read it.

> **Case: The Make-Whole Call** (Chapter 8): Modern corporate bonds often use "make-whole" calls that compensate the bondholder. Older bonds with traditional call features offer no such protection.

> **Key takeaway**: Never buy a bond without understanding the call provisions. Read the prospectus.

## Confusing Coupon Rate with Yield

The coupon never changes. Yield changes with market price. Investors mistakenly think their bond "earns" the coupon regardless of purchase price.

## Buying Bonds Without Understanding Maturity Risk

Long-term bonds offer higher yields but much greater price volatility.

> **Case: The 2008 Crisis** (Chapters 3, 8): Even investment-grade bonds dropped in price during the crisis as credit spreads widened. Investors who needed to sell before maturity took significant losses.

> **Key takeaway**: Match bond maturities to your investment horizon. Don't buy long bonds if you may need the money soon.
