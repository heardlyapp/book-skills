# Principles: Bond Pricing and Yields

> Source: The Bond Book, Chapters 3-4

## Price and Yield Move in Opposite Directions

The most important bond concept. When interest rates rise, existing bond prices fall. When rates fall, prices rise. All bonds are subject to this — regardless of issuer, credit rating, or insurance.

> **Case: The 30-Year Treasury** (Chapter 3): A 30-year Treasury at 5% loses ~15-20% of its value if rates rise to 6%. If rates fall to 4%, it gains proportionally. The author repeats "All bonds are subject to interest rate risk" three times to emphasize the point. This is the most critical lesson in the book.

> **Key takeaway**: Interest rate risk affects every bond. Understanding this is the foundation of wise investing.

## Yield to Maturity (YTM)

The total return if held to maturity, accounting for purchase price, coupon, and time remaining.

> **Case: Buying Below Par** (Chapter 4): A 4% coupon bond bought at $950 has YTM > 4% because the $50 gain at maturity adds to your return.

> **Case: Premium Bonds** (Chapter 4): A 6% coupon bond bought at $1,050 has YTM < 6% because you lose $50 at maturity.

## Duration

A measure of interest rate sensitivity. Duration 5 ≈ 5% price change per 1% rate change. Longer maturity = higher duration = higher risk.

## Credit Ratings

AAA to D ratings from Moody's, S&P, Fitch. Investment grade (BBB- and above) vs speculative/junk (BB+ and below).

> **Key takeaway**: Use YTM to compare returns. Use duration to compare risk. Use credit ratings to assess default risk.
