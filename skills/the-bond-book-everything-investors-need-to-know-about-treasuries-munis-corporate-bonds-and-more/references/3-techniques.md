# Techniques: Choosing Bond Types

> Source: The Bond Book, Chapters 6-11

## Treasury Bonds

Issued by the U.S. government. Backed by full faith and credit — zero default risk. Interest is exempt from state and local taxes. Available as bills (< 1 year), notes (2-10 years), bonds (> 10 years), and TIPS (inflation-protected).

> **Case: The Treasury Auction** (Chapter 6): Treasuries are sold through competitive auctions. Individual investors can buy directly at TreasuryDirect.gov without paying a broker commission. This makes Treasuries the most cost-effective bond for small investors.

> **Key takeaway**: Treasuries are the risk-free benchmark. Every other bond is priced relative to Treasuries.

## Municipal Bonds (Munis)

Issued by states, cities, and local agencies. Interest is exempt from federal income tax and often from state tax for residents of the issuing state.

> **Case: The Tax-Exempt Advantage** (Chapter 9): For investors in high tax brackets, munis can offer significantly higher after-tax returns than taxable bonds. Formula: tax-equivalent yield = municipal yield / (1 - tax rate). An investor in the 35% bracket needs this calculation.

## Corporate Bonds

Higher yields than Treasuries but with credit risk. Investment grade (BBB- and above) or high yield (BB+ and below).

> **Case: The Callable Corporate** (Chapter 8): Many corporate bonds are callable. The company can redeem them early if rates fall, capping the bondholder's upside. This creates reinvestment risk. Always check the call schedule before buying.

## Zero-Coupon Bonds

Sold at deep discount, no periodic interest, full payment at maturity.

> **Key takeaway**: Match the bond type to your tax situation, risk tolerance, and investment horizon.
