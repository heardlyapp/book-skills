# The Factory Farm System — What It Is and How It Works

> Source: Eating Animals, Chapters 1-3 (All or Nothing, Words/Meaning, Hiding/Seeking)

## Core Framework

Factory farming (also called CAFO — Concentrated Animal Feeding Operation) is the dominant system of animal agriculture in the developed world. Foer's investigation reveals a system that is vastly different from the pastoral images on food packaging.

### The System by Animal

| Animal | Natural Lifespan | Factory Farm Lifespan | Conditions |
|--------|-----------------|----------------------|------------|
| Broiler chicken | 8-15 years | 42 days | 80,000+ birds per building, standing in waste, bred to grow so fast legs can't support weight |
| Egg-laying hen | 8-15 years | 1-2 years | 5-8 hens per battery cage (67 sq in per hen), debeaked without anesthetic |
| Pig | 10-15 years | 6 months | Gestation crates (7 ft x 2 ft) for breeding sows, slatted floors, no straw |
| Beef cattle | 15-25 years | 14-18 months | Feedlots with 100,000+ animals, standing in manure, fed corn (not grass) which causes liver abscesses |

### The Externalized Costs

| Cost Category | Borne By | Example |
|---------------|----------|---------|
| Waste disposal | Neighbors, environment | 500 million tons of animal waste annually, no treatment, stored in open lagoons |
| Antibiotic resistance | General public | 80% of antibiotics sold in US go to farm animals |
| Pandemic risk | Global population | Factory farms are breeding grounds for zoonotic diseases |
| Worker health | Employees | Higher rates of respiratory illness, injury in slaughterhouses |
| Animal suffering | Animals | Lives of chronic stress, pain, and deprivation |

> **Case: The Grandmother's Story** (Introduction)
> Foer's grandmother survived the Holocaust by scavenging food — rotting potatoes, discarded meat scraps, skins clinging to bones. She taught Foer that every part is edible, that one tea bag makes as many cups as you're serving. After the War, she still couldn't shake the desperation — she would make sandwiches from hotel buffets to stash in her bag. She weighed Foer when he arrived for visits and when he left. Her relationship to food was shaped by the experience of near-starvation. Foer uses her story as a moral anchor: food is not just fuel — it's life, survival, memory, and meaning.
> **Key takeaway**: Our relationship to food is shaped by the stories we inherit. Foer's grandmother taught him that food carries the weight of history. The question the book asks: what story will we tell about our food — and what kind of people will we be in that story?

> **Case: The 83% Chicken Statistic** (Influence chapter)
> According to a Consumer Reports study, 83% of all chicken meat sold in the US — including organic and antibiotic-free brands — is contaminated with campylobacter or salmonella at time of purchase. This is not a failure of the system — it's a feature. Factory farms create conditions where pathogens flourish. The bodies they produce are so contaminated that the industry's own washing and chlorinating processes cannot fully clean them.
> **Key takeaway**: The baseline contamination rate of industrial meat is not an accident or a scandal — it's the predictable outcome of the system. If 83% of a product were contaminated with pathogens, the product itself would be considered defective. But because it's meat, we accept it.
