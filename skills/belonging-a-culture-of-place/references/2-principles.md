# Principles: Race, Segregation, and the Politics of Place

> Source: Belonging, Chapters 3-9

## Moved by Mountains

hooks examines how geography shapes identity. Appalachia is stereotyped as uniformly white. hooks reclaims Black Appalachian history and visibility.

> **Case: The Invisible Black Appalachian** (Chapter 3): Black people have lived in Appalachia for generations, but they are erased from the popular image. hooks' own family is part of this erased history. She insists on being seen.

> **Key takeaway**: Place-based identities can exclude and erase. Reclaiming visibility is a political act of resistance.

## Again — Segregation Must End

Legal segregation ended but racial separation in daily life persists. hooks calls for deeper, more meaningful integration.

> **Case: The Integration Failure** (Chapter 7): hooks attended both segregated and integrated schools. Integration became one-way: Black children entered white spaces and had to assimilate. True integration requires mutual transformation.

> **Case: The Black Farmer's Loss** (Chapter 5): Black-owned farmland declined from 15 million acres at its peak to under 2 million today. This land loss represents destruction of Black economic independence and cultural connection.

## Touching the Earth

hooks' foundational essay on Black connection to land. Gardening and farming are forms of healing and political resistance.

> **Key takeaway**: Integration should mean shared space on equal terms — not one-sided assimilation into whiteness. Both sides must change.
