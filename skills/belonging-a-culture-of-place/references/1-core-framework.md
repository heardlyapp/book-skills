# Core Framework: bell hooks and Her Kentucky Roots

> Source: Belonging, Preface and Chapter 1-2

## Who Is bell hooks?

Born Gloria Jean Watkins in Hopkinsville, Kentucky. Author, feminist, social activist. She took the name bell hooks (lowercase) from her maternal great-grandmother to focus attention on her ideas, not her identity.

> **Case: The Return to Kentucky** (Preface): hooks left Kentucky for education and career — Stanford, University of Wisconsin, Yale. After years of teaching at elite universities, she chose to return. She writes: "Choosing to return to the land and landscape of my childhood... I am comforted by the knowledge that I could die here."

> **Key takeaway**: hooks' return was not retreat but conscious reclamation. Choosing where to live and die is a mindful act.

## Kentucky Is My Fate

The opening essay on returning home. The hills represent both freedom and oppression.

> **Case: Before and After** (Chapter 2): hooks divides her life into "Before" (the hills where nature was sanctuary and race didn't define everything) and "After" (town life dominated by white supremacy). Nature was "a place of refuge, a place for healing wounds."

> **Case: The Hills as Freedom** (Chapter 2): hooks writes about roaming freely in the Kentucky hills, learning self-reliance and independence. This early experience shaped her lifelong politics of liberation.

> **Key takeaway**: hooks' relationship with Kentucky is layered — love and pain coexist. Belonging means holding both truths.
