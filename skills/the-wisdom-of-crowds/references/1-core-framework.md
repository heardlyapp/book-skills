# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *The Wisdom of Crowds* by Joe Abercrombie — Parts VII-IX.

## The Five Frameworks

### Framework A: The Three Factions (Parts VII-VIII)

Every successful revolution has three factions. The novel shows them in stark clarity:
- **The Idealist (Risinau)** — Speeches, visions, "the Great Change is come." No plan for governance. His manifestos bulge from his pockets; his answers for practical problems are empty.
- **The Destroyer (Judge)** — "The world doesn't need changing. It needs burning." No interest in building. Pure negation, pure destruction.
- **The Opportunist (Pike)** — Former Arch Lector who was the Weaver all along. He surfed the revolution from within the system. He doesn't care about the change — he cares about who comes out on top.

> **Case: The March on Adua** (Part VII, "Change"): Vick watches the three leaders ride toward Adua. Risinau talks of "high ideals" and butterflies. Judge talks of burning. Pike says nothing. The moderates already lost control of their own movement before they reached the gates.
> **Key takeaway**: If you don't control your extremists, they control you. The moderate leader who can't check the radicals will be consumed by them.

### Framework B: The Crowd Paradox (Parts VII-VIII)

"The people" is not a single entity. The crowd that tears down the gates is the same crowd that loots the shops, lynches the nobles, and then can't decide who should run the city.

> **Case: The Gates of the Agriont** (Part VII, "The Little People"): Soldier Parry refuses to shoot the old laundry-woman. Sergeant Hope kills his own captain and opens the gates. The Breakers surge in, cheering. Fifteen minutes later, they're looting, killing civilians, and flying severed heads on spikes. The same historical moment contains liberation, murder, and chaos — simultaneously.
> **Key takeaway**: Revolutions don't have a single character. They contain all possibilities at once. What emerges depends on who seizes the moment.

### Framework C: Justice vs. Process (Parts VIII-IX)

When the Breakers take the Agriont, they call it "people's justice." But without process — courts, evidence, standards, appeals — justice is indistinguishable from vengeance.

> **Case: The Fountain Execution** (Part VII, "The Little People"): Senior under-secretary Ettenbeck is dragged to a fountain and drowned by the mob. His crime? He worked in Agricultural Taxation. The crowd doesn't know his name or what he did. They kill him because he's "one of those Closed Council bastards" — and then they move on. No trial. No evidence. Just a head stuffed in a fountain.
> **Key takeaway**: When the institutions that protect due process are torn down, the first people to die are usually the wrong ones.

### Framework D: The Personal in the Political (Parts VII-IX)

Every character in the revolution has private motives that have nothing to do with the public cause. The book shows this relentlessly.

> **Case: Shawley and Furnevelt** (Part VII, "The Little People"): Shawley doesn't join the Breakers for freedom or equality. He joins because he has a grudge against Furnevelt — a business debt. Under cover of the revolution, he murders Furnevelt and chases his daughter through the streets. The Great Change is just cover for a personal score-settling.
> **Key takeaway**: When the old order collapses, every personal grudge becomes a political act. Order your house before the chaos comes.

### Framework E: The Collapse of Institutions (Parts VIII-IX)

The book's final arc shows what happens when every institution has been torn down. The prisons are open. The courts are burned. The army has dissolved. The king has surrendered. There is nothing left to enforce order — and the mob discovers that freedom without structure is just war.

> **Case: Orso's Surrender** (Part VIII, "Bring Out the King"): Orso walks out alone, surrenders to the mob, and is spared — not because the mob is merciful, but because they have no idea what to do with a captive king. "What happens now?" he asks. "The people will decide," says Pike. But the people have no mechanism to decide anything. The revolution can tear down the palace but can't build a government.
> **Key takeaway**: The hardest part of revolution is not winning — it's governing. If you haven't planned the day after, you haven't planned at all.
