# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *The Wisdom of Crowds* by Joe Abercrombie.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The Pre-Surrender Audit

**Before you join a movement (or launch one):**
1. List the people in leadership. Classify each: idealist (Risinau), destroyer (Judge), or opportunist (Pike).
2. If the destroyers outnumber the idealists, get out.
3. If the leader is an opportunist you can't identify, get out.
4. Ask: "What's the plan for day one after victory?" If no one can answer, don't join.

### 2. The Fountain Check

**When a group starts talking about "justice" without process:**
1. Stop the rush to judgment. Ask: "What's the evidence? Who decides? What's the appeal?"
2. If there are no answers, you're watching a lynching with a noble name.
3. The name of the victim tells you everything: Ettenbeck was a tax clerk, not a war criminal.

### 3. The Three-Faction Alliance Strategy

**When you're in a coalition:**
1. Give the idealists the speeches and the public face.
2. Give the destroyers a controlled outlet (investigating genuine abuses, not random vengeance).
3. Watch the opportunist closest of all. They will switch sides at the worst moment.
4. Build governing capacity BEFORE you take power.

### 4. The Orso Maneuver

**When you're facing an overwhelming force:**
1. Do not escalate. Orso stopped the shooting and walked out alone.
2. Present yourself with full dignity. The mob was silenced by the sight of a king who looked like a king.
3. Force them to make a decision, not just react. "What happens now?" is the most destabilizing question you can ask a crowd.

### 5. The Coin-Toss Reality Check

**When you're tempted to judge others:**
1. Ask Broad's question: "Are you in a different position through virtue or luck?"
2. The line between Broad (hero) and the dead soldier (corpse) was circumstance.
