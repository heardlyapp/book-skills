# Core Stoic Practices

> Source: The Little Book of Stoicism, Chapters 4-8

## The Exercises

### Five Essential Practices

| Practice | What It Is | Why It Works |
|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Negative visualization | Imagine losing what you value | Cultivates gratitude, prepares for loss |
| Memento Mori | Remember you will die | Sharpens priorities, kills procrastination |
| Amor Fati | Love your fate | Turns obstacles into opportunities |
| Voluntary discomfort | Choose hardship | Builds resilience, reduces fear |
| Journaling | Write your thoughts | Clarifies thinking, tracks progress |

> **Case: Negative Visualization** (Chapter 4)
> The Stoics practiced imagining the worst: your child dies, you lose your job, your house burns down. Not to be morbid — but to prepare. When you imagine loss, two things happen: (1) you appreciate what you have right now, and (2) you are less shocked when loss actually occurs. Seneca advised: "Set aside a certain number of days to live on the smallest and cheapest fare. Make your bed as hard as possible. Then you will realize that a man's happiness depends on nothing except the use of his reason."
> **Key takeaway:** Negative visualization is not pessimism. It is the most powerful gratitude exercise ever invented. When you imagine losing something, you realize how much you value it. The Stoics practiced gratitude by imagining the worst.

> **Case: The View from Above** (Chapter 6)
> Marcus Aurelius practiced the "view from above" — imagining the world from a great height. From this perspective, the things we obsess over — status, wealth, revenge — shrink to nothing. The view from above is a perspective exercise: zoom out until your problems are tiny. Then act accordingly.
> **Key takeaway:** The view from above is the cure for an overinflated sense of importance. Your problems are real. They are also tiny in the scale of the cosmos. Both are true. The trick is holding both perspectives simultaneously.
