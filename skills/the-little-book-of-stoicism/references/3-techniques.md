# The Four Virtues

> Source: The Little Book of Stoicism, Chapters 9-12

## The Cardinal Virtues

### A Framework for Every Decision

| Virtue | Question | Opposite |
|--------|----------|---------|
| Wisdom | Is this true? Do I know what I am doing? | Ignorance |
| Justice | Is this fair? Does it serve others? | Injustice |
| Courage | Am I willing to do what is right? | Cowardice |
| Temperance | Is this the right amount? | Excess |

> **Case: Wisdom** (Chapter 9)
> Wisdom in Stoicism is not about IQ — it is about knowing what is good, what is bad, and what is neither. Most of our suffering comes from misclassifying things: we think wealth is good (it is not — it is indifferent), we think criticism is bad (it is not — it is an external), we think health is good (it is not — it can be taken in an instant). Wisdom is the ability to see clearly. It is the master virtue.
> **Key takeaway:** The Stoics believed that if you see clearly, you will act correctly. Most errors are errors of perception, not intention. Wisdom is not about knowing more facts — it is about seeing reality as it is.

> **Case: Courage** (Chapter 11)
> Stoic courage is not about fighting — it is about endurance. Seneca wrote: "Sometimes even to live is an act of courage." Courage is speaking the truth when it is easier to lie. It is facing your fear without running. It is waking up and doing what needs to be done. Marcus Aurelius reminded himself every morning: "I will meet with meddling, ungrateful, arrogant people — but I have seen the beauty of good and the ugliness of evil. I know my own nature."
> **Key takeaway:** Stoic courage is quiet, patient, and daily. It is not the courage of the battlefield — it is the courage of the morning meeting, the difficult conversation, the hard choice. This is the courage most of us need.
