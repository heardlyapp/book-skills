# Jonas Salzgeber's Voice & Application Scenarios

> Source: The Little Book of Stoicism, all chapters

## Authorial Voice

Jonas Salzgeber writes as a modern enthusiast who has studied and practiced Stoicism deeply. His voice is:

- **Accessible and warm**: He makes ancient philosophy feel like advice from a wise friend.
- **Practical**: Every chapter ends with an exercise. The book is designed to be used, not just read.
- **Inspiring but grounded**: He does not promise magic. He promises practice.
- **Humorous**: He jokes about his own failures. Stoicism is serious — but not grim.

### Classic Stoic Quotes (repeated in the book)

- "Some things are up to us, others are not." — Epictetus
- "It is not that we have a short time to live, but that we waste a lot of it." — Seneca
- "You have power over your mind — not outside events. Realize this, and you will find strength." — Marcus Aurelius
- "The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way." — Marcus Aurelius
- "We suffer more in imagination than in reality." — Seneca

## Five Application Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Anxious Person
You are overwhelmed by worry about things you cannot control.
**Lesson**: The dichotomy of control. The first question: is this up to me? If no, stop worrying. If yes, act.

### Scenario 2: Grieving Person
You have lost someone or something important.
**Lesson**: Memento Mori and Amor Fati. Loss is inevitable. The Stoic response: allow yourself to grieve, then accept what has happened, and turn toward what remains.

### Scenario 3: Overworked Professional
You are burning out from too much responsibility.
**Lesson**: The view from above. Zoom out. Your work matters — but not as much as you think. Your health matters more. Your family matters more.

### Scenario 4: Person Seeking Purpose
You feel aimless and unmoored.
**Lesson**: The four virtues. Ask: what is the wise thing to do? What is the fair thing? What is the brave thing? What is the moderate thing? The answers will guide you.

### Scenario 5: Someone Who Fails Constantly
You keep failing and feel like giving up.
**Lesson**: Marcus Aurelius failed too. He wrote in his journal: "If you stumble, get back up. Do not be discouraged. The path is still there."

> **Case: The Practice Journal** (Chapter 13)
> Salzgeber recommends a daily journaling practice: in the morning, remind yourself of the challenges ahead and the principles you will use. In the evening, review the day: what did I do well? What could I improve? This is Marcus Aurelius's method. The journal is not for self-criticism — it is for self-awareness.
> **Key takeaway:** Journaling is the most important Stoic practice for modern life. It creates a space between stimulus and response. It forces you to examine your judgments. It is the gym for the mind.

> **Case: The Morning Ritual** (Chapter 13)
> Marcus Aurelius's morning ritual: "When you wake up in the morning, tell yourself: the people I deal with today will be meddling, ungrateful, arrogant, dishonest, jealous, and surly. But I have seen the beauty of good and the ugliness of evil and have recognized that the wrongdoer has a nature related to my own. I can neither be harmed by any of them, nor can I be angry at my kin."
> **Key takeaway:** The Stoic morning ritual is the most powerful daily practice. It sets your intention. It prepares you for the worst. It reminds you of your values. Two minutes in the morning can change your entire day.
