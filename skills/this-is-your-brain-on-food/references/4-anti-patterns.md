# Anti-Patterns: Nutritional Mistakes

> Source: This Is Your Brain on Food, throughout

## Looking for a Magic Bullet

The most common mistake: searching for a single food or supplement that will fix everything. No single food cures depression. The overall eating pattern matters far more than any one ingredient.

> **Case: The Tryptophan Myth** (Chapter 3): Turkey is rich in tryptophan. People assume eating turkey will boost serotonin and improve mood. But tryptophan competes with other amino acids to cross the blood-brain barrier — eating tryptophan-rich food doesn't reliably increase brain serotonin. The Thanksgiving nap myth is widespread but scientifically inaccurate.

> **Key takeaway**: Food works through patterns, not individual ingredients. The Mediterranean diet works because of its cumulative effect, not any single superfood.

## Orthorexia Nervosa

Becoming so obsessed with "clean" eating that it becomes a disorder. Naidoo dedicates a section of the OCD chapter to this caution.

> **Case: The Perfect Eater** (Chapter 7): Patients who restrict their diets so severely to avoid "bad" foods that they develop anxiety around eating. The cure: flexibility. 80/20 rule — eat well 80% of the time, allow treats 20%. Perfection is not the goal — consistency is.

## Assuming Natural = Safe

Natural supplements can interact with medications. St. John's Wort interferes with antidepressants. High-dose fish oil can thin blood. Always consult a doctor before adding supplements.

> **Key takeaway**: Food complements treatment. It doesn't replace it. Never stop prescribed medication without doctor supervision.
