# Anti-Patterns

## 1. Top-Down Solutions

**The mistake**: Designing development programs from a headquarters without understanding local context.

**Fix**: Start by listening. Work with local women leaders. Adapt programs to local culture and needs.

> **Case: The Failed Sewing Program** (Chapter 3): An NGO donated sewing machines to a community — but the women there had no training, no market access, and the machines broke with no way to repair them. The program failed because it was designed without understanding local needs.
> **Key takeaway**: Good intentions without local understanding create waste. The most effective programs are designed with, not for, the community.

## 2. Ignoring Men

**The mistake**: Trying to empower women in isolation from their families and communities.

**Fix**: Include men as partners. When men understand the benefits of gender equality, they become powerful allies.

## 3. Looking for Silver Bullets

**The mistake**: Expecting one intervention to solve everything.

**Fix**: Systemic change requires multiple, coordinated interventions. Education alone isn't enough — you also need economic opportunity, legal rights, and cultural change.

## 4. Impatience

**The mistake**: Expecting fast results and moving on when they don't come.

**Fix**: Generational change takes generations. Commit for the long term and measure progress over decades, not months.

> **Case: The Quick Exit** (Chapter 6): A foundation funded a women's education program for two years. When test scores didn't improve immediately, they cut funding. The local program collapsed. Real change in educational outcomes takes 5-10 years minimum.
> **Key takeaway**: Long-term commitment is essential. Quick-hit funding creates dependency and disappointment, not lasting change.
