## 7 Principles of Mimetic Theory

### 1. Desire Is Borrowed

You do not want what you want because you want it. You want it because someone else wants it. Your desires are imitative.

### 2. The Model Becomes the Rival

The person whose desire you imitate becomes your rival for the same object. The closer the model, the more intense the rivalry.

> **Case: The Friend's Girlfriend** (Chapter 3): Girard notes that we often desire what our friends have — especially their partners. The closer the friend, the stronger the mimetic pull. This is why betrayals happen between friends, not strangers.
> **Key takeaway**: Mimetic rivalry is most intense among equals. The people closest to you are your most powerful models — and your most dangerous rivals.

### 3. The Object Becomes Secondary

In intense mimetic rivalry, the object itself becomes irrelevant. The rivalry is the point. Two people who want the same thing eventually care more about winning than about the thing itself.

### 4. The Underground Is Self-Aware Trapping

The underground man knows he is trapped in mimetic desire. He sees it clearly. He cannot escape. Self-awareness is not the same as freedom.

### 5. Violence Comes from Mimetic Crisis

When mimetic rivalry spreads through a community, it creates a crisis of "all against all." The scapegoat mechanism resolves this crisis by directing collective violence against a single victim.

> **Case: The Scapegoat in Demons** (Chapter 6): Girard reads Dostoevsky's Demons as a study of collective mimetic possession. The revolutionary group becomes possessed by shared desire for destruction. When the group turns on one of its own (Shatov), the scapegoat mechanism is enacted in miniature.
> **Key takeaway**: Groups in mimetic crisis will find a scapegoat. The victim is not the cause — the victim is the solution to the group's internal tension.

### 6. Resurrection Is Possible

Dostoevsky's later works (especially The Brothers Karamazov) point to a way out: renunciation of mimetic desire, acceptance of love, conversion.

### 7. Literature Reveals Truth

Novelists like Dostoevsky, Proust, and Flaubert discovered mimetic desire before psychologists named it. Literature is a form of knowledge.
