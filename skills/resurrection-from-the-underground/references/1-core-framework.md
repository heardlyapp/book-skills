## The Girard Framework: Mimetic Desire and the Arc to Resurrection

Girard reads Dostoevsky's novels as a sequential revelation of mimetic desire. Each novel explores a different aspect: Notes from Underground (the pure state of mimetic envy), Crime and Punishment (mimetic rivalry leading to murder), The Idiot (the failure of the anti-mimetic hero), Demons (collective mimetic possession), The Brothers Karamazov (the family as mimetic system and the possibility of redemption).

**The Key Insight**: Dostoevsky discovered mimetic desire before there was a name for it. His characters do not act from internal psychology — they react to each other's desires.

> **Case: The Underground Man's Revenge** (Chapter 2): The underground man is invited to a dinner party by former schoolmates. He knows he does not belong. He goes anyway. He hates them. He wants to impress them. He cannot leave. His desire to be accepted is entirely mimetic — he only wants their approval because they do not give it. When they ignore him, he hates them more. When one shows him kindness, he rejects it.
> **Key takeaway**: The underground man is not a rebel — he is a prisoner of borrowed desire. He cannot desire anything authentically, which is his tragedy.

> **Case: Raskolnikov's Napoleon Complex** (Chapter 4): Raskolnikov murders the pawnbroker because he wants to prove he is like Napoleon — above conventional morality. His desire is entirely mimetic: he wants what Napoleon had (power, transcendence, the right to kill). The murder does not liberate him — it traps him in guilt and exposure.
> **Key takeaway**: The desire to be "extraordinary" is itself borrowed. Raskolnikov's crime is not the act of a superior man — it is the act of a man trapped in mimetic rivalry with an imagined model.
