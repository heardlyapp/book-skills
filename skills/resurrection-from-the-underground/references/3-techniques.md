> Source: Resurrection from the Underground, Chapters 1-8

## Practical Techniques

### 1. The Desire Audit

Track a desire you have today. Ask: "Where did this desire come from? Did I choose it, or did I absorb it from someone else?" The answer reveals your mimetic models.

### 2. The Model Identification

Identify the people whose desires you imitate. They may be friends, rivals, celebrities, or abstract figures (like Raskolnikov's Napoleon). Naming the model is the first step to freedom.

> **Case: The Underground Man's Models** (Chapter 2): Girard identifies the underground man's models: his schoolmates, the officer he cannot surpass, the prostitute he wants to save. Each model reveals a different layer of borrowed desire. The underground man does not have one model — he has many, and they contradict each other.
> **Key takeaway**: We have multiple mimetic models, often in conflict. The experience of being pulled in different directions by different models is the experience of the underground.

### 3. The Rivalry Escalation Check

When you find yourself in conflict, ask: "Is this about the object — or about winning?" If winning has become more important than the thing itself, you are in mimetic rivalry.

### 4. The Scapegoat Awareness

When a group is angry, notice who they blame. The scapegoat is almost never the real cause — the cause is internal tension that needs a release. The scapegoat is a symptom.

> **Case: The Brother's Rivalry** (Chapter 8): In The Brothers Karamazov, Dmitri and Ivan are rivals for their father's inheritance and for Grushenka. The rivalry intensifies until Dmitri is accused of parricide. He did not kill his father — but the mimetic system demanded a victim. He is the scapegoat.
> **Key takeaway**: The accused is not always guilty. The system needs someone to blame. Girard's theory reveals the mechanism.
