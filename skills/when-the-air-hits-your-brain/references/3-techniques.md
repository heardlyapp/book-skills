# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *When the Air Hits Your Brain* by Frank Vertosick — practical methods extracted from the residency grind.

## Practical Tools from the OR

### Technique 1: The Post-Mortem Call (The Gary Protocol)

**When you've made a career-threatening mistake:**
1. Call someone who will tell you the truth — not a peer, not a mentor who coddles, someone who's been in the fire
2. Listen to their story of failure (Gary's ER thoracotomy story)
3. Accept their analysis: "You didn't create their disease. You were asked to help."
4. Do not resign in the heat of guilt. Wait a week. If you still want to quit, then quit.

> **Case: After Charles Bognar** (Chapter 11): Vertosick called Gary in New York. Gary told him about the steak knife patient he let die in the ER. "You didn't kill him; you were asked to step in and prevent him from dying on his own… and you couldn't." The call saved Vertosick's career.

### Technique 2: The One-Look Rule

**When you're worried about a patient or situation:**
- Go. Look. Now.
- A terrible bedside mistake is better received than expert inaction from a distance
- "Never stand when you can be sitting, never sit when you can be lying down" — but when it's your patient, break every comfort rule

> **Case: Billy Renaldo** (Chapter 8): Gary didn't accept the "hysteria" diagnosis from a distance. He went, examined, and decided on emergency surgery despite clean X-rays. If he'd stayed on the phone, Billy would have been a quadriplegic.

### Technique 3: The Pre-Op Human Check

**Before any major decision:**
1. Ask: "Who is this person?" — Name, job, family, hobbies
2. Ask: "What would they want? Not what does their scan show, what does their life story suggest?"
3. Ask: "Would I do this on my father/mother/sibling?"

> **Case: Mrs. Janeway** (Chapter 13): The nursing home wrote her off as Alzheimer's. "The horse is out of the barn." But Vertosick couldn't shake the thought: what if it's the tumor causing the dementia? He called her daughter. He found out who Mrs. Janeway was before she got sick — a sharp insurance office manager. He operated. She walked out in a business suit.

### Technique 4: The Corkscrew Drill Recovery

**When things go wrong mid-operation (or mid-project):**
1. Don't freeze. Put the largest suction in the wound (take the most direct corrective action).
2. Get help immediately — "pull the emergency light."
3. Accept the temporary fix, even if it's ugly (cotton balls, packing, temporary clips).
4. Clean up the mess, then debrief.
5. Don't close before you're stable. "Don't stop the bleeding until it's actually stopped."

### Technique 5: The "Death and Doughnuts" Debrief

Every week, the department reviewed complications and deaths. The format:
1. Present the facts without spin
2. Accept that some outcomes are PD (patient's disease), not PCP (poor choice of physician)
3. Separate the lesson from the guilt
4. Move on to the next case

**To apply to any high-stakes field:**
- Schedule a weekly, no-blame review of what went wrong
- Accept that sometimes it's the disease, not the doctor
- Give yourself permission to learn

### Technique 6: The Lead Jacket (Low-Tech Workaround)

**When you or your team is struggling:**
- Find the simplest possible intervention, not the most advanced
- Andrew's MS left him unable to control his limbs. The solution wasn't a drug — it was a lead jacket weighted with bars that forced his arms to work harder for feedback.
- Low-tech, cheap, and it worked because it addressed the actual mechanism of the problem.

> **Case: Andrew in London** (Chapter 9): The philosophy student with cerebellar ataxia from multiple sclerosis. The lead jacket let him comb his hair. "Nietzsche said, 'Whatever doesn't kill me makes me stronger.' I now know this as fact."
