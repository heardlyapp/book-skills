# 4 — Anti-Patterns

> Source: *When the Air Hits Your Brain* by Frank Vertosick — failures and near-failures across a decade of training.

## Common Mistakes Exposed by the Book

### Anti-Pattern 1: The Overconfidence Spiral

**The trap:** Success builds on itself until you believe you've mastered the game. You start thinking: "These aren't so tough."

**Why it fails:** The attending warned Vertosick: "You aren't a neurosurgeon when you clip your first aneurysm. You become a neurosurgeon when an aneurysm first blows up in your face." Two cases later, Charles Bognar's aneurysm blew. The pattern is predictable: overconfidence → complication → devastation.

> **Case: Charles Bognar** (Chapter 11): Vertosick had clipped several aneurysms successfully. "I became dangerously inflated." He was "just looking around the backside" when the aneurysm tore. The neck ripped off the parent artery. Charles died. The lesson: when you think you've mastered something, that's exactly when you're about to hurt someone.

### Anti-Pattern 2: Emotional Overinvestment

**The trap:** "If I care enough, I can save everyone." You become too attached, too emotionally invested.

**Why it fails:** "The very fact that medical ethics forbids treating your immediate family is proof that we shouldn't get so involved with a patient that we are made nervous by the possibility of failure." Rebecca's case broke Vertosick's surgical psychopath facade because he visited her every night, formed an attachment, watched her slowly die.

> **Case: Rebecca Hobson** (Chapter 10): Vertosick kept visiting the terminal baby against department norms. "Rebecca responded to the world that had greeted her… she refused to die." When she finally did, his facade cracked. The lesson: the right amount of caring is enough to motivate excellence, not enough to destroy you when you fail.

### Anti-Pattern 3: Emotional Underinvestment (Full Psychopathy)

**The trap:** "I'll detach completely. Patients are just problems to solve."

**Why it fails:** Gary showed no remorse after killing Andy Wood with an arachnoid knife slip. Vertosick watched him become a "complete surgical psychopath" and wondered: is this the price of survival? The answer came later: "Caring makes the hands shake, but it also makes us dread disaster and work with every fiber of our being to avoid it."

> **Case: The Andy Wood Nightmare** (Chapter 7): Gary's knife slipped, punctured the aneurysm during surgery. Five minutes of brain ischemia. Andy survived but was ventilator-dependent, blind, deaf. Eventually his parents asked to turn off the vent. Gary showed zero remorse. He had become Filipiano — "People who cry at funerals shouldn't become undertakers."

### Anti-Pattern 4: Mistaking Static Images for Living Humans

**The trap:** "The scan tells me everything I need to know."

**Why it fails:** The London neurologist's critique. Americans use scanning toys instead of knowing their patients. Vertosick once operated on a woman's spinal tumor without knowing her age.

> **Case: The London Ward** (Chapter 9): A Pakistani grocer with leg weakness and a spinal mass. The surgeon dismissed the MRI finding of an astrocytoma and diagnosed tuberculosis — solely because the patient was from Pakistan. Three weeks of TB treatment. By the time they operated, the patient was near-paraplegic. Nationality-based diagnosis, not evidence-based.

### Anti-Pattern 5: The "It's Just Arthritis" Trap

**The trap:** Accepting a "common" diagnosis when the symptoms don't quite fit.

**Why it fails:** Grace Catalano was told her leg pain was "just arthritis" by her family doctor. She was in a wheelchair. It was lumbar stenosis — a treatable condition. A simple decompression. She walked.

> **Case: Grace Catalano** (Chapter 13): "Oh, Doctor, you are my last hope. I have arthritis so bad in my back and legs." — "My family doctor says that it's just arthritis and I have to live with it." Vertosick did a laminectomy. She walked down the hall with her twin granddaughters. The lesson: common diagnoses can be wrong. When the symptoms don't match, look again.
