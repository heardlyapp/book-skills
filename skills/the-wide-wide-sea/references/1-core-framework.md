# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *The Wide Wide Sea* by Hampton Sides — Prologue, Books 1-5.

## The Three Frameworks

### Framework A: The First Contact Arc (Books 1-3)

Sides traces Cook's third voyage through Tahiti, Tonga, New Zealand, the Pacific Northwest, and Hawaii. The pattern repeats: arrival generates awe and generosity, exchange builds relationships, normalization breeds tension, conflict emerges from accumulated misunderstandings, and violence ends the encounter.

> **Case: The Hawaiian Arrival** (Book 3, Chapter 22): When Cook's ships Resolution and Discovery appeared off the coast of Maui in November 1778, the Hawaiians treated them as divine. The timing was extraordinary: Cook arrived during the Makahiki festival, which honored the god Lono. The ships' sails resembled the tapa cloth banners of Lono. Cook walked ashore and was met by prostrate priests. For the next several weeks, he was treated as a returning god.

> **Case: The Warm Sandalwood Welcome** (Book 2, Chapter 10): On the island of Tongatapu earlier in the voyage, Cook was welcomed with feasts, dances, and gifts. The Tongan chiefs understood reciprocity perfectly. They gave freely and expected equally generous gifts in return. Cook, trained in the Royal Navy's economy of exchange, initially matched their generosity. Over time, as resources dwindled, the imbalance grew.

### Framework B: The Overstaying Principle (Books 3-5)

Cook's fatal error: after the Makahiki festival ended, the Hawaiian priests made clear that Lono's presence was no longer required. Cook sailed away, encountered a storm, damaged his ship, and returned to Kealakekua Bay for repairs. The second arrival was very different from the first.

> **Case: The Return to Kealakekua Bay** (Book 5, Chapter 37): Cook returned to Hawaii in February 1779 after his ship's mast was damaged in a storm. The bay was eerily empty. The priests were puzzled — why had the god returned? The chief, Kalani'ōpu'u, was less welcoming. Tensions escalated over several weeks: a Hawaiian was killed in a dispute over a stolen goat, a ship's cutter was stolen. Cook responded by attempting to take the chief hostage. It was the last thing he ever did.

### Framework C: Expedition Leadership (Books 1-5)

Cook commanded a crew of 200+ men on a voyage that lasted four years. He kept scurvy at bay through strict nutritional discipline (fresh fruits whenever possible, sauerkraut, citrus). He maintained order through a mix of firm discipline and distributed responsibility. But by the end of the third year, his isolation from his officers, his temper, and his growing inflexibility concerned even his most loyal followers.

> **Case: The Floggings** (Book 2, Chapter 13): On the voyage south from Tahiti, Cook ordered several floggings for theft and desertion. He was not unusually harsh by naval standards of the time. But the frequency of punishment increased in the later stages of the voyage — a sign of rising tension and Cook's eroding patience.
