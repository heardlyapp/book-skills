# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *The Wide Wide Sea* by Hampton Sides.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The "Overstaying Your Welcome" Check

Ask three questions: (1) Are resources — goodwill, attention, or material — being depleted by my presence? (2) Have the initial welcoming gestures stopped? (3) Am I receiving signals that I should leave (indirect hints, scheduling conflicts, reduced access)?

> **Case: The Chief's Last Gift** (Book 5, Chapter 36): Kalani'ōpu'u, the Hawaiian chief, made several gestures that Cook should leave. He offered to help repair the ship quickly. He reduced the supply of food. He stopped attending meetings. Cook interpreted these as problems to be solved. They were signals to be read.
> **Key takeaway**: When hospitality cools, don't problem-solve harder. Read the signal and leave.

> **Case: The Nootka Sound Gift Exchange** (Book 4, Chapter 27): On the Pacific Northwest coast, Cook encountered the Nuu-chah-nulth people. They traded sea otter pelts for buttons, nails, and metal. The exchange was fair and understood on both sides. Cook noted their sophisticated negotiation skills. When reciprocity is balanced, relationships work.
> **Key takeaway**: Fair exchange maintains relationships. When the balance shifts, trust shifts.

In any cross-cultural relationship, periodically ask: what have I given lately? Not money — but attention, respect, presence. Cook's initial success in Tonga and Tahiti came from matching the generosity of his hosts.

### 3. The Expedition Tension Thermometer

As a long-term project leader, measure: frequency of disciplinary actions, complaints, isolation from subordinates. Cook's floggings increased, his officers stopped disagreeing with him, and he spent more time alone in his cabin. All warning signs.

### 4. The Secret Instructions Audit

When your mission has two conflicting goals (exploration vs. empire, science vs. profit), be honest about the conflict. Cook's "secret instructions" to claim lands contradicted his public commitment to "amity and friendship."

### 5. The Myth Protector

When people put you on a pedestal: surround yourself with people who knew you before. Encourage dissent. Ask for critical feedback. Cook had no one who would tell him he was wrong by the end.
