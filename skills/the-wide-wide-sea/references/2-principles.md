# 2 — Key Principles

> Source: *The Wide Wide Sea* by Hampton Sides — Books 1-5.

## The 7 Principles

### 1. First Contact Is a Moment of Extraordinary Fragility

The first encounter between two radically different cultures is a once-in-history event. How it unfolds determines everything that follows. Cook understood this — his instructions from the Admiralty emphasized "amity and friendship." But the same instructions also demanded territory claims.

### 2. Hospitality Has a Shelf Life

The Hawaiians were generous hosts when Cook arrived during the Makahiki festival. When the festival ended, the welcome ended. Cook's failure to recognize this was fatal.

> **Case: The Tahitian Transformation** (Book 3, Chapter 18): When Cook first arrived in Tahiti on earlier voyages, he was welcomed as a potential ally. By the time of his third voyage, the Tahitians had learned from European contact — they were more wary, less generous, more calculating in trade. The innocence of first contact had been lost in a decade.

### 3. Reciprocity Is the Basis of Trust

Pacific island societies operated on gift exchange. Cook succeeded when he matched generosity with generosity. He failed when he took without giving.

### 4. The Leader Is the Last to Know When Things Are Going Wrong

Cook's officers saw the deterioration in Hawaii before Cook did. Some tried to warn him. His growing isolation from his command staff prevented him from seeing what they saw.

### 5. The Myth Can Kill

> **Case: The Lono Theory** (Book 5, Chapter 33-39): The question that scholars still debate: did Cook actually believe he was Lono? Sides presents evidence that at minimum, Cook enjoyed the treatment and may have begun to believe the myth. When the apotheosis collapsed, Cook's judgment collapsed with it. The leader who believes his own mythology is dangerous to himself and everyone around him.

### 6. Small Misunderstandings Escalate

Cook was killed because a boat was stolen. The attempt to recover it through hostage-taking escalated into a beach confrontation. The violence was not inevitable. Each step seemed like a reasonable action in the moment.

### 7. Exploration and Exploitation Are the Same Coin

Cook was an Enlightenment explorer and a British naval officer. He mapped the Pacific with extraordinary accuracy. He also claimed lands for the Crown. The two missions were inseparable.
