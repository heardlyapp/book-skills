# 4 — Overthinking and Analysis Paralysis

> Source: "13 Things Mentally Strong Women Don't Do" by Amy Morin

## Anti-Pattern: Overthinking Masquerading as Careful Thinking

Overthinking is one of the most common habits that undermines women's mental strength. It feels productive — you're thinking, analyzing, considering all angles — but it produces nothing.

> **Case: The Decision Paralysis** (Chapter 5 — Don't Overthink Everything): "One of Morin's clients spent six months researching which car to buy. She read reviews, compared safety ratings, calculated resale value — but never test-drove a single vehicle. Her overthinking prevented her from making any decision at all." Morin uses this story to show that overthinking is a form of avoidance disguised as diligence.
> **Key takeaway**: Overthinking is not careful thinking. Careful thinking leads to a decision. Overthinking leads to more thinking. Set a deadline for decisions and gather enough information — not all of it.

## Anti-Pattern: The Rumination Trap

Ruminating — replaying past events or worrying about future ones — is a major drain on mental energy.

> **Case: The Replay Loop** (Chapter 5): "A woman Morin worked with would replay conversations in her head for days after they happened, analyzing every word she said and wondering how she could have done better. This rumination left her exhausted and unable to focus on the present." Morin explains that rumination is a habit that can be broken.
> **Key takeaway**: Rumination feels like problem-solving but isn't. The brain treats it as productive because it's active, but it's actually looping. The fix: set a specific "worry time" each day, and when the rumination starts, tell yourself "I'll think about this during my worry time."
