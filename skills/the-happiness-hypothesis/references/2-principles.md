# The Negativity Bias — Why Bad Is Stronger Than Good

> Source: The Happiness Hypothesis, Chapters 2, 4, 5

## The Primacy of Negative

Our brains evolved to detect threats first and pleasures second. An animal that missed a reward might lose a meal. An animal that missed a threat might lose its life. The result: negative stimuli, events, and feedback have more psychological impact than positive ones.

### The LIKE-O-METER

Haidt describes an automatic evaluation system — the LIKE-O-METER — that instantly judges everything as good or bad. It's always running and mostly outside conscious control. This system shows the negativity bias: the same stimulus is judged more harshly when framed as a loss than as a gain.

### The Cortical Lottery

People are born with different baseline levels of positivity. Some people's LIKE-O-METER defaults to "slightly positive" (optimists). Others default to "slightly negative" (pessimists). This is the S in the happiness formula — the genetic set range.

### How to Change Your Mind (The Elephant)

| Technique | Effect | Mechanism |
|-----------|--------|-----------|
| Cognitive-behavioral therapy | Best-evidence psychological treatment | Teaches the Elephant new patterns by repeatedly exposing automatic thoughts and replacing them |
| Meditation | Reduces reactivity | Weakens the Elephant's grip; teaches watching without reacting |
| Medication (SSRIs) | Raises the set range for some | Directly affects the Elephant's emotional baseline |
| Gratitude practice | Counteracts hedonic adaptation | Forces attention to positives, compensating for negativity bias |
| Exposure | Reduces fear | Teaches the Elephant that feared outcomes don't occur |

> **Case: The Cortical Lottery (Chapter 2)**
> Haidt presents research showing that happiness has a strong genetic component. Twin studies show that identical twins reared apart have more similar happiness levels than fraternal twins reared together. About 50% of happiness variance is explained by genetic set range. But this is not destiny — the V (voluntary activities) in the happiness formula accounts for 40% of variance. The set range is the baseline, not the ceiling.
> **Key takeaway**: Your happiness baseline is partly inherited, but you have more control than you think. The 50% set range is not a trap — it's a starting point. The V in the formula (voluntary activities) gives you 40% leverage. You can move within your range and even shift it over time.

> **Case: The Negativity Bias in Relationships** (Chapter 4)
> Haidt cites marriage researcher John Gottman's work: the ideal ratio of positive to negative interactions in a stable marriage is about 5:1. Below that, the relationship is at risk. The negativity bias means that one cutting remark can undo an evening of warmth. This doesn't mean "never criticize" — it means criticism must be delivered in a context of abundant positivity. The 5:1 ratio is not a rule — it's a mathematical consequence of the way our brains process positive vs negative information.
> **Key takeaway**: If you want a healthy relationship, you need roughly five times more positive interactions than negative ones. This is not just nice-to-have — it's necessary to counteract the brain's negativity bias. Check your ratio.
