> Source: She's Come Undone, Wally Lamb (1992). A novel about survival.

# Core Framework — Trauma, Survival, and the Long Road Back

## The Model

Dolores's journey: trauma → silence → weight as armor → breakdown → hospitalization → therapy → telling her story → rebuilding → learning to live.

> **Case: The Childhood Trauma** (Early chapters): Dolores's childhood is marked by losses: her father leaves, her mother has a breakdown, her grandmother — her anchor — dies when she is eleven. Then the worst happens: a neighbor's son rapes her. She tells no one. She does not tell her mother. She does not tell her grandmother before she dies. The silence becomes a weight she carries through her entire life.
> **Key takeaway**: Unspoken trauma does not disappear. It goes into the body. Dolores's weight gain begins after the rape. The body remembers what the mind tries to forget.

> **Case: The Weight as Armor** (Throughout): The novel traces Dolores's weight gain across decades. At her heaviest, she weighs over 300 pounds. She calls herself "the whale." She captures her life through the television, watching others live while she hides. The weight is not a failure. It is armor. It protects her from a world that has hurt her. It keeps people at a distance. It makes her feel big when she feels small.
> **Key takeaway**: When you feel unsafe in the world, your body will find ways to protect you. Weight is armor. The question is not how to lose the weight but how to feel safe enough to put the armor down.

> **Case: The Breakdown and Hospitalization** (Later chapters): Dolores's marriage fails. She attempts suicide. She is hospitalized. In the hospital, she begins to tell her story. She participates in group therapy. She starts to understand the connections between her childhood trauma, her weight, her compulsive eating, and her depression. The hospital is not a prison. It is the first safe place she has ever had.
> **Key takeaway**: Sometimes you need to fall apart completely before you can put yourself back together. The breakdown is not the end. It is the necessary collapse of a structure that was never going to hold.
