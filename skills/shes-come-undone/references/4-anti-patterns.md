> Source: She's Come Undone, Wally Lamb (1992).

# Anti-Patterns — Hiding, Silence, and Waiting to Be Saved

## The Central Fallacy

The most dangerous assumption: that someone else will save you. Dolores waits to be rescued — by her mother, by her husband, by a man, by a therapist. She learns, painfully, that no one can save her but herself. People can help. But the work is hers.

> **Case: The Marriage as Rescue** (Mid-novel): Dolores marries a man named Dante. She believes marriage will fix her. It does not. He has his own problems. He is not capable of being her savior. The marriage becomes another disappointment. Dolores learns what many learn: another person cannot fill the hole inside you. Only you can do that.
> **Key takeaway**: No one else can fill the hole inside you. A partner, a child, a friend — none of them can do the work that only you can do. Love helps. But it does not heal.

> **Case: The Television as Life** (Throughout): Dolores spends years watching television instead of living. She knows the characters better than her neighbors. She cares about fictional lives more than her own. The TV is not a harmless escape. It is a substitute for living.
> **Key takeaway**: Any activity that substitutes for life is dangerous. Whether it is TV, social media, work, or anything else. You cannot scroll your way to happiness.

> **Case: The Silence** (Throughout): Dolores does not tell anyone about the rape for decades. The silence protects her from having to feel the pain. It also prevents her from healing. The novel suggests: silence is the most dangerous anti-pattern of all. Not because speaking guarantees healing but because silence guarantees that healing will never begin.
> **Key takeaway**: Silence protects nothing. It preserves the wound unhealed. Speaking does not guarantee healing. But it is the precondition for it.
