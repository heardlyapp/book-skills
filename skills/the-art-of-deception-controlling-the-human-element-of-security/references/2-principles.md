# Principles: Pretexting and Information Gathering

> Source: The Art of Deception, Chapters 2-6

## The Power of Pretexting

A pretext is a fabricated scenario. The social engineer researches the target, gathers names and titles, then uses that information to build a believable story.

> **Case: The New Employee Ploy** (Chapter 2): A social engineer calls IT support posing as a new employee in a different city. He says he needs help setting up remote access. He uses correct internal terminology, knows the names of managers, and sounds legitimate. He gets full network access.

> **Case: The Lost PDA** (Chapter 4): A social engineer calls claiming to have found a company PDA. He offers to return it, asking for the owner's name. The helpful receptionist provides it. The social engineer now has an internal name for the next call.

> **Key takeaway**: Pretexting works because the social engineer does their homework. The more information they have, the more believable their story.

## Small Information, Big Damage

Seemingly harmless pieces combine into devastating intelligence.

> **Case: The Dumpster Dive** (Chapter 5): A social engineer retrieves a phone directory, a project memo, and a vendor invoice from company trash. With these three documents, he can now call any employee by name, reference active projects, and impersonate the vendor.

## Building Trust Through Rapport

Social engineers don't demand — they build rapport. Friendly conversation, shared interests, small favors.

> **Key takeaway**: The research phase is more important than the attack. Information equals credibility. Trust is built through preparation.
