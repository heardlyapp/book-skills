# Anti-Patterns: Security Mistakes

> Source: The Art of Deception, Chapters 14-15

## Relying Only on Technology

The biggest mistake: buying better firewalls and ignoring the human element.

> **Case: The Secure Company** (Chapter 14): A company invested millions in technical security — encryption, firewalls, IDS. A social engineer called the help desk, pretended to be a remote employee, and got VPN credentials. Millions in technology bypassed by one phone call.

> **Key takeaway**: Technology is important — but it's useless against a socially engineered human. Invest equally in awareness.

## Assuming Security Is "Their Job"

Every employee is a security guard. Cleaning staff, receptionists, temps — all are targets.

> **Case: The Temp Worker** (Chapter 14): A social engineer gets hired as a temporary worker. No one questions him because he has a badge. He spends two weeks collecting passwords, document templates, and insider knowledge.

> **Key takeaway**: Security is everyone's job. The cleaner who holds the door for a "delivery person" may be the weakest link.

## No Verification Procedures

When anyone can call and request a password reset, there is no security.

> **Case: The Single Call** (Chapter 15): In many organizations, a single phone call can get a password reset. Mitnick's fix: require verification through a second channel. Call-back, manager approval, or in-person ID.

> **Key takeaway**: Without verification procedures, every employee is a potential vulnerability.
