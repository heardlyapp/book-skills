# Reference: Incommensurability

## Overview
Incommensurability is Kuhn's most controversial idea. It means that competing paradigms cannot be fully compared because they use different standards, concepts, and methods.

## Case: Different Worlds
Kuhn argues that scientists in different paradigms "live in different worlds." The world of an Aristotelian physicist is different from the world of a Newtonian physicist. They see different things when they observe motion. The concepts they use (force, mass, motion) have different meanings. There is no neutral observation language that can bridge the gap.

## Case: Translation Failure
If two paradigms are incommensurable, their concepts cannot be fully translated. A Newtonian concept of "force" cannot be expressed in Aristotelian terms. This does not mean the paradigms are incomparable — we can compare their problem-solving effectiveness. But we cannot compare them point by point as if they were addressing the same problems in the same way.

## Case: The Rationality Question
Incommensurability raises the question: if paradigms cannot be compared by a neutral standard, is the choice between them rational? Kuhn's answer is complex. The choice is not fully rational in the algorithmic sense, but it is guided by shared values (accuracy, consistency, simplicity, fruitfulness). These values are not rules but criteria that scientists apply differently.

## Case: The Duck-Rabbit
The Gestalt switch figure of the duck-rabbit is Kuhn's visual metaphor for paradigm change. The same lines can be seen as a duck or a rabbit, but not both at once. Similarly, the same data can be interpreted within either paradigm, but not both simultaneously. The shift between paradigms is experienced as a sudden reorganization of perception, not as a gradual adjustment.
