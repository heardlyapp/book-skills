# Reference: Science as a Social Process

## Overview
Kuhn's book is as much about the sociology of science as about the philosophy of science. The scientific community's structure and norms shape how knowledge is produced and what counts as knowledge.

## Case: The Role of Consensus
Science requires consensus. Without shared paradigms, normal science cannot function. The pre-paradigm phase ends when a community achieves consensus on a paradigm. The community's agreement is what makes normal science possible. Kuhn argues that this consensus is essential — but it also means that science is shaped by social factors: who holds authority, which institutions train scientists, which journals publish research.

## Case: Resistance and Conversion
Paradigm shifts are not accepted because of logical arguments. They are accepted because of conversion — a process that involves persuasion, faith, and eventually generational change. Kuhn famously quotes Max Planck: "A new scientific truth does not triumph by convincing its opponents and making them see the light, but rather because its opponents eventually die, and a new generation grows up that is familiar with it."

## Case: Progress After Kuhn
Kuhn's model challenges the traditional view of scientific progress as cumulative and teleological. But he does not argue that science does not progress. Science progresses through normal science (puzzle-solving) and revolution (paradigm shifts). The progress is measured by the paradigm's ability to solve puzzles. There is no final truth, but there is progress away from less adequate paradigms and toward more adequate ones.
