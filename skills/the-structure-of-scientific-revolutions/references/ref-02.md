# Reference: Anomaly and Crisis

## Overview
Anomalies are observations that the paradigm cannot explain. At first, they are ignored or dismissed. But when they accumulate, they create a crisis that can lead to revolution.

## Case: The Copernican Anomalies
The Ptolemaic system (Earth-centered) was the dominant paradigm for 1,500 years. It predicted planetary positions with reasonable accuracy. But anomalies accumulated: the retrograde motion of planets required increasingly complex epicycles. By the 16th century, the system had become so cumbersome that it was losing predictive power. Copernicus's heliocentric model was initially a rival paradigm that explained the anomalies more simply.

## Case: The Discovery of Anomalies
Kuhn emphasizes that anomalies are not self-evident. They are only recognized as anomalies within a paradigm. Before the paradigm exists, every observation is equally anomalous. The paradigm provides the standards that make certain observations stand out as problems. This is why pre-paradigm sciences produce less progress: without a shared paradigm, every observation is potentially anomalous, and there is no basis for consensus.

## Case: The Crisis Response
When anomalies become serious, the scientific community enters a crisis. Scientists propose modifications to the paradigm, ad hoc explanations, and alternative frameworks. The period is characterized by professional uncertainty, debate, and division. Younger scientists are more likely to propose and accept new paradigms; older scientists resist. The crisis ends when a new paradigm emerges that resolves the anomalies and attracts a consensus.
