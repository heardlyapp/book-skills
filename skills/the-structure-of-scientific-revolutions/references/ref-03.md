# Reference: The Paradigm Shift

## Overview
A paradigm shift is not an addition to existing knowledge — it is a transformation of the framework itself. Kuhn compares it to a political revolution or a Gestalt switch.

## Case: The Copernican Revolution
The shift from Ptolemy to Copernicus is Kuhn's central example. The Copernican model was not more accurate initially — it required complex epicycles too. It gained acceptance because it was more elegant, because it resolved anomalies in a unified way, and eventually because better data (Tycho Brahe) and better theory (Kepler's ellipses) made it more accurate. The revolution took over a century and required a new generation to become the scientific establishment.

## Case: The Chemical Revolution
Lavoisier's oxygen theory replaced the phlogiston theory of combustion. Phlogiston explained combustion as the release of a substance. Oxygen explained it as the absorption of a substance. The two theories used different concepts, different categories, and different interpretations of the same experiments. Kuhn describes how phlogiston chemists literally saw something different when they looked at combustion.

## Case: The Einstein Revolution
Einstein's relativity replaced Newtonian mechanics for high-speed phenomena. Newton's theory was not "wrong" — it remained accurate for everyday speeds. But Einstein's framework reconceptualized space, time, mass, and energy. The paradigm shift was dramatic: concepts that had been absolute (time, simultaneity) became relative. The shift required a Gestalt switch in how physicists thought about the world.
