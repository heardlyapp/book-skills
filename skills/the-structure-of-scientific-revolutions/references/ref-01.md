# Reference: Normal Science

## Overview
Normal science is the everyday work of scientists within an established paradigm. It is puzzle-solving, not discovery. Scientists do not question the paradigm — they apply it.

## Case: Puzzle-Solving
Normal science operates like a jigsaw puzzle. The paradigm provides the framework (the picture on the box), and scientists fit the pieces (solving specific problems). The goal is not to question the framework but to demonstrate skill in applying it. This is why normal science is so productive: scientists know exactly what problems are worth solving and what methods are appropriate for solving them.

## Case: Textbook Science
Science textbooks present the current paradigm as the final truth. They rewrite history to make the paradigm seem inevitable. The pre-paradigm phase and the revolutionary transition are erased. Students learn the paradigm as the way the world really is. This is essential for training — it gives new scientists a shared framework — but it distorts the history of science.

## Case: The Cumulative Illusion
Normal science creates the appearance of cumulative progress. Each generation of scientists builds on the work of the previous one. But the progress is within the paradigm, not across paradigms. When the paradigm shifts, much of the previous work becomes irrelevant. The cumulative appearance of normal science hides the discontinuous nature of scientific revolution.

## Case: The Role of Exemplars
An exemplar is a concrete problem solution that serves as a model for normal science. Students learn science by studying exemplars — not by learning abstract rules. The paradigm is embedded in these examples. Einstein's theory of relativity is not just a set of equations; it is a way of seeing the world that students absorb through working through specific problems.
