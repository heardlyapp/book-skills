# Practical Exercises — Stoic Techniques for Daily Life

> Source: Meditations, Books 2, 3, 4, 7, 8, 10, 12

## The Stoic Toolbox

Marcus's Meditations is not a systematic textbook — it's a personal journal of exercises he used to train his mind. These are the key techniques.

### 1. Memento Mori

"You could leave life right now. Let that determine what you do and say and think." (Book 2, 11)

Marcus returns to the shortness of life obsessively — not because he's morbid but because death-awareness is the sharpest tool for cutting through triviality. When you're upset about a meeting, remember: you will die. Is this worth your precious time?

**Exercise**: Imagine every person you meet today is on their last day. How would you treat them? Now imagine the same of yourself.

### 2. Negative Visualization (Premeditatio Malorum)

"When you kiss your child good night, say to yourself: tomorrow you may be dead." (Book 11, 34)

This sounds dark but it's the opposite — it's gratitude training. By imagining the loss of what you love, you appreciate it more now. The technique also prepares you for adversity: if you've already imagined losing your job, your health, your reputation — when it happens, you're ready.

### 3. The View from Above

"Think of the whole universe of matter and how small your share of it. Think of the whole of time, how brief your moment." (Book 9, 30)

Cosmic scale as a perspective tool. Imagine the Earth from space. Your city. Your building. Your body. Your problem. The scale reduces everything to its true proportion.

### 4. The Physician's Diagnosis

"When something happens that seems unbearable, examine it: what part of it is truly unbearable? Usually it's your judgement that makes it so, not the event itself. And what about the event itself — can you bear it? Of course you can." (Book 10, 3)

Break down any problem into its physical components. An insult: just sound waves. A loss: atoms rearranging. An injury: nerve signals. The body can handle it. The question is whether your mind can.

### 5. The Two-Question Evening Review

"What have I done today that was wrong? What could I have done better?"

Marcus doesn't describe his evening routine explicitly, but the entire Meditations is evidence of self-examination. The Stoic practice of nightly journaling: examine your actions, your reactions, your judgements. Don't beat yourself up — learn.

> **Case: Marcus on the Plague and the Marcomannic Wars** (Books 2-3)
> Marcus wrote much of the Meditations while on campaign against the Germanic tribes (the Marcomannic Wars) and while the Antonine Plague was devastating the Roman Empire (5 million dead). He was facing military defeat, political betrayal, and a pandemic — and his response was to write passages like: "The universe is change. Life is opinion." Not denial — acceptance. Not surrender — disciplined action within the sphere of control.
> **Key takeaway**: The Meditations was not written in a library. It was written in a tent, in the mud, surrounded by dying men, while an empire was cracking. This is what gives the words their weight. Stoicism is not a philosophy for peaceful times — it is a philosophy for exactly these moments.

> **Case: The Retreating Enemy** (Book 2, 17)
> "Remember how long you've been putting this off, how many extensions the gods have given you, and you didn't use them. At some point you have to realize what it is you want. There is a limit to the time assigned to you, and if you don't use it to free yourself, it will be gone and will never return." This passage captures the urgency of Marcus's message. He is not speaking to a hypothetical audience — he is speaking to himself, in the middle of the night, probably after a day of battle, reminding himself that the time to become good is now.
> **Key takeaway**: Procrastination on virtue is the most common and most dangerous form of delay. There is no "someday" for becoming the person you want to be. The only day is today.
