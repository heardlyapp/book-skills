# Anti-Patterns — What Takes You Away from Virtue

> Source: Meditations, throughout; especially Books 2, 5, 6, 8, 9, 11

## The Six Traps Marcus Warns Against

### 1. Caring About Reputation More Than Character
"You want praise from people who curse each other behind their backs? You want to be well thought of by people who don't even think well of themselves?" (Book 8, 14)

Marcus returns to this repeatedly: the pursuit of fame is absurd because (a) the people whose approval you seek are flawed, (b) their approval won't last, and (c) even if it did, it means nothing compared to the quality of your own character. "What is reputation? A bubble. What is fame? The noise of people who will forget you in a generation."

### 2. Blaming Others Instead of Taking Responsibility
"Someone blames you? Don't agree with their assessment. Someone honors you? Don't let it go to your head. The good and the bad are within you." (Book 8, 24)

The reflexive response to a problem is to ask "Whose fault is this?" Marcus says: the only question that matters is "What can I do about it?" Blame is a form of surrender — it hands power over your emotional state to someone else.

### 3. Pursuing Pleasure as Life's Goal
"Pleasures are deceptive. When they promise you happiness, they deliver dependence." (Book 9, 1)

Marcus is not a Puritan — he doesn't say pleasure is bad. He says making pleasure your goal is self-defeating because pleasure-seeking leads to addiction, not fulfillment. The goal of life is not pleasure but virtue; pleasure, when it comes, is a byproduct.

### 4. Procrastinating Virtue
"Stop wandering about in circles. At some point you have to start living the life of a good person. Put an end to your hesitation." (Book 5, 9)

The most common Stoic failure is knowing the right thing and not doing it. Marcus is impatient with this. "Waste no more time arguing what a good person should be. Be one." The gap between knowing and doing is the only gap that matters.

### 5. Complaining About Circumstances
"Don't be overheard complaining — not even to yourself." (Book 8, 9)

Complaining is a trap because it reinforces the belief that external events have power over you. Marcus's rule: if you can change it, change it. If you can't, accept it. Complaining is the worst of both worlds — you neither change the situation nor accept it.

### 6. Letting the Past or Future Control the Present
"The present is all you have to live in. The past is gone. The future is unknown. To let either one poison your present is to refuse the only life you have." (Book 3, 10)

Regret and anxiety are the twin thieves of presence. Marcus's cure: focus entirely on what is in front of you right now. The Stoic definition of greatness: doing the present task well, without distraction, without complaint, with full attention.

> **Case: Marcus on Alexander the Great and His Mule Driver** (Book 6, 24)
> "Alexander the Great and his mule driver both died. The same. And they now share the same state — absorbed into the same generative principle." Marcus repeatedly uses this comparison to puncture the illusion of status. Alexander conquered the known world; his mule driver carried his baggage. Both are dead. Both are dust. The difference that seemed so important while they lived has vanished entirely.
> **Key takeaway**: Status anxiety is cured by death-awareness. Alexander and his mule driver. The billionaire and the beggar. The emperor and the slave. In the end, they are indistinguishable. This is not to diminish ambition — but to ask: are you pursuing what actually matters?

> **Case: The Poison of Envy** (Book 6, 20)
> "Envy is the opposite of the Stoic way. You see someone else succeeding and you feel diminished. But the universe is abundant, not scarce. Another person's success does not reduce you. The Stoic asks: did they act virtuously? If yes, they deserve it. If no, why are you jealous of something that has no real value?"
> **Key takeaway**: Envy is a judgement error — it assumes that life is a zero-sum game and that another's gain is your loss. The Stoic views virtue as the only good, and virtue is not scarce. Another person's virtue enhances the world; it does not diminish you.
