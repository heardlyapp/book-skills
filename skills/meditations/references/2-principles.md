# Emotions, Judgements, and the Way of the Prohairesis

> Source: Meditations, Books 4, 5, 7, 9, 11

## The Core Stoic Principle of Emotion

"It is not events that disturb people, it is their judgements concerning them." This sentence (from Epictetus, which Marcus absorbed deeply) is the operational core of Stoic emotional practice. Emotions are not automatic — they are the result of a judgement we make about an event. Change the judgement, change the emotion.

### The Three-Step Emotional Protocol

| Step | Action | Question to Ask |
|------|--------|-----------------|
| 1 | Pause | Is this in my control or not? |
| 2 | Examine | What judgement am I making about this event? |
| 3 | Replace | What would be a truer, more helpful judgement? |

### The Ten Stoic Passions to Watch For

Marcus frequently warns against these emotional distortions:
1. **Anger** — The most destructive. "How much more painful the consequences of anger are than the things that provoke it."
2. **Fear of death** — "To fear death is to fear something you cannot experience."
3. **Anxiety about the future** — "The present is all you have. The future belongs to the universe."
4. **Desire for praise** — "What is praise, after all? The noise of people who don't know you."
5. **Envy** — "You want praise from people who curse each other behind their backs."
6. **Pleasure-seeking** — "Pleasure is neither good nor bad. What matters is whether your pursuit of it compromises your character."
7. **Self-pity** — "Stop feeling sorry for yourself. You are better than that."
8. **Resentment** — "The best revenge is not to become like the one who wronged you."
9. **Attachment to externals** — "Remember: things have no power over you. Only your judgements do."
10. **Blaming others** — "This happened because of him! No. This happened, and you can choose how to respond."

> **Case: Marcus on Anger** (Book 11, section 18)
> Marcus devotes a remarkable passage to anger. He compares it to smoke: "Anger is like smoke — it's a sign that something is burning inside, but it doesn't tell you what." He argues that anger is always a mistake because it clouds the judgement that you need to solve the actual problem. "To be angry at something is to turn your back on reason." He also notes that anger is often a form of laziness — it's easier to be angry than to understand.
> **Key takeaway**: Anger is not a tool or a signal — it's a failure of understanding. The Stoic response to a problem is not anger but action. If you can fix it, fix it. If you can't, why be angry?

> **Case: The View from Above** (Book 7, section 48; Book 9, section 30)
> "You have seen a hand, a foot, a head severed from the rest of the body. That's what you make of yourself when you isolate yourself from others." But the view from above is also cosmological: picture the vast sweep of time, the endless generations before and after, the tiny stage of your life. "Think of the whole universe of matter and how small your share of it. Think of the whole of time, how brief your moment." This exercise shrinks problems to their true proportion.
> **Key takeaway**: Scale is a perspective tool. The problem that feels enormous in your current frame shrinks to nothing when placed against the backdrop of eternity and the cosmos. This is not to dismiss your pain but to help you see it accurately.
