# Key Principles — Prequel: An American Fight Against Fascism

## I. American Fascism Was Homegrown

"It didn't come from Germany — it was made in America." The Nazi agents exploited existing American movements: populist demagogues (Huey Long), antisemitic radio priests (Coughlin), paramilitary groups (Silver Shirts), and establishment figures (Philip Johnson). Case: Lawrence Dennis wrote "The Coming American Fascism" — an original American text, not a German import.

## II. Congress Had Nazi Collaborators

"Members of the United States Congress were actively working with Nazi agents." Viereck's network inside Congress included Senator Lundeen, Senator Reynolds, Representative Fish, and Senator Wheeler. They used franking privileges, introduced Nazi talking points, and protected each other. Case: "Viereck used Congressional franking to distribute Nazi propaganda."

## III. The Media Was Weaponized

"Father Coughlin's radio broadcasts reached tens of millions." Newspapers, radio, and direct mail were used to spread antisemitism, isolationism, and pro-fascist propaganda. Case: Henry Ford's newspaper, The Dearborn Independent, published the Protocols of the Elders of Zion.

## IV. The Resistance Was Courageous and Effective

"Leon Lewis built a spy network that infiltrated every major fascist group in Southern California." Journalists (Dillard Stokes, John C. Metcalfe), prosecutors (O. John Rogge, William Power Maloney), and citizen spies risked everything. Case: "Lewis's network fed information to law enforcement for over a decade."

## V. The System Failed — But It Also Worked

"The sedition trial of 1944 ended in a mistrial. Most defendants walked free." But the fascist movement was seriously disrupted. Key figures were marginalized. Case: "The fight against fascism was never really won — it was postponed."

## VI. The Parallels to Today Are Uncomfortable

"Every tactic the 1930s fascists used is back." Direct mail → social media. Radio priests → podcast demagogues. Isolationist Congress members → modern populists. Conspiracy theories → QAnon. Case: "The same arguments used to oppose entering WWII are used today against international alliances."

## VII. Democracy Requires Active Defense

"The fight against fascism is never over." The book's central lesson: democracy doesn't defend itself automatically. It requires vigilance from citizens, journalists, prosecutors, and elected officials. Case: "The antifascists of the 1930s didn't wait for permission — they organized."
