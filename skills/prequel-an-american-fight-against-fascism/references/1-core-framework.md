# Core Framework — Prequel: An American Fight Against Fascism

## Prologue: The House of the Vampire

George Sylvester Viereck, a German immigrant and American citizen, published a gay vampire novel in 1907. He "worshipped Wilde as one of his three life models, alongside Napoleon and Christ." By the 1930s, Viereck had become one of the most effective Nazi agents operating in America. He used his literary credentials and charm to infiltrate Congress and spread Nazi propaganda. Case: Viereck was "George Sylvester Viereck's first collaborators inside Congress" — he recruited Senator Ernest Lundeen and his staff.

## Chapters 1-3: Early Infiltration

**The Glass House.** Philip Johnson, the famous architect, was an open admirer of Hitler and fascism. He traveled to Germany in the 1930s and returned as an enthusiastic supporter. Case: Johnson attended Nazi rallies and came back "as an American fanboy of Hitler."

**"Come to My Feast."** Lawrence Dennis wrote "The Coming American Fascism" and argued that American democracy was a failed experiment. He was an intellectual architect of American fascist thought. Case: Dennis argued fascism was America's natural next step.

**Umwege (Detours).** Father Charles Coughlin, the "Radio Priest," reached tens of millions through his weekly broadcasts. He was virulently antisemitic and pro-fascist. "He had an audience in the tens of millions."

## Chapters 4-9: The Movement Grows

**Huey Long.** "Louisiana was not quite ready" — Long gave America a 1930s test-drive for dictatorship. His populism, control of state institutions, and demagogic style were a blueprint.

**Silver Shirts.** William Dudley Pelley believed Jesus had visited him multiple times and "pined after the job of America's Hitler." His paramilitary "Silver Legion" was modeled after Nazi brownshirts.

**Tikkun Olam.** The antifascist resistance. Leon Lewis, an attorney in Southern California, built a spy network that infiltrated fascist groups and fed information to law enforcement. Case: "The antifascist spymaster of Southern California, American hero."

## Chapters 14-18: Congress and Media

**Congressional Collaboration.** Senator Ernest Lundeen and his wife Norma were Viereck's key collaborators. Senator Robert Rice Reynolds was a "rip-roaring pro-fascist pol." Representative Hamilton Fish was "on good terms with senior officials in Hitler's Germany." Case: Viereck used Congressional franking privileges to distribute Nazi propaganda.

**Hollywood!** The film industry fought back. Hollywood produced antifascist films and Leon Turrou, an FBI agent, sparked a new kind of cinema. Case: Warner Bros released "Confessions of a Nazi Spy" in 1939.

## Chapters 19-22: Justice and Sedition

**O. John Rogge.** The Justice Department prosecutor who pursued the great sedition trial of 1944, bringing 30 fascist leaders to trial. "He was up to his eyebrows in fascist/Nazi treachery for more than a decade."

**William Power Maloney.** "A hard-charging and cantankerous Justice Department prosecutor." He took on the Viereck case despite political pressure.

**Epilogue: The Storm Will Pass.** Most of the fascists escaped serious punishment. The sedition trial ended in a mistrial. Many returned to public life. "The fight against fascism in America was never really won — it was postponed."
