# 2. Principles — The Laws of Persistence

> Source: "Grinding It Out" by Ray Kroc (1977). Principles extracted from Kroc's autobiography, particularly Chapters 1-5 (early career and franchise founding) and 8-12 (growth and expansion).

## The 7 Principles of Grinding It Out

### Principle 1: Stay Green to Keep Growing
Kroc's favorite saying: "As long as you're green you're growing, as soon as you're ripe you start to rot." He lived this by constantly looking for the next opportunity — even after he had "made it." When he discovered McDonald's at age 52, he was still selling Multimixers and paper cups. He never stopped looking.

| Stage | Age | What he was doing |
|-------|-----|------------------|
| Paper cup salesman | 20s-30s | Selling Lily cups, learning sales |
| Multimixer salesman | 40s-early 50s | Own business, still selling |
| McDonald's franchisor | 52+ | Started the chain from scratch |

> **Case: Kroc's Double Workday** (Chapter 3): In his early twenties, Kroc worked two jobs: selling paper cups during the day and playing piano at radio station WGES at night. He would start at 7 AM selling cups, arrive at the station at 6 PM for two hours of piano, take a break from 8-10 PM, then return until 2 AM. The only break was Sunday. When asked why he pushed so hard, Kroc said "I never considered my dreams wasted energy; they were invariably linked to some form of action."
> **Key takeaway**: Persistence is not a single heroic act — it's the accumulation of thousands of ordinary days where you keep showing up.

### Principle 2: Seize the Current When It Serves
Kroc framed his entire life philosophy around Shakespeare's lines from Julius Caesar: "There is a tide in the affairs of men, which, taken at the flood, leads on to fortune; omitted, all the voyage of their life is bound in shallows and in miseries." When he saw the opportunity in McDonald's, he acted immediately — mortgaging his home, borrowing money, and committing his entire future to the venture.

> **Case: The Multimixer Decision** (Chapter 1): Kroc was 52, diabetic, arthritic, and had lost his gall bladder and most of his thyroid. He had a secure job selling Multimixers. But when he saw eight machines running at McDonald's, he knew this was his tide. He didn't calculate or deliberate — he flew to San Bernardino the next day, watched the operation for hours, and convinced the McDonald brothers to let him franchise their system. The entire decision took days, not months.
> **Key takeaway**: When the flood tide of opportunity comes, you don't need more analysis. You need courage and speed. The opportunity will not wait for you to be ready.

### Principle 3: Obsess Over the Customer's Experience
Kroc understood that a seemingly minor flaw — a slightly stale fry, a dirty parking lot, a grumpy employee — could destroy the trust built by millions of perfect meals.

> **Case: The Parking Lot Obsession** (Chapter 1): When Kroc first visited McDonald's, the thing that impressed him most was not the hamburgers but the cleanliness. "There were no flies swarming around the place. The men in the white suits were keeping everything neat and clean as they worked. That impressed the hell out of me, because I've always been impatient with poor housekeeping, especially in restaurants. I observed that even the parking lot was being kept free of litter."
> **Key takeaway**: The customer judges your business by everything they see, touch, and smell — not just the product they came for. The parking lot IS the product.

### Principle 4: Build Systems That Survive People
Kroc believed that a business should not depend on the brilliance of any single individual. It should run on systems so reliable that anyone can execute them.

### Principle 5: Choose Partners By Their Potential, Not Their Past
Kroc chose franchisees not by their business experience but by their character and willingness to work. His best franchisees were often people with no restaurant experience at all.

### Principle 6: Maintain Control Through Ownership
The real estate model was not just about profits — it was about control. By owning the land, Kroc could enforce quality standards with the ultimate threat: eviction.

### Principle 7: Prepare for a Lifetime, Not a Quarter
McDonald's took 22 years from founding to IPO. The real estate model sacrificed short-term profits (buying land is expensive) for long-term wealth (land appreciates). Kroc thought in decades.
