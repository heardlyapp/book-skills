# 3. Techniques — The Kroc Methods

> Source: "Grinding It Out" by Ray Kroc (1977). Techniques described throughout the book, particularly Chapters 2, 4, 6, and 10 covering franchise development, real estate, and training.

## Scaling the System

### The Franchise Selection Method

Kroc's approach to franchisees was radically different from his competitors. While other franchisors sold territories to anyone with cash, Kroc was selective:

- He required franchisees to work in the restaurant themselves — no absentee owners.
- He rejected investors who wanted to buy multiple locations as a portfolio.
- He preferred couples who would work together and reinvest in the business.

> **Case: The Man Who Wasn't Interested** (Chapter 4): When the popular entertainer Arthur Godfrey expressed interest in a McDonald's franchise, Kroc turned him down. Godfrey had the money and the fame, but he wouldn't work the restaurant himself. Kroc said no. This was almost unheard of in franchising at the time, but it reinforced the principle: the operator must be present.
> **Key takeaway**: Money is not the only qualification for a franchisee. Commitment and willingness to do the work matter more. Kroc would rather have no growth than bad growth.

### The Real Estate Model

Harry J. Sonneborn, McDonald's first president, devised the most important technique in the Kroc playbook:

- McDonald's Corporation leases or buys land at key intersections.
- McDonald's builds the restaurant building.
- The franchisee leases both land and building from McDonald's.
- The lease includes a percentage rent clause (a share of sales).

**Why this works**: The franchisee needs to succeed for McDonald's to collect rent. This aligns incentives perfectly. And McDonald's owns appreciating assets — the land — which grow in value regardless of how hamburger sales fluctuate.

> **Case: Sonneborn's Insight** (Chapter 6): Sonneborn was not a hamburger man — he was a finance guy who had worked at Tastee-Freez. He told Kroc: "You are not in the hamburger business. You are in the real estate business." This single insight transformed McDonald's from a struggling franchise operator into a real estate powerhouse. Under Sonneborn's leadership, McDonald's created Franchise Realty Corporation, the division that would buy and lease properties.
> **Key takeaway**: The most valuable part of your business model might be invisible to customers. Look for the hidden leverage point — it's often in the supporting structure, not the product itself.

### Hamburger University

In 1961, Kroc opened Hamburger University in the basement of a McDonald's in Elk Grove Village, Illinois. It was not a publicity stunt — it was a quality control system.

- Every franchisee and manager must complete the program.
- Curriculum covers everything from cooking procedures to financial management.
- Instructors are experienced operators, not academics.
- The program ensures that the system is understood and followed.

> **Case: The First Class** (Chapter 8): "Our first class had eighteen students. We awarded them degrees in 'Hamburgerology.'" The mock seriousness of the degree masked a very real strategic purpose: if every manager learned exactly the same procedures, the product would be identical everywhere. Kroc understood that training is not a cost — it's the only way to scale quality.
> **Key takeaway**: When you expand geographically, you need a training system that standardizes behavior. Hamburger University was McDonald's quality control department disguised as a school.

### The Supplier Partnership Model

Kroc built long-term relationships with suppliers and treated them as partners, not vendors:

- He gave suppliers guaranteed volume in exchange for product consistency.
- He shared his quality specifications openly and expected suppliers to meet them.
- He invested in supplier innovation (e.g., the potato curing process).

**Counterintuitive**: In an era when most businesses squeezed suppliers for the lowest price, Kroc paid premium prices for premium quality. He believed that a fifteen-cent hamburger could use the best beef available if the system was efficient enough.

### The "Clean as You Go" Rule

Every McDonald's employee was trained to clean continuously, not just at closing time. The grill was scraped between every batch. The floor was swept during slow periods. The parking lot was patrolled for litter. This wasn't just about appearance — it was a signal: "We care about everything."
