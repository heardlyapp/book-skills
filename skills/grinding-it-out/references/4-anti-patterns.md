# 4. Anti-Patterns — When Kroc Got It Wrong

> Source: "Grinding It Out" by Ray Kroc (1977). Anti-patterns from Chapters 2-3 (the McDonald brothers deal) and Chapters 7-9 (franchise struggles and the buyout).

## Mistake 1: The Handshake Deal (Partnership Failure)

**What it looks like**: You trust someone and make a deal based on verbal agreements and optimism, without legal protections.

**Why it fails**: When the business grows, the incentives that aligned you initially will diverge. Without a written agreement that anticipates this divergence, the partnership becomes a battlefield.

> **Case: The McDonald Brothers Deal** (Chapter 2): Kroc's original 1954 contract with Mac and Dick McDonald gave him the right to franchise their system — but with terrible terms. The brothers retained control over the menu, the building design, and any changes to the system. They also had a clause that Kroc could not change the building design without their approval. As Kroc's chain grew, the brothers became increasingly obstructionist — refusing to approve new designs, blocking menu innovations (like the Filet-O-Fish), and demanding renegotiation. Kroc called them "the millstone around my neck." In 1961, Kroc had to buy them out for $2.7 million — a price he considered extortion. It was the most expensive handshake in business history.
> **Key takeaway**: A partnership deal must be structured to survive growth. If your partner can block your progress at every turn, you haven't made a deal — you've made a prison.

## Mistake 2: Underestimating the Difficulty of Scaling

**What it looks like**: You succeed with a small operation and assume you can simply copy-and-paste it to new locations.

**Why it fails**: Every new location is a new business with its own challenges: supply chain, staff training, local market conditions, real estate costs.

> **Case: The Des Plaines Disaster** (Chapter 3): Kroc's first franchise opened in Des Plaines, Illinois, in 1955 — a copy of the San Bernardino original. But the french fries came out terrible. Kroc had watched the process in California and believed he understood it, but he missed the critical step: potatoes need to be cured for several weeks after harvest to develop the right sugar content. He spent months and thousands of dollars experimenting before figuring it out. He later said: "That was just one of the many mistakes I would make in my dealings with the McDonald brothers."
> **Key takeaway**: What you observe in a successful operation is only the visible part of the system. The invisible parts — timing, relationships, tacit knowledge — are often the most important. Document everything before scaling.

## Mistake 3: Cash Flow Optimism

**What it looks like**: You focus on revenue growth and assume cash flow will follow.

**Why it fails**: Fast-growing franchise businesses consume cash faster than they generate it. Each new location requires capital for construction, inventory, and staff training before it generates a single dollar of revenue.

> **Case: The Cash Crunch** (Chapters 3-4): In the early years, Kroc was constantly scrambling for cash. He sold franchises for $950 each — a fraction of the true cost. He took partners who provided capital but demanded control. He was turned down by 27 banks before finding one willing to lend. His personal finances were a disaster: "I was 52 years old, had diabetes and incipient arthritis, had lost my gall bladder and most of my thyroid gland, but I was convinced the best was ahead of me." The early franchisees who struggled made Kroc's financial problems worse, because he couldn't collect royalties from failing operators.
> **Key takeaway**: Growth consumes cash. Before you scale, secure a capital lifeline that will survive the period between opening new locations and collecting their profits. If you can't survive 18 months of zero revenue, you can't scale.

## Mistake 4: Letting Partners Have Control Over Critical Decisions

**What it looks like**: You give a partner veto power over product, design, or strategy changes because you need their approval or expertise.

**Why it fails**: When the market changes, you need to adapt quickly. If your partner can say no, you're paralyzed.

> **Case: The Filet-O-Fish Block** (Chapter 7): In the early 1960s, a Cincinnati franchisee named Lou Groen noticed that his Catholic customers wouldn't eat meat on Fridays. He developed a fish sandwich — the Filet-O-Fish — and asked Kroc for permission to sell it. Kroc loved the idea, but the McDonald brothers vetoed it because it violated their original menu. It took years of pressure before the brothers relented and allowed the Filet-O-Fish on the menu. By that time, competitors had already captured much of the Lenten market.
> **Key takeaway**: If your business model depends on a partner's approval for product innovation, you're building a business that will be out-innovated by competitors who can move faster. Make sure your agreement gives you the right to evolve.
