# The Social Gradient Explained

> Source: The Health Gap, Chapters 2, 3, 7

## Why Status Matters

### The Psychosocial Pathways

| Factor | How It Affects Health |
|--------|----------------------|
| Control over work | Low control = higher stress hormones, higher heart disease |
| Social position | Relative status affects self-worth and health behaviors |
| Autonomy | Ability to make decisions protects health |
| Stress | Chronic stress causes inflammation, hypertension, disease |

> **Case: Control and the Civil Servants (Chapters 2-3)**
> The Whitehall II study asked a simple question: is it the resources associated with higher status that protect health, or is it something about the experience of being higher status? The answer: control over work was the most important factor. Civil servants with low control over their work had worse health regardless of salary. Even executives who felt they had no control were worse off than secretaries who felt they had high control. The feeling of control—autonomy—is protective. Its absence is toxic.
> **Key takeaway:** Control is the key variable. Not money. Not education. Not health insurance. The feeling that you have control over your life is the single most protective factor for health. This finding has enormous implications for how we design work, schools, and communities.

> **Case: The Status Syndrome (Throughout)**
> Marmot's term for the phenomenon: "status syndrome." Across countries, across time, across diseases: the higher your social position, the better your health. This is true in rich countries (UK, US, Japan) and poor countries (India, Brazil). It is true for heart disease, cancer, mental illness, and infectious disease. It is true for men and women. It is true regardless of healthcare system. Status syndrome is a universal phenomenon: hierarchy itself is toxic to those at the bottom.
> **Key takeaway:** Status syndrome is Marmot's single most important concept. It means: inequality is not just about poverty. It is about where you stand in relation to others. Even if your absolute standard of living is high, a low relative position damages your health. This is the fundamental reason why more equal societies have better health outcomes.
