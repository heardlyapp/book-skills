# What Works — Policy Solutions

> Source: The Health Gap, Chapters 4, 5, 6, 9, 10, 11

## The Marmot Review Recommendations

### Six Policy Priorities

| Priority | Key Action |
|----------|-----------|
| 1. Best start in life | Prenatal care, parental leave, early childhood education |
| 2. Education and capabilities | Quality schools, lifelong learning, skills training |
| 3. Fair employment | Good jobs, control at work, minimum wage, job security |
| 4. Healthy standard of living | Income support, affordable housing, living wage |
| 5. Healthy communities | Green spaces, public transport, social connection |
| 6. Prevention | Public health investment, not just medical treatment |

> **Case: Equity from the Start (Chapter 4)**
> Marmot argues that the most effective intervention is the one that happens earliest. Children born into poverty start life behind: lower birth weight, less cognitive stimulation, more stress. The gap widens through school and into adulthood. Intervention in early childhood—prenatal nutrition, parental leave, high-quality childcare—has the highest return on investment. The example: Scandinavian countries invest heavily in early childhood and have the smallest health gaps in the world.
> **Key takeaway:** Early childhood is where the health gap begins — and where it can be most effectively closed. Every dollar spent on early childhood returns more in lifetime health savings than any medical intervention. Prevention starts before birth.

> **Case: Japan and the Social Gradient (Chapters 2, 9)**
> Japan has one of the smallest health gradients in the world. The gap in life expectancy between the highest and lowest social classes is much smaller than in the US or UK. Why? Japan has low income inequality, universal healthcare, a strong social safety net, and a cultural emphasis on social cohesion. The lesson: the health gap is not inevitable. Countries with more equal social structures have smaller health gaps.
> **Key takeaway:** Japan proves that the health gap can be narrowed. The gradient is not a law of nature — it is a consequence of social policy. Japan's example: reduce inequality, invest in the social determinants, and health outcomes improve for everyone, not just the poor.
