# The Social Determinants of Health

> Source: The Health Gap, Introduction, Chapters 1, 2

## Core Framework

### The Gradient

| Class of civil servant | Relative mortality risk |
|------------------------|------------------------|
| Administrative (top) | 1.0 (baseline) |
| Professional/Executive | 1.6 |
| Clerical | 2.2 |
| Other (lowest grade) | 4.0 |

> **Case: The Whitehall Studies (Chapters 1-2)**
> Marmot's landmark Whitehall studies followed British civil servants over decades. All had stable jobs, steady incomes, and access to the National Health Service. Yet there was a clear gradient: the lower someone's grade in the civil service hierarchy, the higher their risk of heart disease, cancer, stroke, and death from all causes. The gradient existed at every level—secretaries had worse health than managers, who had worse health than executives. This could not be explained by poverty or access to healthcare. The key factor was control: how much control did people have over their work and their lives?
> **Key takeaway:** The Whitehall studies are the most important evidence in social epidemiology. They prove that health inequality exists at every level of society, not just between rich and poor. The mechanism: lack of control and autonomy is physically harmful.

> **Case: The Organisation of Misery (Chapter 1)**
> Marmot opens with a patient in a psychiatry clinic in 1960s Sydney. The woman is depressed, her husband beats her, her son is in prison, her daughter is pregnant. The psychiatrist prescribes red pills. Marmot, then a medical student, thought: "Why treat her depression and send her back to the conditions that caused it?" This question became his life's work. The "organisation of misery" is how society structures conditions that make people sick. The alternative: the "organisation of hope" — societies that empower people to control their lives.
> **Key takeaway:** The opening anecdote is the book's thesis in miniature. Medicine treats symptoms. The real treatment is changing the conditions of life. This is not a medical problem — it is a political and social problem.
