# Anti-Patterns — What Society Gets Wrong

> Source: The Health Gap, Chapters 2, 8, 11

## The Five Fallacies

### 1. "Healthcare Determines Health"
**Error**: "If everyone has access to doctors, health gaps will close."
**Reality**: Healthcare accounts for ~10-20% of health outcomes. The rest is the conditions of life.

### 2. "It's About Personal Responsibility"
**Error**: "Poor people make bad choices."
**Reality**: Choices are constrained by circumstances. Blaming individuals ignores the conditions that shape choices.

### 3. "Only Extreme Poverty Matters"
**Error**: "The health gap is only about the very poor."
**Reality**: The gradient exists at every level. The gap between the middle class and the upper class is as real as the gap between the poor and the middle class.

### 4. "Inequality Is Inevitable"
**Error**: "Some people will always be healthier than others."
**Reality**: Some countries (Japan, Sweden, Norway) have much smaller gradients. Inequality is policy, not destiny.

### 5. "The Economy vs. Health"
**Error**: "We cannot afford to reduce inequality."
**Reality**: Inequality is expensive. The costs of the health gap—lost productivity, healthcare spending, social instability—far outweigh the cost of reducing it.

> **Case: Individual Responsibility Myth (Chapter 2)**
> Marmot directly challenges the idea that health gaps are caused by individual lifestyle choices. Yes, smoking, diet, and exercise matter. But why do some people smoke more? Why do some people have worse diets? The answer: stress, lack of control, low social status. Telling a stressed, low-control worker to "eat better and exercise" is like telling a drowning person to swim harder. The conditions need to change—not just the individual.
> **Key takeaway:** The individual responsibility argument is convenient for those who benefit from inequality. It places the burden on the victim. Marmot's evidence: when conditions improve, behaviors change. Empowerment, not blame.

> **Case: The Glasgow Effect (Chapter 8)**
> Glasgow, Scotland has some of the worst health outcomes in Western Europe — far worse than similar cities in England. The difference cannot be explained by poverty alone. Researchers identified a "Glasgow effect" — an unexplained excess of mortality linked to the city's history of deindustrialization, social fragmentation, and loss of control. The lesson: places can be sick. Community-level factors — social cohesion, trust, hope — affect health independently of individual circumstances.
> **Key takeaway:** The Glasgow effect proves that health is not just about individuals — it is about communities. When a community is broken, health suffers. The solution must be community-level, not just individual-level.
