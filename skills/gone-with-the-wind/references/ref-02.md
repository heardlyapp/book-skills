# Reference: Rhett Butler and the Love Story

## Overview
The relationship between Scarlett and Rhett is one of literature's great love stories — and one of its most tragic.

## Case: Rhett Butler
Rhett is charming, cynical, and deeply in love with Scarlett. He sees through her manipulations. He is the only man who truly understands her. But his love is not unconditional. He leaves when he realizes she will never love him as he loves her.

## Case: The Tragedy
Scarlett does not realize she loves Rhett until it is too late. She spends the novel pursuing Ashley, who she cannot have. Rhett spends the novel loving her, waiting for her to see him. In the end, she understands — but he has stopped caring.

## Case: The Famous Line
"Frankly, my dear, I don't give a damn" is Rhett's final line. It is devastating because it is not anger — it is exhaustion. He has loved her completely, and she has not loved him back. Now he no longer cares. The line is one of the most famous in literary and cinematic history.

## Case: The Misunderstood Love
Scarlett's feelings for Ashley are not love but obsession. She loves the idea of him — the gentleman, the Old South, the world that was. Rhett represents the reality she does not want to face.

## Case: The Counterpoint
Melanie and Ashley's marriage is the counterpoint to Scarlett and Rhett. Melanie is gentle, kind, and devoted. Ashley is honorable and gentle but weak. Their relationship is based on genuine affection, not passion.

## Case: The Ending
Scarlett returns to Tara. She has lost Rhett. She has lost Bonnie. She has lost Melanie. All she has is the land. She decides to think about everything tomorrow. The ending is both tragic and hopeful.
