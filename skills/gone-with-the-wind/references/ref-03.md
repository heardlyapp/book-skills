# Reference: The Old South

## Overview
The novel is set in a specific historical moment: the Civil War and Reconstruction. The Old South is both setting and character.

## Case: Plantation Life
Tara and Twelve Oaks represent the plantation system. It is a world of wealth, leisure, and slavery. Mitchell describes it romantically, which is the source of the novel's controversy.

## Case: The War
The Civil War destroys everything. Scarlett's world collapses. The war is depicted from the Confederate perspective, focusing on the suffering of Southern civilians.

## Case: Reconstruction
After the war, the South is occupied by federal troops. Former slaves are free. The plantation system is destroyed. Carpetbaggers from the North profit from the chaos. Scarlett must navigate this new world.

## Case: The Lost Cause
The novel is a product of the "Lost Cause" mythology — the romanticization of the Old South and the minimization of slavery's brutality. This is the source of the novel's enduring controversy.

## Case: The Land
"The land is the only thing in the world worth working for, worth fighting for, worth dying for." Tara represents home, identity, and survival. Scarlett's connection to the land is the most authentic thing about her.

## Case: The Transformation
The war transforms Scarlett from a self-centered belle into a pragmatic survivor. She learns that the old rules no longer apply. She must work, scheme, and fight to survive.

## Case: The New South
After the war, a new South emerges. Scarlett's business ventures (sawmill, store) represent the new capitalist order. She is a woman succeeding in a man's world.
