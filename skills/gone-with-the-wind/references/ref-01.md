# Reference: Scarlett O'Hara

## Overview
Scarlett O'Hara is one of the most complex heroines in American literature. She is selfish, manipulative, brave, and unforgettable.

## Case: The Southern Belle
At 16, Scarlett is spoiled, vain, and accustomed to being the center of attention. She is a product of the plantation system — raised to be ornamental, not useful. Her obsession with Ashley Wilkes drives the early plot.

## Case: The Survivor
When the war destroys her world, Scarlett reveals hidden depths. She works in the fields, kills a Union soldier, runs a business, and marries for money. Her pragmatism shocks those around her. She does what she must.

## Case: The Mother
Scarlett's relationship with her children is complex. She loves them but is not naturally maternal. Her resilience is ultimately for them as much as for herself.

## Case: The Transformation
Scarlett changes more than any other character. The war strips away her illusions and forces her to become strong. She loses her innocence, her beauty, her wealth, and nearly everything she values. What remains is her will to survive.

## Case: The Flawed Heroine
Scarlett is not a sympathetic character in the conventional sense. She is selfish, manipulative, and often cruel. But she is also brave, resourceful, and fiercely determined. Her complexity is what makes her enduring.
Scarlett O'Hara is unforgettable precisely because she is not a simple heroine. She is a survivor.
This makes her one of literature's most enduring characters.
