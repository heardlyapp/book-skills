# Reference: The Subscription Pivot

## Overview
Netflix's original business model was pay-per-rental. Customers paid $4 per DVD plus $2 shipping. The model had a fundamental problem: customers would rent one DVD, keep it for weeks, and not rent another. The company needed a different model.

## Case: The Problem with Pay-Per-Rental
The pay-per-rental model was simple but flawed. Customers had no incentive to return DVDs quickly. A customer who kept a DVD for a month paid the same as a customer who watched it in one night. Netflix was losing money on every rental. The company needed a model that aligned incentives: make customers happy to return DVDs quickly so they would rent more.

## Case: The Subscription Decision
The switch to subscription was not a stroke of genius — it was a desperate response to a failing business model. In September 1999, Netflix launched the subscription: $15.95 per month for unlimited rentals, no due dates, no late fees. Customers could keep DVDs as long as they wanted, but the subscription model made Netflix's revenue predictable. The key insight: customers who kept DVDs longer were not costing Netflix more because they were paying the same monthly fee.

## Case: The Death of Late Fees
Blockbuster made $800 million a year in late fees. Netflix eliminated them. This was a competitive advantage that Blockbuster could not match without destroying its own revenue model. The subscription model was not just better for customers — it was strategically brilliant, because Blockbuster could not copy it without destroying its own profitability.
