# Reference: Survival

## Overview
Netflix went public in May 2002, just as the dot-com crash was ending. The timing was perfect — and almost didn't happen. The company was running out of money.

## Case: The IPO Timing
Netflix filed for its IPO in early 2002, when the dot-com crash was at its worst. Most internet companies had failed. Venture capital had dried up. The IPO market was frozen. Netflix's IPO raised $82 million, but only after the company had already cut costs to the bone. Randolph describes the IPO as a near-death experience: if the timing had been six months earlier or later, Netflix might not have survived.

## Case: The Blockbuster Offer That Wasn't
In 2000, Netflix approached Blockbuster with an offer: buy us for $50 million. Blockbuster's CEO, John Antioco, listened to the pitch and declined. The decision was rational from Blockbuster's perspective: why buy a money-losing startup with a tiny customer base? But it was the most consequential decision in the history of both companies.

## Case: Cost Cutting to Survive
During the dot-com crash, Netflix cut everything. Employees took pay cuts. Marketing was slashed. Randolph and Hastings worked without salaries. The company moved to cheaper offices. Every dollar counted. The survival was not elegant — it was brutal. But Netflix emerged from the crash with a business model that worked and a path to profitability.

## Case: The Profitability Milestone
Netflix reached profitability in 2003 — a crucial turning point. The company had 1 million subscribers and was growing fast. The subscription model was working. The recommendation algorithm was improving. The logistics system was efficient. Profitability proved that the business model was not just viable but scalable.
