# Reference: The Blockbuster Battle

## Overview
The battle between Netflix and Blockbuster is one of the great business stories of the internet age. A tiny startup took on a giant and won.

## Case: Blockbuster's Online Launch
Blockbuster launched its own online DVD rental service in 2004. It had every advantage: brand recognition, relationships with studios, buying power, and deep pockets. But Blockbuster's online service was designed to protect its stores, not to compete with Netflix. Blockbuster charged higher prices for online, limited the selection, and required customers to return DVDs to stores. It was a half-hearted effort.

## Case: The Cuban Bet
In 2005, Blockbuster CEO John Antioco made a bet: invest heavily in the online service to compete with Netflix. The board supported him. Blockbuster's online service grew — faster than Netflix's in some periods. But the investment required closing stores and sacrificing profits. The board lost patience and forced Antioco out in 2007. The new CEO refocused on stores. The online service was starved of investment.

## Case: The Death Spiral
Without a strong online offering, Blockbuster's stores became increasingly irrelevant. Customers were defecting to Netflix. Store revenue declined. Blockbuster closed locations. The decline accelerated. By 2010, Blockbuster was bankrupt. The company's failure was not inevitable — it was the result of specific management decisions that prioritized short-term profits over long-term survival.
