> Source: Lamb: The Gospel According to Biff, Christopher Moore (2002).

# Techniques — Humor as Truth, Anachronism as Insight, and the Sidekick's Perspective

## Practical Methods

> **Case: The Anachronisms** (Throughout): Biff's narration is full of anachronisms. He describes ancient Judea in terms a modern reader can understand. He says things like "he looked at me like I'd just ordered coffee in a Chinese restaurant." The anachronisms are not lazy writing. They are a deliberate technique for making the ancient accessible and showing that human nature has not changed.
> **Key takeaway**: When explaining something unfamiliar, use familiar terms. The anachronism is a bridge across time.

> **Case: The Jokes at the Foot of the Cross** (Climax): Even at the crucifixion, Biff jokes. He does not joke because he does not care. He jokes because he cares so much he cannot bear the weight of it. Humor is the only thing holding him together. His jokes are not disrespect. They are love.
> **Key takeaway**: Joking at serious moments is not disrespect. It is survival. Sometimes laughter is the only way to keep breathing.

> **Case: The Sidekick's Gospel** (Frame): The novel is not the Gospel of Jesus. It is the Gospel of Biff. The story is told from the perspective of the best friend, not the main character. This changes everything. The sidekick sees things the hero cannot: the small moments, the doubts, the private jokes. The sidekick's perspective is more intimate than the hero's.
> **Key takeaway**: The most important stories are often told by the people on the edge of the frame. Their perspective reveals what the center cannot see.
