## The Tuchman Framework: August 1914 — The Month That Determined the War

Tuchman's framework is narrative history: she follows the first month of WWI day by day, from the Austrian ultimatum to Serbia (July 28) through the Battle of the Marne (September 6-10). Her thesis: the plans that were supposed to produce a short, decisive war instead produced a stalemate that would last four years.

**The Key Insight**: The war plans were designed for speed but produced paralysis. The Schlieffen Plan, which was supposed to end the war in 6 weeks, instead ensured that the war would last 4 years.

> **Case: The Schlieffen Plan's Fatal Flaw** (Chapter 6): The Schlieffen Plan required German armies to march through Belgium, violating Belgian neutrality. This guaranteed British entry into the war. The plan also required the German right wing to be overwhelmingly strong — but von Moltke weakened it, sending troops to the Eastern Front and Alsace. The plan was rigid — it could not adapt to reality.
> **Key takeaway**: The Schlieffen Plan was a masterpiece of military theory that failed in practice. Its rigidity made it a trap, not a strategy.

> **Case: The Paris Taxis** (Chapter 16): When the German First Army swung east of Paris, exposing its flank, General Gallieni saw the opportunity. He commandeered every taxi in Paris — over 600 vehicles — to rush French troops to the front. The "Taxis of the Marne" became a symbol of improvisation winning over rigid planning.
> **Key takeaway**: The Battle of the Marne was won by flexibility, not by following the plan. Gallieni's improvisation defeated Moltke's rigid adherence to a failed plan.
