## Practical Techniques

### 1. The Assumption Audit

Identify the core assumptions behind any plan. Ask: "What has to be true for this plan to work? How confident am I that those things are true?" The Schlieffen Plan failed because its assumptions were wrong.

### 2. The Communication Check

In any crisis, communication is the first thing to break. Von Moltke lost contact with his armies. Establish communication protocols before the crisis — not during.

> **Case: The Lost Order** (Chapter 15): At the critical moment of the Marne, Moltke sent General Hentsch to assess the situation. Hentsch made decisions that Moltke should have made. The chain of command broke down. Orders arrived late. The German right wing retreated when it should have attacked.
> **Key takeaway**: In a crisis, communication is everything. If the leader cannot communicate, the plan is dead.

### 3. The Contingency Planning

The Schlieffen Plan had no backup plan. When it failed, the Germans had no alternative. Every plan needs a Plan B.

### 4. The Pace of Change vs. Plans

The technology of war changed faster than the generals' thinking. They planned for a Napoleonic war and got industrial slaughter. The same mismatch happens in any field when strategy does not keep pace with technology.

> **Case: The Cavalry Charge** (Chapter 11): French cavalry charged German machine guns in August 1914. The result was predictable: men and horses slaughtered. The generals had not updated their tactics for modern weapons.
> **Key takeaway**: If your strategy was designed for a different era, it will fail in this one. Update your assumptions or lose.
