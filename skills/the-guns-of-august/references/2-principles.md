## 7 Principles of Military Catastrophe

### 1. Rigid Plans Fail When Reality Changes

Every plan assumed the war would be short. Every plan failed. The plans were beautiful — and wrong.

### 2. Speed Is Not Enough

The Schlieffen Plan depended on speed. But the technology of defense (machine guns, artillery) had outpaced the technology of attack (infantry, cavalry). Speed against firepower is suicide.

> **Case: The Belgian Forts** (Chapter 7): The Germans expected to march through Belgium in days. Instead, Belgian forts at Liège held out for 11 days — using massive artillery that the Germans had not anticipated. The delay wrecked the Schlieffen timetable.
> **Key takeaway**: The enemy always has a vote. The plan that depends on the enemy cooperating is a fantasy.

### 3. Communication Breakdown Is Fatal

Von Moltke lost communication with his armies at the critical moment. He could not coordinate the Marne battle. His orders arrived too late or not at all.

### 4. Morale Is Not Enough

Plan XVII depended on "élan vital" — French fighting spirit. The French attacked with courage and were slaughtered by German machine guns. Courage cannot overcome technology.

### 5. A Leader's Personality Shapes History

Joffre's calm stubbornness saved France. Moltke's nervous collapse doomed Germany. The difference between the two men determined the Battle of the Marne.

> **Case: Joffre's Silence** (Chapter 10): During the darkest days of August, Joffre remained calm. He ate well. He slept. He did not panic. His subordinates were amazed — and his calm gave them confidence. Meanwhile, Moltke was having a nervous breakdown at headquarters.
> **Key takeaway**: Leadership matters most when things go wrong. The calm leader saves the day; the panicked leader loses it.

### 6. The Enemy's Assumptions Are Your Advantage

Gallieni exploited von Kluck's assumption that Paris would not defend itself. The German plan left the city alone — and that assumption created the opportunity for the Marne counterattack.

### 7. Catastrophe Is Avoidable — Until It Isn't

The war could have been stopped at multiple points. It was not. Each decision made the next one harder to avoid. By August 4, the machinery was in motion and could not be stopped.
