## 6 Anti-Patterns of Strategic Thinking

### 1. "The Plan Will Survive Contact with the Enemy"

No plan survives first contact. The Schlieffen Plan was doomed from the moment the Belgian forts held.

### 2. "Speed Is Everything"

Speed without flexibility is a liability. The Germans moved fast — into a trap.

> **Case: Von Kluck's Exposed Flank** (Chapter 14): Von Kluck's First Army marched so fast that it outstripped its supply lines and exposed its flank to Paris. Speed without intelligence is not speed — it is recklessness.
> **Key takeaway**: Speed is valuable only when combined with awareness. Moving fast into a trap is worse than moving slowly in the right direction.

### 3. "Technology Will Win"

Technology does not win wars — strategy does. The Germans had better artillery and machine guns. They lost the Marne because of strategic errors, not technological inferiority.

### 4. "The Enemy Will Cooperate"

Every war plan assumes the enemy will behave as expected. The enemy rarely cooperates. The Belgians did not roll over. The Russians mobilized faster than expected. The assumptions were wrong.

### 5. "This War Will Be Short"

The universal assumption before 1914 was that the war would be short. It was catastrophically wrong. The assumption that "this will be quick" is almost always wrong.

### 6. "The Leaders Know What They Are Doing"

The leaders of Europe in 1914 were not monsters — they were men who made mistakes that cost millions of lives. Competence is not guaranteed by position.

> **Case: The Russian Mobilization** (Prologue): Russia mobilized its army to support Serbia — triggering the German mobilization. But Russia's mobilization was slow and chaotic. The assumption that Russia was a "steamroller" proved wrong at Tannenberg.
> **Key takeaway**: Reputation is not capability. Russia's vast army was poorly led and poorly supplied. The power that everyone feared was a paper tiger.
