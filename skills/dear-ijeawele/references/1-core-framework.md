# 1 — The 15 Suggestions Framework

> Source: "Dear Ijeawele, or A Feminist Manifesto in Fifteen Suggestions" by Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie

## The Core Framework

A letter from Adichie to her friend Ijeawele on how to raise her newborn daughter Chizalum as a feminist. Fifteen suggestions, each a practical principle for parenting that challenges gender conditioning at every level — from the language we use to the toys we buy.

### The 15 Suggestions

| # | Suggestion | Core Insight |
|---|------------|--------------|
| 1 | Be a full person | Motherhood is part of you, not all of you |
| 2 | Do it together | Fathers are parents, not helpers |
| 3 | Teach her that "gender roles" is nonsense | "Because you are a girl" is never a reason |
| 4 | Beware Feminism Lite | Conditional equality is not equality |
| 5 | Teach her to read | Books make her free |
| 6 | Teach her to question language | Words contain our prejudices |
| 7 | Teach her about difference | Difference is normal, not threatening |
| 8 | Never use marriage as a goal | Her life is not about finding a husband |
| 9 | Teach her about sexuality | Shame-free, honest, respectful |
| 10 | Reject likeability | Please don't raise your daughter to please |
| 11 | Teach her about romantic love | Love should not mean losing herself |
| 12 | Reject anti-men feminism | Feminism is about equality for everyone |
| 13 | Teach her about culture critically | Culture is made by people; it can be remade |
| 14 | Teach her to question | Curiosity is more important than compliance |
| 15 | Teach her about money | Financial independence is freedom |

> **Case: The Blue Outfit** (Suggestion 3): Adichie went to buy a baby outfit. The girls' section had pale pink. The boys' section had vibrant blue. She bought blue — it would look better against brown skin. The cashier was horrified: "Blue for a girl?" The toy section was also gendered: boys got active toys like trains and cars; girls got passive dolls. Adichie wished toys were arranged by type, not gender.
> **Key takeaway**: Gender conditioning starts at infancy, in the clothes we buy and the toys we offer. Pink-blue is a marketing invention, not a biological truth.

> **Case: The Helicopter and the Doll** (Suggestion 3): Adichie saw a 7-year-old Nigerian girl in a U.S. mall fascinated by a remote-control helicopter. "No," her mother said. "You have your dolls." The girl replied: "Mummy, is it only dolls I will play with?" Adichie wonders: what if that girl had become a revolutionary engineer?
> **Key takeaway**: When we place the straitjacket of gender roles on young children, we limit their potential. Let her explore helicopters and dolls alike.

> **Case: Feminism Lite and Prime Minister May** (Suggestion 4): A progressive British newspaper described Theresa May's husband: "Philip May is known in politics as a man who has taken a back seat and ALLOWED his wife, Theresa, to shine." Reverse it: "Theresa May has ALLOWED her husband to shine." It makes no sense. "Allow" is about power, not equality. A husband is not a headmaster; a wife is not a schoolgirl.
> **Key takeaway**: Language reveals hidden power structures. "Allow" in a marriage signals inequality. Equal partners don't "allow" each other to shine.
