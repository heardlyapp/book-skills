# 4 — Anti-Patterns: What to Avoid

> Source: "Dear Ijeawele, or A Feminist Manifesto in Fifteen Suggestions" by Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie

## Anti-Pattern 1: Feminism Lite

**The mistake:** Conditional equality — believing in equal rights but with caveats. "He is the head and you are the neck." "He drives but you sit in the front seat." This is not feminism. It's patriarchy with a kinder face.

> **Case: The "Angry" Woman** (Suggestion 4): A writer once described Adichie as "angry," as though being angry were shameful. She is angry — about racism, about sexism. But she realized she's angrier about sexism than racism because with sexism, she often feels lonely. People who easily acknowledge race injustice dismiss gender injustice. "I cannot tell you how often people I care about have dismissed or diminished sexist situations."
> **Key takeaway**: Feminist Lite allows people to feel progressive while maintaining the status quo. Real feminism demands full equality, not conditional niceness.

## Anti-Pattern 2: The Likeability Trap

**The mistake:** Raising a daughter to be pleasing, agreeable, and liked. This is the single most dangerous pattern in raising girls.

> **Case: The Baby Play Group** (Suggestion 3): An American friend observed that mothers of baby girls were very restraining, constantly saying "don't touch" or "stop and be nice." Baby boys were encouraged to explore and almost never told to "be nice." The theory: parents unconsciously start very early to teach girls to be small and good, and boys to be bold and free.
> **Key takeaway:** "Be nice" is not a virtue — it's a cage. Kindness matters. Likeability is optional. Please don't raise your daughter to please.

## Anti-Pattern 3: The Marriage Goal

**The mistake:** Implicitly or explicitly teaching your daughter that her ultimate achievement is finding a husband.

> **Case: The Cooking Debates** (Suggestion 3): Nigerian social media debates about whether wives must cook for husbands. Adichie's response: "The knowledge of cooking does not come pre-installed in a vagina. Cooking is learned. It is a life skill that both men and women should have." The obsession with women cooking is rooted in marriage being seen as a prize for women. If we stopped conditioning women to see marriage as a prize, we'd have fewer debates about wives needing to earn that prize through cooking.
> **Key takeaway:** Teach your daughter to cook because feeding yourself is a life skill, not because a future husband requires it. Marriage is a choice, not a destiny.

## Anti-Pattern 4: The "Behind the Scenes" Power

**The mistake:** Teaching girls that real power for women is exercised invisibly. That she should let the man be "the head" while she maneuvers "behind the scenes."

> **Case: Ikenga's Claim** (Suggestion 4): "Even though the general idea is that my father is in charge at our home, it's my mother who is really in charge behind the scenes." Adichie: "He thought he was refuting sexism, but he was making my case. Why 'behind the scenes'? If a woman has power then why do we need to disguise that she has power?"
> **Key takeaway:** Power should not require disguise. Teach your daughter that she can be powerful openly, without apology, without having to pretend someone else is in charge.

## Anti-Pattern 5: The "Allowed" Vocabulary

**The mistake:** Using language of permission between equal adults. "He allowed her to work." "She allowed him to cook."

> **Case: Philip May "Allowed" Theresa May to Shine** (Suggestion 4): When Theresa May became British PM, the press said her husband "allowed" her to shine. Reverse it: "Theresa May has allowed her husband to shine." It makes no sense. "Allow" is about power. "Permission and being allowed — and it is nearly only used that way — should never be the language of an equal marriage."
> **Key takeaway:** Remove "allow" from your vocabulary when describing adult relationships. A husband is not a headmaster. A wife is not a schoolgirl.
