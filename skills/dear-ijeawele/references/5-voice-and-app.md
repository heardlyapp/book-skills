# 5 — Adichie's Voice + 5 Application Scenarios

> Source: "Dear Ijeawele, or A Feminist Manifesto in Fifteen Suggestions" by Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie

## Adichie's Voice

Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie is a celebrated novelist (Half of a Yellow Sun, Americanah) and essayist (We Should All Be Feminists). Her voice in this book: warm, personal, unflinching. Written as a letter to a friend, it reads like a conversation over tea — intimate, digressive, occasionally funny, always precise. She doesn't lecture; she shares.

> **Case: The Origin of the Book** (Preface): This began as a Facebook post on October 12, 2016. Adichie wrote it after a friend asked how to raise her newborn daughter as a feminist. The post went viral. This book is an expanded version. The letter format is not a literary device — it's a real letter to a real friend.
> **Key takeaway**: The most powerful feminist writing is personal. Theory matters, but a letter to a friend about how to love her daughter carries more weight than any manifesto.

> **Case: The Rereading as a Mother** (Preface): Adichie revisits the letter years later as a mother herself. "Now that I, too, am the mother of a delightful baby girl, I realize that I wrote that letter as much for myself as for my friend." She acknowledges that motherhood has changed her perspective — and that she is now determined to try to live by her own suggestions.
> **Key takeaway**: Feminist principles are aspirational, not perfectionistic. Adichie doesn't claim to have it all figured out. She's trying, just like every parent.

## Key Quotes

> "Culture does not make people. People make culture. If it is true that the full humanity of women is not our culture, then we can and must make it our culture."

> "Please don't raise your daughter to please."

> "Being a feminist is like being pregnant. You either are or you are not. You either believe in the full equality of men and women or you do not."

> "A husband is not a headmaster. A wife is not a schoolgirl."

> "The knowledge of cooking does not come pre-installed in a vagina."

## Application Scenario 1: Family Pressure About Traditional Roles

*"My mother-in-law says I should stay home with the baby. My husband agrees with her."*

**Use:** Suggestion 1 + Anti-Pattern 1

**Playbook:**
1. Double-income is historically the real Igbo tradition (women farmed and traded before colonialism)
2. "Traditional" is selectively invoked — ask what specific tradition benefits whom
3. A full person is a better parent. Your working is a gift to your child
4. Your husband is not "helping" — he is equally responsible

## Application Scenario 2: Daughter Told to Be "Ladylike"

*"My 6-year-old is called 'bossy' at school for leading group activities."*

**Use:** Suggestion 6 + Anti-Pattern 2

**Playbook:**
1. "Bossy" is a gendered word. A boy doing the same is called a "leader"
2. Don't tell her to be nicer. Ask: were you fair? Were you kind? Leadership is not about pleasing
3. Teach her the X Test: if the same behavior in a boy would not be criticized, the criticism is sexist

## Application Scenario 3: Daughter Asks Why She Can't Do What Boys Do

*"Why can't I play football? Why do boys get to climb trees?"*

**Use:** Suggestion 3 + Technique 4

**Playbook:**
1. "Because you are a girl" is never a reason. Ever.
2. Tell her: you can do anything a boy can do. Gender does not determine ability.
3. The helicopter-and-doll story: show her that adults often limit children based on outdated ideas
4. Encourage the exploration. Let her try. She might not succeed perfectly, but let her try

## Application Scenario 4: Teacher Says Girls Are Better at Reading, Boys at Math

*"My daughter's teacher says girls naturally excel in language, boys in math."*

**Use:** Suggestion 3 + Suggestion 7

**Playbook:**
1. That's a stereotype, not a biological fact. Research shows no innate difference in math ability
2. Teach her about difference — but difference is about individuals, not gender groups
3. Show her counterexamples: women mathematicians, engineers, scientists
4. The most damaging thing is telling her what she "should" be good at based on gender

## Application Scenario 5: Raising a Son Who Is a Feminist

*"I have a son, not a daughter. Does this book apply to me?"*

**Use:** Suggestion 2 + Suggestion 12

**Playbook:**
1. Feminism is for everyone. Boys need feminism too — it frees them from narrow masculinity
2. Teach him to share domestic work. Cooking and cleaning are life skills, not women's work
3. Teach him that "being a man" does not mean being unemotional, dominant, or in charge
4. Teach him about consent and respect — the same principles apply to everyone
5. The world would have better men if their fathers had been actively present. Be that father
