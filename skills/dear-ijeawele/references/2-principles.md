# 2 — Principles of Feminist Parenting

> Source: "Dear Ijeawele, or A Feminist Manifesto in Fifteen Suggestions" by Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie

## Principle 1: Be a Full Person, Not Just a Mother

Adichie's first and most foundational suggestion: "Motherhood is a glorious gift, but do not define yourself solely by motherhood. Be a full person. Your child will benefit from that."

> **Case: Marlene Sanders' Advice** (Suggestion 1): The pioneering American journalist Marlene Sanders, first woman to report from Vietnam, told a younger journalist: "Never apologize for working. You love what you do, and loving what you do is a great gift to give your child." Adichie adds: "You don't even have to love your job — you can merely love what your job does for you — the confidence and self-fulfillment that come with doing and earning."
> **Key takeaway**: A full, fulfilled mother is the best gift you can give your child. Your working is not a deprivation for your child — it's a model of a whole human being.

> **Case: The "Traditional Mother" Argument** (Suggestion 1): Ijeawele's sister-in-law says she should stay home because "Chudi can afford not to have a double-income family." Adichie's response: Tell her that a double-income family is actually the true Igbo tradition — mothers farmed and traded before British colonialism, and trading was exclusively done by women in some parts of Igboland.
> **Key takeaway**: "Tradition" is often selectively invoked. People use "tradition" to justify what they want, not what actually was. Arm yourself with history.

## Principle 2: Shared Parenting Is Equal Parenting

A father is as much a verb as a mother. "Chudi should do everything that biology allows — which is everything but breastfeeding."

> **Case: The Language of Help** (Suggestion 2): When we say fathers are "helping," we suggest child care is a mother's territory into which fathers valiantly venture. It is not. Adichie's friend Nwabu, whose wife left when his kids were young, called himself "Mr. Mom." But he wasn't being "Mr. Mom" — he was simply being a dad. And never say a father is "babysitting." People who babysit are people for whom the baby is not a primary responsibility.
> **Key takeaway**: Language creates reality. "Helping" implies the work isn't his responsibility. It is. Equal parenting means equal responsibility, not "help."

## Principle 3: Question Everything — Especially What Feels "Normal"

> **Case: The "Like a Girl" Sweeping** (Suggestion 3): Adichie was told as a child to "bend down properly while sweeping, like a girl." She wishes she'd been told simply "bend down and sweep properly because you'll clean the floor better" — and that her brothers had been told the same thing. The "like a girl" qualifier is unnecessary. Sweeping is sweeping.
> **Key takeaway**: Remove the "like a girl" qualifier from all instructions. A child is a child. An action is an action. Gender is irrelevant.

## Principle 4: Equality Cannot Be Conditional

> **Case: The "Behind the Scenes" Power** (Suggestion 4): Adichie's friend Ikenga claims that "even though the general idea is that my father is in charge at our home, it's my mother who is really in charge behind the scenes." He thought he was refuting sexism. He was making her case. Why "behind the scenes"? If a woman has power, why must we disguise it?
> **Key takeaway**: Hidden power is not equality. If a woman must exercise power invisibly, that is not progress — it is patriarchy in a different costume.
