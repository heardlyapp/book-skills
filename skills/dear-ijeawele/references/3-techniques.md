# 3 — Practical Parenting Techniques

> Source: "Dear Ijeawele, or A Feminist Manifesto in Fifteen Suggestions" by Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie

## Technique 1: Teach Her to Read and Love Books

"Books will help her understand and question the world, help her express herself, and help her in whatever she wants to become."

> **Case: The Five Cents Per Page** (Suggestion 5): Adichie knows a remarkable Nigerian woman, Angela, a single mother raising her child in the United States. Her child didn't take to reading, so she decided to pay her five cents per page. "An expensive endeavor, she later joked, but a worthy investment."
> **Key takeaway**: If your child doesn't naturally take to reading, find creative incentives. A book-loving child grows into a questioning adult. Pay her, reward her, do whatever it takes.

## Technique 2: The Language Audit — Question Every Word

> **Case: "Princess" vs "Angel"** (Suggestion 6): A friend of Adichie's says she will never call her daughter "princess." People mean well, but "princess" is loaded with assumptions — a girl's delicacy, the prince who will come to save her. This friend prefers "angel" and "star."
> **Key takeaway**: Words are not neutral. "Princess" teaches dependency and rescue narratives. "Star" teaches brightness and self-illumination. Choose your child's vocabulary with intention.

> **Case: The Igbo Joke** (Suggestion 6): There's an Igbo joke used to tease girls who are being childish: "What are you doing? Don't you know you are old enough to find a husband?" Adichie used to say this. Now she chooses not to. She says: "You are old enough to find a job." Because she does not believe marriage should be what young girls are taught to aspire to.
> **Key takeaway**: The jokes we tell children teach them what to value. Replace "find a husband" with "find a purpose." The shift rewires aspiration.

## Technique 3: Teach Her to Question Language Directly

> **Case: The "Lady Mechanic"** (Suggestion 6): A newspaper profile of a mechanic in Lagos described her as a "lady mechanic." Adichie's instruction: "Teach Chizalum that the woman is a mechanic, not a 'lady mechanic.'" Similarly, the TV commercial where a man cooks and his wife claps for him — true progress is when she responds to the food itself, not to the novelty of a man cooking.
> **Key takeaway**: Qualifiers like "lady" before a profession imply the default professional is male. Remove the qualifier. A mechanic is a mechanic.

## Technique 4: Teach Her to Use the X Test

> **Case: The X in Women** (Suggestion 6): "Teach her that if you criticize X in women but do not criticize X in men, then you do not have a problem with X, you have a problem with women. For X insert words like 'anger,' 'ambition,' 'loudness,' 'stubbornness,' 'coldness,' 'ruthlessness.'"
> **Key takeaway**: This is a simple, powerful framework for identifying gendered double standards. A woman is "ambitious" (negative); a man is "driven" (positive). The same trait is judged differently by gender.

## Technique 5: Teach Self-Reliance Through Action

> **Case: The Car Mechanic Trap** (Suggestion 6): When a man hits your car in Lagos traffic and tells you to "go and bring your husband because I can't deal with a woman" — point this out to your daughter. It's not about your car. It's about your perceived worth.
> **Key takeaway**: Everyday sexism is the best teaching material. Use real incidents to show her how misogyny operates — both overt and subtle.
