> Source: On the Road: The Original Scroll, Jack Kerouac. The unexpurgated 1957 classic.

# Core Framework — The Road, Dean Moriarty, and the Mad Ones

## The Model

Kerouac's novel is built on a simple structure: Sal leaves, Sal travels, Sal returns, Sal leaves again. Each journey is a loop that expands his understanding of America, of friendship, and of himself. Dean is the engine that drives every loop.

> **Case: The First Meeting** (Part One): When Sal first meets Dean Moriarty, he describes it as meeting "a sideburned hero." Dean arrives in New York fresh from reform school, full of manic energy, already a legend among their shared friends. Sal is immediately drawn to him. "Dean is the perfect guy for the road because he was actually born on the road." The friendship that follows is the axis on which the entire novel turns. Dean represents everything Sal feels he is missing: spontaneity, fearlessness, the ability to live completely in the moment.
> **Key takeaway**: Some people enter your life and change its direction permanently. You do not choose these meetings. They happen. What matters is whether you are open to them.

> **Case: The Cross-Country Hitchhike** (Parts One-Two): Sal hitchhikes across the country, meeting truck drivers, farmers, migrant workers, and drifters. He sleeps in fields. He runs out of money. He eats where he can. Kerouac writes: "We were all alone in our misery but somehow the road made us brothers." The landscape of America — the vast plains of Nebraska, the Rockies of Colorado, the deserts of Utah — becomes a character in itself. The road is not just a route. It is a spiritual condition.
> **Key takeaway**: Traveling without money, without a plan, without knowing where you will sleep — this is how you see the real country. Comfort insulates you from experience.

> **Case: Dean and the Women** (Parts Three-Four): Dean's inability to stay with one woman is both his glory and his tragedy. He loves them all — Marylou, Camille, Inez — and he leaves them all. He cannot stop moving. When Sal finally wants to settle down with Laura, Dean cannot understand it. The question at the heart of the novel: is Dean's freedom admirable or pathological? The answer is both. He is the most alive person Sal knows. He is also incapable of love as a sustained commitment.
> **Key takeaway**: Freedom and commitment are in tension. The person who cannot commit is not free. They are running. The question is whether you are running toward something or away from everything.
