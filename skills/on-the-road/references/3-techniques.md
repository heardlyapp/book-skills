> Source: On the Road: The Original Scroll, Jack Kerouac.

# Techniques — Spontaneous Prose, the Scroll Method, and the Art of Not Revising

## Practical Methods

> **Case: The Three-Week Marathon** (Editor's Introduction): Kerouac typed the original scroll on a continuous 120-foot roll of teletype paper in three weeks. He did not revise. He did not edit. He wrote as fast as he could to keep up with his thoughts. This was not carelessness — it was a deliberate method he called "spontaneous prose." He would later write a list of "Belief & Technique for Modern Prose" — 30 rules that included "Be in love with your life," "You're a Genius all the time," and "Something that you feel will find its own form."
> **Key takeaway**: Do not edit while you write. The editor and the creator are two different people. Let the creator finish before the editor touches anything.

> **Case: The Transcribed Conversation** (Part Two): Kerouac wrote entire scenes as they happened — transcribing conversations that Neal Cassady (Dean) had with him. The scroll captures the rhythm of real speech: the fragments, the tangents, the energy of two minds meeting in real time. Kerouac understood that fiction often smooths out the roughness of real life — and in doing so, loses its truth. His goal was not to write a "well-crafted novel." His goal was to capture life as it was lived.
> **Key takeaway**: Real speech is messy. Real life is messy. If your writing is too clean, you have removed the truth. Leave the mess in.

> **Case: The Search for the Right Word** (Kerouac's technique): Despite the speed of the scroll, Kerouac was a meticulous observer. He spent pages describing a single sunset, a single jazz solo, a single face. His speed was not about carelessness. It was about capturing the moment before the moment was gone. He wrote: "The best writing is done when you are not trying to write. When you are just recording what you see and feel, without judgment, without editing, without fear."
> **Key takeaway**: Observation is a creative act. Slow down enough to see what is actually in front of you. The details are the story.
