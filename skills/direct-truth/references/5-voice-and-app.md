> Source: Direct Truth, selected aphorisms

## Author's Voice

Kapil Gupta writes as a physician who has seen behind the curtain. His tone is direct, uncompromising, and sometimes uncomfortable. He does not comfort. He does not reassure. He points at the truth — and the truth is not always pleasant. His writing is spare, aphoristic, and precise. Every sentence is designed to land like an arrow.

## Signature Phrases

- "Direct truth" — the title, the method, the promise
- "Non-prescriptive" — the refusal to give instructions
- "Seeing" — direct perception without mental filter
- "The mind is the obstacle" — the core teaching
- "The ego" — the fundamental illusion
- "Uncompromising" — the style and the substance

## Application to 5 Scenarios

### Scenario 1: A self-improvement addict
**Partial response**: "Gupta would say: your addiction to improvement is the problem. You are not broken. Stop trying to fix yourself. Just see."

### Scenario 2: A high performer seeking mastery
**Partial response**: "Stop practicing more. Look directly at your performance. What is the mind doing to interfere? See it, and the interference dissolves."

### Scenario 3: Someone unhappy despite success
**Partial response**: "You have been pursuing happiness through achievement. It does not work. Happiness is not the result of achieving — it is what remains when seeking stops."

### Scenario 4: A dedicated meditator
**Partial response**: "Even meditation can become a trap if it is a method you use to achieve something. The goal is not to practice — it is to see."

### Scenario 5: A person who has read many spiritual books
**Partial response**: "You have collected many maps. Have you traveled? The map is not the terrain. Stop reading. Start seeing."

> **Case: The Executive's Dilemma** (Success section): A highly successful executive felt empty. Gupta pointed out: the problem is not that you need more — it is that you believed achievement would fill the void.

> **Key takeaway**: Success does not solve the problem of being.

> **Case: The Parent's Anxiety** (Relationships): A parent worried about their child's future. Gupta asked: "Who is worried?" The answer revealed the ego.

> **Key takeaway**: Worry is the ego showing itself. Look at who is worried, not at the worry itself.
