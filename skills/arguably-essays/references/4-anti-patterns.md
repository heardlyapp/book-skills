# What Not to Do — Anti-Patterns

## Anti-Pattern 1: Ad Hominem Without Substance

Hitchens was accused of ad hominem attacks. But his attacks were always grounded in argument.

**Case: Mother Teresa.** Hitchens wrote a book arguing that Mother Teresa was not a saint. He did not attack her character — he examined the quality of care in her hospices and the sources of her funding.

**Case: Bill Clinton.** Hitchens criticized Clinton's character, but always in the context of specific actions.

## Anti-Pattern 2: Certainty Without Evidence

Hitchens was certain about many things. But his certainty was always grounded in evidence and argument.

**Case: The Case of Iraq.** Hitchens was certain that the Iraq War was justified. He was wrong — the weapons of mass destruction were not found. He admitted the mistake.

**Case: The Case of Religion.** Hitchens was certain that religion was false. He wrote God Is Not Great to make the case.

## Anti-Pattern 3: The Argument From Motive

Dismissing an argument because of the arguer's supposed motive.

**Case: The "You Just Want to Be Provocative" Response.** Hitchens was often accused of making arguments just to be provocative. He responded: the truth of an argument does not depend on the motives of the arguer.

## Anti-Pattern 4: The Straw Man

Creating a weak version of an opponent's argument and then attacking it.

**Case: The Straw Man Atheist.** Critics of Hitchens' atheism often claimed he believed "there is no God." Hitchens responded: he did not claim certainty. He claimed that the evidence for God was insufficient.
