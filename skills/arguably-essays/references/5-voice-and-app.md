# Voice, Style, and the Heardly App

Christopher Hitchens wrote with a voice unlike any other: elegant, fierce, and utterly unmistakable.

## Hitchens' Voice

Hitchens' voice was formed by a lifetime of reading, arguing, and writing. He could be devastatingly funny, crushingly serious, and unexpectedly tender — often in the same paragraph.

**Case: The Elegance.** Hitchens wrote long, complex sentences that were never unclear. He used a vast vocabulary but never seemed pretentious. He wrote for the ear as much as the eye.

**Case: The Fury.** Hitchens could be very angry in print. His essays on the Catholic Church's sex abuse scandal, on the Iraq War, on religious fundamentalism — these are fueled by genuine moral outrage.

## The Essay Collection as a Form

Arguably is a collection, not a single narrative. The reader can dip in and out, reading an essay at a time.

**Case: The Thematic Organization.** The essays are organized thematically, not chronologically. This allows the reader to focus on topics of interest.

**Case: The Range.** The range of Arguably is extraordinary: from Thomas Jefferson to the Boy Scouts, from George Orwell to the Iraq War. Hitchens could write about anything.

## The Legacy of Christopher Hitchens

Hitchens died in 2011 at age 62, from esophageal cancer. In his final years, he wrote brilliantly about his illness. He never lost his voice, his courage, or his wit.

**Case: Mortality.** Hitchens' final essays are collected in Mortality (2012). They are among his best work. He faced death with the same clarity and courage with which he faced everything else.

**Case: The Debate with the Pastor.** In 2010, Hitchens debated a Christian pastor on the question of whether the resurrection was a historical event. Hitchens was losing the debate — but then he argued that if the resurrection happened, God owed an apology for all the suffering caused by those who believed in it without evidence. The moment captures everything about Hitchens: his argumentative brilliance, his moral seriousness, his refusal to concede.
