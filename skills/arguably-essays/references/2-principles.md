# Principles of Argument

## I. Clarity Is a Moral Duty

Hitchens believed that unclear writing was not just bad style — it was bad character. If you cannot say what you mean, you may not know what you mean.

**Case: The Journalistic Vice.** Hitchens criticized journalists who used jargon to obscure their meaning. He insisted that complex ideas could be stated simply.

**Case: The Academic Vice.** Hitchens was particularly critical of academic writing that was inaccessible to ordinary readers. He believed that knowledge should be shared, not guarded.

## II. Read Your Enemy

Hitchens engaged with his opponents' best arguments, not their worst.

**Case: The Religious Right.** Hitchens had read the Bible carefully. He knew theology. He could argue with believers on their own terms.

**Case: The Political Left.** Hitchens broke with much of the Left over the Iraq War. But he did not caricature his former allies. He engaged with their arguments seriously.

## III. Take Risks

Hitchens took positions that cost him. He lost friends, readers, and reputation. He did not care.

**Case: The Iraq War.** Hitchens knew that supporting the war would alienate him from the Left. He did it anyway.

**Case: The Fatwa.** Hitchens wrote in defense of Salman Rushdie when Rushdie was under a fatwa. He did not hesitate.

## IV. Never Be Boring

Hitchens believed that the first duty of a writer was to be interesting.

**Case: The Wit.** Hitchens was funny. He used humor to make his points. Even in his most serious essays, there is a joke or an unexpected turn of phrase.

**Case: The Unexpected Reference.** Hitchens could move from Thomas Jefferson to David Hume to a pop culture reference in a single paragraph. The range of references kept the reader engaged.
