## The Structure of a Hitchens Essay

Each Hitchens essay follows a recognizable pattern: (1) a provocative opening that grabs attention, (2) a clear statement of the argument, (3) engagement with opposing views, (4) a conclusion that is both conclusive and open-ended.

**Case: The Opening Sentence.** Hitchens often opens with a sentence that forces the reader to pay attention. "Why should we care what the Founding Fathers believed, or did not believe, about religion?" The question is direct, challenging, and it frames the entire essay.

**Case: The Engagement with Opponents.** Hitchens never attacked a straw man. He quoted his opponents directly, engaged with their strongest arguments, and then showed why they were wrong.

## What Made Hitchens' Essays Distinctive

**Clarity of Argument.** Every Hitchens essay has a clear thesis. You always know what he is arguing.

**Case: "Gods of Our Fathers."** The thesis: the American Founders were far more irreligious than commonly believed. Hitchens supports this with specific examples of each Founder's skepticism.

**Case: "The Boy Scouts of America."** The thesis: an organization that discriminates against atheists and gay people should not receive public support.

**Depth of Reading.** Hitchens engaged seriously with his opponents.

**Case: The Bible.** Hitchens had read the Bible carefully. He quoted it. He knew its contradictions. His critique was grounded in knowledge.

**Case: The Founding Fathers.** Hitchens had read Jefferson, Adams, Franklin, and Washington. He knew their letters, their private writings, their public statements.

**Willingness to Be Unpopular.** Hitchens took positions that cost him friends.

**Case: The Iraq War.** Hitchens supported the invasion of Iraq. This put him at odds with much of the Left. He lost friends and readers. He did not change his position.
