# Techniques from Hitchens

## Technique 1: The Opening Gambit

Hitchens' essays begin with a sentence that demands attention.

**How to do it:** Start with a question, a challenge, or a provocative statement. Do not warm up. Start arguing from the first sentence.

**Case: The Question Opening.** "Why should we care what the Founding Fathers believed, or did not believe, about religion?" The question challenges the reader's assumptions and sets up the argument.

**Case: The Statement Opening.** "There are four rules governing literary art in the domain of humor." The reader must know what the four rules are.

## Technique 2: The Embedded Quote

Hitchens never made a claim without evidence. He quoted his sources.

**How to do it:** When making an argument, quote your source directly. Let the reader see the evidence.

**Case: John Adams' Words.** Hitchens quotes Adams directly: "There is a germ of religion in human nature so strong that whenever an order of men can persuade the people by flattery or terror that they have salvation at their disposal, there can be no end to fraud, violence, or usurpation." The quote is more powerful than any paraphrase.

**Case: Jefferson's Private Writings.** Hitchens quotes Jefferson's private letters to show that his public religious observance was hypocrisy.

## Technique 3: The Unexpected Connection

Hitchens connected apparently unrelated subjects.

**How to do it:** Show the reader something they have not seen before. Connect a literary reference to a political argument. Connect a historical event to a contemporary debate.

**Case: Connection to Orwell.** Hitchens often connects contemporary issues to Orwell's writings. Orwell becomes a lens through which to see the present.

**Case: Connection to the Enlightenment.** Hitchens connects contemporary debates about religion and politics to the arguments of the Enlightenment.
