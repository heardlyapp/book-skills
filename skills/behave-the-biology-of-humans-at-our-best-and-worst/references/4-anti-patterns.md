# Common Mistakes — Anti-Patterns

**Anti-Pattern 1: Assuming Free Will Without Evidence.** We assume people freely choose their behavior. The more we understand about the biological and environmental causes of behavior, the less room remains for free will in the traditional sense.

**Case: The Criminal Justice Problem.** We punish people for behavior without understanding its causes. The criminal justice system is based on retribution, not on understanding. Sapolsky argues for a system based on public safety and rehabilitation.

**Anti-Pattern 2: The Modular Brain Fallacy.** The belief that there is a single brain region responsible for each complex behavior. The brain does not work that way.

**Case: The Phrenology Fallacy.** We have moved beyond 19th-century phrenology, but the desire for simple brain-behavior mappings persists. There is no single "aggression center."

**Anti-Pattern 3: Ignoring Context.** We attribute behavior to personality when it is caused by context. The Fundamental Attribution Error is the most common mistake in understanding behavior.

**Case: The Fundamental Attribution Error.** We assume that someone who behaves badly is a bad person. We ignore the context that shaped their behavior.
