# Anti-Patterns

> Source: I Don't Want to Talk About It, various chapters

## The Five Mistakes

### 1. "Real Men Don't Cry"
**Error**: Sadness is weakness.
**Correction**: The ability to feel and express sadness is emotional health, not weakness.

### 2. "I Can Handle It Myself"
**Error**: Independence is the highest value.
**Correction**: Connection is essential to mental health. Needing others is human.

### 3. "Anger Is Better Than Sadness"
**Error**: It is safer to be angry than to be sad.
**Correction**: Anger protects you from sadness. But it also prevents healing. The sadness must be felt.

### 4. "Work Will Fix It"
**Error**: Busyness cures depression.
**Correction**: Workaholism is a symptom, not a solution. It avoids the real issue.

### 5. "I Don't Want to Talk About It"
**Error**: Avoidance is a valid strategy.
**Correction**: The phrase itself is the diagnosis. The refusal to talk IS the problem.

> **Case: The Addicted Executive (Chapter 5)**
> Real describes a successful executive who drank every night, worked 14-hour days, and had no close relationships. He did not think he was depressed. He thought he was successful. His drinking was "just how he unwound." His wife said he was "not present." Real's diagnosis: covert depression, self-medicated with alcohol. The drinking was not the problem — it was a symptom.
> **Key takeaway:** The addicted executive is the most common presentation of covert depression. The man does not feel sad. He feels tired, irritable, and numb. He drinks to feel something — or to feel nothing. The lesson: if you are drinking to cope, you are coping with something.

> **Case: The Angry Husband (Chapter 4)**
> A man comes to therapy because his wife is threatening divorce. He is angry all the time. He yells. He criticizes. He does not understand why. Real's insight: the anger is a defense against the sadness underneath. The man is terrified of losing his wife. But he cannot say that. So he gets angry instead. The anger pushes his wife further away.
> **Key takeaway:** Angry men are often sad men. The anger is a secondary emotion. The primary emotion is fear, grief, or shame. The lesson: look past the anger. Ask: what is he afraid of losing?
