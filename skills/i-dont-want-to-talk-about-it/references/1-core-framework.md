# Covert Male Depression

> Source: I Don't Want to Talk About It, Introduction, Chapters 1-5

## Core Framework

### The Two Faces of Depression

| Overt Depression | Covert Depression |
|------------------|-------------------|
| Sadness | Irritability |
| Crying | Explosive anger |
| Withdrawal | Workaholism |
| Talks about feelings | Refuses to talk |
| Seeks help | Denies anything is wrong |

> **Case: The Man Who Would Not Cry (Chapter 2)**
> Real tells the story of a male patient who had never cried — not at his father's funeral, not at his own son's birth. He was successful, admired, and utterly empty inside. His wife said he was "like a robot." He came to therapy because his wife threatened to leave. Real's diagnosis: covert depression. The man was not sad. He was angry, controlling, and numb. The depression was hidden behind a mask of competence.
> **Key takeaway:** The man who cannot cry is the classic case of covert depression. He is not sad because he does not allow himself to feel. The depression is not absent — it is buried.

> **Case: The Grandiose Self (Chapter 3)**
> Real describes the "grandiose self" — the false self that says "I need no one. I am self-sufficient. I handle everything myself." This is the mask of male depression. The grandiose self is a defense against the shame of needing others. It is exhausting to maintain. It isolates the man from the connection he needs. And it collapses when the man can no longer maintain the facade.
> **Key takeaway:** The grandiose self is the armor of male depression. It looks like strength. It is actually a prison. The lesson: the need for connection is not weakness — it is humanity.
