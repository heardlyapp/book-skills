# Anti-Patterns — The Inner Critic and Other Saboteurs

> Source: The Artist's Way, Chapters 2, 3, 7, 9

## The Five Saboteurs of Creativity

### 1. The Inner Critic (The Censor)
**Voice**: "You're not talented. You're not ready. This is stupid."
**Truth**: The Censor is fear wearing a disguise. It protects you from risk — and from growth.
**Antidote**: Write what the Censor says in Morning Pages. Name it. "There goes the Censor again." Then ignore it.

### 2. The Bludgeon of Ambition
**Voice**: "You should be more successful. You should be further along. You're wasting your talent."
**Truth**: The bludgeon is toxic ambition. It compares your reality to someone else's highlight reel.
**Antidote**: Recognize the bludgeon. It is not your true ambition — it is your insecurity. Your true ambition is patient and gentle.

### 3. Toxic Comparison
**Voice**: "So-and-so is better than you. They're already where you want to be."
**Truth**: Comparison is the thief of joy. It measures your beginning against someone else's middle.
**Antidote**: Make art only you can make. And do not look sideways at what anyone else is doing.

### 4. The Permission Trap
**Voice**: "I need someone to tell me it's okay to do this."
**Truth**: No one will give you permission. You must give it to yourself.
**Antidote**: You have permission. You always had permission. You just didn't know it.

### 5. The Perfectionism Snare
**Voice**: "If it's not perfect, don't bother."
**Truth**: Perfectionism is perfectionism. It stops you from starting.
**Antidote**: "Good enough" is the most productive creative standard.

> **Case: The Writer Who Needed Permission** (Chapter 7)
> A woman in Cameron's workshop revealed that she had been writing secretly for years — and had never shown her work to anyone. She was waiting for someone to tell her she was good enough. Cameron told her: "No one is coming to give you permission. You have to give it to yourself." The woman went home and, for the first time, read a page of her writing out loud — to herself. She cried. She had been waiting for thirty years for permission that only she could give.
> **Key takeaway**: Waiting for permission is a lifelong sentence. You have permission to create, to fail, to suck, to improve. Give it to yourself right now. No one else will.

> **Case: The Bludgeon of Shoulds** (Chapter 9)
> Cameron describes "The Shoulds" — all the things you think you should be doing. You should be more successful. You should be further along. You should be making money from your art. The Shoulds are not your true voice — they are internalized expectations from parents, society, and culture. When you hear a Should, ask: says who? The answer is almost never "me."
> **Key takeaway**: "Should" is the most dangerous word in the creative vocabulary. It comes from outside, not from within. When you hear it, question it. Your true creative voice does not speak in shoulds.
