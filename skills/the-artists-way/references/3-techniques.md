# Creative Blocks — What They Are and How to Clear Them

> Source: The Artist's Way, Chapters 3, 5, 6, 7

## The Three Types of Blocks

### 1. Fear
**Manifestation**: "I'm not good enough." "What if it's terrible?" "People will laugh."
**Solution**: Acknowledge the fear. Write about it in Morning Pages. Then do the work anyway. Fear does not go away — you just learn to work alongside it.

### 2. Perfectionism
**Manifestation**: "If I can't do it perfectly, I won't do it at all." "I'll wait until I'm ready."
**Solution**: Give yourself permission to be bad. The first draft, the first sketch, the first attempt — quality is irrelevant. The goal is quantity of output.

### 3. Past Trauma
**Manifestation**: A teacher or parent criticized your creative work. A painful rejection. A memory of embarrassment.
**Solution**: Write about the trauma. Cry about it. Let it go. The person who hurt you is not in the room anymore. You are.

> **Case: The Corrosive Critic** (Chapter 3)
> Cameron tells the story of a woman whose mother had told her at age 7 that her drawing was ugly. She had not drawn since. Thirty years later, she was still blocked by the words of a woman who was dead. In Morning Pages, she wrote about her mother. She wrote about the drawing. She wrote about the hurt. After three weeks of pages, she bought a set of watercolors. She painted a small, imperfect flower. She cried. She had not painted since she was 7. The block was not about talent — it was about a wound that had never healed.
> **Key takeaway**: Most creative blocks are old wounds — criticisms that were internalized decades ago. They are not rational. You cannot argue with them. You must write about them, acknowledge them, and let them go.

> **Case: The "Serious" Artist** (Chapter 6)
> A man came to Cameron and said he was a serious artist. He was blocked. He couldn't work. Cameron asked: "When was the last time you had fun with your art?" The man couldn't remember. He had become so obsessed with being "good" and "serious" that he had lost the joy of making art. Cameron gave him an assignment: make something deliberately bad. A terrible painting. A silly poem. The man resisted — he didn't want to make bad art. Cameron insisted. When he finally did it, he remembered why he loved making art: the play, not the product.
> **Key takeaway**: Art made without joy is not sustainable. If you have lost the pleasure of making, you are not blocked — you are bored. The cure: make something bad on purpose. The permission to fail is the permission to play.
