# 2 — The Psychosis Link

> Source: "Tell Your Children" by Alex Berenson

## The Biological Mechanism

THC acts on CB1 receptors in the brain, which are concentrated in areas involved in emotion, memory, and perception. In high doses, THC can produce temporary psychotic symptoms. In vulnerable individuals, this can trigger lasting psychosis.

## The Evidence

- Multiple longitudinal studies show increased psychosis risk in cannabis users
- The risk is dose-dependent: higher THC = higher risk
- The risk is age-dependent: younger users = higher risk
- Genetic vulnerability plays a role (COMT gene variation)

> **Case: The London Study** (Chapter 4): Berenson highlights a study from King's College London that found that users of high-THC cannabis (skunk) were significantly more likely to experience psychosis than users of traditional low-THC cannabis. The study controlled for other factors including pre-existing mental health conditions.
> **Key takeaway**: High-THC cannabis is not just stronger — it's qualitatively more dangerous. The psychosis risk is specifically linked to high-potency products.

> **Case: The Dunedin Study** (Chapter 6): The Dunedin Multidisciplinary Health and Development Study in New Zealand followed 1,000 people from birth. It found that cannabis use before age 18 was associated with a significant decline in IQ — even after controlling for education and other drug use. The decline was not seen in those who started using cannabis as adults.
> **Key takeaway**: The adolescent brain is uniquely vulnerable to cannabis. Starting young can cause lasting cognitive impairment that doesn't occur in adult-onset users.
