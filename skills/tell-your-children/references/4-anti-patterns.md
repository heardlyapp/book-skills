# 4 — Policy Failures

> Source: "Tell Your Children" by Alex Berenson

## Anti-Pattern 1: Harm Minimization Narrative

**The mistake:** Downplaying the risks of marijuana to support legalization. Berenson argues that some legalization advocates have minimized or hidden evidence of harm.

## Anti-Pattern 2: Profit Over Public Health

**The mistake:** Treating marijuana as a consumer product rather than a controlled substance. The industry has incentives to maximize consumption, especially of high-THC products.

## Anti-Pattern 3: Regulatory Gaps

**The mistake:** Legalizing without adequate regulation of potency, marketing, or age restrictions. Many legal states have fewer restrictions on cannabis than on alcohol.

> **Case: The Edible Problem** (Chapter 9): Berenson examines the issue of cannabis edibles, which are often packaged to look like candy. "Children have been hospitalized after eating cannabis edibles they mistook for regular candy. In Colorado, calls to poison control for children under 6 rose sharply after legalization."
> **Key takeaway**: Legalization without regulation creates new harms, especially for children. The edible market is a case study in regulatory failure.

> **Case: The Marketing Machine** (Chapter 10): Berenson documents how the cannabis industry markets high-THC products with names like "Girl Scout Cookies" and "AK-47." "The industry knows that higher THC means higher addiction risk — but also higher profit margins." He argues the industry follows the same playbook as Big Tobacco.
> **Key takeaway**: Follow the money. The cannabis industry has incentives to maximize addiction, not minimize it. Regulation should account for this.
