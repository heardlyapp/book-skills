# Common Digestive Problems and Solutions

> Source: Gut, Chapters 1, 2, 4

## The Four Most Common Problems

### Reflux (GERD)
**Cause**: The lower esophageal sphincter (LES) doesn't close properly, allowing stomach acid to flow back into the esophagus.
**Solutions**: Don't lie down after eating; eat smaller meals; avoid trigger foods (fatty, spicy, acidic); elevate the head of your bed. Occasional acid reflux is normal; chronic reflux can damage the esophagus.

### Constipation
**Cause**: Slow transit of stool through the colon, often due to low fiber intake, dehydration, or lack of movement.
**Solutions**: Increase fiber gradually (aim for 30-40g/day from diverse plant sources); drink plenty of water; exercise regularly. Prunes (dried plums) have a specific scientific basis: they contain both fiber and sorbitol, a natural laxative.

### Bloating
**Cause**: Excess gas production by gut bacteria, often from fermenting undigested carbohydrates (FODMAPs).
**Solutions**: Identify trigger foods (onions, garlic, beans, wheat, dairy) through an elimination diet. Eat slowly. Chew thoroughly. Some bloating after large meals is normal.

### Diarrhea
**Cause**: Rapid transit through the colon, preventing water absorption.
**Solutions**: Stay hydrated (add electrolytes). Eat binding foods (bananas, rice, applesauce, toast — the BRAT diet). Identify and eliminate trigger foods. Acute diarrhea is usually viral; chronic diarrhea needs medical evaluation.

> **Case: The FODMAP Connection** (Chapter 4)
> FODMAPs (Fermentable Oligosaccharides, Disaccharides, Monosaccharides, and Polyols) are short-chain carbohydrates that are poorly absorbed in the small intestine. When they reach the colon, gut bacteria ferment them, producing gas. The standard FODMAP elimination diet involves removing high-FODMAP foods for 2-4 weeks, then systematically reintroducing them to identify triggers. About 70% of people with IBS improve on a low-FODMAP diet.
> **Key takeaway**: Many digestive problems are caused not by foods that are "bad" but by foods that are poorly absorbed by your specific gut. The FODMAP approach is not a permanent diet — it's a diagnostic tool to identify your personal trigger foods.

> **Case: The Vagus Nerve Experiment** (Chapter 2)
> The vagus nerve connects the brain to the gut. Enders describes an experiment: when the vagus nerve is stimulated, the digestive system enters "rest and digest" mode — blood flow increases, digestive enzymes are released, and peristalsis (the wave-like muscle contractions that move food) becomes regular. When the vagus nerve is inhibited (by stress, anxiety, or pain), digestion slows or stops. This is why stress causes digestive problems: the body prioritizes survival over digestion.
> **Key takeaway**: The gut-brain connection operates through the vagus nerve. When you are stressed, your gut knows. You can consciously activate the vagus nerve through slow, deep diaphragmatic breathing — which tells your body it's safe to digest.
