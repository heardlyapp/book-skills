# How Digestion Works — The Anatomy of the Gut

> Source: Gut, Part 1: Gut Feeling

## Core Framework

The digestive tract is a continuous tube from mouth to anus, but each section has a specialized function. Understanding these functions is the foundation of gut health.

### The Digestive Tract

| Organ | Length | Function | Key Fact |
|-------|--------|----------|----------|
| Mouth | — | Mechanical breakdown, digestion of carbohydrates by saliva | Chewing triggers the rest of the digestive system |
| Esophagus | 25 cm | Transport food to stomach | Lower esophageal sphincter prevents reflux |
| Stomach | Pouch-like | Acid and enzyme breakdown of food | Produces hydrochloric acid strong enough to dissolve metal |
| Small intestine | 5-7 meters | 90% of nutrient absorption | Lined with villi and microvilli that increase surface area 600x |
| Large intestine | 1.5 meters | Water absorption, housing the microbiome | Contains 100 trillion bacteria |
| Rectum and anus | — | Elimination | Two sphincters: one voluntary, one involuntary |

> **Case: The Vomiting Mechanism** (Chapter 2)
> The gut has a sophisticated defense system: vomiting. When the gut detects something toxic, it sends a signal to the brain, which coordinates a complex sequence: salivation, sweating, increased heart rate, contraction of the abdominal muscles, relaxation of the esophageal sphincter, and forceful expulsion of stomach contents. Enders explains that vomiting is not a failure of the system — it's a highly coordinated defense mechanism that has evolved to protect us from poisoning.
> **Key takeaway**: Even the most unpleasant bodily functions are sophisticated products of evolution. Vomiting is not a bug — it's a feature. Understanding this helps us respect our body's defense systems rather than trying to suppress them.

> **Case: The Structure of the Intestinal Wall** (Chapter 1)
> The small intestine is lined with finger-like projections called villi, which increase the surface area 600 times — making the intestine the size of a tennis court if flattened. Each villus is covered with microvilli (the "brush border"). This enormous surface area allows for efficient nutrient absorption. Damage to villi (as in celiac disease) dramatically reduces nutrient absorption, regardless of how well the rest of the digestive system functions.
> **Key takeaway**: The efficiency of digestion depends not just on what you eat but on the health of your intestinal surface. The "tennis court" of absorption surface is your most important digestive organ. Protect it.
