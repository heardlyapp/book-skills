# Techniques — How the Battle Was Actually Fought

## Urban Combat Techniques (Rattenkrieg)

### The Stalingrad Assault Squad

The basic tactical unit of the Stalingrad defense was the assault squad: 6-8 men armed with sub-machine-guns, grenades, and sharpened spades for silent killing. These squads operated independently, infiltrating through sewers, rubble, and burnt-out buildings. They were the product of what soldiers called "the Stalingrad Academy of Street Fighting." Each squad carried:

- 1-2 sub-machine-guns (PPSh-41) for close-quarter fire
- 4-6 hand grenades per man ("pocket artillery")
- A sharpened sapper's spade for silent sentry elimination
- 1-2 anti-tank grenades or Molotov cocktails
- A knife (often homemade)

> **Case: Sewer Combat** (Ch. 10): Rodimtsev's 13th Guards Division sent six sappers into a sewer shaft that ran beneath a German stronghold. They crawled through human waste and debris, carrying 300 pounds of explosive in packs. After placing the charges beneath the German position, they retreated and detonated — the explosion destroyed the building and killed 40 German soldiers. The sappers' casualty rate was the highest of any specialization.
> **Key takeaway**: In urban combat, the "impossible" route (sewers, collapsed walls, underground passages) is often the most effective.

### The Breakwater Defense System

Chuikov's signature tactic: create fortified "breakwaters" — buildings that German attacks would flow around, not through. Each breakwater was a multi-story structure with:

**Ground floor**: Machine-gun nests covering approach roads, supported by anti-tank rifles
**Upper floors**: Sniper positions and observation posts with field telephones to artillery on the east bank
**Basement**: Command post, ammunition store, and medical station
**Courtyard/exterior**: Half-buried T-34 tanks and anti-tank guns in prepared positions

When German tanks attacked with infantry, the defenders' priority was to separate them. Mortar teams dropped bombs just behind the leading tanks to drive off their supporting infantry. Once separated, the anti-tank gunners would engage the tanks. The approach routes were pre-mined by sappers working at night.

> **Case: The "Layered Cake" Building** (Ch. 10): Near the mouth of the Tsaritsa river, a large brick warehouse on the Volga bank became a microcosm of the battle. At one point, Germans held the top floor, Russians held the floor below them, and more Germans held the floor below that. Fighting was conducted by throwing grenades through holes knocked in floors and ceilings, firing sub-machine-guns through gaps, and listening for footsteps above or below. Every uniform was the same dun-colored dust — it was impossible to tell friend from foe at a distance.
> **Key takeaway**: In multi-story combat, vertical awareness is as important as horizontal. Never assume you hold a building just because you hold part of it.

### Sniper Culture and the Cult of the Expert

The 62nd Army actively cultivated specialist killers. Vasily Zaitsev, a shepherd from the Urals who became the most famous sniper in the Soviet army, was elevated to propaganda hero status. His methods became doctrine:

- **Hide and wait**: Zaitsev would spend 12-18 hours in a single position, motionless, waiting for a German soldier to make a mistake
- **Decoys**: Use a helmet on a stick to draw enemy fire and reveal the sniper's position
- **Counter-sniper**: Pairs of snipers would work together — one to bait, one to shoot the German sniper who exposed himself
- **Creative mounting**: Zaitsev attached a sniper scope to an anti-tank gun to engage machine-gun nests through their loopholes (the mass-produced shells proved too inconsistent for precision, but the innovation showed initiative)

> **Case: Zaitsev vs. the German Sniper School** (Ch. 10): German intelligence reportedly sent a Wehrmacht sniper school director to Stalingrad specifically to eliminate Zaitsev. A three-day duel followed in the ruins of the Tractor Factory. Zaitsev and his spotter, Nikolai Kulikov, identified the German's position by the careful way he used his scope (no reflection, no movement during daylight). They set a decoy — a glove raised slowly above a parapet — and when the German fired, Zaitsev shot him through the scope of his own rifle. The story, though romanticized by Soviet propaganda, became legendary.
> **Key takeaway**: Creating "expert cults" around specific combat skills raises the standard across the entire force. The 62nd Army's slogan was "Look after your weapon as carefully as your eyes."

## Artillery Tactics

### Massed Artillery on the East Bank

Chuikov's most important tactical innovation: move all heavy artillery to the east bank of the Volga. Because the front line in Stalingrad was only a few hundred yards deep in places, placing artillery on the west bank was both dangerous and inefficient. On the east bank, massed batteries could:

- Fire over the city into German rear areas and staging points
- Smash German battalions forming up for attack
- Counter-battery against German guns
- Provide fire support on pre-registered targets

Observation officers concealed themselves like snipers in ruined buildings, with field telephones to the batteries across the river. When they spotted a German concentration and called in coordinates, the volume of fire was devastating. Vasily Grossman wrote: "On the other side of the Volga it seemed as if the whole universe shook with the mighty roaring of the heavy guns. The ground trembled."

> **Case: The Katyusha** (Ch. 10): The Soviet BM-13 multiple rocket launcher was mounted on a truck chassis and hidden behind the high Volga bank. A battery would reverse out, almost to the water's edge, fire all 16 of its 130-mm rockets in rapid succession, then drive back under cover. The 5-foot rockets produced a heart-stopping crescendo that many soldiers mistook for an air attack. The weapon's nickname came from the most popular Russian song of the war — "Katyusha," about a girl promising to keep her love alive for her fiancé at the front.
> **Key takeaway**: A mobile, fast-firing weapon system with a terrifying signature can have psychological effects far beyond its explosive payload.

## Night Operations

Chuikov made night the Soviet ally. His doctrine included:

- **Relentless night attacks**: Small squads probing German lines every night, never letting them rest
- **U-2 harassment bombing**: Silent glider attacks from the obsolete biplanes
- **Flare deception**: Periodic flares fired into the sky to simulate an imminent attack, triggering nervous German fusillades
- **Sound and light discipline**: Soviet soldiers trained to move silently in the dark; the Germans, by contrast, fired at any sound, often triggering chain-reaction fusillades up and down the line

> **Case: The 25 Million Rounds** (Ch. 10): In September 1942 alone, German forces in Stalingrad expended over 25 million rounds of ammunition. A significant portion was wasted on night firing triggered by flares, nervous sentries, and false alarms. A German soldier wrote: "If only you could understand what terror is. At the slightest rustle, I pull the trigger and fire off tracer bullets in bursts from the machine-gun." The Soviets deliberately kept up the tension night after night.
> **Key takeaway**: Use night operations to exhaust your enemy, deplete their ammunition, and destroy their morale. A tired army makes mistakes.
