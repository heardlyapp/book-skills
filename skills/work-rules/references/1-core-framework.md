# Core Framework: The Google Hiring Machine

Bock's central thesis: **hiring is the single most important people activity in any organization.** A bad hire propagates through the entire system. A great hire elevates everyone around them.

## The High Bar Principle

Never compromise on quality. Ever. A-players hire A-players; B-players hire C-players. Every hire sets the bar for the next one. Bock's rule: only hire people who are better than you in some meaningful way. Not "as good as" — better.

**In practice:** Create a "hiring bar" that's higher than any individual manager's judgment. Use a hiring committee (not individual managers) to make final decisions. Require unanimous approval.

> **Case: The Too-Good-to-Be-True Resume** (Chapter 3): A candidate had the perfect resume — Ivy League, top consulting firm, impressive results. Every manager who interviewed him wanted to hire him. But the hiring committee noticed something: his answers were rehearsed, his examples sounded practiced, and he couldn't explain why his results happened (he'd been lucky, not skilled). They passed. Six months later, another company hired him and he flamed out. The committee's objective process saved Google from a bad hire.
> **Key takeaway:** Great resumes can hide weak candidates. Structured interviews and objective assessment catch what gut feelings miss.

## The Structured Interview

Google proved that unstructured interviews ("Tell me about yourself") have near-zero predictive validity. Structured interviews — where every candidate is asked the same questions, scored on the same scale, by multiple interviewers — are far more reliable.

**Four types of questions:**
1. **Role-specific** — Can they do the job?
2. **General cognitive ability** — Can they learn and solve problems?
3. **Leadership** — Do they take initiative? Can they influence without authority?
4. **Googleyness** — Do they fit the culture (without being a clone)?

## The Manager-Free Hiring Model

At Google, managers don't make hiring decisions for their own teams. A separate hiring committee evaluates candidates against the company-wide bar. The manager has input but not veto. This prevents managers from hoarding talent or making biased decisions.

> **Case: The Rejected Hire** (Chapter 5): A Google director fought for weeks to hire a candidate who was "perfect" for his team. The hiring committee said no — the candidate had strong skills but the committee felt he lacked curiosity. Eighteen months later, the rejected candidate was hired by a competitor, where he achieved mediocre results and left. The director admitted: "I would have made a bad hire and blamed the system. The system saved me from myself."
> **Key takeaway:** Managers are biased — they want to fill the role, they're swayed by likability, they hire in their own image. An objective system protects the organization.


## The Hiring Committee
At Google, the hiring committee is separate from the manager who wants to hire. The committee reviews all feedback, conducts their own assessment, and makes the final decision — including the power to overrule the hiring manager. This structure prevents any single person from lowering the bar.
