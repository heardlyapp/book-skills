# Techniques: Google's People Tools

## Technique 1: The OKR System
Google's goal-setting system: Objectives (aspirational, qualitative) and Key Results (measurable, quantitative). OKRs are set quarterly, are public within the organization, and are not tied to compensation (so people set ambitious goals without fear).

**Key rules:** 5-10 OKRs per quarter. Objectives are ambitious (70% completion is success). Key Results are binary or measurable. OKRs are shared with everyone.

## Technique 2: The Calibration Process
Managers don't make performance ratings alone. A calibration committee reviews all ratings together, comparing people across teams to ensure consistency. This surfaces bias ("Is this manager rating everyone too high?") and ensures fair differentiation.

> **Case: The Inflated Ratings** (Chapter 7): A Google manager rated all 12 members of his team as "Exceeds Expectations." The calibration committee reviewed the ratings against other teams and found that the average across the company was only 22% "Exceeds." The manager was inflating because he didn't want to have difficult conversations. The committee held him accountable and adjusted the ratings to reflect actual performance differences.
> **Key takeaway:** Individual managers will overrate to avoid conflict. Calibration forces honest differentiation and protects the integrity of the system.

## Technique 3: The 360-Degree Peer Review
Before performance reviews, gather peer feedback from roughly 5-8 colleagues who have worked closely with the person. Ask specific behavioral questions (not "Is X good?" but "What's one thing X could do to be more effective?"). Share the results transparently.

## Technique 4: Manage the Two Tails
Invest disproportionately in your best people (challenge them, promote them, compensate them) AND your struggling people (coach them, give them clear improvement plans). The middle — the vast majority — will manage themselves if you focus on the tails.

**Key insight:** When you invest at the tails, the middle naturally improves because they see what excellence looks like and what underperformance leads to.

## Technique 5: The Nudge Framework
Small changes in choice architecture produce big changes in behavior:
- Want people to save for retirement? Auto-enroll them (nudge) rather than asking them to opt in.
- Want people to eat healthier? Put healthy food at eye level and make unhealthy food harder to reach.
- Want people to use less energy? Show them how their usage compares to their neighbors.

> **Case: The Cafeteria Experiment** (Chapter 12): Google's cafeterias wanted people to eat healthier. They didn't ban junk food or post nutritional warnings. They simply moved the salad bar to the front of the cafeteria and put the burgers in a harder-to-reach corner. Salad consumption increased by 25%. The same food, different layout, different choices.
> **Key takeaway:** You don't need to tell people what to do. Design the environment so the right choice is the easy choice.


## Technique 6: The Exit Interview That Actually Works
Google redesigned exit interviews from "Why are you leaving?" (which people answer dishonestly) to "What would have kept you?" (which surfaces real insights). They also conducted "stay interviews" — asking current employees "What keeps you here?" and "What would make you leave?"
