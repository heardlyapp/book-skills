# Anti-Patterns: What Google Avoided

## ❌ Paying Everyone Equally
The most common compensation mistake: giving everyone the same raise or bonus percentage. Bock argues this is fundamentally unfair because performance follows a power law — the top 1% may produce 10x or 100x more than the average. Pay should reflect this.

**Google's approach:** Wide pay variation based on performance. Top performers get dramatically more. Average performers get solid, market-competitive pay. Low performers get no increase and are managed out.

> **Case: The 10x Engineer** (Chapter 10): Bock describes research showing that top software engineers can be 10x or even 100x more productive than average engineers. Despite this, most companies pay their best engineers only 20-30% more. Google paid their stars 2-5x more, recognizing that losing one top performer cost more than hiring five average ones.
> **Key takeaway:** If you want top talent, you must pay for top talent. Equal pay ensures average results. Unfair pay — in favor of high performance — is actually the fairest system.

## ❌ Confusing Development with Evaluation
The annual performance review tries to do two contradictory things at once: evaluate past performance AND develop future potential. Bock argues these should be completely separate. When you evaluate, people can't develop (they're defensive). When you develop, you can't evaluate (they're open). Have two separate conversations.

## ❌ Letting Managers Make Hiring Decisions
Individual managers are biased: they want to fill the role quickly, they hire people they like personally, they unconsciously hire in their own image. Giving managers sole hiring authority leads to worse decisions. Use a hiring committee for final approval.

## ❌ Status Symbols That Create Hierarchy
Corner offices, executive dining rooms, reserved parking, first-class travel for executives only — these create an "us vs them" culture that undermines collaboration. The CEO who parks in the same lot as everyone else sends a powerful message.

> **Case: The No-Status Experiment** (Chapter 6): When Google eliminated executive perks, some leaders worried it would demotivate senior people. The opposite happened. Junior employees felt more respected. Senior employees felt liberated from the expectation of special treatment. Collaboration across levels increased. The leaders who complained most later admitted they preferred the flatter culture.
> **Key takeaway:** Status symbols don't help the people who have them — they help the people who don't by creating aspiration. But they hurt everyone by creating distance. Eliminate them.

## ❌ Avoiding Failure
Bock argues that organizations that punish failure create cultures of hiding — problems are concealed until they become crises. Google tried to reward thoughtful failure: failures that produced learning, were caught quickly, and were shared transparently.


## ❌ Relying on Exit Interviews
Standard exit interviews ask the wrong questions at the wrong time. People leaving give politically safe answers. Google learned more from anonymous surveys of current employees and from "stay interviews" than from exit interviews.
Google's learning: the best data comes from people who are happy, not from people who are leaving.
