# Principles: Google's People Philosophy

## Principle 1: Give Your Work Meaning
People don't come to work to "do a job" — they want to be part of something important. Bock argues that every role, no matter how mundane, connects to a larger mission. The janitor at NASA didn't clean floors — he helped put a man on the moon. Leaders must make the connection between daily work and meaningful impact explicit.

## Principle 2: Trust Liberates
Google's core cultural insight: **people will rise to the level of trust you give them.** Give slightly more freedom than you're comfortable with. If you're not nervous, you haven't given enough. This means: remove approval gates, default to "yes," share information broadly, and let people shape their own work.

> **Case: The 20% Time** (Chapter 6): Google famously allowed engineers to spend 20% of their time on projects of their choosing. This policy produced Gmail, Google News, AdSense, and many other products. But Bock notes that the policy mattered less than the principle behind it: "We trust you to decide how to spend your time." The trust itself — not the specific projects — was what created the innovation culture.
> **Key takeaway:** Specific policies (20% time, free food) are expressions of a deeper principle: trust. If you copy the policies without the trust, you get the form without the substance.

## Principle 3: Data > Opinions
Google brought the same rigor to people decisions that it brought to product decisions. Before making any major people policy change, they ran experiments. They measured everything. They used data to challenge assumptions. Bock's finding: many common HR practices (annual reviews, standard raises, behavioral interviewing) are not supported by evidence.

## Principle 4: Default to Yes
Most organizations default to "no" — new ideas need approval, exceptions need sign-off, risks need permission. Google tried to default to "yes." When an employee wanted to do something, the answer was "yes" unless there was a compelling reason to say no. This shifted the burden from justifying action to justifying restriction.

## Principle 5: Eliminate Status Symbols
Bock's list of eliminated status symbols at Google: no corner offices, no reserved parking (the CEO parked in the same lot as interns), no executive titles on org charts, no special dining rooms. Every status symbol creates an invisible hierarchy that discourages honest communication.

> **Case: The Corner Office** (Chapter 6): When Google moved into a new building, there were no private offices — not even for executives. Sergey Brin and Larry Page sat in open cubicles alongside engineers. A visiting CEO asked: "How do you get any work done?" Brin replied: "How do you get any truth told to you?" The open space was a deliberate design choice to flatten hierarchy.
> **Key takeaway:** Your physical environment shapes your culture. Every corner office, every reserved parking spot, every executive floor sends a message: "Some people matter more than others."
