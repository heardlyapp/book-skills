# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *Perfect* by Ellen Hopkins — entire verse novel, all POVs (Cara, Kendra, Sean, Andre, Conner).

## The Four Frameworks

### Framework A: The Perfection Trap

Each of the four teen protagonists is chasing a different version of "perfect" — and each version is destroying them.

- **Kendra: The Perfect Body** — Model-thin. She starves herself. Her mother, a plastic surgeon, buys her a nose job. At 109 pounds and 5'10", the anesthesiologist cancels her surgery. She is emaciated. She sees "fat."
- **Sean: The Perfect Athlete** — Steroids. Rage. Loss of Cara. "Second best means you lose. Period." His father died a sports hero. He's trying to fill that shadow.
- **Cara: The Perfect Daughter** — Grades. Leadership. Community service. Stanford. The perfect boyfriend, Sean. But she's a lesbian and doesn't know how to tell anyone.
- **Andre: The Perfect Son** — His father wants him in business. He wants to dance. His father thinks art makes you "gay."

> **Case: Kendra's Rhinoplasty** (pages 543-555): Kendra is wheeled into surgery for a nose job. Before anesthesia, the anesthesiologist Cheryl notices her skeletal arms, her ribs, her wasted body. "You are skin and bones." She cancels the surgery. Kendra's mother the plastic surgeon didn't see it — because she didn't want to. The one person who told the truth was a stranger.
> **Key takeaway**: People who love you can be blind to what's really happening. Sometimes the truth comes from a stranger who has the courage to say what everyone else is ignoring.

### Framework B: Body as Battleground

Every character is at war with their own body. Kendra starves, Sean pumps, Kendra's mom transforms people surgically, Cara runs to feel free.

> **Case: Sean's Steroid Cycle** (pages 48-64, 104-108): Sean takes anabolic steroids and growth supplements to build his body for baseball. He gets bigger, stronger, and angrier. When he sees Cara kissing Dani on the ski slope, his first instinct is rage. The steroids fuel that rage. "Anger feels better than the pain of losing someone."
> **Key takeaway**: What you do to your body to be "perfect" changes who you are. Sean was always competitive. The steroids made him dangerous.

### Framework C: Identity Under Pressure

Every character is wearing a mask. The book's central drama is the gap between the person everyone sees and the person who actually exists.

> **Case: Cara's Awakening** (pages 461-477, 531-541): Cara dates Sean — the perfect guy. Everyone expects them to be together. But at a queer spring break party with Dani, she kisses a girl for the first time. "My heart sunbursts in my chest." She realizes she has been playing a role her whole life. The hardest part isn't coming out to her parents — it's coming out to herself.
> **Key takeaway**: You cannot know who you are until you give yourself permission to find out. Permission comes from within, not from others.

### Framework D: The Mask and the Cost

Secrets are the thread that runs through every story. Conner's suicide attempt is the consequence of a life spent pretending. The aftermath forces everyone else to confront their own masks.

> **Case: Conner's Return** (pages 491-493, 539-541): Conner appears at the movie theater, then at Easter service. He looks "healthy enough" but the family avoids him. Kendra confronts him: "I heard you tried to die. That right?" Conner's response: "Next time I'll have to try harder." The pain doesn't disappear just because he's out of the hospital.
> **Key takeaway**: A suicide attempt is not a cry for attention. It's a cry of exhaustion from carrying a mask too heavy to bear. The response is not silence — it's presence.
