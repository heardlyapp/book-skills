# 2 — Government Failure and Institutional Paralysis

> Source: "And the Band Played On" by Randy Shilts

## The Unwanted Epidemic

The most damning theme of the book: the government knew enough to act but chose not to. The reasons were political, not scientific.

> **Case: The CDC Underfunding** (Part II — 1982): "The CDC's budget for AIDS research in 1982 was less than $1 million. The agency's director had to beg for more funding from a White House that didn't want to hear about the disease. Meanwhile, the number of cases doubled every six months." Shilts documents the systematic underfunding.
> **Key takeaway**: The CDC knew the disease was spreading exponentially. They knew it was blood-borne by 1982. But they could not get the funding or political support to mount a serious response. The epidemic was outpacing the response from the very beginning.

> **Case: The Blood Supply** (Part II — The Blood): "Despite knowing that AIDS was transmitted through blood, the blood banking industry resisted screening. They worried about the cost, the legal implications, and the public panic. For two years, the blood supply was not adequately protected. Thousands of hemophiliacs were infected." Shilts presents this as one of the most preventable tragedies.
> **Key takeaway**: The blood supply scandal is the clearest example of institutional failure. The knowledge existed. The tests existed. But economic and political interests delayed implementation. The result: thousands of people who contracted AIDS through medical treatment — people who had done nothing wrong — died preventable deaths.
