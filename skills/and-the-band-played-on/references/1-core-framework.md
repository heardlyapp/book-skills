# 1 — The Early AIDS Epidemic

> Source: "And the Band Played On" by Randy Shilts

## The First Deaths

The book opens with the first known cases — young gay men dying of diseases that should not have killed them. No one understood why. The medical establishment was baffled, and then indifferent.

> **Case: Patient Zero** (Prologue — The Beginning): "The story begins with a single man — Gaëtan Dugas, a Canadian flight attendant. He was not the source of the epidemic, as the media later unfairly labeled him. But his story illustrates how AIDS spread: through a network of sexual connections that crossed continents, long before anyone knew the disease existed." Shilts introduces the controversial figure of "Patient Zero."
> **Key takeaway**: The "Patient Zero" framing was sensationalized and unfair to Dugas, but the underlying point is important: the disease was spreading silently because no one was looking for it. It moved through sexual networks across cities, countries, and continents, undetected.

> **Case: The First Reports** (Chapter 1 — 1980): "In 1980, doctors in Los Angeles and New York began seeing young gay men with Kaposi's sarcoma — a cancer that normally only affected elderly men. Then came the pneumocystis pneumonia. Something was destroying their immune systems, and no one knew what." Shilts describes the first medical inklings that something was wrong.
> **Key takeaway**: The first cases were medical mysteries. The doctors who reported them were often dismissed. The unusual presentation — healthy young men dying of rare diseases — should have triggered an urgent response. Instead, it triggered confusion and, soon, indifference.
