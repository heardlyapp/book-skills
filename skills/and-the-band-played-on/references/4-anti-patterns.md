# 4 — Scientific Rivalries and Treatment Delays

> Source: "And the Band Played On" by Randy Shilts

## Anti-Pattern: Science as Competition

The scientific response to AIDS was slowed by rivalry, ego, and the profit motive. While researchers fought over credit and patents, people died.

> **Case: The Gallo-Montagnier Feud** (Part IV — The Discovery): "French researcher Luc Montagnier identified the virus that causes AIDS in 1983. American researcher Robert Gallo confirmed the link in 1984. Instead of collaborating, they fought — over credit, over patents, over who discovered it first. The dispute delayed blood testing by at least a year." Shilts documents one of the most damaging scientific rivalries in history.
> **Key takeaway**: The Gallo-Montagnier dispute was a disaster. While they argued over who should get credit, the blood supply was not being screened, drug development was stalled, and diagnostic tests were delayed. Science is supposed to be collaborative. This was the opposite — and people died as a result.

## Anti-Pattern: The AZT Bottleneck

The first AIDS drug was approved in 1987, but the path to approval was slow, expensive, and exclusive.

> **Case: The AZT Controversy** (Part V — Treatment): "AZT was the first drug approved for HIV. But it was highly toxic, had severe side effects, and was priced at $10,000 per year. Activists argued that the drug was rushed without adequate safety testing but also that affordable alternatives were being suppressed." Shilts documents the painful birth of AIDS treatment.
> **Key takeaway**: AZT was a mixed blessing. It was better than nothing but far from good. The slow drug development pipeline, the high prices, and the lack of alternatives were all failures of a system that was not designed for an epidemic. Treatment access became the next front in the AIDS war.
