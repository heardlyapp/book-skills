# 3 — Community Response and Activism

> Source: "And the Band Played On" by Randy Shilts

## The Community Saves Itself

When government failed, the gay community organized. The response was a model of grassroots public health — faster, more creative, and more effective than any government program.

> **Case: San Francisco's Response** (Part III — The Community): "San Francisco's gay community created a comprehensive response — safe sex education, volunteer buddy systems, a dedicated AIDS ward at San Francisco General, and a community-based research network. It was the most effective response in the country, and it came entirely from the community." Shilts documents the San Francisco model.
> **Key takeaway**: The San Francisco model showed what was possible when a community took responsibility for its own health. The city's prevention efforts reduced infection rates among gay men faster than any other city. The lesson: community-based public health works, especially when government is absent.

> **Case: The Rise of ACT UP** (Part V — 1987): "ACT UP — the AIDS Coalition to Unleash Power — was founded in 1987. Its tactics were confrontational: die-ins, disruptions of FDA and Wall Street, and media stunts. It worked. ACT UP forced the FDA to accelerate drug approval, pressured drug companies to lower prices, and changed the conversation about AIDS." Shilts describes the most famous AIDS activist organization.
> **Key takeaway**: ACT UP proved that direct action could change policy. They were angry, well-organized, and scientifically literate. They read the research, understood the regulatory process, and targeted the bottlenecks. They didn't just protest — they won.
