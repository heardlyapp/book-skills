# Reference: Receiving Forgiveness

## Overview
Pope Francis emphasizes the sacrament of Reconciliation as the ordinary means by which Catholics receive God's mercy. He wants to remove every obstacle that prevents people from experiencing the freedom of forgiveness.

## Case: The Sacrament of Reconciliation
Confession has four essential parts: contrition (sorrow for sin), confession (telling your sins to a priest), absolution (the priest's prayer of forgiveness), and satisfaction (performing the penance). Francis emphasizes that God's forgiveness is given freely — the priest does not "earn" or "merit" it. The priest is a channel, not the source. The sacrament is a celebration, not a punishment.

## Case: The Examination of Conscience
Before confession, Catholics are encouraged to examine their conscience — to review their lives honestly in the light of God's commandments. Francis suggests using the Beatitudes, the Ten Commandments, and the Works of Mercy as guides. The examination is not meant to produce despair but to prepare the heart to receive mercy. The goal is not to catalog sins but to recognize one's need for God.

## Case: The Scandal of God's Mercy
Pope Francis acknowledges that God's mercy can be scandalous — especially to those who consider themselves righteous. The prodigal son's father did not wait for the son to earn forgiveness. He ran to meet him. This offends our sense of justice: it seems unfair that sinners can be forgiven while the faithful struggle. But Francis argues this is exactly the point: God's love is not fair in the way we calculate fairness. It is extravagant, excessive, and undeserved.
