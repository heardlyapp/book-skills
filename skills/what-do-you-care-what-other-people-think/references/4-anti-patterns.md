# Anti-Patterns: What Feynman Fought Against

## ❌ Deferring to Authority
The most dangerous intellectual trap. NASA management told Feynman the shuttle was safe. Experts produced complex probability models. Senior engineers assured him the O-rings were within specifications. Feynman ignored all of it and looked at the physical evidence.

> **Case: The Management Shuffle** (Part 2, "The Rogers Commission"): During the Challenger investigation, Feynman discovered that NASA engineers had known about O-ring problems for years. They had raised concerns repeatedly. Each time, management dismissed them or demanded more data. By the time management admitted there was a problem, seven astronauts were dead. Feynman's finding: the disaster wasn't a technical failure. It was a management failure — specifically, a failure to listen to people who knew the truth.
> **Key takeaway:** The most dangerous people in any organization are those who say "trust me" when they should be saying "here's the evidence." Authority without evidence is just power.

## ❌ Using Jargon to Hide Ignorance
Feynman was merciless with people who used complex language to obscure weak thinking. "If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough" — and many people don't understand what they're talking about.

## ❌ Groupthink and Consensus Bias
When everyone agrees, Feynman got suspicious. He believed that consensus was often a sign that nobody was thinking independently. Healthy disagreement produces better answers than polite agreement.

## ❌ Certainty Without Evidence
The people who are most certain are often the most wrong. Feynman distrusted anyone who claimed to know the answer without showing their work. His greatest intellectual rage was reserved for those who expressed certainty without evidence.

> **Case: The Certainty of NASA Management** (Appendix F): In Feynman's appendix to the Rogers Commission Report, he wrote scathingly about NASA's "certification" process. Engineers had certified the shuttle safe using probability calculations Feynman called "nonsense." They had assigned a 1 in 100,000 chance of catastrophic failure. The actual number, based on the O-ring evidence alone, was closer to 1 in 100. Certainty without evidence killed seven people.
> **Key takeaway:** Certainty is not a virtue. Doubt is. The willingness to say "I don't know" and to question your own assumptions is the foundation of truth.

## ❌ Caring What Others Think
The book's central warning: living your life for the approval of others is a form of intellectual and emotional slavery. Feynman didn't dress like a professor, didn't talk like a professor, didn't play the academic game. He was himself. That authenticity was his superpower.

## ❌ Ignoring Contradictory Evidence

Feynman's most famous critique of NASA was not that they lacked data — it was that they had the data and chose to ignore it because it contradicted their narrative. Multiple flights had shown O-ring damage. Each time, engineers found a way to rationalize it as "within limits." The anti-pattern: when evidence contradicts your belief, your instinct is to explain it away rather than change your belief.

**The fix:** Treat every piece of contradictory evidence as a gift. It's showing you where your model is wrong. Thank it. Adjust your model.
