# Principles: Feynman's Code

## Principle 1: The Truth Doesn't Care About Feelings
Feynman's single most important principle: nature cannot be fooled. The universe doesn't care about your beliefs, your politics, your reputation, or your feelings. Evidence is the only authority. When the evidence contradicts your position, your position must change — not the evidence.

## Principle 2: Authority Is Not a Valid Argument
An idea is not true because an expert says it, a textbook states it, or a majority believes it. Feynman treated Nobel laureates the same way he treated undergraduates: with respect for their evidence, not their status. When he said something that contradicted a Nobel Prize winner, he was expected to show his work. Same standard for everyone.

> **Case: The Doubting Nobel Laureate** (Chapter 5, "Who the Hell Is Herman?"): Feynman describes being challenged by a famous physicist who insisted Feynman's calculation was wrong. Feynman asked to see his evidence. The physicist couldn't produce it — he just "knew" it was wrong because it didn't match his intuition. Feynman walked him through the math. In the end, the physicist admitted: "You're right. I was just sure you were wrong."
> **Key takeaway:** Even the smartest people are wrong more often than they think. Confidence is not evidence. Always ask: "How do you know?"

## Principle 3: Doubt Everything, Including Yourself
Feynman's greatest strength was his willingness to doubt his own conclusions. He scrutinized his own reasoning as rigorously as he scrutinized others'. "The first principle is that you must not fool yourself — and you are the easiest person to fool."

## Principle 4: Simplify Relentlessly
Feynman believed that any idea that cannot be explained in simple terms is either not understood or not true. He spent enormous effort simplifying his own understanding — not just for communication, but for his own clarity. The simpler the explanation, the deeper the understanding.

## Principle 5: Follow Curiosity, Not Career
Feynman famously said he did physics "for fun." He worked on problems because they were interesting, not because they were important. His most celebrated contributions (quantum electrodynamics, the Challenger investigation) came from following his curiosity, not from strategic career planning.

> **Case: The Rotating Plate** (Chapter 1): Feynman was watching a student toss a plate in the cafeteria. He noticed the plate wobbled and spun at a specific ratio. Out of pure curiosity, he calculated the equations of motion. It was trivial physics, nothing important. A colleague asked why he was wasting time on it. Feynman shrugged. That "wasted time" led to his Nobel Prize-winning work on quantum electrodynamics.
> **Key takeaway:** "Important" work often comes from "unimportant" curiosity. Follow what interests you, even if it seems pointless. The connections will reveal themselves later.

## Principle 6: Admit Ignorance Freely
Feynman had no problem saying "I don't know." He believed that pretending to know was a form of dishonesty that prevented learning. His willingness to be ignorant in public — to ask "dumb" questions — was one of his greatest strengths.
