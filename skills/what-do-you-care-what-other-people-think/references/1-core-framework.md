# Core Framework: The Feynman Method

Feynman's approach to understanding anything rests on a few deceptively simple principles that together form a complete system for learning, thinking, and discovering truth.

## The Feynman Technique

The most famous of Feynman's methods has four steps:
1. **Choose a concept** you want to understand.
2. **Explain it in plain language** as if teaching it to someone with no background.
3. **Identify gaps** — where your explanation breaks down or becomes fuzzy, that's where you don't actually understand.
4. **Review and simplify** — go back to the source material, fill the gaps, then simplify further.

Feynman believed that jargon and complexity are often masks for incomplete understanding. If you truly understand something, you can say it simply.

> **Case: The "Why?" Game** (Chapter 1, "The Making of a Scientist"): As a child, Feynman's father taught him by asking endless "why" questions. When reading about dinosaurs, his father didn't just say "that dinosaur is 25 feet tall." He explained: "If that dinosaur stood in our front yard, his head would reach our second-floor window, but his head would be too big to fit through the window." The abstract number became concrete understanding.
> **Key takeaway:** True understanding means being able to translate abstract concepts into concrete, relatable experience. If you can't visualize it, you don't know it.

## First Principles Thinking

Feynman distrusted analogies and received wisdom. He insisted on starting from the most basic, undeniable facts and reasoning upward. This is how he solved problems that stumped others — he didn't look at what experts said. He looked at what the evidence said.

> **Case: The Challenger Investigation** (Part 2): When NASA presented Feynman with complex probability charts showing the shuttle's risk was 1 in 100,000, he didn't accept the number. He asked basic questions: "What materials are the O-rings made of? What happens to them in cold?" He conducted a simple experiment at the hearing — dropping O-ring material into ice water — and demonstrated that the cold made them brittle. His first-principles approach blew through NASA's obfuscation.
> **Key takeaway:** When faced with complexity, don't accept the framing you're given. Go back to the most basic facts. Test them yourself. Build up from there.

## The Scientific Attitude

Feynman defined science not as a body of knowledge but as a method: doubt, question, test, revise. The key is intellectual honesty — you must be willing to be wrong. The moment you decide you're right and stop testing, you've left science.

**Key insight:** The opposite of scientific thinking is not religion or ignorance — it's certainty. The moment you're certain, you've stopped thinking.

## The Art of Not Fooling Yourself

Feynman's most famous lecture, "Cargo Cult Science," describes how people mimic the form of science without the substance. Like South Pacific islanders building fake runways to summon airplanes, organizations create the appearance of rigor (reviews, certifications, sign-offs) while missing the essential ingredient: honest doubt.

The lesson for everyday life: check yourself before you trust yourself. Ask: "What would prove me wrong?" If you can't answer that question, you're not doing science — you're running a cargo cult.
