# Anti-Patterns

## 1. Starting with Rules Instead of Procedures

**The mistake**: Posting a list of rules on the first day.

**Fix**: Rules tell students what NOT to do. Procedures tell them what TO do. Teach procedures, not rules.

## 2. Inconsistency

**The mistake**: Enforcing procedures inconsistently — sometimes requiring them, sometimes ignoring them.

**Fix**: Consistency is the most important quality in classroom management. Enforce procedures every time, with every student.

> **Case: The Inconsistent Teacher** (Chapter 5): A teacher had a procedure for handing in homework but didn't enforce it consistently. Some days she collected it at the door, some days during class, some days not at all. Students never knew what to do. The procedure could have worked — but inconsistency killed it.
> **Key takeaway**: A procedure that isn't consistently enforced is worse than no procedure at all. Consistency builds the habit.

> **Case: The Administrator's Test** (Chapter 14): An administrator could tell within five minutes of walking into a classroom whether procedures were in place. In a well-managed room, students were working, transitions were smooth, and the teacher was teaching. In a chaotic room, students were confused, and the teacher was managing behavior. The difference was always procedures.
>
> **Key takeaway**: The presence or absence of procedures is immediately visible. An outside observer can tell within minutes whether a classroom is well-managed. Procedures are not invisible — they determine the entire environment.

## 3. Starting the Year Without a Plan

**The mistake**: Showing up on the first day without a detailed, scripted plan.

**Fix**: The first day must be scripted minute by minute. Wing it, and you'll spend the rest of the year catching up.

## 4. Focusing on Content Before Procedures

**The mistake**: Jumping straight into teaching content without establishing procedures first.

**Fix**: Spend the first week establishing procedures. The content will be learned more efficiently in a well-managed classroom.

## 5. Blaming Students for Chaos

**The mistake**: Attributing classroom chaos to "difficult students."

**Fix**: Chaos is almost always a failure of procedures, not a failure of students. Fix the procedures, and the behavior follows.
