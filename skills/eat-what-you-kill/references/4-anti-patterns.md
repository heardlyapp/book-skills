# 4 — Objection Handling Anti-Patterns

> Source: "Eat What You Kill" by Sam Taggart

## The Objection Framework: Diagnose Before You Respond

Taggart's first rule: determine whether the objection is a **Deal-Breaker**, **Smoke Screen**, or **True Objection**.

| Type | What it is | How to handle |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| **Deal-Breaker** | A genuine, non-negotiable barrier | Acknowledge and move on. Don't waste time. |
| **Smoke Screen** | A vague excuse hiding the real objection | Gently probe. "If we could solve X, would you be interested?" |
| **True Objection** | A real concern that can be addressed | Address it directly with evidence and empathy. |

> **Case: The "I'll Think About It" Trap** (Chapter 61): Taggart explains that "I'll think about it" is almost always a smoke screen. The prospect needs more value, more trust, or more urgency — but won't tell you that directly. The fix: don't accept "I'll think about it." Use a Tie-Down: "What specifically would you like to think about?" Force the real objection to surface.
> **Key takeaway**: A vague objection is a hidden objection. Your job is to make it visible before you can address it.

## Objection Handling Techniques

### FFF — Feel / Felt / Found
1. "I understand how you **feel**." (Empathy)
2. "Many of my clients **felt** the same way." (Normalizing)
3. "What they **found** was…" (Proof + solution)

### IIO — Identify / Isolate / Overcome
1. **Identify**: What is the specific objection?
2. **Isolate**: "If we could solve this one concern, would you be ready to move forward?"
3. **Overcome**: Present evidence, reframe, or offer a solution.

### ARAT — Agree / Restate / Antidote / Transition
1. **Agree**: "That's a fair point."
2. **Restate**: "So you're concerned about X..."
3. **Antidote**: "Here's how we handle that..."
4. **Transition**: "Does that address your concern?"

## Anti-Pattern: Selective Hearing

The mistake: only hearing objections that confirm your fear that the prospect won't buy. Selective hearing makes you miss buying signals. Train yourself to hear both — objections AND signals.

> **Case: The Blind Spot** (Chapter 62): Taggart describes salespeople who hear "I'm not interested" and immediately check out mentally, missing the prospect's next sentence: "…but tell me more about what you offer." The prospect is testing you. If you give up at the first sign of resistance, you fail the test.
> **Key takeaway**: Don't stop listening after the first objection. The real signal often comes right after the resistance.

## Anti-Pattern: Taking Rejection Personally

> **Case: The Slamming Door** (Introduction): At 13, when someone was rude or slammed the door, Taggart "learned to shrug it off." He kept his emotional state stable regardless of the prospect's reaction. This is the cardinal rule of carnivore mindset: the rejection is not about you.
> **Key takeaway**: Every slammed door is just a data point. One more "no" in the ledger. Never make it mean something about your worth as a person or salesperson.
