# 3 — Pitch and Presentation Techniques

> Source: "Eat What You Kill" by Sam Taggart

## The Five-Step Opening (First 30 Seconds)

Taggart's opening framework for when you're face-to-face with a prospect:

1. **The Opening** — Your first 3 seconds. Confidence, posture, eye contact. You've already been judged before you speak.
2. **The What** — What you're offering. Clear, simple, benefit-focused. 5 seconds.
3. **The Why** — Why they should care. Emotional hook. 10 seconds.
4. **The Pullback** — Give them an out. "If this isn't for you, no problem — I'll be on my way." Disarms resistance.
5. **The Transition** — Move into the conversation naturally. "Mind if I ask you a quick question?"

> **Case: The Curb Painting Script** (Introduction): At 13, Taggart wrote his own sales script for curb painting. Decades later, his dad found the old computer files — "the script I wrote for my reps. It was a pretty good script for a kid with no sales training. It included stage directions like 'Slow down here… Pause after this line… Now change your inflection to get excited.' It all came from trial and error."
> **Key takeaway**: The best sales scripts are born from experience, not theory. Taggart's childhood scripts evolved through constant testing. The 5-step opening formalizes what he learned through thousands of door knocks.

## The Needs Audit

Before you present anything, uncover what the prospect actually needs. Ask questions. Listen more than you talk. The Four Types of Questions:
- **Open-ended** (explore needs)
- **Closed** (confirm specifics)
- **Leading** (guide toward a solution)
- **Reflective** (deepen understanding)

## Value = Emotion + Logic

Every sale must satisfy both. Logic justifies the purchase to the rational mind. Emotion drives the decision. Without emotion, the prospect stays neutral. Without logic, they don't trust the decision.

> **Case: The Emergency Pitch** (Introduction): At 13, Taggart's curb painting pitch was: "If visitors ever had trouble seeing your address from the street, they might have a big problem if they needed the police or fire department in an emergency." This is the perfect blend of emotion (fear of emergency) and logic ($20 fixes it). "If my two-minute pitch worked (which it usually did), I'd use a stencil and spray-paint the curb, and ten minutes later I'd be off to the next house."
> **Key takeaway**: A pitch that combines emotional urgency with a clear, logical solution is unstoppable. Taggart learned this at 13 and it still works.

## The Pain Tunnel

Guide the prospect to feel the pain of their current problem before you present your solution. The deeper they feel the pain, the more valuable your solution becomes.

## Selective Amnesia

After a rejection, forget it instantly. Every new prospect is a clean slate. Don't carry yesterday's rejections into today's conversations.
