# Core Framework — The Dhammapada

## What the Dhammapada Is

The Dhammapada is a collection of 423 verses in Pali, attributed to the Buddha. It is part of the Pali Canon (Tipitaka) and is the most widely read Buddhist scripture. The title means "The Path of Truth" or "The Path of Dharma." Translated by Valerie J. Roebuck for Penguin Classics (2010). Case: The Dhammapada has been translated into more languages than any other Buddhist text.

## Chapter 1: Twins (Yamaka-vagga)

The opening chapter sets the theme: "All that we are is the result of what we have thought." Mind is the forerunner of all experience. Verses on opposites — good and evil, happiness and suffering — emphasizing that our mental states determine our experience. Case: "If with an impure mind you speak or act, then suffering follows you like the cart follows the ox."

## Chapter 2: Awareness (Appamada-vagga)

"Awareness is the path to the deathless. Unawareness is the path to death." Mindfulness is the central practice. Verse 21-32 emphasize that the aware person shines like the moon freed from clouds. Case: "The awake are always active, striving for peace, never resting."

## Chapter 10: The Rod (Danda-vagga)

All beings fear punishment, all fear death. "Putting yourself in the place of another, you should neither kill nor cause to kill." This chapter on non-violence (ahimsa) is the ethical foundation of Buddhism. Case: "All beings tremble at violence. Life is dear to all."

## Chapter 11: Old Age (Jara-vagga)

The impermanence of all things. "What is laughter, what is joy, when the world is always burning?" A reminder that youth and beauty fade, and the wise seek what does not decay. Case: "This body is worn out with age, a nest of diseases, fragile and breaking."

## Chapter 20: The Path (Magga-vagga)

The Eightfold Path: Right View, Right Intention, Right Speech, Right Action, Right Livelihood, Right Effort, Right Mindfulness, Right Concentration. "Of all paths, the Eightfold Path is the best." Case: "This is the path — there is no other — for the purification of insight."
