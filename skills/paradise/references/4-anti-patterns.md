# 4. Anti-Patterns — Systemic Failures That Made the Fire Deadly

> Source: "Paradise" by Lizzie Johnson (2021). Anti-patterns drawn from Chapters 2-3 (the conditions), 10-11 (the failures during the fire), and 16-20 (the aftermath and reckoning).

## Mistake 1: The Single Point of Failure — One Road for 27,000 People

**What it looks like**: A community has only one viable evacuation route, and that route passes through the most dangerous part of the fire zone.

**Why it fails**: When the Skyway — Paradise's main road — became clogged with traffic, the entire evacuation system collapsed. People abandoned their cars and ran on foot. The fire caught them. Some were found in their vehicles; others were found outside them, having tried to escape on foot and failed.

**Also fails because**: The single road was two lanes with no shoulder in many sections. When cars broke down or crashed, they couldn't be passed. The road created a bottleneck that turned a fast evacuation into a deadly traffic jam.

> **Case: The Skyway Traffic Jam** (Chapters 7-8): As the fire descended on Paradise, the Skyway became a parking lot. People abandoned their vehicles, leaving engines running and doors open. Fire trucks couldn't get through. Ambulances couldn't reach patients. Some residents drove onto sidewalks and lawns to bypass the gridlock; others were trapped. The single evacuation route was the community's fatal vulnerability.
> **Key takeaway**: Your community's evacuation capacity is determined by its bottleneck, not its average road capacity. If you only have one way out, you don't have a way out.

## Mistake 2: Deferred Infrastructure Maintenance

**What it looks like**: A utility company, government agency, or business delays essential maintenance for years, even decades, because the cost is high and the failure seems unlikely.

**Why it fails**: Infrastructure ages silently. A 90-year-old transmission tower doesn't look dangerous — until the wind blows at 50 mph and a $10 part (the "cobra" clamp) fails, starting a fire that destroys a town.

> **Case: PG&E's Cobra Clamp** (Chapters 1, 11, 20): The Camp Fire was started by a hook on a PG&E transmission tower that was built in the 1920s. The hook held a type of aluminum "C" clamp called a "cobra." In high winds, the cobra failed, allowing a power line to contact the steel tower. PG&E had known its transmission infrastructure was aging and under-maintained. Internal reports warned of the risk. But replacing thousands of towers across California was expensive, so maintenance was deferred. The cost of deferral: $30 billion in liabilities, 85 lives, and the destruction of a town.
> **Key takeaway**: Deferred maintenance is not a cost-saving measure — it's a bet against disaster. When the bet fails, the cost is exponentially higher than the maintenance would have been.

## Mistake 3: Development Without Risk Assessment

**What it looks like**: Homes, schools, and hospitals are built in high-risk areas without adequate infrastructure to support evacuation or fire defense.

**Why it fails**: Paradise's growth was driven by affordability — it was one of the cheapest places to live in California. But affordability masked vulnerability. Homes were built with wood-shake roofs. Streets were narrow and winding. There was no central sewer system, which limited commercial development and kept the tax base too small to fund adequate emergency services.

> **Case: The Wildland-Urban Interface** (Chapter 2): Paradise was a classic example of the "wildland-urban interface" — the zone where human development meets undeveloped wilderness. As California's housing crisis deepened, more people moved into these high-risk areas because land was cheap. But there were no zoning restrictions, no requirements for fire-resistant construction, and no limits on density. The market incentivized building in the most fire-prone locations.
> **Key takeaway**: Affordable housing in high-risk areas is not affordable — it's subsidized by disaster risk. The true cost is paid by insurance systems, government disaster funds, and the lives of residents.

## Mistake 4: Information Fragmentation During Crisis

**What it looks like**: Multiple agencies, each with part of the picture, fail to communicate effectively during a fast-moving emergency.

**Why it fails**: During the Camp Fire, Cal Fire, the Paradise Police Department, the Butte County Sheriff's Office, and PG&E each had different information about where the fire was, where it was going, and who needed to evacuate. Cell towers burned, cutting off communication. Radio systems had dead zones. The result: some neighborhoods received evacuation warnings after the fire had already arrived.

> **Case: The Cell Tower Failure** (Chapter 7): Within the first hour of the fire, multiple cell towers burned. Residents who had received reverse-911 calls couldn't call back to confirm. People trying to reach family members got busy signals. Social media became the primary information channel — but social media was also full of rumors, misinformation, and conflicting reports.
> **Key takeaway**: Communication systems fail exactly when they're needed most. Build redundancy: satellite phones, ham radios, neighborhood communication networks that work without cell towers.
