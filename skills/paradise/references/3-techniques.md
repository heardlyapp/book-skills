# 3. Techniques — Evacuation, Rescue, and Recovery Methods

> Source: "Paradise" by Lizzie Johnson (2021). Techniques observed during the Camp Fire response, from Chapters 3-15 (the fire and immediate aftermath) and 16-20 (recovery).

## Before the Fire: Preparation Techniques

### Defensible Space Protocol
The Butte County Fire Safe Council taught residents a systematic approach:
- Clear all vegetation within 30 feet of structures
- Trim tree branches at least 10 feet from roofs and chimneys
- Use fire-resistant roofing materials (no wood shakes)
- Create a 100-foot buffer zone on sloped properties
- Keep grass mowed and irrigated near structures

### The "Go Bag" Method
Experienced residents kept pre-packed bags:
- Medications and medical devices
- Important documents (insurance, deeds, IDs)
- Phone chargers and portable batteries
- Cash (ATMs fail during disasters)
- Changes of clothing and basic toiletries
- Pet carriers and pet food

## During the Fire: Evacuation Techniques

### The Pre-Evacuation Decision
Captain Matt McKenzie and Cal Fire knew that the key to survival was getting people out before the fire arrived. They made the call to evacuate based on wind speed, fuel moisture, and the fire's rate of spread — not waiting for official confirmation.

> **Case: The "Iron Maiden" Hospital Evacuation** (Chapters 5-6): At Feather River Hospital, staff improvised an evacuation when formal plans failed. Patients were carried down staircases on backboards. The hospital's bus — nicknamed "the Iron Maiden" because its air conditioning had broken — was pressed into service. Nurses loaded the most critical patients first. The decision tree: most mobile patients first (they could walk), then bedridden patients (needed carrying), then patients on life support (slowest to move). This triage-like approach saved everyone inside.
> **Key takeaway**: In an improvised evacuation, prioritize by mobility and dependency. The fastest-to-move go first, freeing capacity for the most difficult.

### The Bus Driver's Rescue Protocol
Kevin McKay's approach to rescuing children during the fire:
1. Ignore official channels when they're silent — act on local knowledge
2. Drive toward known danger areas (children's homes) not away from them
3. Communicate with parents through any available means (bus window, shouting, hand signals)
4. If a road is blocked, find an alternative — even if it means driving through flames

> **Case: Kevin McKay's Route Through Fire** (Chapter 9): When the fire hit during his morning bus route, Kevin knew some children would be home sick or stranded. The road was clogged with evacuating cars. He drove the shoulder, then the wrong direction on a one-way road, then through a burning field. His training in the school district's safety protocols helped him make split-second decisions: keep the windows closed, keep moving, don't stop for anything. Every child on his route survived.
> **Key takeaway**: Evacuation routes are guidelines, not laws. When survival is at stake, use every available option — shoulders, wrong-way lanes, unpaved surfaces. Keep moving.

## After the Fire: Recovery Techniques

### Toxic Debris Removal
After the fire, the EPA classified Paradise's debris as hazardous waste. Asbestos from burned homes, lead from paint, and arsenic from treated lumber contaminated the ash. The removal process:
1. Hazard assessment by EPA teams
2. Removal of visible debris (appliances, vehicles, batteries)
3. Soil testing for contamination
4. Removal of the top 6 inches of soil in contaminated areas
5. Final testing and certification

### The Rebuilding Decision Framework
Residents faced an agonizing choice: rebuild in the same location or leave. Factors:
- Insurance payout (many were underinsured)
- FEMA buyout availability
- Emotional attachment to the land
- Fear of another fire
- Community ties

> **Case: The Mayor of Nowhere** (Chapter 17): After the fire, Paradise's mayor, Jody Jones, became known as the "mayor of nowhere" — the town physically didn't exist anymore, but she still had a job to do. She pushed FEMA for faster housing, advocated for mental health services, and led the debate on whether to rebuild. Her approach: acknowledge the trauma, provide practical support, and give people time to make their own decisions about returning.
> **Key takeaway**: Leadership after disaster is not about having answers — it's about creating the conditions for the community to find its own answers.
