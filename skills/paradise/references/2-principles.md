# 2. Principles — Lessons from Disaster

> Source: "Paradise" by Lizzie Johnson (2021). Principles extracted from the collective experience of Paradise's residents, first responders, and survivors across all 20 chapters and the epilogue.

## The 7 Principles of Wildfire Resilience

### Principle 1: Speed Changes Everything
Standard evacuation plans assume hours of warning. The Camp Fire gave minutes. In the town's mobile home parks, residents had an especially difficult time evacuating because they couldn't drive away quickly. The fire moved faster than cars could travel on the choked Skyway.

**What this means for you**: If you live in a wildfire-prone area, your evacuation plan should assume you have 5 minutes — not 5 hours. Keep your car pointed toward the exit. Pack a go bag. Know two routes out.

### Principle 2: Your Neighbors Are Your First Responders
Official fire engines and ambulances couldn't reach many areas of Paradise during the fire. The people who saved the most lives were ordinary residents who knocked on doors, offered rides, and shared information.

> **Case: Kevin McKay and Bus 963** (Chapter 9): Kevin McKay, a school bus driver making $11 an hour, drove his 22-year-old bus, Bus 963, through flames and smoke to rescue children. With no official evacuation order, Kevin trusted his own judgment. He drove into the fire zone, picked up children who had been separated from their parents, and navigated through burning debris. The bus's rear tires began to melt, but he kept driving. Every child on his route survived.
> **Key takeaway**: In the gap between official response and immediate need, ordinary people make extraordinary decisions. Train your community, not just your emergency services.

### Principle 3: Defensible Space Is Non-Negotiable
Homes that survived the Camp Fire had one thing in common: cleared brush around them. The fire department's pre-fire inspections and community education programs on defensible space saved some structures that would otherwise have burned.

> **Case: The Paradise Fire Safe Council** (Chapters 3, 14): Before the fire, the Butte County Fire Safe Council had worked for years to educate residents about clearing vegetation, using fire-resistant building materials, and creating evacuation plans. After the fire, the structures that met defensible space standards were disproportionately likely to survive. The Council's work was not abstract — it was life-saving infrastructure.
> **Key takeaway**: Prevention is not glamorous, but it works. Every dollar spent on defensible space, fire-resistant roofing, and community education saves multiples in disaster response costs and lives.

### Principle 4: Information Infrastructure Is Critical Infrastructure
During the Camp Fire, cell towers burned within the first hour. Radio communication was patchy. Residents didn't know where the fire was, which roads were open, or whether their families were safe.

### Principle 5: Recovery Is a Marathon, Not a Sprint
The fire burned in a day. Recovery took years. Toxic debris removal alone took six months. Insurance companies fought claims. FEMA housing was slow to arrive. Mental health crises emerged months after the fire, when the initial shock had worn off.

### Principle 6: Accountability Must Follow Disaster
PG&E, California's largest utility, had known its transmission infrastructure was failing. A "cobra" clamp on a 90-year-old tower had broken — a failure that had been predicted by internal reports. The company filed for bankruptcy in 2019, citing $30 billion in potential liability.

### Principle 7: The Land Remembers
The Konkow legend and the geological history of Butte County both tell the same story: fire has always been part of this landscape. The question is not whether fire will return, but whether we will be ready.
