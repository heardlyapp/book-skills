# 1. Core Framework — The Anatomy of a Megafire

> Source: "Paradise: One Town's Struggle to Survive an American Wildfire" by Lizzie Johnson (2021). The five-part structure of the book (Kindling, Spark, Conflagration, Containment, Ash) provides the core framework.

## The Five-Stage Disaster Framework

Johnson's book is structured around five chronological stages, each revealing a different dimension of disaster.

### Stage 1: Kindling — The Conditions for Disaster

The Camp Fire did not start on November 8, 2018. It started decades earlier, when the conditions for a catastrophic fire were put in place. By 2018, Butte County had received only 0.88 inches of rain since May 1 — a historic low. The jet stream was sluggish, failing to push rain clouds over the Sierra Nevada. Plants withered. Trees died. The forest floor was carpeted with dry fuel.

Meanwhile, the town of Paradise had grown from a tiny settlement into a community of 27,000 people, built on a ridge accessible by only one main road. More than 280 miles of private roads twisted to abrupt dead ends. Many homes had wood-shake roofs. Invasive Scotch broom coated the hillsides. PG&E's electrical infrastructure — including a 90-year-old transmission line on Jarbo Gap — had received decades of deferred maintenance.

> **Case: The Konkow Legend** (Author's Note, Part I): A Konkow tribal couple shared a centuries-old legend with Johnson: two boys had accidentally started a wildfire that killed most of the tribe, forcing survivors north. The legend illustrated that catastrophic wildfire was not new to this landscape — it was cyclical. The Konkows once managed the land with controlled burns, a practice European settlers suppressed. The legend frames the entire book: fire is natural; the problem is how we've mismanaged the land.
> **Key takeaway**: Disaster is cyclical, not exceptional. Native peoples understood this and managed accordingly. Modern development erased that knowledge at great cost.

### Stage 2: Spark — The Trigger Event

On the morning of November 8, 2018, gale-force winds (50+ mph gusts) caused a hook on a PG&E transmission tower — built in the 1920s — to fail. A type of aluminum "C" clamp called a "cobra" broke, allowing a live power line to contact the steel tower. White-hot arcs of electricity superheated the air, igniting the dry brush below. Within minutes, a small fire became a running inferno.

### Stage 3: Conflagration — The Disaster Unfolds

The fire burned 80,000 acres in its first 12 hours. It moved so fast that standard evacuation protocols were useless. The fire created its own weather — a pyrocumulus cloud that towered 30,000 feet into the atmosphere. Wind speeds inside the fire exceeded 70 mph, hurling embers miles ahead of the flame front.

> **Case: The Hospital Evacuation** (Chapters 5-6): Feather River Hospital, Paradise's largest employer, had 1,300 employees and dozens of patients when the fire hit. The hospital had no formal wildfire evacuation plan. Staff carried patients down staircases, loaded them into private vehicles, and eventually commandeered a school bus nicknamed "the Iron Maiden" because of its broken air conditioning. Nurses had to decide which patients to carry and which to leave. The hospital burned to the ground after everyone escaped.
> **Key takeaway**: In a fast-moving disaster, formal plans fail. Improvisation, courage, and local knowledge save more lives than official protocols.

### Stage 4: Containment — The Response

Cal Fire Captain Matt McKenzie and his crew at Station 36 faced a fire that defied standard tactics. The fire was moving faster than any engine could outrun. They shifted from offense (trying to stop the fire) to defense (protecting structures and evacuating people). The mutual aid system brought firefighters from across the state, but by the time they arrived, the town was already gone.

### Stage 5: Ash — The Aftermath

Eighty-five people died. The town was 95% destroyed. PG&E filed for bankruptcy. FEMA set up temporary housing. Toxic ash from burned homes (containing asbestos, lead, and other hazardous materials) had to be removed before rebuilding could begin. The question of whether to rebuild in the same fire-prone location divided the community.
