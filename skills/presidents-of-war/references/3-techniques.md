# Crisis Decision-Making and the Escalation Pattern

> Source: Presidents of War, Chapters 1, 6, 7, 11, 16

## The Beschloss Escalation Pattern

Beschloss identifies a recurring pattern across American wars:

### Stage 1: The Provocation
A real or manufactured incident (Maine explosion, Gulf of Tonkin, Lusitania sinking, Pearl Harbor) creates a public demand for action.

### Stage 2: The President Decides
The president debates internally (often with divided advisors) and decides to use force.

### Stage 3: Initial Success
The early phase of the war goes well. Public support is high. The president is praised.

### Stage 4: The Plateau
The war becomes harder than expected. The enemy adapts. Casualties mount.

### Stage 5: Escalation
The president sends more troops, expands the bombing, widens the war. Each escalation is presented as the final step.

### Stage 6: The Crisis
The war reaches a turning point (Tet 1968, Chosin Reservoir 1950, Second Bull Run 1862). The president must make the hardest decision.

### Stage 7: Resolution or Protraction
The war ends (either through victory, negotiated settlement, or withdrawal) — or it drags on indefinitely.

> **Case: Madison and the Burning of Washington** (Chapter 1)
> The War of 1812 was the first test of the young republic's war powers. President Madison was a weak commander-in-chief. He failed to coordinate the military, failed to fund the war adequately, and failed to prevent the British from marching into Washington and burning the White House. Madison fled the capital and was nearly captured. The war was a near-disaster for the United States — but it produced the Star-Spangled Banner, the Battle of New Orleans (Jackson), and a renewed sense of national identity.
> **Key takeaway**: The first wartime president set a low bar. Madison showed that a weak commander-in-chief can endanger the nation. Every subsequent president has known: failure in war can mean the end of your presidency — and possibly the nation.

> **Case: The Spanish-American War and Yellow Journalism** (Chapter 7)
> McKinley was reluctant to go to war with Spain. But the Hearst and Pulitzer newspapers whipped up public outrage over Spanish atrocities in Cuba. The USS Maine exploded in Havana harbor — and the newspapers blamed Spain, despite the cause being most likely an accidental internal explosion. McKinley gave in to the war fever. The war lasted 10 weeks and was won easily. But it began the American empire (Philippines, Puerto Rico, Guam) and set the precedent that media-driven war fever could push a reluctant president into conflict.
> **Key takeaway**: The president who is pushed into war by public opinion and media pressure may win the war but lose control of its consequences. McKinley won the Spanish-American War but lost the debate about what to do with the Philippines — and the American empire that followed has been controversial ever since.
