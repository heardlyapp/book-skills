# Michael Beschloss's Voice & Application Scenarios

> Source: Presidents of War, all chapters; Author's Note

## Authorial Voice

Michael Beschloss is America's preeminent presidential historian. His voice is:

- **Narrative and dramatic**: Beschloss writes history as story. The presidents are characters, the decisions are plot points, the consequences are tragic.
- **Judicial but not partisan**: He judges presidents by their decisions, not their parties. Lincoln is praised; Polk is criticized; LBJ is pitied and condemned.
- **Constitutional in perspective**: The Constitution is his reference point. He measures presidential war-making against the Founders' intentions.
- **Democratic in sympathy**: He is on the side of Congress and the people, not the executive. His heroes are presidents who respected constitutional limits; his villains are those who exceeded them.

### Classic Beschloss Quotes

- "The Constitution gave Congress the power to declare war. The president is commander in chief. The ambiguity has never been resolved."
- "Every American president who has taken the nation into war has underestimated its cost."
- "The Gulf of Tonkin Resolution was based on an event that may never have happened. Fifty-eight thousand Americans died."
- "Lincoln suspended habeas corpus. FDR interned Japanese-Americans. Even great presidents make terrible decisions in war."
- "The best wartime leaders have seen combat themselves. The worst have not."

## Five Application Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Citizen Wondering About Presidential Power
You see presidents ordering military action without congressional approval and wonder: is this normal?
**Lesson**: It has become normal — but that doesn't make it constitutional. Beschloss traces the normalization. The first major war without a declaration was Korea (1950). The pattern has accelerated since. The War Powers Resolution (1973) tried to reassert congressional authority but has been largely ignored.

### Scenario 2: Public Official Considering a War Vote
You're in Congress and the president asks for authorization to use military force. How do you decide?
**Lesson**: Beschloss's framework: demand evidence of the provocation, demand a clear definition of success, demand an exit strategy, and demand that the president be honest about the cost. The Gulf of Tonkin Resolution passed because Congress didn't ask hard questions.

### Scenario 3: Citizen Trying to Understand Current Conflicts
The news reports airstrikes, troop deployments, and military actions. It's confusing.
**Lesson**: Use Beschloss's pattern: is there a clear provocation? Has Congress authorized this? What is the stated goal — and is it achievable? What is the exit strategy? The same questions that should have been asked before every American war.

### Scenario 4: Student of Leadership
You're studying leadership and want to understand the difference between effective and failed wartime presidents.
**Lesson**: Lincoln, FDR, and Eisenhower were effective because they combined strategic clarity with operational flexibility and constitutional constraint. Polk, LBJ, and Nixon failed because they lacked one of these elements — usually constraint.

### Scenario 5: Person Thinking About the Constitution
The Constitution's war provisions seem outdated. Should they be revised?
**Lesson**: Beschloss doesn't propose specific amendments, but the book makes clear: the system of checks and balances has broken down on war powers. Congress has been too willing to delegate its authority. The president has been too willing to exceed it.

> **Case: The War Powers Resolution of 1973** (Chapter 17, Aftermath)
> Congress passed the War Powers Resolution over Nixon's veto. It requires the president to notify Congress within 48 hours of committing forces and limits engagement to 60 days without congressional authorization. Since 1973, every president has considered the resolution unconstitutional — and no president has fully complied with it. The resolution has never been tested by the Supreme Court.
> **Key takeaway**: The law that was supposed to constrain presidential war power has been systematically ignored. The constitutional tension between the executive and Congress on war powers remains unresolved. The Founders did not anticipate a permanent standing army, a global military presence, or a world where the US is at war for decades.

> **Case: The Lessons of Vietnam** (Chapters 16-17, Conclusion)
> Beschloss ends with the lessons of Vietnam: the president who deceives Congress and the public about war destroys his own ability to lead. LBJ's tragedy is that he was a great domestic policy president (Civil Rights Act, Great Society) who destroyed his presidency by escalating a war he didn't believe in. The lesson: the president who cannot be honest about war cannot be effective in peace.
> **Key takeaway**: Presidential honesty is not just a moral virtue — it's a practical necessity. The president who lies about war loses the trust of Congress, the public, and the military. That trust, once lost, cannot be regained. LBJ and Nixon proved this. Every president since has lived in the shadow of their failures.
