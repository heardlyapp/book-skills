# Anti-Patterns — Executive War-Making Failures

> Source: Presidents of War, Chapters 6, 7, 16, 17

## The Five Presidential War Errors

### 1. The Provocation Trap
**Pattern**: Provoke an incident, then use it as justification for war. Polk's Mexican War, McKinley's Maine, Johnson's Gulf of Tonkin.
**Correction**: Congress and the public should demand independent investigation of any incident used as a war pretext before authorizing force.

### 2. The Credibility Obsession
**Pattern**: "If we withdraw, we lose credibility." The belief that honor, reputation, and global credibility require staying in a losing war.
**Correction**: The most credible position is a realistic assessment of interests, not a stubborn refusal to admit error.

### 3. The One-More-Troop Illusion
**Pattern**: Each escalation convinces the president that just a little more force will turn the tide. "One more division." "One more bombing campaign."
**Correction**: After the first escalation fails to produce the desired outcome, further escalation is unlikely to succeed — yet presidents repeatedly try.

### 4. The Advisory Groupthink Trap
**Pattern**: Presidents surround themselves with advisors who share their assumptions and suppress dissent.
**Case**: LBJ's "Tuesday Lunch Group" — the same small group of advisors met weekly, reinforced each other's views, and excluded dissenters. No one seriously questioned the premises of the Vietnam War after 1965.

### 5. The Constitutional End-Run
**Pattern**: Presidents bypass Congress through UN resolutions (Truman, Korea), congressional resolutions of authority (Johnson, Vietnam), or unilateral action (Nixon, Cambodia).
**Correction**: The Framers gave Congress the war power for a reason. Bypassing Congress is not efficiency — it's a violation of the constitutional order.

> **Case: The Gulf of Tonkin Deception** (Chapter 16)
> On August 2, 1964, the USS Maddox exchanged fire with North Vietnamese torpedo boats in the Gulf of Tonkin. On August 4, the administration reported a second attack — which was later proven to have been based on faulty intelligence and possibly did not occur at all. LBJ used the incident to push the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution through Congress, giving him authority to "take all necessary measures to repel any armed attack." This resolution became the legal basis for the Vietnam War. The resolution passed unanimously in the House and with only two dissenting votes in the Senate.
> **Key takeaway**: The most consequential war authorization in American history was based on an event that may never have happened. The president asked for authority to respond to an attack — and then used that authority for a war that lasted 11 years and cost 58,000 American lives.

> **Case: The Secret Bombing of Cambodia** (Chapter 17)
> Nixon expanded the Vietnam War into Cambodia and Laos — countries where the US was not at war. He ordered the bombing of Cambodian territory in secret, falsifying Air Force records to hide the operation from Congress and the public. When the secret was revealed, it destroyed what remained of Congressional trust in the presidency and was a major factor in the War Powers Resolution of 1973.
> **Key takeaway**: Presidential secrecy in war is not just about operational security — it's about avoiding accountability. Nixon's secret bombing of Cambodia is the most extreme example of a president lying to Congress about what the military was doing. The War Powers Resolution was Congress's attempt to reclaim its constitutional authority — but it has never been constitutionally tested.
