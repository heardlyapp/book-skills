# The Collapse Framework — Five Factors

Diamond identifies five factors that contribute to societal collapse. The fifth is the most critical because it involves choice.

**Factor 1: Environmental Damage.** Societies inadvertently destroy their own resource base. The classic case is Easter Island, where the forest was cut down to move the moai statues. Without trees, the islanders could not build canoes, soil eroded, and the population starved.

**Factor 2: Climate Change.** Natural climate shifts can push a society past its limits. The Maya collapse in the 9th century was partly caused by prolonged drought that made agriculture impossible in the Yucatan peninsula.

**Factor 3: Hostile Neighbors.** Invasion and war can destroy even a well-managed society. The Viking Greenland colony was ultimately destroyed by Inuit aggression after climate change had weakened them.

**Factor 4: Loss of Friendly Trade Partners.** Losing access to trade removes a safety valve. When the Greenland Vikings lost contact with Norway, they could no longer import iron, wood, and other essential goods.

**Factor 5: Societal Response.** This is where choice enters. The Greenland Vikings refused to adopt Inuit hunting techniques and died out. Icelanders adapted and survived. The difference was the fifth factor.

**Case: Easter Island.** The classic case of Factor 1 being sufficient on its own. The forest was destroyed by a society that did not understand the consequences of deforestation. The society collapsed into civil war and cannibalism.

**Case: The Greenland Vikings.** Factors 1, 2, 4, and 5 combined. The Norse damaged their fragile environment, the climate cooled, their trade link with Norway was broken, and they refused to adapt their lifestyle. They chose to starve as Europeans rather than survive as Inuit.

**Case: Rwanda.** Factor 5 was decisive. Population pressure on land (Factor 1) created conditions for genocide. But the genocide itself was a choice.
