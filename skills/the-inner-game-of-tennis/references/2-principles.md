## 7 Principles of the Inner Game

1. **Self 1 Must Quieten for Self 2 to Perform** — the inner critic must be given a job that doesn't interfere. Focus on a concrete detail (the seams of the ball) to quiet the mind.

2. **Non-Judgmental Awareness Is the Foundation of Learning** — watch what happens without labeling it good or bad. The data you collect without judgment is more useful than the critique you produce from judgment.

> **Case: The Error-Free Practice** (Chapter 3): Gallwey describes practicing without judgment. Instead of saying "that was a bad shot," simply note: "the ball landed six inches beyond the baseline." This neutral observation allows Self 2 to adjust naturally without triggering Self 1's anxiety.

3. **Trust Is More Powerful Than Effort** — the harder you try, the more you interfere. Letting go produces better results than forcing.

4. **Mistakes Are Data, Not Failures** — every error contains information. Non-judgmental observation extracts that information. Criticism blocks it.

5. **The Best Coaching Creates Discovery** — telling someone what to do is less effective than creating conditions for them to discover what works.

6. **Focus Quiets the Mind** — a concrete focal point (the ball, the breath, a sensation) gives Self 1 something non-judgmental to do, freeing Self 2.

7. **The Inner Game Applies to Everything** — tennis, music, business, relationships. Any activity where the inner critic interferes can benefit from the same principles.

> **Case: The Competitive Player** (Chapter 9): A tournament-level player was stuck at his plateau. Gallwey had him practice visualization — imagining perfect shots without judgment. Within two weeks, his actual performance improved. Self 2 learned from the visualization because Self 1 was not interfering.
> **Key takeaway**: The mind can learn as effectively through focused visualization as through physical practice — as long as Self 1 stays quiet.
