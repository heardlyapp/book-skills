> Source: The Inner Game of Tennis, Chapters 3-6 — practical techniques

## 1. Focus on the Seams

The simplest technique: focus all attention on a concrete detail. Gallwey asks tennis students to watch the seams of the ball as it comes toward them. This gives Self 1 something to do that doesn't interfere with Self 2.

## 2. The "Bounce-Hit" Method

Say "bounce" when the ball bounces, "hit" when the opponent hits it. This keeps the mind focused on external events rather than internal judgment.

## 3. The Awareness Practice

Perform a skill while paying attention only to physical sensations — the feel of the racket, the sound of contact, the movement of your body. No judgment. No analysis. Just awareness.

> **Case: The Sound Experiment** (Chapter 5): Gallwey had a student play while focusing only on the sound of the ball hitting the strings. The student's accuracy improved without any technical correction. The sound was a neutral focal point that kept Self 1 busy without judgment.

## 4. The Let-It-Happen Drill

Instead of trying to make a shot happen, let it happen. Set up a situation, relax, and trust your body to respond naturally. If you miss, simply observe what happened and try again.

> **Case: The Trust Practice** (Chapter 6): A player who had been struggling with his serve was told to stop thinking about technique entirely. Just toss the ball and swing. The first serve was a fault. The second landed perfectly. By not trying, he allowed Self 2 to do what it knew how to do.
