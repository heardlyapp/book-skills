# The Mind — Vrittis, Kleshas, and the Path to Stillness

> Source: The Yoga Sutras of Patanjali, Book 1, Sutras 5-11; Book 2, Sutras 1-9

## The Five Vrittis (Mental Modifications)

Patanjali classifies all mental activity into five categories. None is inherently bad — yoga is about gaining mastery over them, not eliminating them.

| Vritti | Meaning | Example |
|--------|---------|---------|
| Pramana | Right knowledge | Direct perception, inference, reliable testimony |
| Viparyaya | Wrong knowledge | Mistaking a rope for a snake |
| Vikalpa | Imagination/verbal delusion | Thinking about something that has no reality |
| Nidra | Sleep | Deep sleep as a mental state |
| Smriti | Memory | Recalling past experiences |

Each vritti can be either painful (klishta) or non-painful (aklishta) depending on whether it is rooted in kleshas.

## The Five Kleshas (Afflictions)

The root causes of suffering:

| Klesha | Meaning | How It Operates |
|--------|---------|-----------------|
| Avidya | Ignorance of the real | Mistaking the impermanent for permanent, the impure for pure, the painful for pleasurable, the non-Self for the Self |
| Asmita | Egoism | Identifying the power of the Seer with the instrument of seeing (the mind) |
| Raga | Attachment | Pleasure experienced leads to craving for more |
| Dvesha | Aversion | Pain experienced leads to avoidance and hatred |
| Abhinivesha | Clinging to life | Even the wise fear death; the instinct for self-preservation |

### Kriya Yoga (Book 2, Sutras 1-2)

Patanjali prescribes a practical three-part method for dealing with kleshas:
1. **Tapas (austerity)** — Willingly accepting discomfort to burn away impurities
2. **Svadhyaya (self-study)** — Studying scriptures and reflecting on one's own nature
3. **Ishvara Pranidhana (surrender)** — Letting go of the results of practice

> **Case: The Stranger Who Became a Father** (Commentary on Sutra 1.2)
> Satchidananda explains how mental modifications create our reality: a boy who has never seen his father sees a stranger at the door. He runs to his mother saying "there's a stranger at the door." She comes, sees her long-lost husband, and introduces him as the boy's father. The boy then says "Oh, my Daddy!" The same person was a stranger one moment and a father the next. Nothing changed externally — only the mental modification changed.
> **Key takeaway**: Our entire experience of the world is filtered through mental modifications. The "same" situation can be heaven or hell depending on how the mind interprets it. The world is neutral — the mind colors everything.

> **Case: The Swiss Cheese Analogy** (Commentary on Sutra 1.2)
> Satchidananda describes how the three parts of the mind (manas, buddhi, ahamkara) work together: a smell from the kitchen is registered by manas, identified as Swiss cheese by buddhi, and claimed by ahamkara ("I should have some"). This sequence — sensation, discrimination, identification — happens so quickly we don't notice it. But each step is a vritti, and each vritti pulls us further from the peaceful state that is our natural condition.
> **Key takeaway**: The mind's natural state is peace. Vrittis disturb that peace by creating desires. But the process is mechanical and can be observed. By watching the sequence of manas → buddhi → ahamkara without participating in it, we can remain in the peaceful state.
