# The Eight Limbs of Ashtanga Yoga

> Source: The Yoga Sutras of Patanjali, Book 2, Sutras 29-55; Book 3, Sutras 1-8

## The Eight Limbs

Patanjali presents the eight limbs as an integrated path, not a sequential checklist. Each limb supports the others, and progress in one facilitates progress in all.

### The External Limbs (Bahiranga)

| Limb | Meaning | Practice |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Yama** | Restraints (5) | Ahimsa (non-violence), Satya (truthfulness), Asteya (non-stealing), Brahmacharya (celibacy/right use of energy), Aparigraha (non-possessiveness) |
| **Niyama** | Observances (5) | Shaucha (purity), Santosha (contentment), Tapas (austerity), Svadhyaya (self-study), Ishvara Pranidhana (surrender to the Divine) |
| **Asana** | Posture | Steady, comfortable physical position for meditation. Not about advanced poses but about being able to sit still |
| **Pranayama** | Breath control | Regulation of the breath to calm the mind and prepare for deeper practices |
| **Pratyahara** | Sense withdrawal | The senses cease to be drawn to external objects. The mind turns inward |

### The Internal Limbs (Antaranga)

| Limb | Meaning | Practice |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Dharana** | Concentration | Fixing the mind on a single point or object |
| **Dhyana** | Meditation | Uninterrupted flow of awareness toward the object |
| **Samadhi** | Absorption | The mind becomes one with the object of meditation. The meditator, meditation, and meditated merge |

> **Case: The Importance of Ethics Before Meditation** (Commentary on Book 2, Sutra 29)
> Satchidananda emphasizes that yama and niyama come first for a reason. A person who lies, steals, and harms others cannot still their mind in meditation. The guilt, fear, and mental agitation created by unethical conduct make concentration impossible. This is not a moral judgment — it's a practical observation. The mind that is at peace with its conduct is the mind that can sit still.
> **Key takeaway**: Meditation is not a technique you can practice regardless of how you live. Ethical living (yama and niyama) is not optional — it's the foundation. If you cannot meditate, examine your conduct first, not your technique.

> **Case: Asana as Preparation, Not Goal** (Commentary on Book 2, Sutra 46)
> Satchidananda clarifies that asana simply means "a steady, comfortable posture" for meditation. The elaborate physical yoga popular in the West is a later development. Patanjali's asana is the third limb — a means to an end, not the end itself. The goal of asana is to be able to sit still for extended periods without discomfort or fidgeting. If you can do that, you have mastered asana — regardless of whether you can touch your toes.
> **Key takeaway**: Patanjali's asana has nothing to do with advanced poses. It's about being able to sit still. This reframes the entire physical yoga practice: poses are preparation for sitting, not an end in themselves.
