# The Four Books of the Yoga Sutras

> Source: The Yoga Sutras of Patanjali, Books 1-4; commentary by Sri Swami Satchidananda

## Core Framework

The Yoga Sutras are organized into four books (padas), each building on the previous. Together they present a complete path from the human condition to full liberation.

### The Four Padas

| Book | Name | Focus | Key Content |
|------|------|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Samadhi Pada | The goal of yoga | Definition of yoga, vrittis, kleshas, the two disciplines (practice and detachment), stages of samadhi |
| 2 | Sadhana Pada | The practice | The eight limbs, kriya yoga, detailed treatment of kleshas, how to practice when obstacles arise |
| 3 | Vibhuti Pada | The powers | Siddhis (supernormal powers) that arise from practice, the internal limbs, samyama |
| 4 | Kaivalya Pada | Liberation | The nature of the liberated mind, the distinction between Purusha and Prakriti, the final state |

### The Definition of Yoga (Sutra 1.2)

Yogash citta vritti nirodhah — "Yoga is the restraint of the modifications of the mind-stuff."

Satchidananda explains: this single sutra contains both the definition and the practice. If you can control the rising of the mind into ripples, you will experience Yoga. Everything else in the text explains how to achieve this.

### The Seer and the Seen (Sutras 1.3-1.4)

When the mind is still, the Seer (true Self) abides in its own nature. At other times, the Self appears to assume the forms of the mental modifications. The entire path is about realizing this distinction.

> **Case: The Prison Inmate's Transformation** (Commentary on Sutra 1.2)
> Satchidananda tells the story of speaking to prison inmates. He pointed out that the guards and the inmates were in the same building. To the inmates it was a prison; to the guards it was a workplace. The walls were the same. The difference was in the mind. Several inmates took this teaching to heart, continued a yogic life after their release, and were even thankful for their prison experience. The external circumstance did not change — their relationship to it did.
> **Key takeaway**: The same external condition can be bondage or freedom depending on your mental attitude. Yoga does not change the world — it changes your relationship to the world. If the mind changes, everything changes.

> **Case: The Face in the Mirror Analogy** (Commentary on Sutra 1.3)
> Satchidananda uses a vivid analogy: your face is the Seer. A mirror is the mind. If the mirror is corrugated, curved, or dirty, you see a distorted reflection of your face. But you don't think your face has changed — you know the mirror is faulty. Similarly, when the mind is agitated or impure, the reflection of the Self appears distorted. You don't need to fix yourself — you need to clean and still the mirror of the mind.
> **Key takeaway**: The Self is never diminished or distorted. It only appears that way due to the condition of the mind. The goal of yoga is to clean and still the mental mirror so the Self is seen clearly.
