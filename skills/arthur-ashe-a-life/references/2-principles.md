# Principles from Arthur Ashe

## I. Excellence Defies Racism

Ashe's response to a racist society was not to fight it directly but to excel despite it. His excellence was his first and most powerful statement.

**Case: The UCLA Years.** Ashe arrived at UCLA with a weak academic background from segregated schools. He graduated on time. He won the NCAA tennis title. He proved that a Black man from the segregated South could compete and succeed at the highest level.

**Case: The US Open Victory.** When Ashe won the US Open in 1968, it was the year Martin Luther King Jr. and Robert Kennedy were assassinated. His victory was a moment of hope.

## II. Use Your Platform

Ashe believed that athletes had a responsibility to speak out.

**Case: The Davis Cup.** Ashe risked his place on the US Davis Cup team by insisting that the team would not play against apartheid South Africa. He was willing to sacrifice his career for his principles.

**Case: The March on Washington.** Ashe did not attend the 1963 March on Washington — he was competing in a tennis tournament. He regretted this for the rest of his life. He resolved never to miss another opportunity to stand up for justice.

## III. Dignity Is Not Weakness

Ashe was often criticized for being "too moderate." He did not shout. He did not threaten. He persisted.

**Case: The Country Club.** Ashe was denied entry to certain country clubs even after he won Wimbledon. He did not picket. He did not sue. He simply noted the injustice and moved on.

**Case: The AIDS Disclosure.** When Ashe disclosed his HIV status, he did so with dignity. He did not seek sympathy. He sought action.

## IV. Education Opens Doors

Ashe believed that education was the most powerful tool for liberation.

**Case: The NJTL.** The National Junior Tennis League combined tennis with education. Ashe believed that access to tennis was not enough — young people needed access to education.

**Case: The Books.** Ashe wrote three volumes of A Hard Road to Glory, a history of the African-American athlete. He believed that history was essential to understanding the present.
