# Reference: Big Tech

## Overview
The rise of Google, Amazon, Facebook, and Apple has created concentrations of private power comparable to the trusts of the Gilded Age — but much harder to challenge under modern antitrust law.

## Case: Google's Search Monopoly
Google controls approximately 90% of the global search market. It pays Apple $15 billion+ per year to remain the default search engine on Safari. It uses its dominance in search to favor its own products (shopping, travel, maps) over competitors. The EU has fined Google billions for anticompetitive behavior. Wu argues that Google's monopoly over information is a clear antitrust problem — but under the consumer welfare standard, it is hard to challenge because Google's search service is free.

## Case: Amazon's Marketplace Dominance
Amazon controls approximately 40% of US e-commerce. It operates a marketplace where third-party sellers account for 60% of sales — while Amazon also sells its own products on the same platform. Amazon collects data from third-party sellers and can use it to compete with them. Wu argues that Amazon's monopoly power harms suppliers, competitors, and workers — but because Amazon keeps prices low for consumers, it is difficult to challenge under current antitrust law.

## Case: Facebook's Acquisition Strategy
Facebook (Meta) acquired Instagram in 2012 and WhatsApp in 2014. Wu argues that both acquisitions were essentially defensive: Facebook bought potential competitors to eliminate the threat they posed. Under traditional antitrust law, these acquisitions would have been challenged. Under the consumer welfare standard, Facebook argued that the acquisitions did not harm consumers (the services remained free) — so they were approved.
