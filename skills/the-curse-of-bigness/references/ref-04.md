# Reference: The New Antitrust Movement

## Overview
The new antitrust movement — variously called "Hipster Antitrust," the "New Brandeis Movement," or "Neo-Brandeisian" — argues for a return to the original goals of antitrust: protecting democracy and competition, not just consumer prices.

## Case: Lina Khan's Amazon Paper
Lina Khan, now FTC Chair, wrote a landmark law review article in 2017 titled "Amazon's Antitrust Paradox." She argued that Amazon's monopoly could not be challenged under the consumer welfare standard because Amazon kept prices low — even as it crushed competitors and squeezed suppliers. Khan proposed a return to a structural approach: evaluating a company's power rather than its prices. The article made her the intellectual leader of the new antitrust movement.

## Case: Elizabeth Warren's Platform
Elizabeth Warren proposed breaking up Big Tech in her 2020 presidential campaign. Her proposal: designate large tech companies as "platform utilities" and regulate them like railroads. A platform utility would be prohibited from owning both the marketplace and the participants in that marketplace (e.g., Amazon could not sell its own products on its marketplace). Warren's proposal represented the return of structural antitrust thinking to mainstream politics.

## Case: The Bipartisan Antitrust Bills
In 2021-2022, a bipartisan group in Congress proposed several antitrust bills targeting Big Tech. The most significant was the American Innovation and Choice Online Act, which would have prohibited platforms from preferencing their own products. The bills had support from both Democrats and Republicans but faced intense lobbying opposition from the tech industry. None of the bills passed in the 117th Congress, but they demonstrated the growing political consensus for reform.
