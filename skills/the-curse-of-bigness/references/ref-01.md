# Reference: The History of Antitrust

## Overview
Antitrust law in America begins with the Sherman Act of 1890, a response to the enormous trusts that dominated the Gilded Age economy. The Act's purpose was not primarily economic — it was democratic.

## Case: Standard Oil
The Standard Oil case (1911) was the first great antitrust victory. John D. Rockefeller's oil company controlled 90% of US oil refining. The Supreme Court ordered Standard Oil broken into 34 independent companies. The breakup is still visible today: Exxon, Chevron, ConocoPhillips, and others. The case established that Section 2 of the Sherman Act prohibited monopolization, not just monopoly. The key was that Standard Oil had used abusive practices to acquire and maintain its monopoly.

## Case: Louis Brandeis's Vision
Louis Brandeis, later a Supreme Court Justice, was the intellectual godfather of the antitrust movement. He argued that bigness itself was a curse. A company could be too big regardless of how it treated consumers. "We can have democracy, or we can have great wealth concentrated in the hands of a few, but we cannot have both." Brandeis's vision — antitrust as a defense of democratic institutions — was the dominant framework for the first 80 years of antitrust.

## Case: The AT&T Breakup
The AT&T breakup (1984) was the most significant antitrust action between Standard Oil and the Microsoft case. AT&T, the Bell System, had a monopoly on telephone service in the United States. The government sued under the Sherman Act. AT&T agreed to be broken up: the long-distance business remained AT&T; the local phone companies became seven regional "Baby Bells." The breakup led to dramatic innovation in telecommunications — including the development of the cellular and internet industries.
