# Reference: Democracy and Monopoly

## Overview
Wu's central argument is that concentrated economic power is fundamentally incompatible with democratic governance. The title "The Curse of Bigness" refers to the political curse, not just the economic one.

## Case: The Gilded Age Parallels
The Gilded Age (1870-1900) was a period of extreme corporate concentration and political corruption. The trusts controlled senators directly (they appointed them, effectively). The Sherman Act was a political response to a political crisis. Wu argues that the New Gilded Age (2000-present) has produced similar concentration and similar corruption — but the response has been much weaker.

## Case: Media Concentration
Monopoly in media — local newspapers owned by chains, television stations owned by conglomerates, social media controlled by one company — threatens democratic discourse. When one company controls the news, the search engine, and the social network, it has extraordinary power over what people see, read, and believe. Wu argues that media concentration is an antitrust problem, not just a journalism problem.

## Case: Lobbying Power
The largest tech companies spend hundreds of millions of dollars on lobbying. Amazon, Google, Facebook, and Apple are among the largest corporate lobbyists in Washington. This lobbying power has been used to block antitrust enforcement and regulation. Wu argues that this is not just a problem of corporate influence — it is a structural feature of concentrated economic power.
