# Reference: The Chicago School Revolution

## Overview
The 1970s and 1980s saw a revolution in antitrust thinking. The Chicago School, led by Robert Bork, argued that antitrust should focus only on consumer welfare — measured by prices.

## Case: Robert Bork's Paradox
Robert Bork was a Yale law professor who wrote "The Antitrust Paradox" (1978). He argued that antitrust had become confused, pursuing multiple conflicting goals (protecting small businesses, preserving democracy, promoting competition). His solution: narrow antitrust to a single goal — consumer welfare, measured by prices. Bork's framework was adopted by the Reagan administration, which dramatically reduced antitrust enforcement. The paradox: Bork set out to save antitrust and instead killed it.

## Case: The Microsoft Case as a Turning Point
The Microsoft case (1998-2001) was the first major test of the consumer welfare standard. The government argued that Microsoft's bundling of Internet Explorer with Windows was anticompetitive. Microsoft argued that consumers benefited from a free, integrated browser. The court found Microsoft violated the Sherman Act but imposed only behavioral remedies (sharing APIs, allowing OEMs to promote rival software). The remedies were widely considered ineffective.

## Case: The Decline of Enforcement
After Microsoft, antitrust enforcement nearly disappeared. The FTC and DOJ brought fewer and weaker cases. The consumer welfare standard made it almost impossible to challenge monopolies that kept prices low — which described almost every major technology company. Concentration increased across the economy. The period from 2000-2016 was the "antitrust ice age."
