# Techniques — The Pivot, the Rebrand, and the Licensing Strategy

## Practical Methods

> **Case: The Famicom Design** (Chapter 6): The Famicom was designed to look like a toy, not a computer. It was red and white. It had a friendly shape. It came with two controllers permanently attached. Nintendo did not market it as a "video game system." They marketed it as a "home entertainment system." The rebrand was deliberate and brilliant.
> **Key takeaway**: If your category has a stigma, do not fight it. Create a new category. Sometimes the best strategy is to refuse to play the existing game.

> **Case: The Licensing Model** (Chapter 7): Nintendo required all third-party developers to license their games through Nintendo. Nintendo manufactured the cartridges. Nintendo controlled the supply. If a game was bad, Nintendo could refuse to release it. This gave Nintendo absolute quality control.
> **Key takeaway**: If you can control the platform, you control the quality. It is a responsibility, not just a power.

> **Case: The Mario Marketing** (Throughout): Nintendo marketed Mario as a character, not a product. Mario had a personality. He had a world. He had friends and enemies. Children did not want to play a Nintendo game. They wanted to play with Mario.
> **Key takeaway**: People love characters, not products. Create a character. Give them a world. The product is just the vehicle for the character.
> **Key takeaway**: A character is a relationship, not a logo. Mario is a friend. Your brand should be too. People do not buy products. They buy relationships.
