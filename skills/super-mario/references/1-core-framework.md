# Core Framework — The Near-Death Experience and the Creative Pivot

## The Model

Nintendo was a failing playing card company. It pivoted to toys, then to electronics, then to video games. Each pivot was driven by necessity. The pattern: crisis → creative response → success → new crisis → repeat.

> **Case: The Donkey Kong Gamble** (Chapter 3): In 1981, Nintendo of America was on the verge of collapse. They had one last chance: an arcade game called Donkey Kong. The game was designed by a young artist named Shigeru Miyamoto who had never designed a game before. The main character was a carpenter named Jumpman — later renamed Mario. The game was a massive hit. It saved Nintendo.
> **Key takeaway**: Your most important product may come from your least experienced person. Give the newcomer a chance. They might save the company.

> **Case: The 1983 Crash** (Chapter 6): The video game industry collapsed in 1983. Hundreds of companies went bankrupt. Atari buried millions of unsold cartridges in the desert. Everyone said video games were a fad. Nintendo saw an opportunity. They designed the Famicom (NES) to be a toy, not a computer. They avoided the stigma of the crash by positioning it as something new.
> **Key takeaway**: When an industry collapses, the survivors are those who redefine what the industry is. Nintendo did not try to revive video games. They created a new category.

> **Case: The Seal of Quality** (Chapter 7): After the crash, consumers did not trust video games. Nintendo created the Seal of Quality: every NES game had to be licensed and approved by Nintendo. It was a guarantee of quality. It rebuilt trust. It also gave Nintendo total control over the market.
> **Key takeaway**: Trust is the most valuable currency. If the industry has lost trust, do not compete on features. Compete on trust.
