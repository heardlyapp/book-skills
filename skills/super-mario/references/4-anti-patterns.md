# Anti-Patterns — Feature Bloat, Spec Wars, and Forgetting the User

## The Central Fallacy

The most dangerous assumption of the video game industry: that better graphics and faster processors make better games. Nintendo proved that superior gameplay beats superior technology every time. The Wii had weaker hardware than the PlayStation 3 and Xbox 360. It outsold both.

> **Case: The Spec Wars** (Late chapters): Sony and Microsoft competed for the most powerful console. They added more RAM, faster processors, better graphics cards. Nintendo focused on gameplay, not graphics. The Wii was less powerful but more fun. It won the generation.
> **Key takeaway**: Do not compete on specs. Compete on experience. The customer does not buy specs. They buy what the product does for them.

> **Case: The Atari Crash** (Chapter 6): The 1983 crash was caused by too many bad games. The market was flooded with garbage. Consumers stopped trusting the category. The crash was not inevitable. It was caused by an industry that prioritized quantity over quality.
> **Key takeaway**: Too many bad products can destroy an entire category. Quality control is not just good business. It is survival.

> **Case: The Rushed Sequel** (Throughout): When Nintendo rushed a sequel or licensed a game to a bad developer, it showed. The game sold less. The brand was damaged. Nintendo learned: every game represents the brand. A bad Mario game hurts Mario.
> **Key takeaway**: Every product you release is an advertisement for your brand. A bad product does not just fail. It damages everything else you have built.
