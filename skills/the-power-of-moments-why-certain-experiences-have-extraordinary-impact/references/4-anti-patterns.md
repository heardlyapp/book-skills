# Anti-Patterns: Moment Mistakes

> Source: The Power of Moments, Chapters 7-10

## Leaving Moments to Chance

The biggest mistake: assuming defining moments happen naturally and can't be designed. Most organizations leave the most memorable experiences to luck.

> **Case: The Average Customer Experience** (Chapter 7): Most companies focus on eliminating negative experiences (complaints, errors) but never intentionally create positive peaks. The result: no negative experiences, but also no memorable ones.

> **Key takeaway**: Eliminating the negative is necessary but not sufficient. You must intentionally create positive peaks.

## Adding Rather Than Designing

Throwing extras (discounts, freebies, stickers) at an experience doesn't create a defining moment.

> **Case: The Hotel Freebie** (Chapter 8): A hotel that adds a free drink at check-in hasn't designed a moment — they've added a transaction. A hotel that creates a surprise popsicle concierge has designed a peak.

> **Key takeaway**: Design, don't just add. A structured peak is worth more than any collection of extras.

## Ignoring the End

The end of an experience is disproportionately important (peak-end rule), yet it's often the most neglected part.

> **Case: The Fading Goodbye** (Chapter 9): Parties and events that end awkwardly (people drifting away, looking at phones) leave a negative memory. A deliberate closing — a toast, a final song, a thank-you — creates a positive end.

> **Key takeaway**: Design the end as carefully as the beginning. The ending shapes the entire memory.
