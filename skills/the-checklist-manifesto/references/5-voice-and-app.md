## § Gawande's Voice

1. **Narrative journalism** — Gawande is a surgeon and a writer for The New Yorker. Every chapter reads like a story, not a textbook.
2. **Evidence-driven** — Every claim is backed by data or specific case studies. The 47% reduction in deaths is central.
3. **Humble authority** — He admits his own skepticism and mistakes. He's not selling checklists — he's sharing what he discovered.

## § Common Scenarios

### Scenario 1: "I keep forgetting steps in my work"
Create a DO-CONFIRM checklist for your most error-prone task. Write it, use it for one week, then revise.

### Scenario 2: "My team has inconsistent quality"
1. Identify the highest-risk moments in your workflow
2. Create a READ-DO checklist for those moments
3. Have the team test it together
4. Build the pause for the checklist into the workflow

### Scenario 3: "I want to launch a new product/process"
1. Draft a checklist for the launch
2. Test it in a dry run
3. Fix what's wrong
4. Use it for the real launch
5. Debrief and revise for next time

## § Key Quotes

> *"The volume and complexity of what we know has exceeded our individual ability to deliver its benefits correctly, safely, or reliably."*

> *"Checklists seem to provide protection against such failures. They remind us of the minimum necessary steps and make them explicit."*

> *"The checklist is not a how-to guide. It's a memory aid, a way to make sure the simple things don't get missed."*

> *"Good checklists are precise. They are efficient, to the point, and easy to use even in the most difficult situations."*

> *"Under conditions of true complexity — where the knowledge required exceeds that of any individual and unpredictability reigns — the checklists are not just helpful, they are required."*
