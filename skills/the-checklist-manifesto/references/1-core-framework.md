> Source: *The Checklist Manifesto*, Chapters 1-6

## The Problem of Extreme Complexity

**The core insight:** The volume of knowledge in modern professions (medicine, aviation, construction, finance) has grown beyond any individual's capacity to remember and apply correctly.

> *"The volume and complexity of what we know has exceeded our individual ability to deliver its benefits correctly, safely, or reliably."*

**3 types of problems (Chapter 1):**
1. **Simple** — Known steps, predictable outcome. Like baking a cake. Recipes work.
2. **Complicated** — Requires expertise and coordination. Like sending a rocket to the moon. Multiple people, multiple systems. Checklists are essential.
3. **Complex** — Unpredictable, every instance is different. Like raising a child. No checklist can fully manage this, but checklists help with the complicated sub-parts.

---

## The Two Types of Checklists (Chapters 2-3)

### DO-CONFIRM
Perform the steps from memory or routine, then pause and check the list to confirm you've done everything. Used for routine procedures where the sequence is familiar.

### READ-DO
Read each step and perform it before moving to the next. Used for unfamiliar or crisis situations where you can't rely on memory.

## Good Checklist Design (Chapter 6: The Checklist Factory)

**5 key design rules:**
1. **Keep it to 5-9 items** — The limit of working memory
2. **Use simple, precise language** — Conversational, not technical jargon
3. **Fit on one page** — No flipping pages in a crisis
4. **Test in the real world** — A checklist that works in a conference room may fail in the field
5. **Revise based on feedback** — The checklist must evolve based on actual use

> **Case: The surgical checklist** — The original WHO checklist had 19 items. After field testing, it was cut to 7 critical items. The 7-item version reduced complications by 36%.
