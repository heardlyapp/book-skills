# Reference: Poverty and Resilience

## Overview
Ruth McBride raised twelve children alone in the Red Hook housing projects of Brooklyn. She was on welfare, worked multiple jobs, and never had enough money — but her children never doubted that they would succeed.

## Case: The Welfare Years
After Dennis died, Ruth went on welfare and worked as a domestic servant. She raised eight children alone until she married Hunter Jordan, a Black man who worked at the Brooklyn Navy Yard. Even then, money was always short. Ruth cooked in bulk, sewed clothes, and made every dollar stretch. She never complained about being poor because she had been poorer as a child. Poverty was a condition she had grown up with, not a shame she felt.

## Case: The Education Mandate
Ruth had one rule for her children: you will go to college. She did not ask if they wanted to go. She did not ask how they would pay. She told them they were going, and they went. Every McBride child graduated from college. Several earned advanced degrees. Ruth believed that education was the one thing no one could take from you — and she was right.

## Case: The Sibling Bond
The twelve McBride children formed a tight bond. They shared bedrooms, hand-me-downs, and the experience of being raised by a mother who was both their greatest champion and their toughest critic. They fought, they protected each other, and they all knew that their mother's love was the foundation of everything they had achieved. The siblings are a testament to what happens when a family faces poverty together and refuses to be defined by it.
