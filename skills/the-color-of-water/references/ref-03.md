# Reference: Race and Family

## Overview
The McBride family's experience of race was complex: a White mother, a Black father (and later step-father), and twelve children who were visibly Black but had a White mother. They lived at the intersection of every racial divide in America.

## Case: Walking While Black
James describes the everyday experience of being a Black teenager in New York — the police stops, the storekeepers who followed him, the teachers who expected less. His mother could not protect him from these experiences because she could not see them. She knew racism existed but did not experience it personally. This created a gap between mother and son that neither could fully bridge.

## Case: The Interracial Family in Public
The McBride family attracted attention wherever they went. A White woman with Black children was assumed to be their nanny or their social worker. Ruth handled these encounters with a combination of dignity and defiance. She did not explain herself. She did not apologize. She existed as an act of resistance against the idea that families should be color-coded.

## Case: The Siblings' Experiences
The twelve McBride children had different skin tones — some light enough to pass, some dark enough to be presumed non-white. They experienced racism differently. James describes the irony of being too dark to be accepted as his mother's son but not knowing what it meant to be Black without the context of a Black mother. The siblings' varied experiences of race taught James that race is not biology — it is what other people see and how they treat you because of it.
