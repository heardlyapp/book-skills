# Reference: Faith as a Foundation

## Overview
Ruth McBride converted from Judaism to Christianity before marrying Dennis McBride. But her conversion was not a rejection of God — it was a rejection of the religion that had been used to justify her abuse and her family's hypocrisy.

## Case: The Black Church
Ruth became a devoted member of the Black Baptist church. She attended services multiple times a week, taught Sunday school, and found in the church a community that accepted her without question. The Black church became the spiritual home she had never had in Judaism. James describes the church as the center of their lives — not just for worship but for social connection, emotional support, and community organizing.

## Case: The Jewish Ghost
Despite her conversion, Ruth never fully left Judaism. She told James that she believed in God, not in religion. She kept elements of Jewish practice (she would not eat pork, though she served it to her children). She spoke Yiddish phrases unconsciously. James describes the sense of a Jewish ghost haunting his mother's life — a past she had rejected but could not escape.

## Case: The Question of God
Throughout the book, James wrestles with his own faith. He was raised in the church, left it as a young man, and returned to it as an adult. He does not pretend to have easy answers. His mother's faith was simple and absolute. His own is more complicated. The book does not resolve this tension — it lives in it. James's relationship with God, like his relationship with his mother, is a work in progress.
