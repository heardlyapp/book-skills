# 3 — Letting Go and Witnessing: Practices from Absolute Tao

> Source: "Absolute Tao: Subtle is the way to love, happiness and truth" by Osho

## Technique 1: Witnessing (Sakshi) — The Core Practice

Witnessing is the heart of Osho's approach to meditation. Not sitting with eyes closed repeating mantras, but cultivating pure awareness in every moment. Watching thoughts as they arise, without identifying with them, without pushing them away, without engaging.

> **Case: Osho on Witnessing** (Chapter 8): "Don't do meditation, be meditation." Osho distinguishes between "doing" meditation (which is still the ego trying to achieve something) and "being" meditation (which is a natural state of awareness). The first keeps you in the cycle of effort. The second is effortless — it's your nature, just covered by layers of thinking.
> **Key takeaway**: Stop "practicing" meditation as a technique to achieve something. Instead, bring awareness into whatever you're doing. Washing dishes, walking, listening — do it with full presence. That's witnessing.

### How to Practice (as described by Osho)

1. **Start with everyday actions.** While walking, watch your feet touch the ground. While eating, taste each bite. While talking, listen to your own voice. Don't add anything — just notice.
2. **Watch thoughts like clouds.** Don't analyze them, don't judge them, don't engage them. They come, they go. You are not the thoughts — you are the one who sees the thoughts.
3. **Don't try to stop thinking.** Trying to stop thoughts is like trying to make the river stop flowing. Just step back to the bank. The river flows; you watch. Over time, the river slows down — not because you forced it, but because you stopped feeding it.

## Technique 2: Not-Doing (Wu Wei Practice)

Wu wei is not a technique you can "apply" — it's the natural result of letting go of control. But there are ways to practice the letting go.

> **Case: The Sage Claims No Credit** (Chapter 1): "He acts, but does not appropriate; accomplishes, but claims no credit." Try this as a practice: do your work fully, with total engagement — but then let go of the outcome. Don't check who's watching. Don't count the recognition. Do it because it's in front of you, not because it feeds your identity.
> **Key takeaway**: The practice of wu wei is: act completely, then release completely. No residue. No looking back. No "I did that." The action is complete when it's done.

### Practical Exercises

1. **The Residue Check**: At the end of each task, check: is there a lingering "I did this" feeling? Notice it. Let it go. The action was complete when it ended. Anything after is ego's commentary.
2. **The Waiting Experiment**: Next time you're in a hurry, stop. Don't push. Wait one breath. See if the universe needs your hurry to function. It doesn't.
3. **The Water Mirror**: Sit by water — a lake, a river, a glass. Watch how water responds. It reflects perfectly, then the next moment it's gone. No trace. Do your work like that.

## Technique 3: Non-Attachment to Opposites

When you find yourself grasping for one outcome (success, approval, pleasure), pause and see its opposite (failure, rejection, pain). They are not enemies — they are twins. You cannot have one without the other.

> **Case: Lao Tzu's First Sutra** (Chapter 1): "Being and non-being interdepend in growth; difficult and easy interdepend in completion; long and short interdepend in contrast; high and low interdepend in position; tones and voice interdepend in harmony; front and behind interdepend in company." Every pair is a unity. The sage doesn't try to get one without the other.
> **Key takeaway**: When you want only success, you're already creating the conditions for the fear of failure. The practice is: invite both. Let success come, let failure come. They are both aspects of the same thing. Your center is not in either — it's in the space between.

### Practice: The Opposite Meditation

When you feel strongly attached to something (a person, a result, an identity):
1. Name it: "I want X."
2. Name its opposite: "And if X doesn't happen, Y will come."
3. Feel both as equal possibilities. Stay in the middle.
4. Notice: nothing bad happens in this space. The fear was in the grasping, not in the outcome.

## Technique 4: Letting Meaning Go

Osho says: "There is no meaning." This is not nihilism — it's freedom. The search for meaning is the mind's attempt to control life by understanding it. Tao says: don't understand it. Live it.

> **Case: Chapter 6 — There Is No Meaning** (Chapter 6): Osho explores the idea that existence has no inherent meaning — and that's not a problem. The search for meaning is itself the disease. When you stop looking for meaning, you start living. A flower doesn't search for meaning. It blooms. That's enough.
> **Key takeaway**: The practice: spend one hour without asking "why." Just do. A tree doesn't ask why it grows. A bird doesn't ask why it sings. Be like that. Meaning emerges from living, not from thinking about living.

## Technique 5: Emptiness Practice

> **Case: The Utility of Not-Being** (Chapter 7): "Thirty spokes join at the hub. It is the hole at the center that makes the wagon useful. Clay is shaped into a pot. It is the empty space within that makes the pot useful. Doors and windows are cut into walls. It is the emptiness that makes the room useful." The same is true for your life.
> **Key takeaway**: Practice creating space. Empty your schedule. Sit in silence without looking at a screen. Leave gaps in conversation. The quality of your life is determined by the spaces you leave — not by what you fill them with.
