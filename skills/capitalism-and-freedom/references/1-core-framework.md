# The Central Argument

> Source: Capitalism and Freedom, Chapters 1, 2, 13

## Core Framework

### Economic Freedom and Political Freedom

| Type of Freedom | What It Means |
|----------------|--------------|
| Economic | Choose your work, spend your money, start a business |
| Political | Vote, speak, assemble, worship |
| Friedman's claim | You cannot have political freedom without economic freedom |

> **Case: The Central Thesis (Chapter 1)**
> Friedman's core argument: economic freedom is an end in itself — and a necessary condition for political freedom. In a market economy, power is dispersed. No single authority controls where you work, what you buy, or how you live. In a centrally planned economy, the government controls all of these — and anyone who controls your livelihood controls your vote. The market is the only system that separates economic power from political power.
> **Key takeaway:** The central thesis is not about efficiency — it is about power. Markets disperse power. Government concentrates it. Dispersed power protects freedom. This is why Friedman is not just an economist — he is a political philosopher.

> **Case: The Role of Government (Chapter 2)**
> Friedman identifies four legitimate functions of government: (1) protect against foreign enemies, (2) enforce contracts and property rights, (3) maintain domestic order, and (4) provide limited public goods that the market cannot provide. Everything else — regulation, welfare, education, licensing — should be questioned. Government action must pass a rigorous test: does it increase freedom more than it limits it?
> **Key takeaway:** Friedman's framework for evaluating government is simple and powerful: every government action reduces someone's freedom. The question is whether the reduction is justified by a greater gain in freedom or security. The burden of proof is always on the government.
