# The Role of Government and Markets

> Source: Capitalism and Freedom, Chapters 2, 5, 8

## Friedman's Framework

### What Government Should Do

| Legitimate | Illegitimate |
|-----------|-------------|
| National defense | Price controls |
| Police and courts | Occupational licensing |
| Property rights enforcement | Tariffs and trade barriers |
| Public goods (limited) | Most welfare programs |

> **Case: The Case Against Occupational Licensure (Chapter 9)**
> Friedman argues that occupational licensing — requiring a license to work as a barber, a doctor, or a plumber — is anti-competitive and anti-freedom. It restricts entry into professions, raises prices, and protects existing practitioners from competition. The stated purpose (protecting consumers) is often a cover for protecting incumbent professionals. Alternative: let consumers decide through reputation and market mechanisms.
> **Key takeaway:** Licensing is a classic example of Friedman's framework: a policy that claims to protect the public but actually restricts freedom and competition. The lesson: always ask who benefits from regulation. The answer is often the regulated, not the public.

> **Case: The Social Responsibility of Business (Chapter 8)**
> Friedman's most famous argument: "The social responsibility of business is to increase its profits." Executives who spend shareholder money on social causes are imposing taxes without representation. Business should follow the rules of the game (law and ethics) and focus on its core mission. Social problems should be solved by individuals, charities, or government — not by corporate executives spending other people's money.
> **Key takeaway:** Friedman's corporate social responsibility argument is the most controversial part of the book. It challenges the assumption that businesses should be agents of social change. His point: if you want to help society, do it with your own money, not your shareholders'.
