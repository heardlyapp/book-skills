# Anti-Patterns — What Friedman Opposed

> Source: Capitalism and Freedom, Chapters 2, 5, 8, 9, 10

## The Five Errors

### 1. The Nirvana Fallacy
**Error**: "Government can solve any market failure perfectly."
**Correction**: Government failure is often worse than market failure. Compare real government with real markets, not ideal governments with real markets.

### 2. The Good Intentions Trap
**Error**: "If the goal is good, the policy must be good."
**Correction**: Good intentions do not guarantee good outcomes. Incentives matter more than intentions.

### 3. The Anti-Competition Impulse
**Error**: "Competition is wasteful. Central planning is efficient."
**Correction**: Competition is the discovery process. Central planning lacks the information to make good decisions.

### 4. Treating Symptoms, Not Causes
**Error**: "Unemployment is too high; the government should create jobs."
**Correction**: Government-created jobs often make the underlying problem worse.

### 5. The Equality Trap
**Error**: "Equal outcomes are more important than freedom."
**Correction**: Pursuing equality at the expense of freedom destroys both.

> **Case: The Minimum Wage (Chapters 10-11)**
> Friedman argued that minimum wage laws hurt the people they are supposed to help. A minimum wage prices low-skill workers out of the labor market. Employers will not hire someone whose labor is worth less than the minimum wage. The alternative: the negative income tax helps low-income workers without creating unemployment.
> **Key takeaway:** The minimum wage debate is a perfect example of Friedman's approach: look at incentives, not intentions. The intention is to help low-wage workers. The effect is to price them out of jobs. Good intentions, bad outcomes.

> **Case: Rent Control (Chapter 1)**
> Friedman uses rent control as an example of government failure. Rent control is intended to make housing affordable. It actually reduces the supply of rental housing — landlords convert buildings, stop maintaining them, or exit the market. The people who need housing most cannot find it. The lesson: price controls always have unintended consequences.
> **Key takeaway:** Rent control is Friedman's favorite example of the law of unintended consequences. The cure is worse than the disease. Every price control teaches the same lesson: when you prevent prices from signaling scarcity, scarcity gets worse.
