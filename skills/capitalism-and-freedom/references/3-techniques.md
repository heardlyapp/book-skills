# Specific Policy Proposals

> Source: Capitalism and Freedom, Chapters 3, 6, 11, 12

## Friedman's Policy Toolkit

### Key Proposals

| Policy | Problem It Solves | How It Works |
|--------|------------------|--------------|
| School vouchers | Failing public schools | Parents choose schools; competition improves quality |
| Negative income tax | Inefficient welfare | Cash grant replaces bureaucracy |
| Floating exchange rates | Fixed rate crises | Market sets currency values |
| Money supply target | Inflation | Fed targets growth rate of money supply |

> **Case: School Vouchers (Chapter 6)**
> Friedman's most influential domestic policy proposal. He argued that government should fund education but not necessarily provide it. Give parents a voucher equal to the per-pupil cost, and let them choose any school — public, private, religious. Competition would improve all schools. The proposal has been partially implemented in various forms (charter schools, voucher programs in some states) and remains controversial.
> **Key takeaway:** School vouchers are Friedman's most practical legacy. The idea: separate funding from provision. Government pays for education; parents choose where. Competition raises quality. Monopoly (the public school system) stagnates.

> **Case: The Negative Income Tax (Chapters 11-12)**
> Friedman proposed replacing the entire welfare system with a single program: a negative income tax. If your income falls below a threshold, the government pays you the difference. Simple, transparent, and does not create the perverse incentives of the existing welfare system (which often penalizes work and marriage). The idea influenced tax reform (the Earned Income Tax Credit) but was never fully implemented.
> **Key takeaway:** The negative income tax is Friedman at his most creative: use the tax system to help the poor without creating a massive bureaucracy. The lesson: simplicity is a virtue in policy. Complex programs hide perverse incentives.
