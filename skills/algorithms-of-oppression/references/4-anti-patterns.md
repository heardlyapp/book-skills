# What Not to Do — Anti-Patterns

## Anti-Pattern 1: The Technological Solutionism Trap

The belief that bias can be fixed with better technology alone. Noble argues that bias is social, not just technical.

**Case: The Data Fix.** Some suggest: "Just train the algorithm on better data." Noble argues that better data alone will not solve the problem if the commercial incentives and social structures remain unchanged.

**Case: The Code Fix.** "Just fix the code" ignores the fact that the code is written by people who are part of a society shaped by racism.

## Anti-Pattern 2: The Neutrality Myth

The belief that algorithms are objective because they are mathematical.

**Case: The Objective Fallacy.** Math is not neutral. The choices about what to measure, what data to use, and what outcomes to optimize for are all value judgments.

**Case: The Fairness Paradox.** Trying to make algorithms "fair" requires defining what fairness means. Different definitions of fairness produce different algorithms. There is no neutral definition.

## Anti-Pattern 3: The "It's Just a Few Bad Results" Trap

Dismissing algorithmic bias as a few anomalous results.

**Case: The Scale Problem.** A few bad results multiplied by billions of searches per day equals massive harm.

**Case: The Systemic Issue.** The bad results are not random. They follow predictable patterns of race, gender, and class.
