# Principles of Algorithmic Justice

## I. Representation Matters

What appears in search results shapes how we see the world. When Black girls are consistently pornified in search, it affects how society sees them and how they see themselves.

**Case: The Barack Obama Search.** When Noble searched for "Barack Obama" in 2008, the first page of image results included a photoshopped image of the President-elect with a bone through his nose and a monkey. The algorithm was not racist — but it had learned from a racist internet.

**Case: The Latina Women Search.** Searching for "Latinas" returns sexually suggestive results. Searching for "professional Latinas" returns different results. The algorithm has learned to associate "Latina" with sexualized content — reducing a diverse group of people to a stereotype.

## II. Transparency Is Required

Tech companies are not transparent about how their algorithms work. This lack of transparency prevents accountability.

**Case: The Black Box.** Google's search algorithm is a trade secret. The company does not disclose the factors that determine ranking. This means there is no way to verify whether the algorithm is biased.

**Case: The Auditing Problem.** No independent auditor can certify that Google's algorithm does not discriminate. The company does not allow outside auditing. The industry polices itself.

## III. Accountability Is Needed

When algorithms cause harm, there must be recourse. Currently, there is none.

**Case: The Harm to Reputation.** A person whose name is associated with false or degrading search results has no effective remedy. They cannot sue Google, because Google is not responsible for the results its algorithm returns.

**Case: The Discrimination in Ads.** When Google's AdWords system showed ads for criminal background checks in response to "Black-sounding names," there was no legal recourse. The ads were not discriminatory by design — but they were discriminatory in effect.
