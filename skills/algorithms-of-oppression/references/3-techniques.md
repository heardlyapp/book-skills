# Practical Action — Techniques

## Technique 1: The Comparative Search

Noble's basic research method: search for the same term with different racial or gender modifiers and compare the results.

**How to do it:** Search for "Black girls" and "white girls" on the same platform. Then try "professional women" and "professional Black women." Document the differences. The comparison reveals the bias.

**Case: The Original Discovery.** Noble's research began when she searched for "Black girls" and found pornography. She then searched for "white girls" — the results were not pornographic. The difference was the starting point of her book.

**Case: The Systematic Documentation.** Noble spent years documenting similar patterns. She searched for "Black women," "white women," "Asian women," "Latina women" — and found consistent patterns of racialized sexualization.

## Technique 2: The Alternative Engine

When one search engine produces biased results, try another.

**How to do it:** Use DuckDuckGo, Ecosia, or other search engines that do not track users. Compare results. Different algorithms produce different biases.

**Case: The DuckDuckGo Test.** Noble tested her searches on alternative search engines. Some produced better results. Some were worse. The point: the problem is systemic, not limited to one company.

## Technique 3: Report and Document

When you find biased results, document them and report them.

**How to do it:** Take screenshots. Share them. Report them to the platform. Public pressure has led to changes in some cases.

**Case: The Twitter Campaigns.** Activists have used social media to publicize algorithmic bias. When a photo of a Black couple was tagged as "gorillas" by Google Photos, the public outcry forced Google to address the issue.

## Technique 4: Critical Consuming

Users need to understand that search results are not objective facts. They are the product of commercial algorithms.

**How to do it:** Do not take search results at face value. Ask: who created this result? Who paid for it? What is its purpose? Be skeptical. Verify information from multiple sources.

**Case: The Filter Bubble.** Search engines personalize results based on your history. This creates a "filter bubble" in which you only see information that aligns with your existing views. Be aware of this.
