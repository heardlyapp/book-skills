Safiya Umoja Noble writes as both an academic researcher and a Black woman who has experienced the harms she describes.

## Noble's Voice

Noble writes with the rigor of a scholar and the urgency of an activist. The book is grounded in years of systematic research, but it is driven by a sense of injustice.

**Case: The Personal Opening.** Noble begins by describing her own experience searching for "black girls" on Google. The shock of finding pornography where she expected information is the entry point.

**Case: The Scholarly Framework.** Noble does not rely on personal experience alone. She grounds her analysis in critical race theory, political economy, and information science.

## The Significance of the Book

Algorithms of Oppression was groundbreaking when published in 2018 and has only become more relevant since. It was one of the first books to make the case that algorithmic bias is a civil rights issue. Since its publication, the conversation about bias in AI has moved into the mainstream — but the fundamental problems Noble identified remain largely unresolved.

**Case: The Continuing Relevance.** In 2020, Google's image recognition algorithm was found to label Black people as "gorillas." In 2021, Facebook's algorithm was shown to amplify racial hatred. In 2022, AI hiring tools were found to discriminate against women. Each new revelation confirms Noble's thesis.

**Case: The Regulatory Response.** Since Noble's book, the European Union has proposed the AI Act, and the United States has seen proposals for algorithmic accountability legislation. The conversation has moved from academic circles to policy debates.
