# Techniques: Practical Decision Tools

## Technique 1: The Decoy Check
Before choosing, identify the decoys. Which options exist only to make another look better? Remove them from consideration and re-evaluate your choice.

## Technique 2: The Pre-Commitment Strategy
Set deadlines with real consequences before temptation arises. Ariely's research shows that students who set their own deadlines perform better than those with no deadlines but worse than those with evenly spaced external deadlines.

> **Case: The Three Deadlines Experiment** (Chapter 6): Ariely gave three classes the same assignment. One class had three evenly spaced deadlines. One had a single final deadline. One could set their own deadlines. The class with evenly spaced deadlines performed best. Self-set deadlines helped but not as much as external deadlines.
> **Key takeaway:** Pre-commitment to specific deadlines is effective. Even better: have someone else hold you accountable to them.

## Technique 3: The Social-Market Boundary Test
Before mixing money with a relationship, ask: is this a social exchange or a market exchange? If it's social, keep money out. If it's market, don't pretend it's a favor.

The self-control problem is best addressed not in the moment of temptation but before it arises. Pre-commitment strategies include setting specific deadlines, creating consequences for failure, removing temptations from your environment, and having accountability partners. These strategies work because they engage your rational self before your irrational self takes over.

> **Case: The Deadlines Experiment** (Chapter 6): Ariely gave three classes the same assignment with different deadline structures. The class with evenly spaced deadlines performed best. Self-set deadlines helped but external deadlines worked even better.
> **Key takeaway:** Pre-commitment works best when deadlines are specific, evenly spaced, and externally enforced.

The most effective pre-commitment strategy is to remove the option to procrastinate entirely. Set deadlines that have real financial or social consequences. Make them public. Tell others about them. The fear of social judgment is a powerful motivator when willpower fails.
The keeping doors open experiment showed that people pay to keep options open even when they have no intention of using them. This is why we accumulate unused subscriptions, unfinished projects, and stagnant relationships. Closing doors is an act of focus and courage.
The procrastination problem is universal. Everyone struggles with it. The key difference between those who overcome it and those who do not is not willpower but strategy. Pre-commitment, external deadlines, and environmental design all work better than trying to be stronger in the moment of temptation.
