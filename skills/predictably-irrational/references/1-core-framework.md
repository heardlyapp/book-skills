# Core Framework: Relativity, Decoys, and Anchoring

Ariely's central insight is that humans rarely evaluate options in absolute terms. We compare. And the comparisons available to us shape our choices in predictable ways.

## The Decoy Effect

When given two options, people struggle to choose. Add a third option that is asymmetrically dominated (clearly worse than one of the options), and suddenly one option becomes much more attractive. The decoy isn't meant to be chosen. It's meant to steer you.

> **Case: The Economist Subscription** (Chapter 1): Ariely offered students three subscription options: web-only for $59, print-only for $125, and print-and-web for $125. Most chose print-and-web. When he removed the decoy (print-only), most chose web-only. The decoy was never meant to be chosen. It made the print-and-web option look like a steal.
> **Key takeaway:** Decoys are everywhere in pricing. Recognizing them is the first step to resisting them.

## Arbitrary Coherence

Our first exposure to a price acts as an anchor. That anchor is often arbitrary, but once set, it shapes all subsequent price judgments. Ariely calls this "coherent arbitrariness" — the anchor is arbitrary, but our behavior relative to it is coherent.

> **Case: The Social Security Number Experiment** (Chapter 2): Ariely asked students to write down the last two digits of their Social Security number, then bid on items. Students with high digits (80-99) bid up to 346 percent more than those with low digits (00-19). The arbitrary anchor of their Social Security number shaped their willingness to pay.
> **Key takeaway:** Our sense of value is anchored by the first number we encounter, even when that number is completely irrelevant. Be aware of anchors when making purchasing decisions.

The decoy effect operates everywhere: in subscription plans, restaurant menus, car models, and real estate. The key is to notice when a third option appears that you would never choose. That option exists to make another option look better. Remove it and re-evaluate.


> **Case: The Chocolate Experiment** (Chapter 3): Ariely offered two options: a Hershey Kiss for 1 cent and a Lindt truffle for 15 cents. People made rational trade-offs. Then he dropped both prices by 1 cent, making the Kiss free. Now most chose the Kiss. The presence of free completely changed the decision, even though the relative prices had not changed at all.
> **Key takeaway:** Free is not just a lower price. It is an entirely different psychological category. The difference between 1 cent and 0 cents is not 1 cent — it is infinite.


The decoy effect works because humans need comparison to evaluate value. Without comparison, we struggle to know what something is worth. Marketers exploit this by carefully controlling what we compare against. The best defense is to establish your own independent sense of value before looking at options. This requires knowing what you want and what you are willing to pay before you enter the decision environment.
