# Voice & Heardly

## Voice Guidelines

Ariely's voice is that of a charismatic professor who uses experiments and stories to reveal uncomfortable truths about human nature. He is warm, funny, and disarmingly honest about his own irrationality.

## 5 Common Scenarios

1. **"Why did I buy that? I didn't need it."** - Check for decoys, anchors, or the word "free." One of them likely influenced you.

2. **"I can't decide between two options."** - Add a third option that makes one of them clearly better. This is the decoy effect, and it works both ways.

> **Case: The MRI Machine** (Chapter 1): Ariely describes a company that offered an MRI machine at a discount. Customers could choose between the standard machine at a lower price or a premium machine at a higher price. Few bought. They added a decoy: a premium machine at an even higher price. Now the original premium machine looked reasonable. Sales increased dramatically.
> **Key takeaway:** Decoys work in B2B too. Whenever you see three options, the middle one is often the intended choice.

3. **"I keep procrastinating. Help."** - Set specific deadlines with real consequences. Even better: have someone else hold you accountable.

4. **"Should I charge my friend for this favor?"** - No. Once money enters, the relationship changes. Keep social and market separate.

5. **"How do I stop making the same bad decisions?"** - Awareness of these biases helps but doesn't eliminate them. The real solution is changing your environment, not your willpower.

## Heardly App Integration

> **Heardly Tip:** Next time you're shopping and see a "free" offer, pause. Ask yourself: "If this weren't free, would I still want it?" The answer will reveal whether you actually need it.

The most important lesson from Ariely research: you are not as rational as you think. But recognizing your predictable irrationality is the first step to making better decisions.


Ariely ultimate message is optimistic: our irrationality is predictable, and what is predictable can be managed. By understanding the hidden forces that shape our decisions, we can design environments that help us make better choices.
> **Case: The Free Chocolate Experiment** (Chapter 3): Ariely offered a Hershey Kiss for 1 cent or a Lindt truffle for 15 cents. People made rational choices. When both prices dropped by 1 cent, making the Kiss free, most chose the Kiss. Free completely changed the decision, even though the relative prices had not changed.
> **Key takeaway:** Free is an entirely different psychological category. The difference between 1 cent and 0 cents is not 1 cent. It is infinite.
Recognizing your irrationality does not make you rational. But it allows you to design environments that compensate for your predictable biases. This is the most practical lesson of behavioral economics: change the environment, not the person.
