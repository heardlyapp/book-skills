# Principles: Free and Social Norms

## Principle 1: Free Is Irresistible
When something is free, our decision-making shorts out. We don't calculate costs and benefits — we just grab it. This leads to irrational choices like choosing free shipping over a larger discount.

## Principle 2: Social Norms vs Market Norms
We operate in two worlds. In the social world, we do favors, help friends, and expect nothing immediate in return. In the market world, we pay for services and expect value. Mixing them creates confusion.

> **Case: The Daycare Late Pickup Fine** (Chapter 4): A daycare introduced a fine for parents who picked up their children late. Late pickups increased. The fine replaced the social norm (guilt about inconveniencing staff) with a market norm (paying for the extra time). When they removed the fine, late pickups stayed high. The social norm had been destroyed.
> **Key takeaway:** Once you introduce money into a social relationship, you cannot easily return. Market norms drive out social norms.

## Principle 3: The Endowment Effect
We value what we own more than what we don't. This leads us to overprice our possessions when selling and resist giving up what we have.

The endowment effect explains why we demand more to give up something than we would pay to acquire it. This is why home sellers overprice and why we hold onto clothes we never wear. Letting go requires recognizing that ownership has biased your judgment.


Understanding social norms and market norms helps navigate relationships at work and in personal life. When someone asks for a favor, assess whether it falls in the social or market domain. If it is social, do it freely. If it is market, establish the terms clearly. Mixing them leads to confusion and resentment.

> **Case: The Sex and Money Study** (Chapter 4): Ariely describes a study where men were asked for a date. When asked for a free date, many agreed. When asked how much they would charge for sex, they were offended. But when asked how much they would pay for sex, they gave specific amounts. The social norm and market norm are completely separate categories.
> **Key takeaway:** Social and market norms are deeply separate in human psychology. Attempting to cross them creates discomfort, offense, or confusion.

The endowment effect is also why we overvalue our own ideas, possessions, and creations. When you create something, you naturally perceive it as more valuable than others do. This is useful for motivation but dangerous for objective evaluation. Get outside perspectives.
Ariely research shows that we are honest up to a point where we have an opportunity to cheat without getting caught. The key insight: people cheat just enough to benefit but still maintain a positive self-image. Understanding this helps design systems that reduce cheating by making dishonesty harder to rationalize.
