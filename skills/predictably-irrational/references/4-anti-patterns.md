# Anti-Patterns: Decision Traps

## ❌ The Free Trap
Grabbing anything labeled "free" without considering whether you actually need it. Free causes us to make wildly irrational trade-offs.

## ❌ Mixing Social and Market
Treating a friendship as a business transaction or a business relationship as a friendship. Both create confusion and damage.

> **Case: The Thanksgiving Dinner** (Chapter 4): Ariely describes a Thanksgiving dinner where a host announced the cost per person and asked guests to pay. The meal was ruined. What should have been a social event became a market transaction. The two worlds cannot be mixed without destroying the social one.
> **Key takeaway:** Keep social and market separate. Pay for services. Do favors for friends. Do not confuse the two.

## ❌ The Endowment Trap
Overvaluing what you own simply because you own it. This makes it hard to sell, trade, or let go of possessions that no longer serve you.

## ❌ Keeping Too Many Doors Open
The inability to close options leads to scattered attention and reduced performance. Closing doors is a choice, not a loss.

## ❌ Expecting Rationality from Yourself
Believing you are immune to these biases. Everyone is susceptible. Awareness helps but does not eliminate the bias.

The expectation trap is powerful because it is self-fulfilling. When you expect something to be good, you experience it as good. When you expect failure, you create it. This applies to products, relationships, and personal performance.

> **Case: The Cola Taste Test** (Chapter 9): In blind taste tests, people could not reliably distinguish Coke from Pepsi. But when told which was which, most preferred Coke. The brand name and expectation shaped the experience.
> **Key takeaway:** Expectations are not neutral. They actively shape experience. Be aware of what you expect and why.
The expectation effect shows that beliefs about quality shape experience. When we believe wine is expensive, we enjoy it more. When we believe a movie will be good, we rate it higher. Expectations are self-fulfilling and can be managed deliberately.
The free trap is especially dangerous because free items often have hidden costs. They take up space, require maintenance, and distract from what actually matters. The true cost of free is rarely zero. Always ask: what am I giving up by choosing this free option?
The power of price effect shows that higher prices change our experience. Expensive wine tastes better. Expensive medicine works better. Price influences perception, which then influences outcome. This is the placebo effect at work in everyday life.
The keeping doors open trap is also known as the fear of missing out or FOMO. It is driven by loss aversion: the pain of losing an option feels greater than the pleasure of focusing. But keeping too many doors open ensures you walk through none of them effectively.
