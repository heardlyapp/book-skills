# Reference: The Cost of Heroism

## Overview
The right stuff culture exacted a heavy price. The men who had it often could not function without it, and the women who married it often paid the highest price of all.

## Case: The Wives
Wolfe gives significant attention to the astronauts' wives — Annie Glenn, Jo Schirra, Louise Shepard, Betty Grissom. They lived in a state of permanent anxiety, knowing that any launch could be their husband's last. They were expected to appear in Life magazine as serene, supportive spouses while waiting to become widows. The code of silence extended to them: they could not express fear or doubt publicly because it would undermine their husbands' image. The wives were casualties of the right stuff culture.

## Case: Gus Grissom's Death
Gus Grissom died in the Apollo 1 pad fire on January 27, 1967 — a test that turned into a death trap when an electrical spark ignited pure oxygen in the capsule. Grissom's death was particularly tragic because he had survived an earlier close call (Liberty Bell 7) and was seen by the other astronauts as one of the best. His death was a reminder that the right stuff could not protect you from bad engineering. The fire was caused by design flaws that NASA had ignored in its rush to meet the lunar deadline.

## Case: The Survivors
The Mercury program ended with the astronaut missions, but the men who flew them lived long afterward. John Glenn returned to space at age 77 on the Space Shuttle. Wally Schirra resigned from NASA and went into business. Deke Slayton finally flew in space on the Apollo-Soyuz mission after being grounded for a heart condition. Gordon Cooper retired and lived quietly. Scott Carpenter left NASA to study oceanography. The survivors proved that the right stuff could translate into a long life — but only if they found a way to leave the pyramid behind.
