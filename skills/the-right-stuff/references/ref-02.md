# Reference: The Mercury 7

## Overview
The seven Mercury astronauts were selected in 1959 from a pool of 110 military test pilots. They became instant national heroes, but the transition from anonymous test pilot to public figure was disorienting.

## Case: The Selection
The selection criteria were designed to find men who could survive the unknown. Eisenhower insisted on military test pilots. NASA's selection board put the candidates through unprecedented psychological and physical testing. The seven men who emerged — Shepard, Grissom, Glenn, Carpenter, Schirra, Cooper, Slayton — were the survivors of an intense competition. Wolfe describes how even the selection process was governed by the right stuff code: no one admitted he wanted to be chosen more than anyone else.

## Case: The Press and the Hero Machine
The astronauts were transformed into national heroes before they had flown a single mission. Life magazine paid $500,000 for exclusive access to their families (split equally among the seven). The press portrayed them as flawless family men. The reality — competitive, anxious, struggling with their new public role — was hidden. Wolfe describes the uneasy relationship between the astronauts and the PR machine that controlled their images. They were heroes, but they were also prisoners of their own fame.

## Case: The Struggle for Control
NASA engineers designed the Mercury capsule as fully automated. The astronauts were passengers. The pilots' response was outrage: they had spent their careers in the pilot's seat and were not about to sit passively while a machine flew itself. They fought for and eventually won manual control systems, including a window (the engineers wanted none), a periscope (replaced with a window), and manual retrorocket firing. This battle — pilots vs. engineers — defined the Mercury program.
