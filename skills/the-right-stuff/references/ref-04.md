# Reference: The Space Race

## Overview
The Mercury program was born from Cold War panic. The Soviet Union's early space successes — Sputnik (1957), Laika (1957), Gagarin (1961) — created a crisis of confidence in American technology and leadership.

## Case: Sputnik and the American Response
When Sputnik launched in October 1957, the American public was stunned. The Soviet Union had beaten the United States into space. The military implications were terrifying: if the Soviets could launch a satellite, they could launch a nuclear missile. The Mercury program was created in direct response to this perceived threat. The astronauts were not just explorers — they were Cold War warriors proving American technological superiority.

## Case: Gagarin's Flight
Yuri Gagarin's orbital flight on April 12, 1961, was another shock. The Soviets had put a man in space before the United States. Alan Shepard's suborbital flight on May 5, 1961 — just three weeks later — was NASA's response. Shepard did not orbit the Earth (he flew a 15-minute parabolic arc), but he proved that the United States could put a man in space. The timing was not coincidental: the Space Race was a sprint, not a marathon, and every milestone was measured against Soviet achievements.

## Case: John Glenn's Orbit
John Glenn's orbital flight on February 20, 1962 — Friendship 7 — was the Mercury program's crowning achievement. Glenn orbited the Earth three times and became the most celebrated American hero since Lindbergh. His flight restored national confidence. Wolfe describes the national catharsis: the moment when America finally caught up. The flight was not just a technical achievement; it was a political triumph that proved the American system could compete with the Soviet system.
