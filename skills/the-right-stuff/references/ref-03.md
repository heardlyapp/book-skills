# Reference: The Psychology of Courage

## Overview
Wolfe's central psychological insight is that the "right stuff" is not courage in the conventional sense but a competitive dynamic driven by shame.

## Case: The Silent Competition
No pilot ever said "I have the right stuff" or "You don't have the right stuff." The quality was demonstrated, never discussed. Every flight was an unspoken competition. Every pilot was constantly being judged by his peers. The judgment was merciless: a pilot who showed fear was seen as lacking the essential quality. This created a powerful incentive to take risks that were not necessary — because a pilot who refused a dangerous mission was admitting that the mission was too dangerous.

## Case: The Demonstration Every Flight
Wolfe writes that every flight was a "demonstration" of the right stuff. A pilot did not just fly a mission; he proved himself, every time, to an audience of his peers. The audience was always watching, always judging. One bad flight could undo years of reputation. This is why test pilots did not stop flying dangerous missions even as they aged, even as the odds caught up with them. The cost of stopping was higher than the risk of continuing.

## Case: The Astronaut's Burden
The Mercury astronauts faced a unique psychological burden: they were heroes before they had done anything. The press had already written their legend. If one of them failed — or worse, refused to fly — the entire narrative would collapse. Wolfe describes the pressure on Gus Grissom after the Liberty Bell 7 sinking. Grissom was blamed (unfairly, he believed) for losing the capsule. The hero machine had turned on him. The psychological toll was immense.
