# Reference: Space Colonization

## Overview
Before we can colonize other star systems, we must learn to live permanently in space. The Moon, near-Earth asteroids, and Mars are the first steps.

## Case: O'Neill Cylinders
Physicist Gerard O'Neill proposed rotating space colonies that would create artificial gravity through centrifugal force. A cylinder 30 km long and 6 km in diameter could house millions of people. The interior could replicate Earth-like conditions: blue sky, clouds, forests, lakes. Materials would come from the Moon or asteroids, where gravity is lower and launching resources into space is far cheaper. O'Neill's proposal was studied by NASA in the 1970s and found to be physically feasible.

## Case: The Space Elevator
A space elevator is a cable stretching from Earth's surface to geostationary orbit (35,786 km). Passengers and cargo climb the cable using electric elevators. The advantage: no rockets needed. The cost of reaching orbit would drop from thousands of dollars per kilogram to perhaps $100. The challenge is material science — the cable must be stronger than anything we can currently produce. Carbon nanotubes and graphene are candidates, but producing a 36,000 km cable of flawless nanotube material is beyond current manufacturing.

## Case: Asteroid Mining
Near-Earth asteroids contain vast quantities of platinum-group metals, water, and other resources. A single metal-rich asteroid could contain more platinum than has ever been mined on Earth. Water from asteroids could fuel spacecraft (hydrogen and oxygen). Planetary Resources and Deep Space Industries were companies founded to pursue asteroid mining. While neither succeeded commercially, Kaku argues that asteroid mining will eventually be profitable as space transportation costs decrease.
