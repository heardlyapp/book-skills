# Reference: The Search for Extraterrestrial Life

## Overview
Are we alone? This is the deepest question in science. Kaku presents the current state of the search and the philosophical implications of the answer.

## Case: The Drake Equation
Frank Drake's 1961 equation estimates the number of detectable civilizations in the Milky Way. The factors include: star formation rate, fraction of stars with planets, habitable planets per system, fraction that develop life, fraction that develop intelligent life, fraction that develop detectable technology, and the lifetime of technological civilizations. Most of these factors remain unknown. Pessimistic estimates yield one civilization (us). Optimistic estimates yield millions. The uncertainty is the Fermi Paradox.

## Case: The Fermi Paradox
Enrico Fermi famously asked: "Where is everybody?" If the galaxy is old (13 billion years) and filled with habitable planets (billions), and if technological civilizations are common, then the galaxy should be filled with evidence of their existence — Dyson swarms, von Neumann probes, interstellar signals. We see nothing. The solutions are grim: civilizations destroy themselves (nuclear war, AI, climate change), the Great Filter is ahead of us, or advanced civilizations are deliberately hiding.

## Case: The Wow! Signal
On August 15, 1977, the Big Ear radio telescope at Ohio State University detected a 72-second signal that was 30 times stronger than background noise. The signal was narrowband (1420 MHz, the hydrogen line — the frequency that scientists would most expect extraterrestrials to use). Astronomer Jerry Ehman wrote "Wow!" on the printout. The signal has never been repeated. It remains the most promising candidate for an extraterrestrial signal ever detected.
