# Reference: Interstellar Travel

## Overview
The nearest star system, Alpha Centauri, is 4.37 light-years away. Getting there within a human lifetime requires propulsion technologies far beyond chemical rockets.

## Case: Solar Sails
A solar sail uses the pressure of sunlight — yes, sunlight has momentum — to push a spacecraft. No fuel required. The acceleration is tiny (about 0.001g) but continuous. Over years, a solar sail could reach 10% of the speed of light, making Alpha Centauri reachable in about 40 years. The Breakthrough Starshot project plans to use lasers (rather than sunlight) to accelerate gram-scale sails to 20% of light speed.

## Case: Nuclear Fusion Rockets
Fusion rockets would use the same reaction that powers the sun — hydrogen fusion — to produce thrust. The theoretical specific impulse (a measure of efficiency) is 300 times that of chemical rockets. A fusion rocket could reach Jupiter in months and nearby stars in decades. The challenge: controlled fusion has not yet been achieved on Earth, let alone in a spacecraft engine.

## Case: The Alcubierre Warp Drive
Theoretical physicist Miguel Alcubierre showed that general relativity allows a "warp bubble" that contracts space in front of a spacecraft and expands it behind. The spacecraft would not move through space — space would move around it. This allows effective faster-than-light travel without violating relativity (nothing is moving faster than light locally). The catch: it requires exotic matter with negative energy density, which has never been produced.
