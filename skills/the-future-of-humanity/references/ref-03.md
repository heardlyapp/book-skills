# Reference: Immortality

## Overview
Kaku argues that radical life extension is not a fantasy but an engineering problem. The first person to live to 1,000 may already be alive.

## Case: Genetic Engineering
CRISPR gene editing can already correct genetic defects. The next step is editing the genes associated with aging. Scientists have identified the pathways: telomere lengthening, sirtuin activation, mTOR inhibition. Kaku interviews researchers who believe aging is not an inevitable process but a disease that can be treated. The first anti-aging therapies are already in clinical trials.

## Case: Nanotechnology
Nanobots — molecular machines the size of blood cells — could patrol the human body, repairing cellular damage, destroying cancer cells, and clearing arterial plaque. Eric Drexler's concept of "nanorobots" is being realized in early prototypes. Kaku describes a future where nanobots swim through our bloodstream, extending healthy lifespan by continuously repairing damage at the molecular level.

## Case: Digital Consciousness
The most speculative path to immortality is uploading consciousness to a computer. Kaku describes whole-brain emulation: scanning every neuron and synapse, then recreating the brain's structure in software. This would allow consciousness to exist independently of the biological body. The challenges are immense (a human brain has ~100 billion neurons with ~100 trillion connections), but Kaku considers it physically possible.

## Additional Perspective: The Ethical Questions
Immortality raises profound ethical questions. Who would have access to life extension? Would it increase inequality between rich and poor? What happens to population growth? Kaku argues that these questions must be addressed before the technology becomes available, not after. He notes that the first person to live past 500 has probably already been born, but whether that person will have a just society to live in depends on decisions we make today.
