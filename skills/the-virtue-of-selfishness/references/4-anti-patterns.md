> Source: The Virtue of Selfishness, Chapters 1-15 — common objections addressed

## 1. The "Selfishness Means Exploitation" Objection

The common view: selfishness means trampling others for your own gain. Rand's response: rational self-interest is not whim-worship. It requires reason, justice, and respect for the rights of others. The rational person does not benefit from exploitation.

## 2. The "Altruism Is Just Kindness" Objection

The common view: altruism just means being nice to others. Rand's response: altruism is a specific moral doctrine — the belief that self-sacrifice is the highest good. Kindness chosen freely is not altruism. It is the trader principle in action.

> **Case: The Generosity Distinction** (Chapter 3): Rand distinguishes between altruism (duty-based self-sacrifice) and voluntary generosity (a trader choosing to give value). One is moral; the other is not.

## 3. The "Objectivism Is Cold" Objection

The common view: Objectivist ethics leave no room for love, compassion, or emotion. Rand's response: love and friendship are values — they must be earned and chosen. An ethics that tells you to love everyone equally devalues love itself.

## 4. The "Capitalism Is Greed" Objection

The common view: capitalism is a system of greed. Rand's response: capitalism is the only system based on individual rights and voluntary exchange. Greed is the violation of rights — capitalism prevents it by protecting rights.

## 5. The "Rights Require Responsibility" Objection

The common view: individual rights must be balanced by responsibilities to society. Rand's response: rights and responsibilities are not opposites. The responsibility to respect the rights of others is inherent in the concept of rights.

> **Case: The Drug Addict's Rights** (Chapter 8): Objectivism holds that a drug addict has the right to destroy his own life — but not the right to force others to pay for his rehabilitation. Rights are freedoms of action, not claims on others.
