> Source: The Virtue of Selfishness, Chapters 1-15 — practical techniques

## 1. The Value Hierarchy

Rank your values in order of importance. Your life and happiness are the ultimate value. All other values (career, relationships, knowledge, art) are means to that end. When values conflict, the higher value must take precedence.

## 2. The Trader's Test

Before any transaction, ask: "Is this voluntary exchange for mutual benefit?" If not — if you are sacrificing yourself or exploiting another — it is not moral by Objectivist standards.

> **Case: The Employer-Employee Relationship** (Chapter 5): Rand argues that employment is a trader relationship — the employee offers time and skill; the employer offers money. Both benefit. There is no exploitation when the exchange is voluntary.
> **Key takeaway**: A voluntary exchange where both parties benefit is the only moral foundation for human interaction.

## 3. The Rational Self-Interest Audit

Ask: "Does this action serve my rational self-interest or am I acting from duty, guilt, or fear?" If the answer is duty, guilt, or fear, you are not acting on Objectivist ethics. Objectivism does not call you to abandon kindness — it calls you to choose kindness because you want to.

## 4. The Rights Check

Before supporting any government policy, ask: "Does this protect or violate individual rights?" A policy that violates rights — by taxing some to benefit others, or regulating voluntary exchange — is immoral by Objectivist standards regardless of its intended consequences.

> **Case: The Government's Role** (Chapter 4): Rand argues that government's only proper function is to protect individual rights through police, courts, and national defense. Any government action beyond that violates the rights it exists to protect.
> **Key takeaway: Individual rights are absolute. A government that violates rights forfeits its moral authority.

## 5. The Altruism Detector

When someone tells you that you "should" do something for others, ask: "Is this genuine voluntary generosity or a demand for self-sacrifice?" Generosity chosen freely is moral. Generosity demanded as a duty is altruism.

> **Case: The Charity Trap** (Chapter 3): Rand distinguishes between voluntary charity (a trader chooses to give) and altruist duty (society demands sacrifice). One is moral — the other is coercive.
