## The Objectivist Ethics

### The Foundation: Life as the Standard of Value

Rand's revolutionary argument: ethics is not a matter of faith, convention, or social utility — it is a matter of survival. For any living being, "good" is what serves its life, and "evil" is what threatens it. For a human being, the standard of value is human life — not any particular individual's life, but life as such, the life of a rational being.

> **Case: The Shipwrecked Sailor** (Chapter 1, "The Objectivist Ethics"): Rand poses a hypothetical: a sailor stranded on a desert island must choose his values. His life depends on his choices. He cannot appeal to society, tradition, or faith. He must use his reason to identify what he needs to survive — food, shelter, water. These are objective values because they serve his life. Rand argues this is the model for all ethical choices.
> **Key takeaway**: Values are not subjective or conventional — they are objective, derived from the requirements of human life.

### The Rejection of Altruism

Altruism, as Rand defines it, is not kindness or generosity. It is the moral doctrine that self-sacrifice is the highest good — that serving others is morally superior to serving yourself. Rand argues this doctrine is fundamentally anti-life. A morality that tells you your own life is not your highest value is a morality of death.

> **Case: The Altruist's Dilemma** (Chapter 2): Rand argues that altruism logically requires the sacrifice of the better to the worse. If the best people are required to sacrifice for the worst, the system destroys the good and rewards the bad. The moral ideal becomes ingratitude, mediocrity, and suffering. Rand calls this "the morality of death."
> **Key takeaway**: Altruism is not kindness — it is a code that demands you value others above yourself. Consistently applied, it destroys the best and rewards the worst.

### The Trader Principle

The only moral relationship between human beings is voluntary exchange for mutual benefit. The trader does not seek to sacrifice himself for others, nor to sacrifice others for himself. He offers value for value. This principle applies to all human interactions — economic, personal, intellectual.

> **Key takeaway**: The trader principle is the application of individual rights to human relationships. Voluntariness and mutual benefit are the only moral foundations for interaction.
