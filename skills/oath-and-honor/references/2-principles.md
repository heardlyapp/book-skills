# 2 — Constitutional Principles

> Source: "Oath and Honor" by Liz Cheney

## The Oath

The oath is not a formality. It's a promise. Cheney treats it as the most important promise she ever made.

## Duty Over Party

Cheney argues that the Constitution does not have a party affiliation. Defending it is not a partisan act — it's a patriotic one.

> **Case: The GOP Conference** (Chapter 6): Cheney describes the Republican conference meetings where members knew Trump was wrong but were afraid to say so publicly. "They would tell me privately that I was right. Publicly, they voted against impeachment." The gap between private belief and public action was the heart of the problem.
> **Key takeaway**: Private conviction without public action is not courage — it's complicity. If you know the truth and say nothing, you are part of the problem.

> **Case: The Phone Call** (Chapter 8): Cheney describes receiving death threats after her impeachment vote. Her family was threatened. Her security detail was increased. She didn't back down. "If they think threatening me will change my vote, they don't know me."
> **Key takeaway**: Courage is not the absence of fear. It's acting despite fear. Cheney was afraid. She voted anyway.
