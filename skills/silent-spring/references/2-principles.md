# Bioaccumulation and Ecological Principles

> Source: Silent Spring, Chapters 3, 4, 5, 6

## How DDT Moves Through the Ecosystem

### Biological Magnification

| Level | DDT Concentration | Factor |
|-------|------------------|--------|
| Water | 0.000003 ppm (3 parts per trillion) | Baseline |
| Plankton | 0.04 ppm | 10,000x |
| Small fish | 0.5 ppm | 166x from plankton |
| Large fish | 2.0 ppm | 4x from small fish |
| Fish-eating birds | 25.0 ppm | 12x from fish |

> **Case: The Robins of Michigan State University** (Chapters 4, 8)
> In 1954, Michigan State University sprayed elm trees with DDT to control Dutch elm disease. The following spring, the campus was silent. Robins that had returned from migration were dying. Autopsies showed DDT in their brains at lethal levels. How did DDT get into birds that ate worms, not elm leaves? The chain: DDT sprayed on elms → fell to ground in leaves → absorbed by earthworms → worms eaten by robins. The DDT did not break down — it persisted in the soil and concentrated in the worms.
> **Key takeaway:** The robins of Michigan State are the perfect example of unintended consequences. The spray was intended for elm beetles. It killed robins instead — because the chemical did not stay on the tree. It moved through the ecosystem. This is the fundamental lesson of ecology: everything is connected.

> **Case: Eggshell Thinning and the Bald Eagle** (Chapters 8, 11)
> Carson showed that DDT caused the eggshells of birds of prey — eagles, ospreys, peregrine falcons, brown pelicans — to become so thin that they broke under the weight of the incubating parent. The bald eagle, America's national symbol, was pushed to the brink of extinction. DDT did not kill adult eagles directly. It accumulated in fish, which eagles ate, and disrupted calcium metabolism, preventing successful reproduction. The population collapsed silently — no dead birds, just no new ones.
> **Key takeaway:** The most dangerous effects of environmental contamination are often invisible. DDT did not cause mass eagle deaths — it caused mass eagle non-births. The population decline was gradual and went unnoticed until almost too late. This is why ecological monitoring is essential: damage may be happening even when we cannot see it.
