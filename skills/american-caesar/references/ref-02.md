# Reference 02: The Korean War — Triumph, Hubris, and Fall

## Overview

The Korean War occupies the final dramatic act of MacArthur's career—a
spectacular strategic triumph (Inchon) followed by a catastrophic strategic
miscalculation (Chinese intervention) and the most consequential civil-military
confrontation in American history (his dismissal by Truman). Manchester treats
this as the tragedy of *American Caesar*, the hubris that undoes the hero.

> "Korea was the stage on which MacArthur's greatness and his ruin played out
> in equal measure. Never had he been more brilliant. Never had he been more
> blind."

## Use Case 1: The Inchon Landing — MacArthur's Greatest Gamble

In September 1950, the Korean War was going disastrously for UN forces. The
remnants of the Republic of Korea (ROK) army and the American Eighth Army were
pinned in the Pusan Perimeter, a shrinking pocket in the southeast corner of
the peninsula. North Korean forces appeared invincible.

**The plan:**
- MacArthur proposed an amphibious landing at Inchon, a port city on Korea's
  west coast, 150 miles behind North Korean lines
- The landing would cut North Korean supply lines, trap their army between
  the assault force and the Eighth Army breaking out of Pusan
- It was audacious bordering on reckless

**The opposition:**
- Every senior officer in the Navy objected. The Joint Chiefs were skeptical
- Inchon's harbor had 30-foot tides, mudflats that extended miles at low tide,
  and a sea wall the landing craft would have to scale
- Navy officers called it "the worst possible place for an amphibious
  operation"
- The window for the landing was measured in hours between tides

**MacArthur's response (from the briefing):**
> "The very arguments you have made—the tides, the mudflats, the sea wall—
> are precisely why the enemy does not expect us. Surprise is the one
> element that can turn the tide of this war."

**The outcome:**
- Inchon was a stunning success. The landing achieved total surprise
- UN forces recaptured Seoul in two weeks
- The North Korean army was routed, driven back across the 38th parallel
- MacArthur had achieved what Churchill called "the most brilliant stroke of
  the war"

**Leadership lesson from Inchon:** The best strategic decisions often look
foolish to conventional wisdom. MacArthur succeeded because he understood
that the enemy's assumptions were his greatest weapon. *Use the enemy's
expectations against them.*

**Manchester's assessment:** "Inchon belongs with Cannae, Agincourt, and
D-Day. It was pure MacArthur: the theatrical flair, the disregard for
orthodoxy, the absolute certainty. It was also the last time he was right."

## Use Case 2: The Chinese Intervention — Hubris Meets Reality

The Inchon triumph intoxicated MacArthur. When UN forces pursued the routed
North Koreans toward the Yalu River (the border with China), Chinese warnings
came through diplomatic channels. MacArthur dismissed them.

**The warnings:**
- Chinese Foreign Minister Zhou Enlai warned via Indian Ambassador K.M. Pannikar
  that China would intervene if UN forces crossed the 38th parallel
- MacArthur's own intelligence (G-2 under General Willoughby) reported no
  evidence of major Chinese deployments
- Manchuria-based aircraft were detected, but MacArthur's staff downplayed
  them

**MacArthur's response:**
- He told Truman at the Wake Island Conference (October 1950) that Chinese
  intervention was "most improbable"
- His reasoning: China had suffered a generation of war and would not risk
  American bombing of its industrial base
- He believed the Chinese "will not spill their blood for a half-baked
  communist regime in North Korea"

**The disaster:**
- In November 1950, Chinese "volunteers" (over 300,000 troops) crossed the Yalu
  and struck the overextended UN forces
- The Eighth Army was routed; the X Corps (MacArthur's right flank) was
  surrounded at the Chosin Reservoir
- The fighting withdrawal from Chosin under extreme cold (temperatures reached
  -35°F) was one of the epic engagements in Marine Corps history
- UN forces fell back to a defensive line below Seoul

**Manchester on MacArthur's blindness:**
> "The general had become a victim of his own legend. He had been right so
> often—about the Pacific strategy, about Japan's surrender, about Inchon—
> that he had lost the capacity to imagine he might be wrong. The Chinese
> proved him wrong, and the revelation destroyed him."

**Leadership lesson:** Success creates confidence; excessive confidence creates
blindness. MacArthur's greatest strategic failure was not reading the enemy—
it was reading his own reputation. *Every strategic victory creates the
conditions for the next strategic failure if you don't reset your assumptions.*

## Use Case 3: The Dismissal — Civilian Control of the Military

After the Chinese intervention, MacArthur became increasingly insubordinate.
He publicly called for bombing Chinese bases in Manchuria, blockading the
Chinese coast, and using Nationalist Chinese (Taiwanese) forces—all policies
his commander-in-chief, President Truman, had expressly rejected.

**The escalation:**
- MacArthur issued a series of press releases and interviews contradicting
  Truman's policy
- He wrote to Speaker of the House Joe Martin, saying "There is no substitute
  for victory"—implying Truman was not seeking victory
- Martin read the letter aloud on the House floor
- This was a direct challenge to presidential authority

**Truman's decision:**
- Truman consulted with the Joint Chiefs, who unanimously supported dismissal
- On April 11, 1951, Truman fired MacArthur
- The announcement was made via press conference—a clumsy communication that
  MacArthur learned about from his own wife before the official word arrived

**The aftermath:**
- MacArthur returned to a hero's welcome—parades in San Francisco, New York,
  Washington
- He addressed a joint session of Congress (the "Old Soldiers" speech)
- Polls showed 69% of Americans supported MacArthur over Truman
- A Senate investigation into the dismissal ultimately supported Truman's
  decision

**Manchester's balanced verdict:**
> "MacArthur may have been right about the strategic danger of China. He may
> have been right that limited war was a contradiction. But he was wrong about
> the one thing that mattered most: in a constitutional republic, the soldier
> serves the civilian. No matter how brilliant the general, he must not
> dictate policy. On this fundamental point, Truman was right, and history
> has confirmed him."

**Leadership lesson:** No matter how right you think you are, some lines
cannot be crossed. In a system, the system must hold.
