# Techniques — On Grief and Grieving

## How to Navigate Grief (Based on Kübler-Ross and Kessler)

### Technique 1: The Stage Observation
When you feel a strong emotion, name it: "This is denial. This is anger. This is bargaining." Naming the stage doesn't fix it — but it helps you recognize you're in a known process. Case: "When anger arises, say: 'I am angry. This is part of my grief.' The naming itself can provide distance."

### Technique 2: The Letter You Never Send
Write a letter to the person you lost. Say everything you never got to say. Don't mail it. Case: Kessler recommends this for unresolved feelings. "The letter is for you, not for them."

### Technique 3: The Anniversary Preparation
Anniversaries of death are powerful. Don't pretend they won't affect you. Plan for them. Case: "On the anniversary of a death, create a ritual — light a candle, visit the grave, look at photos." The technique: acknowledge the day. Don't ignore it.

### Technique 4: The Grief Timeline
Grief has no timeline. But it helps to know that certain time points are harder: 3 months (when support fades), 1 year (anniversaries), 2-3 years (when society expects you to be "over it"). Case: "At three months, the calls stop coming. That's often when grief hits hardest."

### Technique 5: The Permission Slip
Give yourself permission to feel whatever you feel. "I give myself permission to be angry. I give myself permission to cry. I give myself permission to not be okay." Case: "People want you to say you're fine. You don't have to."

### Technique 6: The Guilt Examination
Examine your regrets without judgment. "What could you have done differently?" Acknowledge it. Then: "I did the best I could with what I knew at the time." Case: "Guilt is a form of bargaining — 'If only I had done X, they would still be here.'"

### Technique 7: The Love Continuity Practice
"Love doesn't end when someone dies. It changes form." The technique: keep the person in your life in a new way. Talk about them. Remember them. Case: "You don't have to stop loving someone because they're gone. You learn to love them differently."
