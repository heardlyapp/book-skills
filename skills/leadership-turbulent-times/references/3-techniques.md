## Techniques: Crisis Management & Turnaround Playbooks

### Crisis Management (Theodore Roosevelt — Coal Strike of 1902)

**Situation**: 140,000 coal miners walked off the job, demanding 20% wage increases and shorter workdays. Mine owners refused to negotiate. As winter approached, coal prices soared and poor families faced freezing deaths. The nation was paralyzed.

**TR's Crisis Playbook:**

| Step | Action | Why It Worked |
|------|--------|---------------|
| 1 | **Recognize the crisis belongs to you** — "The steward of the people" | Refused the conventional wisdom that labor disputes were private matters |
| 2 | **Gather independent facts** — Commissioned a neutral expert report (Carroll D. Wright) | Removed ambiguity; made the situation visible to the public |
| 3 | **Invite both sides to the White House** — Unprecedented for a president | Used the prestige of the office to force engagement |
| 4 | **Build public pressure** — Released the Wright Report findings | Let the court of public opinion push the intransigent side |
| 5 | **Threaten intervention** — Prepared to send federal troops to operate the mines | Made the credible threat that inaction was worse than his involvement |
| 6 | **Accept a creative compromise** — Arbitration commission with a "distinguished" figure | Found a face-saving path for both sides |

> **Case: The Operators Refuse** (Chapter Ten): The mine owners — led by George Baer, who called himself "one of the five richest men in New York" — arrogantly refused TR's invitation. Baer said the situation was none of the President's business. TR responded by threatening to send in the army to run the mines. The operators caved.
> **Key takeaway**: When one side refuses to negotiate, your most powerful tool is the credible threat of unilateral action that hurts them more than negotiation would.

> **Case: The Arbitration Commission** (Chapter Ten): TR privately asked financier J.P. Morgan to help pressure the operators. Morgan arranged a compromise: a commission of seven members (including a "distinguished" labor representative — though TR framed it as an "eminent sociologist"). The strike ended after 163 days.
> **Key takeaway**: Creative face-saving mechanisms are essential crisis tools. Both sides walk away claiming victory.

### Turnaround Leadership (FDR — The Hundred Days)

**Situation**: March 1933 — 13 million unemployed, every US bank closed, industrial production halved, farmers foreclosing, suicides rising. "Panic was in the air."

**FDR's Turnaround Playbook:**

| Step | Action | Why It Worked |
|------|--------|---------------|
| 1 | **Name the fear, then dispel it** — "The only thing we have to fear is fear itself" | Acknowledged the emotional reality before offering a path forward |
| 2 | **Act immediately** — Declared a bank holiday on Day 2 | Created a controlled pause instead of letting the panic run |
| 3 | **Communicate directly** — First Fireside Chat (March 12, 1933) | Explained the banking system in plain language; 60 million people listened |
| 4 | **Experiment freely** — "Above all, try something" | Refused ideological purity; tried multiple programs simultaneously |
| 5 | **Build a brain trust** — Diverse advisors with competing views | Avoided groupthink by deliberately including dissenting voices |
| 6 | **Project unshakable confidence** — Personal demeanor of calm optimism | The leader's emotional state is contagious to the entire organization |

> **Case: The Bank Holiday** (Chapter Eleven): On March 6, 1933, FDR declared a national bank holiday — closing every bank in America. It was a breathtaking assertion of executive power. But FDR paired it with an Emergency Banking Act that provided federal backing for sound banks. When banks reopened March 13, deposits exceeded withdrawals. The panic was broken.
> **Key takeaway**: A bold, temporary action paired with a credible plan for recovery can reverse a collapse. The shutdown gave people the safety to stop, think, and regain trust.

> **Case: The Fireside Chat** (Chapter Eleven): "I want to tell you what has been done in the last few days," FDR began, speaking in plain, conversational English. He explained how banks worked — deposits, reserves, lending — as if to a neighbor. "Let me make it clear that the banks will take care of all needs." Overnight, trust was restored.
> **Key takeaway**: Technical mastery means nothing if you can't communicate it. FDR's genius was translating complex economics into stories anyone could understand.

### Communication Techniques Common to All Four Leaders

1. **Match the medium to the message** — Lincoln: letters; TR: press and direct action; FDR: radio; LBJ: one-on-one persuasion
2. **Acknowledge the difficulty before offering the solution** — Never skip the empathy step
3. **Use concrete stories, not abstractions** — FDR's "neighbor next door" framing; Lincoln's "If the Union goes, where will we be?"
4. **Repeat the core message relentlessly** — LBJ drove his civil rights message into every single conversation, every day
5. **Let silence work for you** — LBJ mastered the art of asking a question and waiting. He could out-wait anyone.
