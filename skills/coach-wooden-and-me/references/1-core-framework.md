# Core Framework — Coach Wooden and Me

## Prologue: Why It Took Fifty Years to Write This Book

In 2016, Kareem Abdul-Jabbar received the Presidential Medal of Freedom from Barack Obama. Standing beside Tom Hanks, Michael Jordan, Bruce Springsteen, and others, he felt something was wrong. "Someone was missing. The man who, more than anyone else in my life, was responsible for me standing in that exact same spot." Coach John Wooden had received the same medal from President George W. Bush thirteen years earlier.

Abdul-Jabbar waited seven years after Wooden's death to write this book because he needed perspective. "I could have written a book about him after I left UCLA. Or after I retired from the pros. Or after Coach died. But those books wouldn't have been this book." He had to climb "into the top rafters of Pauley Pavilion" — like Wooden did during practices — to see the big picture.

## Chapter 1: When Worlds Collide — Midwestern Hick Meets Harlem Hoopster

Wooden was born in 1910 on a farm in Hall, Indiana — no running water, no electricity, ate what they grew, Christmas presents were an orange and walnuts. "They called me John-Bob." His father Joshua lost the farm in the Depression after tainted vaccine killed their hogs and drought destroyed their crops. Joshua refused to sue the man who sold the tainted vaccine. "He refused to speak an unkind word against anyone." From his father came the two sets of threes: "Never lie, never cheat, never steal" and "Don't whine, don't complain, don't alibi."

Wooden was a star at Purdue — "The Indiana Rubber Man," first three-time consensus All-American. He was recruited by Knute Rockne for Notre Dame football but chose Purdue to be near his future wife Nell. He punched a player who defied him early in his coaching career. He threw a punch at opposing coach Shelby Shake after being accused of paying referees. "Not the kindly, soft-spoken, fount of loving aphorisms that he later became."

**The phone line snowstorm:** Wooden wanted the Minnesota job but needed to bring his assistant. Minnesota didn't call at the scheduled time due to a snowstorm. UCLA called on time and got his commitment. When Minnesota called an hour later, Wooden refused — he had given his word.

Kareem was born in Harlem, his father a transit cop and jazz trombonist who played backup for Marilyn Monroe's "Happy Birthday" to JFK. His mother was a seamstress from Jim Crow North Carolina who pushed him relentlessly. "I want you to be like Jackie Robinson."

**The Greyhound bus:** At 15, Kareem rode a bus to North Carolina and saw "white grocery store, white luncheonette, colored rest rooms" for the first time. He had to ask an older black man: "Am I allowed to walk on the same side of the street as the white people?"

**The Harlem riots:** In the summer of 1964, Kareem got caught in a violent riot after police shot 15-year-old James Powell. "I had never been so scared in my life. But I'd also never been so angry."

**The recruiting visit:** Wooden was the only coach Kareem's parents invited to their apartment. He talked grades, not basketball. "For most students, basketball is temporary. But knowledge is forever."

## Chapter 2: The Game Is Afoot — It's Never About Winning

**Snug and tug:** Wooden's first lesson to the greatest freshman class in history — how to put on socks and sneakers. "'For want of a nail,'" he quoted Benjamin Franklin. "If you do not pull your socks on tightly, you're likely to get blisters. And blisters will keep you from practicing."

**Freshman year:** Wooden told the freshmen they wouldn't play varsity (NCAA rules didn't allow freshmen). He assigned Kareem to "scout" senior center Doug McIntosh — "I want you to be the best damn backup center in the country." This taught humility.

## Chapter 3: Color Bind

Wooden refused to participate in UCLA's first "Black Awareness" event, calling it separatist. Kareem was furious. But he later learned Wooden had quietly desegrated the Indiana State basketball roster years earlier — the first college basketball team to integrate in Indiana. "He never told anyone. Not even me."

After Martin Luther King Jr.'s assassination, Kareem skipped classes and stopped answering calls. Wooden came to his apartment and simply sat with him. "He never said a word. He just sat there with me because he knew that's what I needed."

## Chapter 4: What Would Wooden Do

Kareem converted to Islam after college. "Coach was a devout Christian, I became a devout Muslim." He expected tension. Instead, Wooden asked genuine questions about his faith. "Tell me what being a Muslim means to you." Wooden didn't just tolerate the difference — he respected it.

**The 1968 boycott:** Kareem boycotted the Olympics to protest racism. He organized a student boycott of UCLA classes. Some boosters wanted him kicked off the team. Wooden publicly supported him. "'He's here to get an education and play basketball. Both of those things are still happening.'"

## Chapter 5: We've Got Trouble, Right Here in Pauley Pavilion

After UCLA, Kareem struggled. The NBA was grueling. His marriage failed. His home burned down, destroying his 5,000-album jazz collection. He considered quitting. Wooden called every week. "He didn't give advice. He just listened."

## Chapter 6: "Time Can Bend Your Knees"

Nell Wooden died in 1985. "When Nell passed, a part of Coach died with her." Kareem attended the funeral and sat with the family. In the years that followed, their roles reversed — Kareem began checking on Wooden. "I would sit with him at his home, quietly watching a Western movie or baseball game on his small TV, just enjoying the familiar warmth and comfort of his presence. It was like going to church."

## Chapter 7: Our Long Day's Journey into Night

Wooden at ninety-nine. The photo of them walking off the court hand-in-hand (2007) — Kareem helping him walk. Their last conversation was about jazz — Kareem explaining that basketball was jazz. "You taught us to play basketball jazz." Wooden died June 4, 2010, at age ninety-nine.
