# Key Principles — Coach Wooden and Me

## I. The Process Is the Reward

"Don't think about winning the game. Just do everything possible to prepare. As long as you know you have done everything possible and you have given your best self on the court, that is your reward. The scoreboard is meaningless." Wooden hated sports movies that ended with the underdog winning — they should freeze-frame at the lesson. "The traveling is the reward, not reaching the destination."

## II. "Never Lie, Never Cheat, Never Steal" and "Don't Whine, Don't Complain, Don't Alibi"

Wooden's father's two sets of threes formed the bedrock of his philosophy. Joshua Wooden lost the family farm because of a tainted vaccine — and refused to sue. "He refused to speak an unkind word against anyone." Coach Wooden was exactly the same — in fifty years Kareem never heard him say something unkind about someone.

## III. Character Over Talent in Recruiting

"I wanted young men who wanted to play for UCLA, and not one that I had to talk into playing for UCLA." Wooden recruited character as much as ability. When a potential recruit snapped at his mother during a home visit, Wooden crossed him off the list. "I did not want a person who was that disrespectful on my team."

## IV. "For Want of a Nail" — Details Matter

Wooden's first lesson was how to put on socks and sneakers. "If you do not pull your socks on tightly, you're likely to get blisters. And blisters will keep you from practicing." Benjamin Franklin's quote: "For want of a nail the shoe was lost, for want of a shoe the horse was lost... and all for the want of a horseshoe-nail."

## V. Silent Support Speaks Loudest

After MLK's assassination, Kareem isolated himself. Wooden came to his apartment and sat with him. "He never said a word. He just sat there with me because he knew that's what I needed." When Kareem boycotted the Olympics, Wooden publicly backed him. When boosters wanted him off the team: "'He's here to get an education and play basketball. Both of those things are still happening.'"

## VI. Friendship Across Difference

"Coach was an old white Midwesterner with old-fashioned ideals; I was a quiet but cocky black kid from New York City who towered eighteen inches over him. He was a devout Christian; I became a devout Muslim. He loved big band music; I loved modern jazz." Their friendship thrived not despite their differences but because of mutual respect for them.

## VII. Walking Together at the End

The most powerful image in the book: Wooden, frail at 97, asking Kareem to hold his hand and help him walk off the court. "I felt tenderness, protectiveness, but not pity or sorrow. I was smiling." Fifty years after Wooden guided him, Kareem guided Wooden. "I knew how much he taught me. I knew I was still following in his footsteps, even though he was beside me."
