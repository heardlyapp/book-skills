# 1 — Core Framework

> Source: *Go for No!* by Richard Fenton & Andrea Waltz.

## The Three Frameworks

### Framework A: The Rejection Reframe

The core idea: success and failure are not opposites. "You can't have one without the other, and if you're going to avoid one, you're going to avoid both." Most people see no as a final verdict. The Go for No! framework sees no as progress.

> **Case: The Insurance Cold Call Experiment** (The Golf Course, Chapter 3): A consultant told Chicago insurance agents to call door to door and start every pitch with: "You don't want to buy any life insurance, do you?" 59 out of 60 said no, but one said yes. At 8 calls per hour, the average agent made one sale per day. The "you don't want to buy" approach — the most negative opening imaginable — worked because it maximized the no's and let the yeses surface naturally.

### Framework B: The Failure Quotient (FQ)

Your FQ is how many times you are willing to fail before succeeding. Lincoln lost 8 of 10 elections. Colonel Sanders got zero restaurants in 2 years of trying. Allen Breed waited 30 years before his first airbag sale. Their FQ was extraordinarily high.

> **Case: The Pike Syndrome** (The Golf Course, Chapter 3): A pike battered its nose against a glass partition for hours, then gave up. When scientists removed the glass, the pike stayed on its side of the tank. Lunch was inches away, but the pike had been conditioned to believe the barrier was still there. Most people are pike.

### Framework C: No-Goals

Instead of "I want 4 sales this week," set "I want 16 no's this week." The result: when you're on a hot streak (3 yeses on Monday), you don't stop — you're behind on your no-goal, so you work harder. The no-goal protects you from the success-slump.

> **Case: The Copier Salesman's Awakening** (The Book's Structure): Eric Bratton had 3 sales on Monday and then stopped for the week. "Success has ruined many a man." The no-goal would have kept him working.
