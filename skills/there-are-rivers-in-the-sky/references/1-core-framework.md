> Source: There Are Rivers in the Sky, Elif Shafak (2024). A novel about a drop of water that witnesses history.

# Core Framework — The Drop as Witness and Storytelling as Survival

## The Model

Shafak uses a single drop of water as a narrative device that connects ancient Nineveh to modern London. The framework: a non-human witness allowed to observe humanity across millennia reveals patterns that human narrators cannot see.

> **Case: The Drop on Nineveh**: A single raindrop falls on the walls of Nineveh, the capital of the Assyrian Empire. It lands on a clay tablet being inscribed by a scribe — the Epic of Gilgamesh. The drop witnesses the creation of the oldest story in the world. Centuries later, the same drop (through the water cycle) falls on London, on Istanbul, on the Thames. It has seen empires rise and fall. It has been drunk by kings and beggars. It has been frozen in ice and flown in clouds. The drop is the thread that connects every character in the novel.
> **Key takeaway**: You are connected to the past in ways you cannot see. The water you drink today fell on the pyramids. Find the threads that connect you to what came before.

> **Case: The Library of Ashurbanipal**: King Ashurbanipal of Assyria assembled the greatest library of the ancient world. He sent scribes across the empire to collect every text they could find. He believed that knowledge was the only thing that could survive the fall of his kingdom. When Nineveh was destroyed, the library was buried under rubble. It stayed buried for 2,500 years. When archaeologists finally discovered the tablets, they found the Epic of Gilgamesh — the story of a king who, like Ashurbanipal, wanted to defeat death through lasting achievement.
> **Key takeaway**: Knowledge outlasts empires. The library was buried, but it was found. What you write today may be read by people in a world you cannot imagine.

> **Case: The Flood Narrative**: The Epic of Gilgamesh contains a flood story that predates the biblical account by over a thousand years. In Shafak's novel, the flood is a recurring motif — the deluge that destroys and the survivors who rebuild. The novel asks: what do we carry with us when the water rises? What do we save? The answer: stories. The flood takes everything except what is written down or remembered.
> **Key takeaway**: When everything is taken from you, you still have what you remember and what you have written. Protect your stories. They are the only things that cannot be drowned.
