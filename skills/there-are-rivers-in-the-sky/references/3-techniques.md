> Source: There Are Rivers in the Sky, Elif Shafak (2024).

# Techniques — Water as Connective Tissue, Story as Bridge

## Practical Methods

> **Case: The Water Cycle as Narrative Device**: Shafak uses the water cycle — evaporation, condensation, precipitation — as a structural principle for the novel. A drop that evaporates from the Tigris becomes a cloud that travels to London. It falls as rain, flows into the Thames, is drunk by a character, becomes part of their blood, is breathed out as vapor, and rises again. This cycle mirrors the novel's structure: characters separated by centuries are connected by the same water molecules. The physical world becomes a metaphor for connection across time.
> **Key takeaway**: You are physically connected to everyone who has ever lived. The water in your body has been in the bodies of kings and slaves, poets and conquerors. You are never alone.

> **Case: The Clay Tablet as Immortality**: Ashurbanipal's scribes inscribed stories onto clay tablets that were then fired — a process that made them virtually indestructible. When the library was buried, the tablets survived. The story of Gilgamesh, lost for two and a half millennia, was read again in the 19th century. Shafak's novel asks: what are you inscribing onto your own clay tablets? What will survive of you? The answer is not your wealth or your status. It is your stories.
> **Key takeaway**: Choose your medium for longevity. Clay tablets outlast paper. Paper outlasts digital. Stories outlast everything. Write your stories in a form that can survive.

> **Case: The Thames as Modern River of Stories**: In the novel's contemporary sections, the Thames flows through London carrying the same water that once flowed through Nineveh. Characters walk along its banks, unaware that the water they see fell on ancient Assyria. The river is a silent witness, holding memory that the characters cannot access. Shafak suggests that cities are built on rivers not just for practical reasons but because rivers connect the living to the dead. They are arteries of memory.
> **Key takeaway**: Find your river — the physical place that connects you to what came before. Visit it. Walk along it. The water that flows past you has seen everything.
