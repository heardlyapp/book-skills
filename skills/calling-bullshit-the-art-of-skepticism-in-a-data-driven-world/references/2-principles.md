# Principles: Statistical Fallacies and Causal Confusion

> Source: Calling Bullshit, Chapters 4-6

## Correlation Is Not Causation

The most violated rule in data analysis. Two variables that move together may be unrelated, share a common cause, or have a causal relationship in either direction.

> **Case: The Butter and Maine** (Chapter 4): Bergstrom shows a classic spurious correlation: US margarine consumption correlates with the divorce rate in Maine. No causal link — the relationship is random. This is why you must always question correlation claims.

> **Key takeaway**: Whenever someone says "studies show X causes Y," ask: how did they rule out coincidence, reverse causation, and hidden variables?

## Selection Bias

When your data sample is not representative of the population, your conclusions are wrong. This is one of the most common and dangerous errors.

> **Case: The Social Media Poll** (Chapter 6): Political opinions on Twitter do not represent the general population. Twitter users are younger, more liberal, and more extreme than average. Surveying only Twitter users introduces selection bias.

> **Key takeaway**: Always ask: who is included in this data — and who is excluded?

## P-Hacking and Statistical Tricks

When researchers test many hypotheses but only report the significant ones, they create false findings.

> **Key takeaway**: Selection bias is everywhere. The most important question: is the data representative of the thing we're trying to understand?
