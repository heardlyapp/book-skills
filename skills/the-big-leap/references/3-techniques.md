## Practical Techniques

### 1. The Zone Audit

Write down how you spend your time. Categorize each activity into one of the four zones. Calculate the percentage. If your Zone of Genius time is under 5%, you are upper limiting.

### 2. The Upper Limit Detection

When something good happens, notice what happens next. Do you procrastinate? Get sick? Pick a fight? Forget something important? That is your upper limit at work.

> **Case: The Post-Conference Breakdown** (Chapter 4): Hendricks describes a pattern where after giving a successful talk or workshop, he would feel a crash — fatigue, self-doubt, or irritability. He learned that this was not exhaustion — it was upper limiting. The success triggered the fear of more. Awareness allowed him to break the pattern.
> **Key takeaway**: The crash after success is not inevitable. It is an upper limit. When you notice it, you can choose a different response.

### 3. The Hidden Fear Inquiry

When you notice an upper limit, ask: "Which of the four hidden fears is active?" Being seen? Expanding? Being a burden? Being a separate self? Naming the fear dissolves its power.

### 4. The Einstein Principle Commitment

Schedule 5 hours per week in your Zone of Genius. Put it on your calendar. Protect it like a meeting with the CEO — because it is.

> **Case: The 5-Hour Shift** (Chapter 5): Hendricks tells of an executive who started spending 5 hours a week on strategic vision (his genius). Within three months, his company's revenue doubled. The time he spent in genius was more productive than the 40 hours he had been spending in excellence.
> **Key takeaway**: Genius time is not a luxury — it is the most productive time you have. The Einstein Principle is a productivity hack, not a spiritual concept.
