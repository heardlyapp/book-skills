## 6 Anti-Patterns of Self-Limiting Behavior

### 1. "I'm Just Not Motivated"

Lack of motivation is often not laziness — it is upper limiting. You unconsciously slow down because the next level of success is frightening.

### 2. "It Was Luck"

When you attribute your success to "luck" or "it wasn't that big a deal," you are upper limiting. You are minimizing your achievement to avoid the pressure of more.

> **Case: The Million-Dollar Deal** (Chapter 2): A sales executive closed the largest deal of his career. His immediate response: "It was luck. The timing was right." He could not say: "I created this through my unique ability." His upper limit would not let him own his genius.
> **Key takeaway**: Diminishing your success is a form of upper limiting. Owning your genius is the leap.

### 3. "If I Succeed, People Will Expect More"

Yes, they will. That is the fear of being seen and the fear of expanding. But more expectation is not a burden — it is a platform for greater contribution.

### 4. "My Body Can't Handle It"

Physical symptoms after success are real — but they are often upper limits, not medical conditions. When you see a doctor and get told "nothing is wrong," consider an upper limit.

### 5. "It's My Partner's Fault"

Relationship upper limits are often blamed on the other person. The partner who picks a fight after a peak experience is not doing it to you — they are doing it for the relationship's upper limit.

### 6. "I Don't Have a Genius"

Everyone has a Zone of Genius. It is not about being the best in the world at something — it is about what you uniquely bring. If you think you do not have one, you are upper limiting on the very idea of genius.

> **Case: The Author Who Stopped Writing** (Chapter 6): Hendricks describes an author who wrote a successful book — and then could not write another word for two years. The fear: the first book was seen by thousands. A second book would be seen by even more. The fear of being seen paralyzed her.
> **Key takeaway**: Success creates visibility. Visibility triggers the fear of being seen. The only cure is to be seen anyway.
