# Techniques — Boundaries

## How to Set and Maintain Healthy Boundaries (Based on Cloud & Townsend)

### Technique 1: The Good Samaritan Test
Ask: "Am I the Good Samaritan who just helped someone in genuine need, or is this person manipulating me to give more than I want to give?" The Samaritan helped, set a limit, and moved on. The boundaryless version stayed three days, lost his business, and resented the victim. If you're feeling resentful after helping, you overstepped your boundary.

### Technique 2: The "No" Muscle Exercise
Start small. Say no to a telemarketer. Decline an invitation you don't want. Refuse an extra task at work. "The 'no' muscle grows with use, like any other muscle." Cloud: "If you can't say no to the little things, you won't be able to say no to the big ones."

### Technique 3: The Consequence Statement
State your boundary + state the consequence for violating it. "If you call me after 9 PM, I won't answer the phone." "If you criticize my parenting in front of the children, we will leave immediately." The consequence is about what YOU will do — not about controlling the other person.

### Technique 4: The Sowing and Reaping Test
Ask: "Am I protecting this person from the natural consequences of their behavior?" If yes, you're interfering with the Law of Sowing and Reaping. "Hunger is a consequence of laziness." Letting someone fail is sometimes the most loving thing you can do.

### Technique 5: The Compliance-Avoidance Balance
"If you always say yes to others' requests (compliant) and never ask for help yourself (avoidant), you're living with reversed boundaries." Fix: practice saying no AND practice asking for help. The two must balance.

### Technique 6: The Guilt Check
When you feel guilty about setting a boundary, ask: "Is this guilt from God (conviction) or from my overly strict conscience (condemnation)?" God's guilt leads to change. False guilt keeps you stuck. "If I am feeling guilty after doing the right thing, the guilt is probably from the wrong source."

### Technique 7: The Boundary File
Write down one boundary you need to set. Write down the exact words you will use. Practice them out loud. Then do it. "Boundaries don't set themselves — you set them." The act of writing and rehearsing makes it real.
