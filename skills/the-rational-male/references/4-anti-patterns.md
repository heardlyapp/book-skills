# Common Mistakes — Anti-Patterns

Tomassi identifies the most common errors men make in intergender dynamics. These are the patterns that keep men stuck.

## Anti-Pattern 1: The Nice Guy Syndrome

The belief that being "nice" entitles a man to romantic success. It does not. Niceness is baseline decency. It is table stakes, not a winning hand.

**Case: The "Good Guy" Who Resents Women.** A man treats women well, opens doors, pays for dates, listens to their problems. He expects romantic reciprocation. When it does not come, he feels cheated. Tomassi: "You are not entitled to sex because you held a door open. You are not a 'nice guy' — you are a guy doing the bare minimum and expecting a reward."

**Case: The Friend Zone.** The "friend zone" is not something women do TO men. It is something men walk into by failing to establish sexual frame early. A man who acts like a friend will be treated like a friend.

## Anti-Pattern 2: The ONEitis Trap

Fixating on a single woman leads to pedestalization, scarcity behavior, and loss of frame.

**Case: The Worship.** A man with ONEitis treats a woman as if she is perfect. He overlooks her flaws, tolerates her disrespect, and lives in fear of losing her. The irony: his pedestalization makes him less attractive. She cannot respect a man who puts her on a pedestal.

**Case: The Abandonment.** When the ONEitis relationship ends, the man is devastated not because of the woman but because of the fantasy he built around her. He was in love with an imaginary version of her. Recovery requires killing the fantasy.

## Anti-Pattern 3: Negotiating Desire

You cannot negotiate genuine attraction. Trying to convince a woman to be attracted to you — through gifts, favors, or logical arguments — is a guaranteed path to failure.

**Case: The Gift-Giver.** A man buys expensive gifts, pays for dinners, offers to fix her car. He is trying to buy attraction. It does not work. Attraction is not a transaction.

**Case: The Logical Argument.** A man tries to explain why she should be with him: "I have a good job, I'm stable, I would treat you well." Attraction does not respond to logic. It responds to frame, presence, and behavior.

## Anti-Pattern 4: The White Knight

The White Knight believes he can save a woman from her problems. He becomes her therapist, her rescuer, her emotional support animal. In exchange, he expects her love.

**Case: The Broken Woman.** A woman with a history of bad relationships, trauma, or instability attracts a White Knight who believes his love will heal her. Instead, she drains him emotionally, then leaves him for a man who does not try to fix her.

**Case: The Savior Schema.** Tomassi argues that the White Knight is not altruistic — he is ego-driven. He wants to be the hero. But women are not projects. A man who treats a woman as a project does not respect her as an equal.

## Anti-Pattern 5: The Unplugged Zealot

After discovering the Red Pill, some men become zealots — aggressively preaching the truth, hating women, blaming society. This is a stage, not a destination.

**Case: The Bitter Phase.** Most men go through a phase of anger after unplugging. They see hypergamy everywhere. They become misogynistic in their rhetoric. Tomassi warns against staying in this phase: "The bitterness is understandable but unproductive."

**Case: The Convert's Arrogance.** The recently unplugged man thinks he has found the secret to all human interaction. He lectures other men. He alienates women. Tomassi: the goal is not to be right — it is to be effective.
