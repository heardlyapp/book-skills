> Source: The E-Myth Revisited, Prologue through Chapter 19 — 6 anti-patterns that kill small businesses

## Anti-Pattern 1: The Technician Trap

The most destructive anti-pattern in small business. You start your business because you're good at the technical work (baking, coding, plumbing). You assume that knowing the work means you can run the business. You end up working harder than any employee, doing the work you love, while the business part of the business falls apart.

> **Case: Sarah's Pie Shop** (Introduction/Chapter 1): Sarah makes the best pies. She opens a shop. She loves making pies. She makes pies all day. She keeps making pies. She stops doing the books, the marketing, the hiring, the systems. The business bleeds money. Sarah is exhausted. She blames the business. But the business is not the problem — Sarah's refusal to stop being a Technician is.
> **Key takeaway**: If you won the "best technician" award and your business is failing, you're winning the wrong game. The game of business is not about technical excellence — it's about organizational design.

## Anti-Pattern 2: The Delegation Trap

"Nobody can do it as well as I can." This is the phrase that kills small businesses. You hire someone, but you can't stop yourself from redoing their work, second-guessing their decisions, or jumping in when a customer complains. You create employees who don't take initiative because you've taught them that you'll overrule them anyway.

> **Case: The Teenage Employee** (Chapter 4): Gerber describes the Adolescence phase: the owner hires a teenager to help. The teenager doesn't do things exactly the owner's way. The owner corrects every mistake. The teenager stops making decisions and starts asking for permission. The owner thinks "I knew I should have done it myself." The teenager thinks "why does he bother hiring me if he's going to do everything anyway?"
> **Key takeaway**: If you can't let someone do it wrong, you can't let them do it at all. And if you can't let them do it at all, you can't grow.

## Anti-Pattern 3: The Comfort Zone Ceiling

Every business owner has a comfort zone — the scale and complexity they feel comfortable operating in. The business cannot grow beyond this ceiling. The only way to grow is to expand the comfort zone — which means doing things that feel uncomfortable, risky, and wrong.

> **Case: The Mechanic Who Can't Delegate** (Chapter 5): A master mechanic opens a shop. He's the best mechanic in town. He hires other mechanics, but they are all less skilled than he is. The quality of the shop's work is capped by the second-best mechanic — who is still worse than the owner. To grow, the owner must design systems that allow average mechanics to produce excellent work. This means documenting his knowledge, standardizing processes, and trusting the system. This feels wrong to him. So the business stays small.
> **Key takeaway**: Your comfort zone is your business's ceiling. If you want the business to grow, you must grow — into the discomfort of systematization.

## Anti-Pattern 4: The "More Work" Solution

When a business is struggling, the owner's instinct is to do more of the work — work longer hours, serve more customers, do more marketing. Gerber calls this "the Technician's solution." It feels productive but it's not — it delays the real solution, which is systemization.

> **Case: The $35,000 Client** (Chapter 3): Gerber describes a consultant who was working 100-hour weeks for a single $35,000 client. The client consumed every waking moment. The consultant couldn't take on other clients. He couldn't fire the client either (too much revenue). The solution was not to work harder for the $35,000 client. The solution was to design a system that allowed a junior consultant to serve that client, freeing the owner to take on new business.
> **Key takeaway**: When you're overwhelmed, the answer is never "work harder." The answer is "design a system that does the work without you."

## Anti-Pattern 5: The Personality-Driven Business

The business that depends on the owner's personality — their charm, expertise, relationships — is not sellable. When the owner leaves, the business has no value. This is the most common mistake of "lifestyle businesses."

> **Case: The Unsalable Business** (Chapter 7): Gerber asks: "What happens to your business if you get hit by a truck?" Most small business owners have no answer. Their business has no value beyond their personal involvement. A franchise, by contrast, can lose its founder and keep operating. The test: can your business survive without you?
> **Key takeaway**: A business that can't survive without you is not an asset — it's a liability disguised as a lifestyle.

## Anti-Pattern 6: Starting Without a Strategic Objective

Most small businesses start without a clear standard. The owner knows they want to "make money" or "do good work" but cannot define what success looks like in measurable terms. As a result, every decision feels arbitrary.

> **Case: The Cleaning Company** (Chapter 13): Gerber contrasts two cleaning companies. One has a Strategic Objective: "We will leave every home cleaner than it has ever been." The other just "cleans houses." The first company has a standard against which every decision is measured. The second company has no standard — every employee decides for themselves what "clean enough" means. Which company can train new employees? Which can franchise? Which can sell?
> **Key takeaway**: A business without a clear standard is not a business — it's a collection of improvisations. The standard is what makes the system possible.
