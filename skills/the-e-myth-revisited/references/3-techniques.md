## 1. The Seven-Step Business Development Program

Gerber's core methodology for designing a business that works without you:

**Step 1: Primary Aim** — What kind of life do you want to live? The business serves this purpose.
**Step 2: Strategic Objective** — What will the business look like when it's complete? Define the standards.
**Step 3: Organizational Strategy** — How will the work be organized? Design the structure first.
**Step 4: Management Strategy** — How will the organization be managed? Define the system of accountability.
**Step 5: People Strategy** — How will people be hired, trained, and developed?
**Step 6: Marketing Strategy** — How will you attract and retain customers systematically?
**Step 7: Systems Strategy** — How will everything be documented and systemized?

> **Case: The Strategic Objective** (Chapter 13): Gerber asks: what standard of performance will define your business? He gives the example of a cleaning company: "We will leave every home cleaner than it has ever been." This is not a mission statement — it's a standard that every system is designed to deliver. When an employee asks "is this clean enough?", the standard answers the question.
> **Key takeaway**: A Strategic Objective is not a goal. It's a standard by which every decision is measured.

## 2. The Working ON Your Business Session

Gerber's technique for actually doing strategic work:

**How to do it**: Schedule one morning per week (same time, same place) to work ON your business. No phone. No email. No customers. Use the Business Development Program as your agenda. Start with your Primary Aim, review your Strategic Objective, check each system.

> **Case: The Four-Hour Block** (Chapter 10): Most small business owners say "I don't have time to work ON my business — I'm too busy working IN it." Gerber's response: "If you can't find four hours a week to work on your business, you will spend the rest of your life working in it." The four-hour block is non-negotiable. Same time, same place, every week.
> **Key takeaway**: Schedule "working ON" time like a meeting with your most important client — because it is.

## 3. The Franchise Prototype Design Method

Design every aspect of your business as if you were creating a franchise manual:

**How to do it**: For every process in your business, ask: "If I had to teach someone else to do this perfectly in one week, what would I write down?" Document it. Test it. Refine it. Repeat. The goal: turn tacit knowledge (in your head) into explicit knowledge (written down).

> **Case: The Operations Manual** (Chapters 8-9): Gerber describes the franchise prototype as having three components: (1) The operations manual — every step of every process, (2) The training program — how to teach someone the manual, (3) The support system — how to help when something goes wrong. Most small businesses have none of these.
> **Key takeaway**: If you can't write it down, you don't have a system. You have a habit.

## 4. The Delegation Framework

How to delegate without losing control:

**Step 1**: Define the outcome — exactly what does success look like?
**Step 2**: Define the standards — what are the quality criteria?
**Step 3**: Define the system — what process should be followed?
**Step 4**: Train the person — teach them the system.
**Step 5**: Let go completely — don't check, don't interfere, don't overrule.
**Step 6**: Create feedback loops — how will results be measured and reported?

> **Case: The Management Strategy** (Chapter 15): Gerber argues that most small business owners delegate tasks but not decisions. The result: employees wait for permission. The solution is "management by system, not by person." Define the boundaries within which employees can make decisions autonomously. The Management Strategy answers: "Who is responsible for what, and how do they know if they're succeeding?"
> **Key takeaway**: Real delegation is transferring decision rights, not just tasks.

## 5. The Primary Aim Exercise

A structured approach to finding your Primary Aim:

**Step 1**: Imagine your life 20 years from now, when everything has gone perfectly. Describe it in detail.
**Step 2**: Ask: What am I doing? Who am I with? How do I feel? What does my day look like?
**Step 3**: Ask: What would I do if I knew I could not fail?
**Step 4**: Ask: If I had all the money I needed, what would I do with my life?
**Step 5**: Write a one-page description of the life you want. This is your Primary Aim.

> **Case: The Letter to Sarah** (Chapter 19): When Sarah (from the introduction) does this exercise, she realizes she doesn't want to be a famous baker. She wants to spend time with her children, have a meaningful connection to her community, and feel proud of her work. The pie shop was supposed to serve this life, not consume it.
> **Key takeaway**: Most people start businesses to achieve a certain kind of life. But the business takes over and destroys that life. The Primary Aim is a compass that keeps the business pointed in the right direction.
