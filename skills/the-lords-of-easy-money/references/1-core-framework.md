# Core Framework: How the Fed Remade the Economy

## The Central Thesis

"Between 2008 and 2022, the Federal Reserve conducted the largest monetary experiment in history. It created trillions of dollars, bought trillions in bonds, and kept interest rates at zero for over a decade. The consequences are still unfolding."

> **Case: The Taper Tantrum** (Chapter 1): In 2013, Fed Chair Ben Bernanke hinted the Fed would slow its bond buying. Markets crashed immediately — the Dow dropped 300 points in minutes, and bond yields spiked. The Fed quickly backed off. This showed that markets had become addicted to easy money.
> **Key takeaway**: Quantitative easing created dependency. The market couldn't function without the Fed's support. Every attempt to "normalize" policy was met with market resistance.

## Key Concepts

| Concept | Definition |
|---------|------------|
| Federal Reserve | U.S. central bank, independent agency, dual mandate (employment + inflation) |
| Quantitative Easing | Central bank creates reserves to buy bonds, lowering long-term rates |
| Federal Funds Rate | Short-term interest rate the Fed controls directly |
| The Fed Put | Implicit guarantee the Fed will rescue markets during downturns |
| Moral Hazard | Risk-taking increases when actors believe the Fed will save them |

> **Case: The Banker Who Warned Us** (Chapter 3): Thomas Hoenig, president of the Kansas City Fed, was a lone dissenter. He argued that QE would create asset bubbles, enrich Wall Street at the expense of Main Street, and eventually cause inflation. He was ignored. His warnings proved prescient.
> **Key takeaway**: There were voices inside the Fed warning about the consequences of QE. They were overruled by the consensus that "extraordinary times called for extraordinary measures."
