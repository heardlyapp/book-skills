# Core Framework: Culture as Strategy

## The Know-It-All Culture

When Nadella became CEO, Microsoft had a culture of internal competition. Divisions fought each other. Windows was king. The culture was hierarchical, political, and resistant to change. Employees prided themselves on being the smartest people in the room. This know-it-all culture was preventing innovation.

## The Learn-It-All Transformation

Nadella introduced the concept of a growth mindset, borrowed from psychologist Carol Dweck. The shift from know-it-all to learn-it-all was not just philosophical. It changed how Microsoft operated. Employees were encouraged to admit they did not know something. Experimentation was valued over certainty. Failure was treated as a learning opportunity, not a career-ending event.

> **Case: The First All-Hands Meeting** (Chapter 1): Nadella held an all-hands meeting shortly after becoming CEO. He asked employees to read a book about the growth mindset. He told them that the culture needed to change. He modeled vulnerability by admitting that Microsoft had lost its way. The message was clear: the old ways were over. A new culture was beginning.
> **Key takeaway:** Culture change starts with the leader admitting that change is needed. Vulnerability is not weakness. It is the foundation of trust.

## The Three Big Bets

Nadella made three strategic bets: cloud computing (Azure), artificial intelligence, and reinventing productivity through Microsoft 365. These bets defined Microsoft's trajectory for the next decade.

> **Case: The Cloud Pivot** (Chapter 2): When Nadella took over, Microsoft had fallen behind Amazon in cloud computing. AWS was the market leader. Microsoft had Azure, but it was a distant second. Nadella made cloud the center of Microsoft strategy. He invested billions. He reorganized the company around cloud. Within five years, Azure was the fastest-growing major cloud platform, rivaling AWS.
> **Key takeaway:** Strategic bets require conviction. Nadella bet the company on cloud when Microsoft was behind. Conviction, investment, and execution closed the gap.
Nadella also focused on Microsoft culture of inclusion. He believed that diversity of thought leads to better products. He pushed for more diverse hiring. He created programs to support underrepresented groups. The shift from a homogenous engineering culture to an inclusive one was part of the broader cultural transformation. Inclusion was not just a values initiative. It was a business strategy for building better products for a diverse world.
Nadella approach to transformation was gradual but relentless. He did not make dramatic changes overnight. He changed the culture slowly, one meeting at a time. He focused on changing what people talked about. Instead of discussing competitors, he discussed customers. Instead of defending old products, he explored new possibilities. The slow, patient approach to culture change was more effective than a dramatic restructuring would have been.
The growth mindset was not just a slogan at Microsoft. It was embedded in performance reviews, hiring criteria, and promotion decisions. Employees were evaluated not just on results but on how they achieved them. Did they collaborate? Did they learn from mistakes? Did they help others grow? These behavioral criteria reinforced the cultural shift. What gets measured gets done.
