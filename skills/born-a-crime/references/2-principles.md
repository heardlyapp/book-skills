# 2 — Key Principles

> Source: *Born a Crime* by Trevor Noah — Chapters 1-18.

## The 8 Principles

### 1. Language is Identity

"Language, even more than color, defines who you are to people." Trevor learned that his appearance marked him as a threat in the townships and an oddity in white areas. But when he spoke Zulu, he was Zulu. When he spoke Afrikaans, he was understood. Language isn't just communication — it's your passport to belonging.

### 2. The System is Designed to Limit You — Act Accordingly

Apartheid's genius was getting the majority to turn on itself. The system was designed to make you believe your place was fixed. Patricia refused this logic. She drove to white neighborhoods, skated on white ice rinks, went to white churches. She didn't wait for permission.

### 3. The Black Tax is Real — But You Can't Pay It Forever

"The curse of being black and poor is that instead of being free to use your skills to move forward, you lose everything just trying to bring everyone else back up to zero." Patricia was the first in her family to earn a salary. Her mother took it all for the family. She had to run away to build her own life.

### 4. Discipline Has Purpose. Abuse Does Not.

Trevor distinguishes clearly: his mother's beatings were discipline — painful, but with a purpose and bounded by love. Abel's violence was terror — no purpose, no boundary, just an expression of rage. The difference is everything.

### 5. Mock the System Before You Fight It

Patricia didn't refuse to bow to the men in Tzaneen. She overdid it so absurdly that she exposed the absurdity of the ritual. "Don't fight the system. Mock the system." Mockery disarms more effectively than confrontation.

### 6. Always Keep an Exit

After the pantry beating, Trevor never let Abel come between him and a door again. This is not paranoia — it's survival. If you're in a dangerous situation, your first priority is keeping a way out.

### 7. Choose Who You Are

At H.A. Jack Primary, Trevor realized he had to choose. He chose black. Not because he looked black — he looked colored. But because the black kids were his people. "Belonging is an act of choosing." You decide which tribe you belong to.

### 8. Forget the Pain, But Remember the Lesson

Trevor received beatings, got his hair burned off, burned down a house. He kept getting up. He wasn't traumatized — he moved forward. "I never let the memory of something painful prevent me from trying something new."
