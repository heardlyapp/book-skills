# 3 — Techniques

> Source: *Born a Crime* by Trevor Noah — practical methods from the memoir.

## Practical Tools

### 1. The Simulcast Technique

Trevor's term: "I would simulcast — give you the program in your own tongue." Speak to people in their language, using their accent, their cadence, their rhythm. This disarms suspicion immediately.

**How to apply:** Before you enter a room, identify the language (literal or metaphorical) of the people you're about to meet. Prepare your opening in that language. Let them hear themselves in your voice.

### 2. The Chameleon Entry

When entering a group where you don't belong: listen first, identify their language, then speak. The mugging example: Trevor waited, heard Zulu, replied in Zulu. He didn't guess — he gathered intelligence first.

### 3. The Over-Compliance Mockery

Patricia's strategy for dealing with sexist customs in Tzaneen. When asked to bow, she didn't refuse; she over-bowed. She groveled so extravagantly that everyone became uncomfortable. "Don't fight the system. Mock the system."

### 4. The "One Good Thing" Memory Practice

Trevor's ability to weather hardship came from a practice: he remembered the moments of joy with his mother — the drive-in movies, the ice rink, the baloney sandwiches in the park — and held those against the months of Mopane worms and garage floors.

### 5. The Letter-Writing Protocol

Trevor and his mom wrote formal business letters to each other about disputes. This practice gave each time to formulate arguments and made the exchange civil rather than emotional. "I have received your correspondence earlier. I am delighted to say that I am ahead of schedule on the dishes…"

### 6. The Pantry Security Check

After Abel's attack, Trevor always kept himself between his own body and the exit of any room Abel was in. "Never again did I let him come between me and a door." This is the one-touch rule: if you need to leave, you are never more than one movement from the door.
